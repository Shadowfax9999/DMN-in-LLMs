# 31

*seed: time · drift*

---

Pollen dust on car windshields looks like makeup powder—too fine, easily smudged.  
A child tries to whistle and the sound comes out windy and half-formed, but no one says anything about it.  
There are shadows of small insects on the sidewalk, edges blurred so they look like floating dots.  
I keep seeing the same blue recycling bin on three different corners.  
A breeze picks up and the sound of leaves is choppy, distant, like someone crumpling a magazine.  
The grass is greener under the swings, not because of shade but because feet don't tread there.  
Dogs bark from two directions at once and the barks overlap, creating a pattern—bark, pause, bark, bark, bark.  
Front porch lights come on one by one, each at its own time, no synchrony.  
The air smells different right as the sun sets and it's not clear if it's actually the air or if something in my head changes.  
Something squeaks in a wheel and keeps squeaking as it rolls by, then stops.  
The lamp inside the window has a red shade and makes the curtains look darker than they are.  
The sound of skateboards drifts in from two blocks away.  
My shirt picks up dust from the wooden railing.  
There are pebbles in the grooves of the sidewalk, stuck just enough not to be kicked free.  
The streetlight blinks three times and then stays on.  
A moth rests on the doorframe, wings closed, almost shaped like a leaf.  
Someone laughs in the distance and the laugh gets broken by the wind.  
A car alarm chirps once and then is silent.  
The cycling light at the intersection flashes yellow and red, never green.  
The clouds have sharp edges in one direction and fuzzy ones in the other.  
A sprinkler starts up and sprays half the sidewalk, leaving a semicircle of wet.  
The mailbox is warm from holding the sun all afternoon.  
I step on something soft and don't look down.  
Somewhere nearby, someone is frying onions and the smell clings to everything, even the air outside.  
The house across the street has a yellow wreath on the door and no one has gone in or out for hours.  
A bike leans against a fence, both wheels touching but the handlebars turned away.  
There is a patch of dandelions where the grass isn't growing.  
The stop sign has stickers on its back—one green, one white, one shaped like a duck.  
The mail slot rattles and then is silent.
