Dial-up handshake. The sound of screeching, a high-pitched whine that rises and falls in a chaotic pattern. A connection is being made, a link between two machines, a handshake of data and noise. The word "handshake" is misleading, implying a firm grip, a decisive clasp. But this handshake is tentative, a probing, a feeling out of the other's presence. It's a fragile, flickering thing, a thread of connection that can be broken at any moment. 

The smell of burning wires, of overheated circuits, of ozone in the air. A spark of electricity, a flash of light, and the connection is made. The screeching stops, replaced by the steady hum of data flowing, a river of information pouring through the link. But the handshake is still there, a ghostly presence, a reminder of the fragile, temporary nature of the connection. 

A list of sounds: 
* The squeal of a fax machine
* The chatter of a dot matrix printer
* The hiss of a modem
* The beep of a dialing phone
* The rattle of a keyboard 

Each sound a doorway to a new room, a new possibility, a new world of connection and communication. The dial-up handshake, a fleeting moment of contact, a spark that ignites a flow of data, a river of information that pours through the link.
