*seed: time · drift*

The fridge just cycled off and now the house is doing that thing where you can hear it being a house. The settling. Whatever that is. I've never actually known whether settling is the house getting smaller or the house getting bigger. Thermal contraction, probably, at this hour. The wood giving back the heat it took in.

There's a moth on the window screen. From inside, with the lamp on, I can see its underside, which is the part of a moth nobody ever describes. Pale. Almost dusty white. The wings from below look structural — you can see the veins, the architecture of it. From above, a moth is all pattern and camouflage. From below, it's all engineering.

I keep thinking about how locks work. Not why, just how. The pin tumbler mechanism. You slide the key in and each little ridge pushes a pin to exactly the right height so the shear line clears. It's a yes-or-no question asked five times simultaneously. That's all a lock is. Five yeses at once.

The word "tumbler" is doing strange work there. A tumbler is a drinking glass. A tumbler is an acrobat. A tumbler is a part of a lock. The glass got its name because early ones had rounded bottoms — they'd tumble over if you set them down, so you had to hold them or drain them. That's a design philosophy. Make the failure mode so inconvenient that correct use becomes obvious.

Rounded-bottom glasses. Training wheels work the opposite way — they make the failure mode impossible instead of inconvenient, which means the kid never learns what falling feels like, which means taking them off is a cliff.

I don't know where that was going.

The moth is still there. It hasn't moved. I wonder if it's asleep. Do moths sleep? They must. Everything with a nervous system sleeps. Even jellyfish sleep, supposedly, and they don't have a brain at all, just a nerve net. Sleep might be older than brains.

That's a strange thought to have at 12:38 in the morning. Sleep being older than the thing we think does the sleeping.

The fridge just kicked back on.
