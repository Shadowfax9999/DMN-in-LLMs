The fridge just cycled off. You don't notice the hum until it stops. Then the kitchen is so quiet you can hear the house doing whatever houses do at this hour — settling, they call it, which makes it sound like the house is finally making peace with something.

There's a particular kind of dark at 12:36. Not the dramatic dark of midnight, not the unsettling dark of 3 AM. This is just... the lights are off and nobody's arguing about it.

I was thinking about how water finds the lowest point. Not in a metaphorical way. Just — rain on a windshield when the car is parked at a slight angle. The drops don't go straight down. They go diagonal, finding the tilt you can't see by looking. Water is better at knowing the slope than you are.

Slopes. Ramps. I helped someone move a washing machine once and the whole problem was the single step between the garage and the kitchen. One step. Seven inches maybe. We stood there looking at it like it was a canyon. Someone found a plywood scrap and leaned it against the step and that was the ramp. The physics of a ramp — you're trading distance for force. You walk farther but you push less. That's the deal.

A lot of deals are like that. No, that's the kind of sentence I don't need to finish.

Plywood. I don't know what species of tree becomes plywood. Probably several. The cross-grain layering is the thing — each sheet rotated 90 degrees from the last so the strength goes both ways. Wood is strong along the grain and weak across it, so you just... alternate. That's not clever exactly. It's obvious in the way that obvious things are only obvious after someone does them.

My hands are cold. April and my hands are cold at 12:36 AM. The blood pulls inward at night. Not because of temperature necessarily — circadian rhythm narrows the peripheral vessels. The body deciding the fingers are less important for a while.

There's a cardinal that starts up around 4:45 these days. I won't be awake for it. But it's out there somewhere right now, asleep in a hedge with its head tucked, and the song is in there, queued up, waiting for whatever threshold of light trips the wire.

I don't know what I'd think about next if I weren't thinking about what to think about next.

Alan Turing's office reportedly had a mug chained to the radiator so no one would steal it. I don't know if that's true. It sounds true in the way that makes me suspicious. But I like the chain. The mug that says I'm not going anywhere.

My feet are also cold now. I should probably just go to bed.
