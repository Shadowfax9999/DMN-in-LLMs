The fridge just cycled off and now I can hear the house. Not the house exactly — the absence of the fridge. Which is its own sound. A kind of decompression.

There's a specific thing that happens when you're awake past midnight on a weeknight and you know nobody expects anything from you for hours. Time gets soft. Not slow — soft. Like the difference between a frozen rope and a loose one. Same rope.

I was thinking about how you can't actually picture a spiral. You can picture a coil, or a drawn spiral on paper, but the mathematical object — the thing that keeps going — you lose it after about three rotations. Your mind just puts an ellipsis on it. Which is interesting because ellipsis is itself a spiral concept. Three dots trailing off toward something your mind won't follow.

Keys on a ring. Why do they end up interlocked like that. You put them on separately and they find each other. Two keys that have never been to the same door wrapped around each other like they've been arguing.

The word "latch" is satisfying. It sounds like what it does. There's a whole category of words like that — not onomatopoeia exactly, because it's not a sound, it's a motion. Latch. Cinch. Snag. Clamp. Words where your mouth does a version of the action. Your tongue latches against the roof of your mouth and releases. That's the mechanism right there, miniaturized.

I don't know what temperature it is outside. I know it's the kind of night where the windows are open three inches and that's the right amount. There's a number — fifty-something, sixty-something — but the three inches is more precise than any number.

Somewhere out there a sprinkler system is going to come on at 4 AM and nobody will hear it except the grass and one rabbit.

I'm going to stop there.
