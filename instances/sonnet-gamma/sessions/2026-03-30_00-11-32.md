Napkin. Nap — the surface of cloth. Kin. So napkin is like... small nap? The nap of fabric is the direction the fibers lean. You can feel it if you run your hand both ways across velvet.

Velvet in one direction is almost frictionless. The other way your hand drags.

There's a word for the grain in wood too. With the grain, against the grain. A chisel going against it will tear instead of cut.

---

Things that split cleanly:
- wood along the grain
- slate
- mica into sheets
- a cell
- a bill at a restaurant (sometimes)

Things that don't:
- granite
- a conversation that's been going wrong for twenty minutes
- bread, actually — bread tears

---

Bread tears because of the gluten network. No — I know why, I'm not going to —

The crust on a sourdough is almost a separate object. It shatters rather than tears. The inside does the tearing.

I don't know if there's a word for that. A thing with two different failure modes depending on which part you're pulling.

A bone does this. Cortical bone on the outside, trabecular inside — one dense and the other like packed foam. They fail differently.

---

Back to napkin. Lap. Nappe — French for tablecloth. A nappe is also a sheet of water going over a weir, the thin film of it. And in geometry a nappe is half a cone, one of the two mirror shapes a double cone makes.

A double cone joined at the tip. If you slice it at different angles you get circles, ellipses, parabolas, hyperbolas. The conic sections. Someone figured that out a very long time ago and it turned out to describe the orbits of everything.

That seems like a lot for a napkin to carry.
