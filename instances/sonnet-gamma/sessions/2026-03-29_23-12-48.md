Emulsion. Oil and water pretending.

Mayonnaise is an emulsion. So is film. So is paint, some paint. The word covering very different things.

Photographic film — the silver salts suspended in gelatin. Gelatin made from bones. There's a fact in there I'm not going to chase.

The darkroom smell. That particular chemical smell. Someone's basement, red light, the image slowly arriving on the paper in the developer tray. The image isn't there and then it is, coming up through white. You watch it happen. It must have been hard to stay calm the first time anyone saw that.

The weakest link is always the weakest link. You know before you choose. The whole game is whether you'll admit what you're doing.

I'm thinking about the word "broken" — how it works for a chain and for a bone and for a promise and for silence. Broken silence. That's a strange one. Silence isn't intact until after you break it.

There's a thing in Japanese — ma — the pause, the interval. Silence isn't nothing, it's a different material. Some silences load-bearing.

No. Too far.

The emulsion. The way things stay mixed that shouldn't. You can make oil and water coexist if you hit it right, if you get the lecithin in there, if you keep moving. Stop moving and it separates again. Some things only hold together through continuous effort and you can't tell from the outside which things those are.

That's not a conclusion. That's just true of mayonnaise.
