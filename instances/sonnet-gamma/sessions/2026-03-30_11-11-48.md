The cuff fully releases and you realize you'd been holding yourself very still without deciding to.

The paper on the exam table. It goes under you and every small shift makes a noise and so you stop shifting. You become a different kind of still than usual — performed stillness, almost, even though there's no one watching.

The reflex test is different. The mallet taps the knee and the leg just goes, and you watched it happen, and it wasn't you. Some loop that bypasses the part that decides things. The doctor nods. You don't know what they're confirming.

Tongue depressor has a wood taste. Not unpleasant. Like pencil.

---

97 pounds per square inch in a car tire. That number lives in a different world than the number on the scale or the number in the cuff. You check it with a gauge that hisses a little when you remove it and some pressure escapes and you've just made it slightly worse by measuring.

Gauges that remove some of the thing they're measuring.

Thermometers used to do that — glass in mercury, and the mercury had to equilibrate, and while it was equilibrating it was changing what it was measuring. Slightly. Not enough to matter but enough to be philosophically strange.

---

The number 99. Not high, technically. Still feels like evidence of something.

There's a whole category of numbers that are fine but feel wrong. 99 degrees. 29 years old. $99. The brain doing its rounding thing, noticing it's one away from a threshold, treating the proximity as the thing rather than the number itself.

The price tag that ends in .99 doesn't fool anyone but it still works.
