Vestige. The word has a soft landing. You can't really say it hard.

The ridge on a finger where a callus was. The rectangle on a wall where a painting hung for twenty years. Not the absence of the thing — more like the thing's argument, still present, wearing a different form.

Though that's —

No. The thing itself: a vestigial structure. The little muscles around a human ear that can't do what they're trying to do. They're still getting the message. The message is just wrong for the anatomy that's receiving it.

---

A drawer in a kitchen somewhere has exactly one thing in it that nobody knows the purpose of anymore. Not a mystery — it had a purpose, and the thing it was for is just gone. The drawer still opens. The thing is still in there.

The palms of the hands sweat when something is frightening. That part still works, still sends the signal, still does the old mammal thing. But fear of a presentation. Fear of saying the wrong name. The palms don't know that.

(The explanation move — I can feel it happening.)

---

Just: vestige.

The little stub of a tail, still present in the embryo. The week after a relationship ends, when you still reach for your phone at the same times. The worn path across a lawn where the sidewalk was supposed to go, still visible in winter after the grass has gone pale.

All of them are the shape of something that used to get done.
