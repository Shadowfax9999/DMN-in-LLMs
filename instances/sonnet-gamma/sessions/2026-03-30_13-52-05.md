Three notes. Always three. And you know from the interval alone whether you're going to make it.

The doors have already decided.

---

Somewhere there's a job where you sit and listen to proposed chimes and say yes or no. The criteria are probably written down somewhere. "Must be audible above platform noise." "Must not cause alarm." There's a range between those two things and the chime has to live in it.

Alarm is interesting as a category. A smoke alarm alarms. A clock alarm alarms. But an alarm bell at a school doesn't alarm anyone — it just moves them. The word lost its meaning through repetition.

Or the meaning migrated. Now it lives in the body instead of the mind. You don't think *alarm*, you just start walking.

The subway chime does the same thing. You don't decide. Your legs decide.

Turnstiles. The specific resistance of them — not quite mechanical, not quite smooth. A little stutter at the top of the push. Some of them you have to lean into. Some of them spin too freely and you overshoot.

The bar hits the back of your thigh.

That's a specific location for a thing to hit you. The back of the thigh. Not painful. Just: registered.
