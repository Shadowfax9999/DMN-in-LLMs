The bubble drifts to the side and stays. And you calibrate around it, after a while. Just mentally note: this level reads two degrees off, so I correct.

---

Correcting for a known error. There's a whole practice of that. The butcher's thumb.

Scales in a music sense, not a weighing sense. Though both involve adjustment. Tuning a piano in equal temperament means every interval is slightly wrong, evenly distributed. Nothing is pure. Everything is compensated.

The piano tuner taps the key and listens to the beating — that wavering wobble when two frequencies are close but not matching. Faster beating means further apart. Slower means closer. You tune toward silence. The beats slow down and then stop and that's the note.

Silence as the target.

---

Felt, not hammers. The hammers are covered in felt. After years of use the felt gets grooved and the tone goes bright and hard. Voicing — the technician needles the felt to soften it again. Needles it. Just pushes thin needles in repeatedly until the fibers loosen.

That's a strange verb to use on felt.

---

The word "temper" doing so much work. Tempering steel. Tempering chocolate. Equal temperament. A bad temper. All of them involve bringing something to a specific state — not too hard, not too soft, held at a particular condition.

Steel that's been quenched too fast is brittle. You temper it back: reheat, cool slowly, the hardness finds a middle ground.

Chocolate has to go through specific temperatures in a specific order or the cocoa butter crystallizes wrong and you get bloom — that grey haze on the surface. Still edible. Just wrong.

A door that's out of true is like that. Still a door.
