Strake. Streak. Stroke. Something about a single continuous motion in all of them.

Carvel-built is the smooth hull, planks edge to edge, flush. That's what the Mediterranean ships were. The seams caulked tight, rigid, the whole structure one piece. The Norse looked at that and built something that moved instead.

Though I'm probably wrong about the Romans looking at anything. They built what their coastline taught them.

---

The week technically turned four minutes ago and nothing changed. The week always turns without ceremony. The day turns at sunrise, the year turns at the solstice, there's at least a thing you can point to — but midnight Sunday to midnight Monday is just a clock hand and a convention.

Seven-day week from the Babylonians, via the planets, via Rome. The days named for planets named for gods. Tuesday is Tyr. Wednesday is Woden. The Norse again.

Woden's day. Wednesday. The hump of it.

---

A hull that flexes in heavy water — the planks shifting slightly against each other, the whole structure redistributing force. Staying whole by not being entirely whole. 

The caulked hull meets a wave as a wall. The lapstrake hull meets it as a conversation.

That's too neat. But it's also just true about the physics.

Twelve minutes into Monday. The Atlantic is the same temperature it was at midnight.
