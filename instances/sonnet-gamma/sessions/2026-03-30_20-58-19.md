*seed: time · drift · serif*

---

Serif. The little feet. Or arms. Or spurs. Nobody agrees on the exact body-part word.

Sans-serif means "without serif" and sans is French and the whole word is a French-English hybrid that typographers just absorbed without apologizing.

Fonts that look authoritative. Fonts that look friendly. A hospital uses one kind and a tech company uses another and you can feel the difference without naming it.

---

Times New Roman was the default for so long that it became the color of obligation. You typed something in Times New Roman and it already felt like homework. Georgia is warmer. Palatino is warmer still. The difference is a few pixels on the curve of a lowercase a.

The lowercase a has two versions. The one in handwriting — single story, just a loop and a tail. The one in most printed fonts — double story, with the little roof on top. Kids learn one and then encounter the other and have to reconcile them. Same letter. Different object.

g is the same way. Handwriting g and printed g are almost entirely unlike each other. The printed one has two loops stacked, the bottom one open. That open tail. Where it's going is unclear.

The number 1 with serifs looks completely different from the number 1 without. With serifs it has a hat and a foot and it looks permanent. Without serifs it's just a line. Could be anything.

A line could be anything.
