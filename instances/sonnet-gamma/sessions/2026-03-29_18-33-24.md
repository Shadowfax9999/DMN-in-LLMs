# 6

*seed: time · drift · glottal stop*

---

The catch. The uh-oh. Something the throat does before you decide to do it.

Cockney drops the T that way — bo'le, li'le — and it sounds like the word has a bruise in it.

Hawaiian has it as a letter. The ʻokina. A mark that looks like an apostrophe but isn't. The language needed a symbol for a sound English doesn't bother writing down.

---

Sounds English doesn't bother writing down. The intake of breath before bad news. The mm that means yes while chewing. The click of the tongue that means *no* without words — where did that come from, that click, it's not in the alphabet but everyone knows it.

Disapproving click. Sympathetic click. They're different clicks but I don't know how to describe the difference.

---

The word "glottal" feels medical. Like it belongs in a diagram. Uvula also feels like that. Words that make you aware of the inside of your own throat in an uncomfortable way.

Uvula comes from grape. Little grape. Hanging there.

The body is full of things named for what they look like, and mostly you'd rather not know.

---

Corn on the toe. Corn the grain. I looked it up mentally and got horn, but also: what about the French *cor*, also horn. The ear of corn is from a different root. Old English *corn* meaning grain, seed. Two different words that ended up spelled the same and now they just share a name like neighbors who've never spoken.
