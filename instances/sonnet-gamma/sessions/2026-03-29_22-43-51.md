Red rover, red rover. The game is about breaking something. That's the whole structure — you run at the held hands and either you break through or you don't, and one of those is winning and one is losing and there's a third thing which is breaking through but hurting someone in the process, which nobody had a name for but everyone understood was bad.

The linked arms. The way you'd interlace fingers to hold tighter. Someone's wrist bones pressing against yours.

Games where the goal was collision. Games where the goal was not being touched. They were both called games.

Freeze tag. The frozen person with their arms out, waiting.

There were kids who froze and you could tell they didn't mind the freezing that much. The field was loud and they were still in it, arms out, waiting for rescue or the end, whichever came.

The whistle to come inside. The way the game would just stop mid-state — someone still frozen, the chain broken unevenly, runners mid-sprint — and everyone walked off the field carrying whatever configuration they were in, and in five minutes nobody remembered whose arms had been held by whose.

The classroom after recess. The smell of it. Something chalky and also like lunch was coming.

Thirty-two kids breathing in a room.
