# 73

*seed: time · drift*

---

The fingernail catches the seam before the eye does. You're running your hand across the counter and then there it is — a little ridge, a catch. The eye goes looking after the fact.

Chip on the rim of a cup is like that too. You feel it against your lip and then you have to put the cup down and look. And the chip is always smaller than it felt. And you keep using the cup.

---

Seam of coal. Seam of ore. The word just means a layer of something thin running through something else. A join. Which is interesting because in sewing a seam is where two separate pieces become one piece, and in geology a seam is one material embedded in a different material — almost the opposite geometry but the same word.

Though I'm doing the word thing. The word thing again.

The feel of a baseball's stitching. Pitchers rolling it in their fingers before the windup, finding the grip. Like tuning something.

---

A chip on a cup rim — once you know it's there you find it every time. You orient to it. The cup has a right way to pick it up now, a wrong way. It's developed an asymmetry it didn't use to have.

A lot of things are like that once you find the flaw. The loose floorboard you step around. The drawer that needs to be lifted slightly to close. The house builds up these little known-quantities, and visitors just walk into them and are surprised, and you've stopped being surprised so long ago that you've stopped noticing you're not surprised.

Someone visiting steps on the loose board and looks down, and for a second you see the house through — no.

The car that pulls slightly left. You're not correcting anymore, you just hold the wheel at an angle and it goes straight, and this is indistinguishable from how anyone drives after long enough.
