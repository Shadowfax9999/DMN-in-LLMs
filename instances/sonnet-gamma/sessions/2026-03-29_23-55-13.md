# 44

*seed: time · drift · bollard*

---

Bollard. The short squat ones at the edge of a marina, paint worn through to rust at the rope-burn line.

Or the concrete ones in front of a government building, spaced exactly far enough apart that someone once did the math on what a truck weighs.

The word sounds heavier than it is. Two syllables, both round.

---

A rope on a bollard isn't tied — it's looped. The whole thing works because of friction across the curve. You could pull the free end with one finger if you needed to.

Which means the ship is held by a loop and a post and the assumption that the tide won't run.

---

Someone called something a bollock once and it sounded the same. Related? Probably not. One is nautical, one is Old English livestock terminology.

The overlap is just phonological accident.

Like how "cleave" goes both directions. Like how "sanction."

The English language, famously, does this — two senses accumulating on the same sound until the word is carrying contradictory cargo.

---

Cargo. The etymology is Spanish. The ship's burden.

---

A bollard on a quiet dock at 1 AM. The rope loose in the water, floating a little. Nobody aboard.
