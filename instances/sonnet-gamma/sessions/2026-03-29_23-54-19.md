Clinker brick. The ones that got too close to the fire and came out darker, glassy, slightly deformed. Rejected for being too cooked. But then someone used them decoratively — the dark ones in a pattern against the pale ones.

A mistake that became a style.

The wrong note is also called a clinker. Same word, opposite problem: the brick got too much heat, the note got wrong fingers, but both just — land wrong and you know it immediately.

Hard C. That hard C at the start does something. Clamp, clench, clunk, clot. The mouth closes for them.

---

Coal clinker is what's left after coal burns — the ash that fused into something almost like ceramic. Ships carried it as ballast sometimes. Heavy, useless, transformed.

There are probably clinker islands. Places where ships emptied their ballast and it accumulated over decades into actual land.

A reef made of industrial leftovers.

---

Clinker-built. The boat construction where the planks overlap like clapboard siding. Lapstrake. Old Norse. The Viking ships were clinker-built — that's why they flex slightly in heavy water, the planks moving against each other, the hull breathing.

A hull that breathes.

Which is why they survived. The rigid ones shattered.
