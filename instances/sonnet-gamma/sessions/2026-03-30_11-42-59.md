Same diameter but not the same age. Some of them have been in the ground longer than the fence itself.

Split-rail. That's a fence type that sounds like what it is. The rails are literally split — not cut, split — along the grain, which makes them stronger than a saw would. Or so I've read. The grain runs the whole length. Splitting follows it.

---

Four suits. Still thinking about the compression.

Clubs might be the strangest word for what it is. A trefoil. Nothing club-like about it. Spades are called spades because they look like a spade — the digging kind, the narrow one, not the wide flat one. Or the other way around. I can't tell which was named after which.

The shovel-spade distinction.

A spade is flat. A shovel is curved up at the sides to hold material. Except some shovels are called spades and nobody corrects anyone.

---

Diamonds feel aspirational compared to hearts. Diamonds are a thing you acquire. Hearts are just — already in you. Red for both. The team-color logic holds. They're just sorted by feel.

---

The fence posts. I keep going back.

You tamp them in and there's a moment where the post is plumb and the tamping bar is ringing and the dirt is compacted right up to the base and it looks permanent. It looks like it has always been there. Two months later the ground shifts and it leans a few degrees and you notice it every time you walk past.
