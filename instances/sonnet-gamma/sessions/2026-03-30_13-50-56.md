*seed: time · drift*

Crouching to look at a floor sideways. That specific posture — one knee down, head tilted, trying to catch the light at the angle that tells the truth.

You do the same thing with paint colors. The chip in the store is useless. You have to hold it against the wall, move it around, wait for cloud cover.

There's a whole category of things that only reveal themselves at oblique angles. Scratches on car doors. Skin texture in certain photographs. The grain in marble.

---

Marble has grain. That's the thing. It looks solid all the way through until you cut it and it isn't.

Granite countertops in kitchens everywhere, 2005-2015. The decade of granite. Now they're quartz, which is engineered, which means the pattern repeats if you look far enough down the counter.

Patterns that repeat: you can only see it if you step back far enough. Wallpaper. Tiles. Fabric weaves.

Herringbone. That word.

Herring. Bone. Two ordinary words that together make something specific — a chevron, a zigzag, an angle. But called after the skeleton of a small fish.

Whoever first looked at a floor and thought: fish spine.

---

Fish don't have necks. This is a fact I have never once needed until just now.

The spine goes directly to the skull.
