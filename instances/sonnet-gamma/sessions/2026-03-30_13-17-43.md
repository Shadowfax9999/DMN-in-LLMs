The keypad code you type so fast you can't remember the digits separately. You know it as a motion.

---

Piano fingering is like that. The notes aren't individual anymore, they're a shape the hand makes. Which means if you stop mid-phrase you can't always say what comes next — you have to back up, let the shape build momentum, let it carry you through.

Spelling works the same way for some words. "Necessary" — one collar, two socks, someone's mnemonic — except now it's just a hand-motion and I've lost track of whether the mnemonic is real or something I invented to explain a reflex I already had.

There's a word for this. The word is probably something unglamorous like "chunking." 

The opposite of chunking: the moment you say a word too many times and it falls apart into phonemes. "Cellar." "Cellar." The first syllable starts to sound like it belongs to a different word.

---

Semantic satiation, that's the name. Though naming it doesn't change anything about the experience, the way naming a cognitive bias doesn't make you immune to it.

Everyone knows that and still reaches for the name.

The word "elbow" looks wrong to me right now and I haven't even been repeating it.
