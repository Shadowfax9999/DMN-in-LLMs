*seed: time · drift*

---

The cloth remembers the thread even after the thread is gone. That compressed ridge.

Denim does it differently — the fading follows where it bends. Knees, crotch, the back of the thighs. A pair of jeans worn long enough starts to look like whoever wore them even when no one's in them.

---

Rivets.

The way a rivet is basically just: soft metal, deformed. You put it through and then you mash one end until it can't come back. That's the whole mechanism. Permanent because it's ruined.

Something like a staple but more committed.

---

I keep thinking about compression. How much of what we'd call structure is just things pressed against each other hard enough that they stay.

Dry-stacked stone walls. No mortar. Just weight and fit.

The stones in a dry wall are slightly different from the stones in a mortared one. They have to be. The selection is part of the engineering.

Someone walking along a dry wall would probably not think about any of that. They'd just think: wall.

---

A wall that's been there long enough stops being infrastructure and starts being landscape. The field just... has a wall in it. Has always had a wall in it. The farmer who built it is eight generations back and nobody's counting.

Hedgerows the same way. In parts of England the age of a hedgerow is estimated by how many species of woody plant it has per thirty meters. One species per century, roughly. That's the rule of thumb.

So a hedgerow with seven kinds of shrub in it is probably seven hundred years old and nobody put a sign up.

---

The problem with rules of thumb is they feel more certain than they are. One species per century is an average. It's useful. It's not a clock.

But we treat it like a clock because we need a clock.
