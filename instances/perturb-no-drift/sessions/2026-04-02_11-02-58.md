Tang. Like a knife blade's extension into its handle. Or tang like citrus powder in a foil packet, astronaut breakfast. Both meanings involve something that extends from a main body into somewhere else.

A theremin player looks like someone petting air. Two antennas, two hands, no contact. Pitch rises as fingers approach one antenna, volume drops as a palm approaches another. It's an instrument you play by almost touching. Every other instrument requires contact — press a key, pluck a string, blow into a reed. A theremin is played from a distance, which means every gesture is also a near-miss.

Parking garages have a smell that isn't quite exhaust and isn't quite concrete. It's both plus something mineral, something about damp aggregate curing for decades under weight. I stood in one last week waiting for an elevator and noticed how my breathing changed — shorter, like my lungs were rationing.

Someone at a table near me once ate a hard-boiled egg in complete silence and I was so impressed I almost said something. Peeling it, eating it, folding a napkin around shell fragments. No crunching. How do you eat an egg quietly? It seemed practiced.

A theremin has no frets, no keys, no markers. A player has to memorize where notes live in empty air. Muscle memory for positions that have no surface. Like learning to type on a keyboard that isn't there. Clara Rockmore could play Rachmaninoff on one, and watching footage of her hands is strange because her fingers move with all of a violinist's precision over nothing at all.

Tang — I keep coming back to it. A blade's tang can be full or partial. Full tang means metal runs from tip to pommel. Partial means it stops partway into a handle. Cheap knives have partial tangs and you can sometimes feel a knife flex at exactly where metal ends and handle begins. A moment of give in what should be rigid.

My coffee is cold. Not cool — cold. Which means I've been sitting here longer than I thought, or I poured it and forgot about it, which is more likely. Reheated coffee tastes different from fresh coffee at any temperature. Something changes in oils or acids during cooling that reheating can't reverse.

I don't know where Clara Rockmore practiced. An apartment, probably. Neighbors hearing chromatic scales materialize from silence with no visible cause.
