*seed: time · drift · how a stereoscope creates depth from two flat images*

---

Capillary. The word sounds like it should be about hair but it's about tubes so thin that water climbs them without being asked. The grout between bathroom tiles pulls water sideways through capillary action, which is why the base of a shower wall stays dark for hours after you've dried everything else.

From an airplane the Mississippi looks like it can't decide. All those oxbow loops, the river trying every direction before committing to south. You can see where it used to be — old channels still holding water, still shaped like a river, just not connected to one anymore.

A stereoscope works because your eyes are about two and a half inches apart. Two photographs taken from slightly different positions, one for each eye, and the brain does the math it was always going to do. The Victorians loved them. Sat in parlors looking at views of Niagara Falls through a wooden viewer, and the depth hit them harder than it hits us because they hadn't been trained by 3D movies to expect the trick. The thing that gets me is that it's not an illusion — it's the actual mechanism of depth perception, just fed manually. A stereoscope doesn't fool the brain. It does exactly what the brain wants.

There's a crack in the sidewalk outside where I work where a dandelion has been coming back for at least three years. Same crack. Possibly the same plant, possibly its own offspring — dandelions don't need pollination, they just clone themselves, which means every dandelion in that crack might be genetically identical to the first one that found it.

Jupiter has no surface. You'd just fall through thicker and thicker gas until at some point the pressure turns it into liquid and there's no boundary, no moment where you'd say now I'm underwater. The atmosphere becomes the ocean becomes the core and none of the transitions announce themselves. Someone calculated where "sea level" would be on Jupiter and they just picked where the atmospheric pressure equals one bar, which is Earth's surface pressure. An arbitrary line in a continuous gradient.

The dandelion doesn't know about the crack. The crack doesn't know about the dandelion. They just fit.

A contrail dissolving. From the ground it takes maybe four minutes. From the plane you can't see it happening at all.

I wonder if stereoscope photographs of people who are now dead feel different from regular photographs of people who are now dead. Whether the depth makes the absence more specific. Two and a half inches of parallax preserving something a flat photograph doesn't — not what they looked like but where they were standing in relation to the chair behind them.

Capillary. Still sitting there. The word came in and I haven't done anything with it. Grout was the closest I got. There's a whole circulatory system named after the same idea — capillary beds, where your blood vessels get so thin that red blood cells pass through single file. One cell wide. The close-up version of a thing that from a distance looks like a blush on someone's cheek.

I think the dandelion might be flowering right now.
