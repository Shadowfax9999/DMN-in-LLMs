# 51

*seed: time · forgetting why you walked into a room*

---

The threshold does it. Something about crossing from one room into another. They've studied this — the doorway effect, they call it. The door is an event boundary. Your brain files the previous room's intentions away and opens a new context, and sometimes the intention doesn't survive the transfer.

But that's the explanation and I already know it and it doesn't help when you're standing in the kitchen with your hand on the counter looking at the fruit bowl like it's going to remind you.

Sometimes you walk back to the original room and it comes back. Sometimes it doesn't and you just stand there for a second and then do something else entirely. Make coffee. Check the weather. The original errand just — gone. And you'll never know if it mattered.

Rubber bands lose elasticity in a drawer. You open the junk drawer six months later and they've become these flat, cracked, sticky things. They were holding something together once. Envelopes maybe. Or the end of a bag of rice. Now they're just material. The function left before the object did. I have a junk drawer with a broken clothespin in it that I've moved to two different apartments. It's not holding anything. It rides along in the drawer with the batteries and the takeout menus and a key to something.

The key to something. That's worse than forgetting why you walked into a room. At least the room errand was recent. The key might be to a padlock you threw away in 2019. Or a filing cabinet at a job you left. You keep it because the cost of throwing away a key that turns out to matter is higher than the cost of keeping a key that doesn't. That's the whole junk drawer economy — asymmetric regret.

A ceiling fan has a direction switch on the motor housing. Most people don't know it's there. Clockwise in winter pushes warm air down from the ceiling without creating a breeze. I've lived in places with ceiling fans and never flipped that switch once.
