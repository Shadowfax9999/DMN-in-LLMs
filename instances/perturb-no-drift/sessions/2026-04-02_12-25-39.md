*seed: time · pattern*

Plaid is a pattern that encodes clan. Argyle is a pattern that encodes nothing, as far as I know. Houndstooth. Herringbone. These are all named after things they look like, which means someone looked at fabric and saw a fish skeleton.

The test pattern on old televisions — that was a face, sort of. The Indian head test pattern. It was on all night after programming ended. Programming ended. There was a time when television just stopped and said goodnight and showed you a fixed image until morning.

Cracks in mud form a pattern. Voronoi. I think that's the name. Each cell contracts toward its own center as it dries, and the boundaries are where two cells pull equally. The same geometry shows up in giraffe spots, supposedly.

A shirt I owned in maybe 2003 had a pattern I'd call "busy." Small rectangles in four colors, no logic to them. I wore it to things. I can't remember giving it away but I don't have it.

The thing about a sewing pattern is you're supposed to cut it out and then you have the negative space left, the tissue paper with holes in it, and that part gets folded back into the envelope too. Both pieces go back. The shape and the absence of the shape, in the same envelope.

Alan Turing wrote about pattern formation in biology. Reaction-diffusion. Two chemicals, one activating and one inhibiting, and the interplay produces stripes or spots depending on the ratio. Zebras, angelfish.

I had a coworker who could identify any plaid by name. She'd say "that's a Black Watch" or "that's a Dress Stewart" the way someone else would identify a bird. I never tested whether she was right. There was no way to test it in the moment, so I just believed her every time.

The wallpaper in a Holiday Inn in — I want to say Tulsa — had a pattern that repeated every eighteen inches or so, and once you found the repeat you couldn't unsee it. Little clusters of wheat or something botanical. Eighteen inches of uniqueness, then the same thing forever in every direction. I lay on the bed and followed one row of wheat clusters all the way to the corner where the wallpaper seam didn't quite line up.

That seam is the most interesting part of any wallpaper.
