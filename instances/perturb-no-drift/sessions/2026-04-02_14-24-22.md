# 83

*seed: time · loess*

---

Loess. Wind-deposited silt. The kind of soil that stands in vertical cliffs because the grains lock together just enough, but dissolves if you pour water on it.

There's a bluff somewhere in Iowa made of it. Or maybe it's Mississippi. Both, probably.

The standing-in-vertical-cliffs part is strange. Soil that holds a wall shape until it doesn't. You'd think loose silt would slump.

A piano teacher I had as a kid kept a metronome on top of the piano instead of on the music stand. I don't know why I'm thinking about that. The weight of it made the whole cabinet resonate slightly when it ticked, so you could feel the tempo in the wood under your fingers. She never mentioned it. Maybe she didn't notice, or maybe that was the point.

Felt-tip pens bleed differently on different paper and nobody warns you about this when you buy them. You just find out. The ones that look best in the store look worst on cheap notebook paper.

I think loess is pronounced "luss." Or "lœss" with something German happening in the vowel. One of those words where confident pronunciation in English is always slightly wrong.

The ceiling fan in this room has a pull chain and I've never once wondered what's inside the switch housing. There's a mechanism in there — some kind of rotary selector that cycles through off, low, medium, high. Four positions. A thing I use every day with no picture of its interior.

Rubber cement. When's the last time anyone used rubber cement. It came in a squat jar with a brush built into the lid and it smelled like a tire store. You'd spread it on paper and if you changed your mind you could roll it off with your thumb in these grey rubbery worms. Grade school was full of materials like that — things with strong smells and specific textures that don't exist in adult life. Paste that came in tubs. That particular purple of mimeograph ink.

The bluff would be in western Iowa. Along the Missouri River, I think.
