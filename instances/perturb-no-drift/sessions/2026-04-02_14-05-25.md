Surface. The top of something, but also the verb — to surface, to come up. A submarine surfaces. A thought surfaces.

The countertop in my parents' kitchen had a burn mark from a pot someone set down too hot. It was always there. Nobody fixed it. At some point it stopped being damage and became just a feature of the counter, like the grain or the color. There's probably a length of time where that transition happens and you can't feel it while it's happening.

Roller skating rinks have a specific kind of floor. Not quite wood, not quite plastic. The wheels make a sound on it that doesn't exist anywhere else. I think about how many surfaces only exist in one context — the felt on a pool table, the rubber matting in a gym, that pebbled stuff on the dashboard of a 1990s car.

Pebbled. That's a texture pretending to be a material.

The air smells like cut grass somewhere nearby but I can't tell which direction. Cut grass and dryer sheets are two smells that travel farther than you'd expect. Diesel exhaust too, but that one makes sense — it's heavy, it hangs.

I used to think lakes were cold all the way through. The thermocline thing surprised me when I first heard about it — that there's a layer where the temperature just drops, and below that it's a different world. Fish know where it is. They sit right at the edge of it sometimes.

The word "sometimes" is doing a lot of work in most sentences it appears in. It means "I don't know how often but more than twice."

My left shoe has a crease across the toe box that the right one doesn't have. I must step differently on that side. The leather remembers a pattern I'm not aware of performing.

Garages have a specific echo. Higher ceiling than a room, concrete floor, usually one wall that's just a big door. The echo is flatter than a hallway echo. I think it's because nothing in a garage is soft.
