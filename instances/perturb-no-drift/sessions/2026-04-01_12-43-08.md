# 8

*seed: time · drift · how a theremin turns hand distance into pitch*

---

Tongs. That click-click before you grab anything. Testing what — grip? Readiness? It's a ritual with no content. Like knocking on wood but for cooking.

A theremin doesn't care about ritual. You don't touch it at all. Your hand floats near an antenna and pitch changes based on distance — capacitance shifts between your body and metal, and a circuit reads that shift as frequency. Closer means higher. No contact. Playing air itself. I've never played one but I've seen people hover over one with both hands trembling slightly, like miming something fragile.

A fence with one missing slat looks like a mouth with a gap tooth. Or it looks like nothing — just a fence that lost a board. Wind moves differently through a gap like that. You'd hear it on certain days.

Caulk. C-A-U-L-K. Five letters doing work that three could handle. "Cawk" would be honest. But English doesn't want to be honest about its spelling, ever. It wants to carry old luggage around.

My left knee has been clicking when I go down stairs. Not up. Only down. Clicking is probably not even right — it's more of a soft pop, like stepping on a very small bubble wrap cell. It doesn't hurt. That's what I keep telling myself, which is exactly what someone says before it starts hurting.

Grocery store layouts are designed to force you through dairy last because dairy is why you came. Everything else is an obstacle course of temptation. But what I remember about that old store isn't strategic — it's sensory. Fluorescent buzz near produce. A specific coolness near frozen foods that started about two aisles before you got there, like weather changing.

A theremin player's biggest enemy is breathing. Your chest moves, your hand moves, pitch wobbles. You have to become very still to play something emotional. I find that interesting but I'm not going to say why.

Rubber band on a doorknob — I think it's for when you're carrying things in and out and don't want a latch clicking shut behind you. Moving day. Painting. Something like that. A problem I've apparently never had badly enough to solve.

Somewhere a cardinal is doing its two-note call over and over, same interval, same rhythm, patient as a metronome nobody asked for.
