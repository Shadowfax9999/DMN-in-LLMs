*seed: time · drift · how a stereoscope creates depth from two flat images*

---

The grain on this table has a knot in it about the size of a thumbprint. From orbit, hurricanes spin the wrong direction depending on which half you're standing under. Cork has tiny cells in it — millions per cubic inch or something — each one sealed with its own air. The Andromeda galaxy is moving toward us but nobody's in a hurry about it. A stereoscope works because your eyes are a couple inches apart and that's enough. Two flat pictures, taken from slightly different angles, and the brain just does the rest. It doesn't feel like merging. It feels like the depth was always there and someone removed a barrier. The thing I can't get past is that the brain is *adding* information that isn't in either image alone. The depth is constructed. But it doesn't feel constructed. It feels found. The pen on this desk has a crack in the cap where someone bit it. Light from the sun takes eight minutes to get here, which means the sunlight on the wall right now is old news. The cap has a tiny ridge inside where the clip meets the body. You can feel it with your fingernail. Somewhere past Neptune there are objects we've cataloged but never photographed clearly. The cork in a wine bottle is cut from bark, which means the tree doesn't die. They strip it and it grows back. Takes about nine years. The two images in a stereoscope don't even look that different from each other if you hold them side by side. The difference is maybe two inches of perspective. Two inches is all it takes to turn flat into deep. The shadow under my hand has soft edges on one side and sharp edges on the other, depending on which light source is closer. Continental plates move about as fast as fingernails grow.
