*seed: time · drift · how a periscope uses two mirrors to see around corners*

The mailbox across the street has a flag up but I can't tell if it's been collected yet. Inside the mailbox, whatever's there — I can't see it. That's the whole point of a mailbox. You trust the container.

A periscope is just two mirrors angled at 45 degrees in a tube. You see what's above you, or around the corner, or over the trench wall. But what you're actually looking at is a reflection of a reflection — the image has been flipped twice, which puts it back to normal. So the thing you see looks right but has traveled a zigzag path to reach you. You can see the surface of the ocean but not the submarine. The submarine can see you.

There's a spider web between the porch rail and the gutter downspout. I can see it because of the angle of the light. In an hour when the sun moves I won't be able to. The web will still be there. The spider is somewhere I can't find — tucked into the corner of the downspout, maybe, waiting for vibration.

My left knee has been stiff since yesterday. I can feel the stiffness but I can't see anything wrong with it — no swelling, no redness. Whatever's happening is underneath. Most of what's happening in a body is underneath.

Two houses down someone's put out a green couch for trash pickup. I can see it from here. I can't see why they got rid of it — it looks fine. Maybe it smells. Maybe something broke inside the frame. Maybe they just got a new one and this is what "new one" means for the old one: the curb, then rain, then the truck.

A periscope lets you look without being in the place you're looking at. Which is also what a window does, or a security camera, or remembering something. You're seeing the thing but you're not there. The image arrives but you stayed where you are.

I can see the recycling bin still has last week's cardboard in it.
