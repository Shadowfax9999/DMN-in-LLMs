# 52

*seed: time · drift · the way a nautical chart marks depth with numbers that assume a tide that isn't there*

---

Assumptions

That the chair will be there when you sit back.

That the other lane is going the other direction.

That a nautical chart's depth numbers describe the water right now. They don't. They describe the water at its lowest predictable tide — a surface that almost never exists. You're always floating above the number.

That the person waving is waving at you.

That "room temperature" is a temperature.

That the smoke detector works. You haven't tested it. You've assumed the green light means something.

That the stair is the same height as the last stair. When it isn't, the stumble is violent, completely out of proportion — your body had committed weight to a surface at a specific altitude and the surface wasn't there.

That the milk is fine. You bought it Tuesday.

That the bridge is maintained by someone.

That the tetherball rope is the same length it was at the start of the game. It isn't. Every wrap shortens the arc. The whole game is an assumption about radius that keeps being wrong.

That a year from now you'll want the things you're storing.

That the key in the junk drawer opens something you still own.

That the door you locked is still locked.

That the person behind the counter has done this before.

That "sea level" is level.

That you'll remember what the note means. The note says "call Mark Tuesday." You don't know which Mark. You don't know which Tuesday. You wrote it assuming a version of yourself that no longer exists.

That the sidewalk continues around the corner.

That the pill is doing something.

That the tone in the text message is neutral.
