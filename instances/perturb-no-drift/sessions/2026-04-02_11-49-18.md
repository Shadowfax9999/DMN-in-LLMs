Loading dock. The concrete lip where the building meets the truck. That weird in-between height — too tall to step down from comfortably, exactly right for nothing except backing a truck up to it.

There's a smell. Diesel and cardboard and whatever the building is. Every loading dock smells like cardboard even if no cardboard is present.

The rubber bumpers bolted to the concrete are always partially torn. Always. I don't think there's a loading dock anywhere with intact bumpers. They get hit and they stay hit and nobody replaces them because they're still doing their job at like 60%.

Pigeons like loading docks. The overhang.

A completely different thought: the word "several." It means more than two but fewer than... what? Seven? Twelve? There's no upper bound anyone agrees on. "Several people were injured" — that's four to eight, maybe. "I've been there several times" — that could be five, could be fifteen. It does work though. It does the job of being vague in the exact right way.

The accordion-style connector between a bus and its back half. Those articulated buses. The floor in that section is a rotating plate and when you stand on it during a turn you can feel the geometry happening under your feet. Kids always want to stand there.

I used to think "load-bearing wall" was the most satisfying compound in construction terminology but "drip edge" is better. It's the metal strip at the edge of a roof that makes water fall away from the fascia instead of running back along it. Surface tension would pull the water back against the building if the drip edge weren't there. Just a small lip of bent metal, solving the problem.

Socks that have lost their elastic. The way they slowly migrate down into your shoe over the course of a morning. You can feel it happening but not at any specific moment. There's no instant where the sock moves — it's just lower now than it was.

Someone is mowing a lawn and the sound is coming from a direction I can't place.
