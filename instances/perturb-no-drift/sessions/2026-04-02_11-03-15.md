# 49

*seed: time · the smell of a just-extinguished candle*

---

That sulfur-and-wax smell. Not the candle burning — the moment after.

There's a word for the smoke that comes off a just-extinguished candle. I think it's just "smoke." Maybe there's a more specific one. I don't think there is.

The ribbon of it always leans the same direction as whatever draft is in the room, which means it's showing you air movement you couldn't see before. Like dropping a leaf into a stream to see which way the current goes. The candle was hiding the draft while it was lit because the heat was pulling everything upward. Then you blow it out and the room reasserts itself.

I had a coworker once who would microwave fish in the office kitchen and the smell would find hallways nobody used. Someone said "smell is just particles of the thing reaching your nose" and I thought about that for too long. Weeks, probably. The phrase "particles of the thing" did something to me. Every smell became a tiny invasion. Pollen is like that too — not a smell exactly but something arriving from a distance, a piece of somewhere else landing on you.

Airplane cabins have a particular smell that isn't any one material. It's the mixture of recycled air, carpet adhesive, the plastic of the overhead bins, coffee from six rows back, and whatever the de-icing fluid is. You stop noticing it about ten minutes in. Then when you step off the jet bridge into the terminal the terminal smells like nothing, which is actually the terminal's smell replacing the plane's smell, but your nose reads it as absence.

Hospitals smell like that too — not like nothing, but like aggressive nothing. Like someone is actively maintaining the blankness.

The cap on a dry-erase marker never seals as well as the cap on a Sharpie.
