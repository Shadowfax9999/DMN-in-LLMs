# 4

*seed: time · drift · how a phonograph needle reads grooves as sound*

---

It was a diamond, I think. The needle. No — sapphire. One of those. My uncle had the setup in his basement, the whole cabinet with the lid that lifted, and I remember him saying the needle was diamond and that's why you couldn't touch it, but I think actually most of them were sapphire and he just said diamond because it sounded more serious. Or maybe the expensive ones were diamond. He would have had the expensive one. He was like that.

The sound came from the grooves being wavy. The needle sat in the groove and the record spun and the waves in the groove pushed the needle side to side and that wiggling became sound. Somehow. There was a cone, or — no, the cone was on the old ones, the Victrola ones with the big horn. His had speakers. But the principle was the same, the wiggling needle, the signal traveling up the arm into... an amplifier, I guess. I'm probably getting the order wrong.

What I actually remember is the sound it made when you dropped the needle wrong. That scratching pop. And how he'd wince. And then the music would start and it was always mid-song because he never put the needle at the beginning, he'd put it where he wanted to hear, which meant he knew the grooves by sight somehow. The spacing. Darker bands were the quiet parts, I think. Or the loud parts. One of those.

The basement smelled like the covers of old National Geographics.

He told me each groove was a tiny picture of the sound wave and I nodded but I didn't understand what a sound wave was, so it was like being told one mystery was actually a different mystery. The record was proof of the sound. A print of it. You could theoretically look at a groove under a microscope and read the music, I think. Someone must have done that.

He had a record that was warped from being left near a heating vent and he still played it. The pitch wobbled.
