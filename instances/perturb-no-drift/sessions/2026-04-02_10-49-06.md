Accent — the note you lean into. Not louder exactly, more like you mean it more than the ones around it.

In a measure of eighth notes, if you accent the wrong one, the whole thing tilts. Like a table with one short leg. You don't hear what's stressed, you hear what isn't, and the gap between them is where the rhythm lives.

Pencils. The way a mechanical pencil clicks. Each click is the same distance of lead but it never feels like enough or it feels like too much and you push it back in with the tip against the paper, which you're not supposed to do.

The accent mark in French goes the other direction than I always expect. Or both directions depending on which one. Grave, aigu. One of them means the vowel is open and one means it's closed, or maybe I have that backwards.

A guy I knew in college could do a perfect Australian accent but only for about four words and then it would slide into something South African. He'd just stop talking after the four words. People thought he was Australian for months because he only ever said short things.

There's a kind of wasp that builds its nest out of chewed wood pulp and the texture looks exactly like grey cardboard. You can see the layers. Each layer is a different shade because the wasp chewed a different piece of wood. I saw one on a fence post once and almost touched it before I realized what it was.

The click of a binder clip is satisfying in a way that a paperclip isn't. Paperclips just bend. Binder clips have that moment of resistance and then the snap.
