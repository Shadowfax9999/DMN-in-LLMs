The way a rubber band sounds different depending on how far you stretch it. That's just tension changing pitch. But the feel of it — the resistance getting sharper right before you think it might snap — that's a different kind of information.

Postal codes. Some countries use letters in them. K1A 0B1 is the Canadian parliament. I don't know why I know that. There's a whole system where the letters alternate with numbers in a specific pattern, and the first letter tells you the province, or the region, or something. I'm not sure which.

Chairs in a doctor's waiting room are always bolted together in rows. Not individual chairs — a single unit. The armrests are shared. You can feel when the person two seats down shifts their weight because the whole thing is one piece of bent steel underneath the upholstery. It makes strangers into a single mechanical system. You're connected at the frame.

The word "slick" sounds like what it means.

I've been thinking about how elevators have that little certificate framed behind glass, usually near the buttons, and nobody ever reads it. There's an inspection date on it. Someone came and checked the cables and the brakes and signed off. The frame is always slightly crooked. The signature is always illegible.

A coffee mug with a chip on the rim — you learn where the chip is and you rotate the mug every time so you drink from the other side. You do this automatically after the second day. Your hand knows the rotation before you've thought about it.

Sycamore bark peels in patches so the trunk looks like camouflage.
