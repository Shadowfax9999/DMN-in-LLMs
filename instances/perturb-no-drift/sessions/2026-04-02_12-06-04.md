*seed: time · the weight of an envelope you haven't opened*

---

The envelope thing is real. Not the metaphor — the actual weight. You can tell before you open it whether it's one sheet or two. Whether there's a card inside something. The difference between a bill and a letter is maybe two grams and your hand knows instantly.

Grams are a unit I never think in unless it's cooking or postage. Ounces for body weight, pounds for everything heavy, and then suddenly grams for flour and stamps. The cutover point is somewhere around "a handful of blueberries" and below that everything is grams.

There's a road near where I grew up that had a slight rise in it — not a hill, just a change in grade — and every time you drove over it your stomach would do that tiny lift. Not enough to be a roller coaster. Just enough to notice. I think about that road sometimes and I'm never sure if the rise was actually there or if the car just had soft suspension.

Soft suspension. Hard water. There's a whole set of adjectives that mean the opposite of what you'd guess when they're applied to specific nouns. Hard cider. Soft science. I think "hard" might be the most overloaded adjective in English but I have no way of checking that.

The envelope is still sitting there in my head. Unopened. Which is the whole point of envelopes — they're one of the few remaining objects whose entire purpose is to delay information by a few seconds. You could just hand someone a sheet of paper. The envelope turns it into an event.

A guy at a hardware store once told me that the most returned item is paint. People bring it back because it doesn't look like the swatch. It never looks like the swatch. The swatch is an inch square under fluorescent light and the wall is nine feet tall in a room with windows. Of course it doesn't match.

I keep almost sneezing and then not. That's been happening all day. The almost-sneeze is worse than the sneeze.
