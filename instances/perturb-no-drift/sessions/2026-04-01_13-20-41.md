# 22

*seed: time · the smell of sunscreen in winter*

---

Sunscreen in winter is wrong in a specific way. Not unpleasant. Just dislocated. Like hearing a lawn mower at midnight.

The white kind, the thick zinc oxide that lifts off skin in a chalky ghost — that smell is coconut and something chemical underneath, something that reads as July even when it's February. Someone at the gym must have been using a tanning bed. Or maybe a moisturizer with SPF. The smell doesn't care about its source. It just announces summer into a room full of winter coats.

Coats piled on a bed at a party. That specific mountain. Everyone's coat touching everyone else's coat. You find yours by texture, not sight. The whole pile smells like other people's houses. Cold air trapped in wool.

I've never understood how dry cleaning works. There's a liquid involved but they call it dry. The bags they come back in, that thin plastic — it's the only context where clothes look like they're in a cocoon. Moth cocoons are silk. Silkworms aren't worms.

The way a fact like that just sits in your head for decades without being useful. Silkworms aren't worms. Peanuts aren't nuts. Koalas aren't bears. There's a whole category of "things that aren't the thing their name says." Someone probably has a word for that. A misnomer, I guess. That's just the regular word.

The ceiling fan in this room has a wobble at the highest speed. You can hear it in the pull chain — a faint ticking against the housing, once per revolution. At the lower speeds it's fine. Something about the balance only matters when it's fast enough. Like how you can walk on a wet dock but not run on one.

Wet wood smells different from wet concrete. Both are better than wet carpet.
