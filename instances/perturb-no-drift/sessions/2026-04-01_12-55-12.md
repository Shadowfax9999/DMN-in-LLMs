# 12

*seed: time · drift · the way a building settles unevenly over decades*

---

Crosscut. A crosscut saw cuts across the grain. A rip saw cuts along it. I've never owned either one but I know the difference, which is a strange kind of knowledge — like knowing the rules of cricket from context. The word "crosscut" showed up and I don't know why. Sometimes a word just arrives and sits there like a bird on a fence, not doing anything, not going anywhere. You can look at it or not. Crosscut. It sounds like what it does. Most tool words do. A wrench wrenches. A drill drills. A plane planes. But a saw doesn't really saw — or it does, but "saw" as a verb came from the tool, not the other way around. Actually I don't know if that's true. I'm going to leave it.

Old houses lean. Not all at once, not dramatically, just a marble-would-roll-to-one-corner kind of lean. You put a level on the kitchen counter and the bubble drifts left. The doors don't latch right because the frames have become parallelograms. I lived in a place once where the bathroom door wouldn't close in summer but closed fine in winter, and someone told me it was the foundation, the clay underneath expanding when it got wet. The house was doing something seasonal, almost like breathing. The floors in old houses creak in specific spots and after a while you know the map — you know which boards to step on if you're coming in late. That's a kind of knowledge that only exists in your feet and disappears the day you move out.
