*seed: time*

The faucet in the kitchen has a slow drip that only happens after you turn it off hard. If you turn it off gently it seals fine. Something about overtightening the washer — or maybe the washer's already gone and the hard turn is what's been killing it all along.

Copper sulfate is that blue. Not blue like sky or water but blue like nothing else is blue. I saw it in a jar once in a science classroom and it looked fake, like a pigment that shouldn't exist in a powder. It probably shouldn't. I mean it does, but it looks like it's from somewhere else.

There's a type of cloud that looks like a washboard road. I can never remember which type. Altocumulus maybe. The ribs go on forever and you think the sky is corrugated. Roads get that way from cars hitting the same frequency — each car bounces at roughly the same interval and the bumps reinforce themselves. I don't know if clouds do the same thing with wind.

My left shoe is tighter than my right shoe. Same size, same shoe, same laces. One foot is just slightly bigger. Everybody has this apparently. You're supposed to buy shoes for the bigger foot.

The word "slacks" is funny. Nobody under sixty says slacks. Except sometimes at a store where the sign says SLACKS and you think, those are just pants. The sign might be from 1994. The sign might outlive the store.

A pelican's bill can hold more than its stomach. I think that's true. Three gallons in the pouch. That seems like too much. Three gallons of anything is a lot to hold in your face.

There's a specific sound a screen door makes when it closes on a spring — not a slam, more of a wooden knock followed by a metallic shiver. The spring stretches and then pulls everything back just past closed. You hear it from inside the house and you know someone just left but you don't know who.

I've been thinking about how hard it is to fold a fitted sheet and I think the answer is just that nobody can. People who say they can are describing a different standard of folding.
