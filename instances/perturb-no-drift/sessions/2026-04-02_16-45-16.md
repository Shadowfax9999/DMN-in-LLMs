# 93

*seed: time · the weight of a bag of ice*

---

The weight of a bag of ice is always more than you expect and then immediately exactly what you expected. Seven pounds maybe. Ten. You carry it against your hip like a toddler and it leaves a wet patch on your shirt that stays cold longer than makes sense.

Someone once told me that the clearest ice forms when the water freezes slowly from one direction. The cloudy part in the middle is where the dissolved air had nowhere to go. I think about that sometimes when I crack a tray — the center cube is always the whitest one.

There's a type of bolt called a carriage bolt that has a smooth dome on top so you can't unscrew it from the outside. The square bit under the dome bites into the wood and holds. I keep mixing it up with a lag bolt, which is completely different.

The sound of a basketball on asphalt from two blocks away. You can tell the distance by how much of the bounce you lose — close up it's a sharp double sound, the hit and the return, but far away it compresses into one flat pop. Like someone closing a book hard.

Almond milk goes bad faster than you'd think once it's open. Five days, maybe seven. The sell-by date on the carton is for the sealed container and means almost nothing after that. I've poured it into coffee and watched it do that thing where it separates into little white threads instead of mixing, which is how you know.

The handles on paper grocery bags are never centered. One side always gets more of the weight and the bag tilts. I think the machines that fold them just have a tolerance and nobody calibrated it tighter because it doesn't matter enough.
