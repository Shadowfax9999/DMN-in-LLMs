Hopscotch. The chalk lines always faded from the edges inward, so by the end of summer the squares looked like they were dissolving into the sidewalk. The numbers went last.

There's a Cortázar novel called that. You can read it in two different orders. I never have.

The smell of a new box of crayons is a specific wax — paraffin, maybe. Somewhere between a candle and a shoe-polish tin. Crayola has that 64-pack where the sharpener is built into the back of the box, and the sharpener never works well. It just chews the crayon into a slightly less blunt shape. You use it anyway because it's there.

A microwave in another room sounds completely different from a microwave in the room you're in. In another room it's just the hum. In the room, there's a higher whine you don't notice until it stops. Most kitchen appliances announce themselves more by stopping than by running. The fridge clicks off and the room gets wider.

Socks on a hardwood floor. The specific commit of deciding to slide.

I think Rhode Island is smaller than some counties in Texas. I don't know which counties. Brewster County might be one. It's the one with Big Bend in it — enormous, almost no one lives there. There's something about a place that's politically organized but basically empty. A county with more square miles than people.

The tab on a soda can — someone had to figure out the geometry of that. The rivet, the lever ratio, the score line in the aluminum that breaks cleanly. Before stay-on tabs people used to pull them off and drop them inside the can or leave them on the ground. Pull tabs everywhere, like little metal fish.

Cotton candy dissolves so fast it almost isn't food. It's a texture pretending to be a flavor. County fairs smell like it mixed with diesel from the generators running the rides.
