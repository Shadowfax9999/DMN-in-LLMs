*seed: time · drift · how a typewriter's escapement mechanism advances the carriage one space per keystroke*

Mute is a strange word for a button that doesn't silence anything, just redirects. Redirects are what the typewriter escapement does — each keystrike trips a lever that lets the carriage slide exactly one tooth forward on a rack. Rack and pinion, that's what it's called, though I'm not sure the typewriter version qualifies. Qualifies is a word that does too much work in job postings. Postings on a bulletin board at a laundromat, handwritten, the phone number cut into little vertical strips at the bottom. Bottom of the page always curls in humidity. Humidity is the thing that makes a room feel smaller than it is. Is there a name for the sound a screen door makes when it doesn't latch on the first try. Try rolling rubber cement off a photograph and you'll pull the image right up. Up is the direction the typewriter carriage wants to go, spring-loaded, and the escapement is the thing that says not yet, not yet, one more letter. Letter spacing on a typewriter is fixed, which is why the lowercase i looks so lonely in the middle of its allotted rectangle. Rectangle of light on a floor from a window in a door. Door hardware comes in more finishes than anyone needs. Needs is a strong word for most of what's in a kitchen drawer. Drawer pulls have a standard screw spacing, something like three inches. Inches might be the last imperial measurement to die. Die is what the mimeograph did, though I couldn't tell you the decade. Decade feels like the wrong unit for how fast rubber cement disappeared from classrooms. Classrooms had that specific clock, the one with the red second hand that moved in whole-second jumps. Jumps like that are called discrete rather than continuous. Continuous motion is what the escapement prevents — it takes the smooth energy of the spring and portions it out, one click at a time. Time to think about something else but the escapement is still in my head, that ratchet sound. Sound of a typewriter is mostly the striker hitting the ribbon hitting the paper hitting the platen, four impacts in maybe eight milliseconds. Milliseconds seem too fast for something you can hear as a single clack.
