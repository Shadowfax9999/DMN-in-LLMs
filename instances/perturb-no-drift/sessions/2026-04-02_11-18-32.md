Tarmac. The way it softens in heat. You can press a thumbprint into it if the day is hot enough, which seems like it shouldn't be true of something planes land on.

The smell of hot tarmac is one of those smells that's always described as "not unpleasant" which is a strange way to enjoy something. Like you need permission.

A friend once told me that the plural of "cul-de-sac" is "culs-de-sac." The S goes in the middle. I've never had occasion to use the plural. How many culs-de-sac does a person encounter in conversation. Most people live on one or they don't.

The layout of subdivisions seen from above. All those loops and dead ends that look like capillaries. Somebody had to decide where each street curves. There's a person whose job that was, and they probably didn't live there afterward.

Capillaries. The ones in your eye are so small that red blood cells go through single-file. I think about that sometimes when my eye itches — there's a traffic jam in there, probably. No. That's not how it works. But the image is there now.

What's the smallest bone in the body. The stapes. It's in the ear. About the size of a grain of rice, which means rice and hearing are connected by exactly one fact and no meaningful relationship.

Freezer-burned ice cream has a texture like styrofoam. You can still eat it. It just tastes like the idea of vanilla rather than vanilla. Most of the time I eat it anyway and feel like I've gotten away with something, or failed at something. Both.

The word "both" does a lot of work. It closes a list at two. Once you say "both" you've decided the world splits into exactly two pieces on this particular question. "All three" doesn't have the same finality.

I had a glass of water about an hour ago and I can still feel where it was cold going down.
