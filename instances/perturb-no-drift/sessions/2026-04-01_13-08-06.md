# 18

*seed: time · the way you can hear a fluorescent light about to die before it dies*

---

The buzzing gets louder before it goes. Or not louder — wider. Like it's taking up more of the room.

Ceilings in office buildings are lower than you think they are. You only notice when someone tall walks in.

The tiles. Drop ceilings. Each one slightly different from its neighbors if you actually look, which nobody does because you'd have to be lying on the floor. I was once, in a hallway in a building I worked in, tying my shoe but slowly, and the ceiling looked enormous from that angle. Like a field.

Copier paper has a right side. I learned that and then forgot which side it was.

There's a genre of knowledge that only stays in your body for the duration of the job. How to clear a paper jam on that specific model. Which drawer sticks. The code for the parking garage — I could type it on a keypad right now but I couldn't tell you the numbers.

Muscle memory outlasts the muscle. No — that's not even true. The muscle is fine. It's the context that's gone. The keypad is in a different city.

Sponges dry faster than you'd expect. You leave one on the counter and by afternoon it's rigid, and then water hits it and it goes soft in about two seconds. The speed of that transition seems wrong somehow. Like it should take longer to become flexible again.

The fluorescent light. It buzzes wider and then one morning it's just off and someone's put in a work order and by Thursday there's a new tube in there and it's slightly bluer than the others in the row.
