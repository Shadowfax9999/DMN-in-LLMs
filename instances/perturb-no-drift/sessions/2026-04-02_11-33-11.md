The way a spoon looks wrong when you see it from directly above. Just a circle with a line coming off it. You'd never know what it was for.

I've been thinking about how many things in a kitchen drawer only make sense from the side. Tongs, a peeler, a whisk. From above they're all just circles or ovals. The drawer is a world of cross-sections pretending to be objects.

There's a kind of plastic that gets cloudy where it bends. CD jewel cases used to do that — the hinge would go white after a few openings. Stress whitening. The molecules pulling apart just enough to scatter light differently. The material is remembering every time you opened it.

No — it's not remembering. It's just damaged. That's fine.

A guy at a bus stop eating a muffin with a fork. Out of a Tupperware container. Full commitment. Napkin on his knee. I think about him probably once a year and I have no idea when I saw him. Could have been 2014.

The ceiling fan in the room I'm not in right now is probably still. Ceiling fans when they're off look like they've never moved. There's no residue of motion in a stopped fan the way there is in, say, a swing that's come to rest — a swing still sways a little, or at least you expect it to. A fan just stops and becomes furniture.

Saffron is more expensive per pound than gold, or it was at some point — the kind of fact that gets repeated without anyone checking whether it's still true. There are a lot of facts like that, circulating on outdated exchange rates.

The sound of someone else's shower through a wall. You can't tell if they're in it or if it's just running. It sounds the same either way. Water doesn't sound different when it hits a person versus a tub floor, or if it does, the wall filters out the difference.

I wonder if that's actually true. It probably hits differently — a shoulder would break the stream up more than porcelain would. But through drywall and insulation, yeah, it probably all just sounds like static.

Grape stems are surprisingly stiff for how thin they are.
