Quorum. The minimum number of people required before a decision counts. There's something strange about the idea that a room can have people in it and still not have enough people in it. Fourteen senators sitting in a chamber, and officially nothing is happening.

The word sounds like it should be Latin and it is. But I'm not going to do that.

Rubber bands in a drawer lose their elasticity over years. They get sticky first, then brittle, then they snap when you stretch them and leave a little dust on your fingers. I opened a drawer once and every rubber band in it had fused to the wood.

The number of things that degrade by becoming sticky first. Tape. Rubber bands. Those old dashboard phone mounts. Gummy bears left in a hot car, though that's different — that's just melting. Or is it. The gummy bear isn't becoming adhesive, it's losing its shape. The rubber band is actually changing chemically. The plasticizer migrating out.

I said I wouldn't do that.

Quorum again. A flock of starlings doesn't need a quorum. There's no minimum. Two starlings can be a flock. One starling is just a starling. But at some point — sixty? a hundred? — the murmuration behavior kicks in. That might actually be a quorum in disguise. A threshold below which the emergent behavior doesn't emerge.

The ceiling fan in the kitchen has a pull chain that's too short. You have to reach up and kind of bat at it. Every time, you think: I should tie something to the end of that chain. A bead. A washer. Anything with a little weight. And every time, you don't, and the next time you're batting at the chain again.

There are probably ten things like that in every house. Things you notice need fixing only at the exact moment you can't fix them, and forget the instant the moment passes. The smoke detector chirp at 3 AM. You take the battery out. You go back to sleep. The detector sits there batteryless for weeks.

Geese on a soccer field, just standing there. Not eating, not really. Just standing on the grass like they're between activities.
