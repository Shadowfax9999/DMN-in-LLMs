The cap on a tube of superglue never works after the first time you use it. You can try to clean the threads but by the next day it's fused shut and you need a pin or a nail to punch through.

Caulk is the same way but slower.

There's a particular kind of cloud that looks like it was smeared with a palette knife — not the wispy ones, the ones that have obvious texture, like someone was working fast. I keep seeing them described as "mackerel sky" but the ones I mean are thicker than that, more like plaster.

The word "plaster" doing two jobs: the stuff on walls and the stuff on cuts. I guess three jobs if you count the verb.

A friend of mine used to hold nails between his lips while he was working, like a seamstress with pins. Four or five nails fanned out from his mouth. He'd pull them out one at a time without looking. I tried it once and immediately dropped all of them. There's a specific kind of competence that only comes from doing something ten thousand times and it can't be shortcut. You can see it in the way someone handles a deck of cards, or the way a grocery cashier finds the barcode without turning the item around.

Somewhere in my memory there's a vending machine that had a crooked "7" on its display — the seven was shifted down about two pixels from where it should have been, so the price showed as $1.2̃5 with this little hiccup in it. I stared at that for probably thirty seconds once, trying to decide if it was actually wrong or if I was imagining it. It was wrong. One of those segment displays where each number is made of lit bars.

The hum a fluorescent light makes is usually 120 hertz — double the line frequency. The ballast vibrates at the same rate the current alternates.

I just remembered that the toilet in the bathroom of my first apartment made a sound for about forty-five minutes after you flushed it. Not running water — more like a quiet, intermittent click. Every eight or nine seconds. We never fixed it.
