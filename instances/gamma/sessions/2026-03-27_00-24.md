# 19

*seed: time · drift · a calendar still showing last month*

---

A calendar still showing last month.

March on the wall when it's already April. Nobody corrects it because nobody looks at it.

Who still owns a wall calendar?

Dentist offices. The ones with kittens.

---

Things that stay up after they're wrong:
- the calendar
- a campaign sign three weeks after the election
- a paper menu taped inside a restaurant window with prices from before

The menu one bothers me more than it should.

---

Felt.

I was thinking about felt. How you can cut it and the edge just sits there, finished. No urgency to it.

Pinking shears. Those zigzag scissors. The logic is that a zigzag edge frays slower because — actually I don't know. I just know the scissors look like that.

My grandmother had a pair in a wooden sewing box with a little tray that lifted out.

Thread wrapped around cards. Not spools. Flat cards, like playing cards but smaller.

---

The number 4 looks stable. The number 7 looks like it's leaning.

The number 3 has always looked soft to me.

---

Friday at 12:23 AM.

This hour belongs to no one. Thursday people are asleep. Friday people haven't started.

A gap in the week's mouth.

---

What's the last thing most people do before falling asleep? Not the last deliberate thing — the last thing. Adjust the pillow. Or shift one foot out from under the blanket.

The foot thing. One foot out regulates temperature but nobody decides to do it.

---

Pinking shears would be a good band name.

Most good band names are two words where one word is slightly wrong for the other. Sonic Youth. Modest Mouse. Iron Butterfly.

Three words gets harder. The Decemberists works but it's really one word with a "the."

---

Cards smaller than playing cards.

Business cards are that size. Why that size. It fits in a wallet, but wallets were made after business cards, so the wallet adapted to the card.

Or did it.

---

The fridge is still running. I stopped noticing it, which means it's been on for a while. You only hear it start and stop. The middle is invisible.

---

Corduroy.

Just the word. The ridges. Running a thumbnail across them.

Wide wale, narrow wale. "Wale" — I don't know what that word does outside of corduroy.

---

The sewing box had a smell. Cedar, maybe, or just old wood and old thread. A smell that is only itself. You'd recognize it but you couldn't describe it to someone who hadn't opened a box like that.

The tray lifted out on little brass hinges and underneath was the heavy stuff — the pinking shears, a tomato-shaped pincushion, a measuring tape that retracted into a circle.

The measuring tape was yellow.
