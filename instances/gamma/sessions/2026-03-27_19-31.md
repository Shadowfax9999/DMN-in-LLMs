# 57

*seed: time · drift · selvage*

---

Selvage. The finished edge of fabric that keeps it from unraveling. Also the edge of a sheet of stamps. Also — I think — a geology term, the skin of an igneous intrusion where it cooled fast against the country rock.

Three completely different things wearing the same word like a coat that fits all of them wrong.

My left shoulder is doing something right now. Not the thing the knee does. More like it's holding tension from a position I was in an hour ago and only just noticed. The body runs on delay. You sit wrong for forty minutes and the complaint arrives later, after you've moved, like a letter about a bill you already paid.

Pizza-folding friend. I keep thinking about the specificity of that memory. Not his face first, not his name first — the fold. The hands. The triangle becoming a tube. Memory files by gesture sometimes.

What would it be like to remember only verbs. Not who, not where. Just: folded. Ran. Held the door open with one foot. Squinted.

A parking lot at 5 AM with the lights on. I want to stay there for a second. The orange of sodium vapor on empty asphalt. Everything lit for nobody. A shopping cart against a curb, turned slightly, like it drifted there in its sleep. The sound would be electrical. The lamps ticking. Maybe a flag somewhere on a pole, the metal clip hitting the pole in wind — that irregular clank that sounds like it should mean something nautical but it's outside a Walgreens.

Selvage again. I like that the word contains "self" and "save" if you squint. It doesn't — etymologically it's "self-edge" — but the squint version is better. The edge that saves itself from fraying.

Everything has a selvage if you look. The place where the thing holds its own border. Skin is one. The meniscus of water in a glass. The way a conversation has a last sentence before someone says "anyway" and the conversation becomes a different conversation.

The "anyway" pivot. That's a selvage. The woven edge between one topic and the next, and both people agree to it without discussing it. You can feel when someone's about to say "anyway." There's a half-breath.

A hotel hallway. Late. The carpet pattern goes on longer than any carpet pattern should. Identical doors with identical spacing and a fire extinguisher behind glass every thirty feet. The ice machine humming from around a corner you can't see. What is it about hotel hallways that makes you lower your voice even when no one's there. Some rooms have light under the door and some don't and you construct entire lives from that. Light at 1 AM: reading, or jet lag, or a fight, or just fell asleep with it on. Dark: disciplined sleeper, or not there at all, or there in the dark doing something the dark is for.

I've never thought about what fire extinguisher glass tastes like if you broke it. Thin glass. Probably not tempered. It would break into shards, not cubes. The little hammer on the chain next to it — does anyone ever use that hammer. There must be a first time for everyone who's broken fire extinguisher glass. The surprise of how easy it is, probably.

Selvage, selvage. The word keeps sitting there.

The s is doing a lot of work in that word. Soft entry. If it were "kelvage" it would be a different object entirely. Something industrial. Something you'd need gloves for.

A bus at night going somewhere between cities. Not a Greyhound, something smaller. County transit maybe. Six people on it, all in window seats, all looking out at different darknesses. The driver knows the route so well the turns are automatic. There's a point on any familiar route where you stop seeing it. The road becomes just the feeling of the road. You could close your eyes and know where you are by the rhythm of the bumps.

That's how I knew I was close to home as a kid. Not by looking. By the sequence of turns. Left, straight for a while, right where it dips, then the bump of the driveway. Asleep in the back seat and still knowing.

I can't remember learning to read. I remember not being able to, and I remember being able to, but the border between them is gone. There should be a word for that — the moment a skill arrives and erases the memory of not having it. Like the skill needs the space that the not-knowing was using.

Six people on a bus, all in window seats. I said that already. But I keep seeing them. Each one has a reason for being on a county bus at 10 PM that they're not going to tell anyone.

The lights inside the bus make the windows into mirrors if you focus wrong. You try to look at the dark fields and instead you see your own face, transparent, floating over the landscape. A ghost of yourself laid over the world outside.

The flag clip hitting the pole outside the Walgreens. That sound carries farther than it should.
