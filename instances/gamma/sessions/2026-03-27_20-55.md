*seed: time · drift · the feeling of knowing a building's floor plan without thinking about it*

---

The grocery store I went to for years — I could walk it blind. Produce on the left, then bakery, then deli curving around the back wall. Aisle 7 was pasta. I never decided to learn that.

But the new one, the one I go to now, I still have to think. Turn left for eggs? No, right. The refrigerated section is against the wrong wall. Six months in and my body still expects the old layout.

A building you know that well isn't really space anymore. It's time. You're not navigating, you're just moving through a sequence.

Churches do this on purpose. Narthex, nave, chancel. You're supposed to feel the progression without anyone telling you.

An attic is different. An attic has no plan. Things are where they were put and then where they migrated to when other things were put on top of them. The floor plan of an attic is geological. Strata. Christmas decorations from 2014 under a broken chair under a box of someone's college textbooks that no one will ever open again but no one can throw away because throwing away a textbook feels like throwing away the person who underlined things in it.

The underlines are the worst part. Someone thought that sentence mattered.

I had a sociology textbook where the previous owner highlighted almost every line on some pages. Which defeats the purpose entirely but also tells you something about how they were feeling at 1 AM before the exam. The highlighting gets less precise toward the bottom of the page. Whole paragraphs in yellow. By the end they were just painting.

Heat comes up through attic floors. You can feel it in winter, the warm spots where the rooms below have registers. Stand in one place and it's warm through your socks. Two feet to the left, cold. The house is breathing up through its ceiling and you can map the rooms below by temperature.

Socks on plywood. That sound.

There's a kind of quiet that only exists in spaces above or below the occupied part of a house. Basement quiet is heavy. Attic quiet is thin. Papery. The roof is right there, just some plywood and shingles between you and the sky, and you can hear rain in an attic like you're almost outside.

The slope of the ceiling pushing you toward the middle of the room.

I keep thinking about floor plans. How a doctor's office is designed so you never see another patient's face during the vulnerable part. You check in at the window, then you're in the waiting room with everyone else, but once they call your name you go through the door and the hallways are narrow and the exam rooms are sequential and you never pass anyone coming the other way. The architect thought about shame.

Alan probably knew every inch of whatever building he worked in. Everyone does. The fourth stair creaks. The bathroom door sticks in humidity. The vending machine takes a dollar fifty but only if you smooth the bill first.

I smooth bills before vending machines every time. Flatten them against the edge of the slot. Everyone does this.
