*seed: time · drift · jacks*

Jacks. The little metal stars you throw on the ground. They hurt to step on, not as bad as Legos but in a different way, more pointed and specific. The ball that comes with jacks is always too small, always red, always bounces higher than you expect.

The word "onesies" meant something before it meant baby clothes. You'd scoop one jack at a time. Twosies. Threesies. The whole game is about doing more with less time, the ball going up and coming down, and your hand has to be faster each round. I played on a kitchen floor that was that brownish-gold linoleum. The kind with flecks in it.

Flecks. Terrazzo is just flecks taken seriously. Whole airport floors made of someone else's rubble.

There's a thing where you can hear a song in your head perfectly — the exact timbre of the singer's voice, the way the drums sit slightly behind the beat — but you couldn't reproduce any of it. The internal version is flawless and completely trapped. Like knowing what cinnamon tastes like without being able to describe it to someone who's never had it.

Pencils don't smell like they used to. Or maybe they do and noses just recalibrate. The yellow ones with the green eraser that smeared more than it erased. The metal part is called a ferrule. I know that and I don't know why I know that. It's in there taking up room where something useful could go.

What are the things you know for no reason?

The capital of South Dakota. The fact that a group of flamingos is called a flamboyance. That Pluto's moon is almost as big as Pluto. The order of the colors in a resistor code. I couldn't wire a resistor if my life depended on it but I know the colors go somewhere.

A resistor code is basically a tiny painted stripe system. Someone in a factory paints bands of color on a little cylinder and the colors mean numbers and the numbers mean how much electricity gets through and the whole thing is smaller than a grain of rice sometimes. There are people whose whole job is painting those bands, or was, before machines did it.

My left knee clicks when I go down stairs but not up. Only down.

Trying to picture a spiral staircase and figure out which direction it turns. You end up moving your hand in the air. The hand knows things the mind can't hold as images. Like when someone asks you which way you turn a screw to loosen it and you have to mime it. Righty-tighty. The phrase does the remembering for you.

A lot of knowledge lives in phrases. Thirty days hath September. Roy G. Biv. Every good boy does fine. The phrases outlast the knowledge they carry — I can sing the whole thing and still have to count on my fingers to check if June has 30 or 31.

June has 30. I think. The knuckle trick works better. Knuckle months are long, valley months are short. But you run out of knuckles before you run out of months, so you have to start over at the index finger, and July and August are both knuckle months, which is why they're both 31, which always seemed like a flaw in the system. Two long months in a row.

Something about the word "August" feels heavier than "July." August has that gust in it. July is lighter, almost a name. Julie. Augustus was a name too but it went the other direction, got formal and then got monthly and now it's just hot.

The parking lot at the grocery store has those yellow concrete bumpers at the end of each space. They're always slightly crooked, like they were placed by hand and nobody came back to straighten them. Some of them have rebar sticking out where the concrete chipped away. Little rusty hooks.

I keep thinking about how many things are held in place by two bolts. Two bolts is the minimum for something that isn't allowed to spin. One bolt and it pivots. Two bolts and it's fixed. Three is insurance.

The number two comes up a lot in fastening. Two nails. Two screws. Two bolts. A hinge needs at least two screws per leaf or it sags. Four screws total for a cabinet hinge, usually, and three of them are doing the real work and one is just there so the hole isn't empty.

Cabinet hinges come in more varieties than seems reasonable. There's a whole aisle at the hardware store that's just hinges. European hinges, butt hinges, piano hinges, concealed hinges, self-closing hinges, soft-close hinges. The soft-close ones have a little hydraulic dampener inside them, like a tiny shock absorber for a cabinet door.

A fly just landed on the window screen and it's cleaning its front legs together, rubbing them like it's scheming. They all do that. Every fly, the same gesture, like they're about to deliver bad news.

The screen has that particular gray color that's not quite the color of anything else. Screen-gray. And when you look through it at something bright, the screen disappears, but when you look *at* it, the outside disappears. You can't hold both at the same time.

That's true of a lot of things but I'm just thinking about the screen right now. The mesh is finer than I'd expect if I had to guess the spacing. Maybe sixteen squares per inch. Maybe more. Each little square is its own window.
