# 31

*seed: time · drift*

---

The underside of a fitted sheet has that scratchy tag and you can never remember which corner it goes in. You rotate. Try again. Wrong. Rotate again. It's a 50/50 chance and somehow you get it wrong three times. There's a math thing about that but I don't care about the math thing.

Fitted sheets are the only piece of fabric most people wrestle. Actually wrestle, like it's resisting. Folding one is just accepting a soft lump.

There's a particular kind of tired where making a bed feels like a project. Not exhaustion, just that low-fuel state where every step becomes its own discrete task. Walk to the closet. Get the sheet. Unfold it. Find the tag corner. You're narrating instructions to yourself for something you've done a thousand times.

The word "crisp" for sheets. Crisp for apples. Crisp for autumn air. Crisp for a new bill. They don't share anything obvious except that feeling of — clean edges, maybe. Resistance when you press.

Hotel beds are tight in a way that feels like being held down. Some people love that. I think most people immediately kick the bottom out on one side. You make a pocket for your feet and then the whole system loosens.

The thing about a pillowcase is you grab the pillow through it and flip the case over. Like turning a sock inside out but in reverse. Like swallowing.

Laundry that's been sitting in the dryer for two days has a specific temperature. Not warm, not cold. Room temperature but it feels different from room temperature because you expected warm.

A ceiling fan pulling chain. That little brass acorn at the end. You reach up and pull it and the fan changes speed but you can never remember if it cycles low-medium-high-off or low-high-medium-off so you just keep pulling until it's the speed you want.

The acorn shape has threads inside it where it screws onto the chain. Tiny threads, like the inside of a light bulb socket but smaller.

My left knee does a thing when I go down stairs where it clicks once, at the same step in the staircase every time. Not pain. Just a click. Like a turnstile.

Turnstiles in the New York subway have that heavy revolving bar and then somewhere they switched to those waist-high doors that swing open. The swinging doors feel less like you're being processed.

Processed. Like cheese.

The light coming through a bedroom window right now, this time of morning in late March, is the kind that makes dust visible. Not the dust itself but the movement of it. You see it and realize the air in the room is never still. Even with the windows closed, even with nothing moving, there's a slow circulation happening.

I'm thinking about the fitted sheet again. The elastic goes around the whole edge but it's tighter at the corners. When the elastic starts to go — on an old sheet — the corners won't grip and the sheet pops off the mattress at 3 AM and you're sleeping on a bare mattress corner with a wad of sheet next to you.

There's a product that's just suspenders for sheets. Clips that go under the mattress. I've never used one.
