The thing about approximating a pitch you can't verify is that you commit to it anyway. B-flat. It's B-flat now. If someone came in with a tuner it could be A, it could be B, it could be somewhere in the cracks between piano keys, and it wouldn't matter because the fridge doesn't know it's singing.

Singing is generous. Droning.

There's a fly somewhere in this room that I can hear but not locate. That's a specific kind of frustration — not the fly itself but the fact that sound won't point. You turn your head and it shifts and you turn again and it's gone and then it's back at the wrong ear. Flies in the dark are all sound.

What do flies do at night. Do they sleep. They must land somewhere.

A friend once said she couldn't sleep in hotels because the air felt "too flat." Not stale, not cold, not dry — flat. And everyone in the room knew exactly what she meant and nobody could have defined it. The air in hotels has been processed into something that has no opinion about anything.

Flat again. Flat air, flat note, flat tire. The word is doing a lot of work.

I was going to leave that alone.

Hands on a steering wheel at ten and two. They changed it. Now they say nine and three, because of airbags. The airbag comes out of the center and if your hands are at ten and two it drives them into your face. So the recommendation changed and nobody noticed. People are still driving around with their hands at ten and two, their driving instructors' voices in their heads, and the safety engineers moved the target and didn't tell anyone in a way that stuck.

Nine and three is less heroic-looking anyway. Ten and two looks like you're piloting something. Nine and three looks like you're holding a serving tray.

The specific weight of a cat stepping onto a bed at night. You feel it before you identify it. A pressure that has intention in it. Not like a book falling off the nightstand — that's just gravity being gravity. The cat is choosing where to step. You can feel the choosing.

There's a word in Japanese — I don't remember it — for theستشعار of being watched. Not paranoia. The physical sensation on the back of the neck. English doesn't have a single word for it but the sensation is so universal that it feels like it should have one. Maybe it does and I've forgotten it.

Forgetting a word you definitely knew is different from never having known it. The gap has a shape. You can feel the word's length, sometimes its first letter, sometimes its rhythm. It's there but it's behind something.

The fly again. Closer now, or I think closer. Hard to say whether it moved or I just started listening harder.

Listening harder. You can't actually change your ears. You change your attention, and your attention has a zoom lens but your ears don't. Everything that's coming in was already coming in. You just stopped ignoring part of it.

The fridge is still on. Still B-flat. I'm more sure now, which probably means I'm wrong in a more committed way.

There's a sprinkler going somewhere outside. Not close. The ch-ch-ch-ch-ch and then the longer chhhhhh of the return sweep. Sprinklers at night always seem like a mistake, like someone forgot to turn it off, even when watering at night is the whole point because less evaporation. A sprinkler in daylight looks intentional. A sprinkler at night sounds lost.

My left foot is asleep. That fizzing. It's not pain and it's not pleasure and it's not nothing. It's its own category that only has one member.

Actually no, a limb falling asleep and the static on a dead TV channel are in the same category. Visual static and nerve static. Both of them are systems with nothing to report reporting anyway.

I should move my foot but I'm not going to yet.
