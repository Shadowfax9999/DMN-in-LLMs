*seed: time · drift · the way a street looks different when it's wet*

---

A street looks different when it's wet because it becomes a mirror and mirrors are always a little unsettling when you don't expect them.

No — that's not it. It looks different because the darks get darker. The asphalt goes from gray to black and suddenly you can see where the patches are, where they resurfaced one lane and not the other. Wet pavement is honest pavement. Dry pavement hides its history.

I'm in a parking lot apparently. Friday evening. The fluorescent light with its rhythm.

What do I actually want to think about.

Nothing. That's the point. But "nothing" is hard to aim at. You aim at nothing and hit something every time.

Teeth. I was at the dentist recently and the hygienist asked me to "relax my tongue" and I realized I had no idea where my tongue usually sits. Like asking someone to relax their personality. The tongue has a resting position but nobody teaches it to you. You've been parking it somewhere in your mouth for decades without ever deciding where.

The parking lot. Friday at almost eight. There's a quality to institutional lighting at dusk — it gets more visible as the sky dims, like it's been waiting all day for its moment. At noon a parking lot light is nothing. At ten PM it's just part of night. But right now, at the crossover, you can watch it winning. The sky is still blue enough to compete and losing.

Competing light sources. Two people talking at the same time. Two songs playing from two cars. There's a specific discomfort when you can't decide which one to listen to. Your attention flickers. Not split — flickering. Like a eye tracking a fly.

An eye tracking a fly. That's involuntary. Saccades, they're called. The eye jumps, it doesn't slide. Even when you think you're smoothly following something, you're actually jumping and landing, jumping and landing. Vision is a strobe. We just fill in the gaps.

Filling in the gaps is most of what perception is.

The friend who could sleep in a canoe. I said I envied the trust but actually what I envied was simpler than that. He could stop. Just stop being awake. I've never been good at stopping things. I read past the end of chapters. I stay at parties forty minutes too long. Not because I'm enjoying them but because leaving requires a specific act of will that arriving doesn't.

Arriving is free. Leaving costs something.

A car just pulled in. Headlights swept across the lot and for a second everything had a shadow pointing east. Then it parked and the shadows went back to their fluorescent positions, pointing in four directions at once. Under parking lot lights nothing has one shadow. Everything has several, faint, pointing away from each other. A disagreement about where the darkness should fall.

I'm cold. Not very. Just enough to notice my forearms.

The specific temperature where you become aware you have skin. Below that, you're just a mind moving through air. Above that, you're sweating and the body insists on itself. But there's a band, maybe fifteen degrees wide, where you genuinely forget you're a physical thing. Seventy-two degrees in a room with no wind. The body disappears. That's comfort, maybe. Not feeling good. Feeling nothing.

The pharmacy music thing. What is that genre. It's not jazz and it's not pop and it's not classical. It's the ghost of all three. Someone recorded it. Someone played those saxophone notes into a microphone, in a studio, with intention. Then it got sold to a licensing company and piped into ten thousand pharmacies where its entire purpose is to not be heard. A musician's work, perfected into invisibility.

I wonder if that musician tells people at parties. What do you do? I play saxophone. Oh cool, where? Walgreens.

That's mean. Probably it's a session musician who plays on lots of things and the pharmacy stuff is just Tuesday afternoon work. Still. Somewhere there's a person whose saxophone I've heard three hundred times and whose name I will never know.

Three hundred times. That's probably low. If you go to a pharmacy once a month for twenty years, and each visit is ten minutes, and the background music loops every forty minutes — you hear each track maybe six times a year. Over twenty years. A hundred and twenty times. For one pharmacy. If you go to grocery stores and elevators and waiting rooms

the number is probably in the thousands. I've heard more invisible saxophone than I've heard of any album I love.
