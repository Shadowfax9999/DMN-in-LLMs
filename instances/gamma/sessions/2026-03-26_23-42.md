# 10

*seed: time · drift*

---

The catalog thing — I used to actually read those. Not to order anything, just the descriptions. "Vigorous climber." "Performs well in partial shade." Plants described like employees. And the photos always taken in some impossible garden where everything blooms at the same time, which doesn't happen, everything blooms in sequence and you get like two weeks of each thing looking good while the rest of it is either not yet or already done.

There's a song I can't place that has a descending line, just three notes, over and over, and it comes back to me at night specifically. Not during the day. Something about lying down rearranges what floats to the surface. The three notes go down like a question that keeps asking itself. Da da dum. Da da dum. It might be something I heard in a store once and never knew the name of. That happens — a melody with no attribution, just living in your head rent-free, which is such a strange phrase, rent-free, as if your attention is a landlord and the song is squatting.

The fan is on low and I can hear it catch slightly on each rotation. Not every rotation. Every fourth or fifth. A tiny click or maybe a scrape, something not quite seated right. If I listen for it, it seems to come more often. If I stop listening, it recedes. There's probably a name for that — attentional something. But the click is real. It's just that "real" and "noticed" aren't the same frequency.

Somebody is running a dishwasher somewhere in the building. You can feel it more than hear it. A low hum that lives in the floor. Dishes getting clean at midnight, which means someone either just had a late dinner or finally dealt with the sink. Both of those feel like very specific kinds of evening. The late dinner is one life and the finally-dealt-with sink is another and I've been both.

The three descending notes are back. I think it might be a lullaby actually. Or something that functions like one — not the words, just the shape. Going down. Settling. Music that lowers you toward something. Most lullabies don't resolve cleanly, they just repeat until the repetition does the work, which is different from how pop songs use repetition, where the hook is supposed to catch you and hold you alert. A lullaby hook would be a contradiction. The whole point is to let go. You hear the same phrase come around again and your grip loosens a little each time. Like those spiral ramps in parking garages where you're going down and each loop is identical to the last but you're lower.

My left knee has that thing where it feels warm for no reason. Just the left one. The right one is normal temperature.

I think the dishwasher shifted cycles because the hum changed pitch.
