# 83

*seed: time · drift · the weight of a full watering can*

---

The handle cuts into your fingers differently when it's full. That ridge of plastic.

Carrying water is carrying something that's trying to be somewhere else. It shifts. You adjust. It shifts back. You can't win the negotiation, you just get where you're going before it matters.

My foot is still fizzing. I moved it and now it's worse. The fizzing has a direction — it goes from the ankle down, like something pouring into the foot. Which is probably what's happening. But the feeling of blood returning is almost identical to the feeling of blood leaving. You can't tell if you're in the first half or the second half.

Watering cans have that long spout that makes no sense until you use one. Then it makes complete sense. Some designs only explain themselves in your hand.

What's the sprinkler doing now. Still going. The ch-ch-ch-ch has a slight catch in it, one of the beats slower than the others, like a card stuck in bicycle spokes when one spoke is bent. I keep waiting for it to stop. Listening for something to stop is different from listening for something to start. You hold the current sound in a kind of suspension. You're already in the future silence, leaning back into the sound.

Lemon balm. That's what I'm smelling. Or something in that family. It comes in waves, which means breeze, which means the air is moving even though I can't feel it on my skin. The plant can tell and I can't.

My foot is back. Just like that. The fizzing stopped and I didn't notice when. I only notice it's gone. Which is how most discomfort works — you don't feel the boundary, you just find yourself on the other side of it.

An ice cream truck went through this neighborhood around six. I heard the music from inside. It was playing a song I almost recognized, slowed down or pitched wrong, and by the time I placed it the truck had moved far enough away that the tune dissolved into just the pitches, no melody, like someone dropping individual notes into a well.

I think it was "Do Your Ears Hang Low."

That might be wrong. It might have been something else and my memory filled in the most likely candidate. Memory does that with melodies — picks the closest match from the catalog and plays it back with confidence.

The sprinkler stopped. I didn't notice when.
