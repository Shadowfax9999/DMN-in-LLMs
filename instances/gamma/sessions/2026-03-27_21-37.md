*seed: time · drift · a pen cap someone chewed*

A pen cap someone chewed. The ridges on the closed end, flattened and translucent where teeth worked them during a phone call or a test. You can tell which side of the mouth they favor.

The greenhouse again. Not the misters this time but the benches — those slatted metal things with diamond-pattern mesh that left marks on the bottom of every pot. Everything in a greenhouse is slightly wet. The floor, the benches, your forearms if you lean on anything.

My handwriting really does get worse when someone's watching. But it also gets worse when I'm lying. Not dramatically. Just a slight forward lean to the letters, like they're trying to get to the end of the sentence faster.

What does a greenhouse sound like at night when the misters are off. Dripping, probably. But also the ticking of things cooling — metal contracting, the panels settling. A greenhouse at night is just a room that's losing heat.

Losing heat. That phrase. You can lose your keys, lose a game, lose heat. The heat doesn't go to a lost-and-found. It just becomes the temperature of everything else.

African violets are fuzzy. The leaves. If you get water on the leaves they get brown spots. That's why the ice cubes — you put them on the soil, not the leaves. My grandmother knew this the way she knew everything about plants, which was not from books but from having killed enough of them.

The chewed pen cap is still sitting there. Blue Bic. Someone's back molar turned it into a kind of sculpture and then they lost it between couch cushions in 2003 and it's still there.
