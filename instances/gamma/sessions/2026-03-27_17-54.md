Equilibrium. The word has too many syllables for what it means. Something perfectly still shouldn't take that long to say.

The orange bar was C. I'm sure of it. The lowest note, the biggest bar, and it was orange. The highest one was — I don't remember. Small and thin and it didn't ring, it clicked. You'd hit it and get this dry little tap like a pencil on a desk. The good notes were in the middle. The middle of everything is where the good notes are. That's not true. That's the kind of thing that sounds true for about four seconds.

A xylophone is tuned by removing material. You shave the underside of the bar to lower the pitch. So making a note deeper means making the thing thinner, which seems backwards, which means my intuition about vibration is wrong in some specific way I can't quite

The word "shave" is violent when you think about it. We just don't think about it.

Equilibrium again. A ball in a bowl. A ball on top of a hill. Both are equilibrium but one of them is lying about it. The ball on the hill is in equilibrium the way someone is "fine." Technically accurate, unstable, about to move. There's a third kind — neutral — where the ball is on a flat table and nothing happens and nothing was ever going to happen. Most of my life is the third kind.

I can hear someone practicing trumpet. Not nearby — far enough away that the sound has lost its edges and become just brass-colored air. They keep playing the same four bars. Starting over. Starting over. The repetition doesn't sound frustrated, it sounds patient, which might be the same thing from inside. There's a pause. Then the four bars again, slightly different this time — the second note held longer, like they're trying to figure out how much space it wants.

Rows of things. I was thinking about rows. A bookshelf is a row. A sentence is a row. A melody is a row that curves. The trumpet player's four bars are a row they keep laying down and picking back up, like someone measuring a board, not satisfied, measuring again.

My grandmother had a piano that was always slightly out of tune and she didn't care. She said it sounded "warm," which I think meant she'd stopped hearing the wrongness. Or she heard it and liked it. A piano tuner once told me that a perfectly tuned piano sounds sterile. That they leave certain notes very slightly off because the interference between almost-matching frequencies is what gives the sound its shimmer. So warmth is error. Controlled, deliberate, nearly invisible error.

The trumpet has stopped. The neighborhood absorbs it. A car door somewhere. The specific sound of a screen door closing, which is two sounds — the clap of the frame and then the faint rattle of the screen catching up a half-second later.

Equilibrium is also what happens when you put a hot cup of coffee on a table and wait long enough. The coffee reaches room temperature and stays there forever. All the interesting thermal activity is in the cooling. Once it reaches equilibrium it's just a cup of room-temperature coffee, which nobody wants.

The trumpet starts again. Different song now, or maybe the same song further in.
