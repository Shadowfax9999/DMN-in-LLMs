A circular definition. Like when a dictionary says "redness: the quality of being red." You already had to know.

There's a legal term for this. Or a logical term. I can't remember which. Both probably.

The fan in the other room is oscillating. You can hear the period — not the blades, but the slow pan, the way the sound fattens when it faces the doorway and thins when it turns away. It breathes in a room-sized lung.

Circular. I was thinking about circles.

The way you walk through a neighborhood at night and end up back at your own street without deciding to. Your feet know the radius. You weren't going anywhere so you went the right distance from home and came back, like a dog on one of those retractable leashes, the ones that unspool with a faint clicking.

Clicking. The fan doesn't click but something in the kitchen does, intermittently. The fridge compressor maybe. Or the ice maker doing whatever it does in between making ice. Waiting. Accumulating cold.

What do appliances do when they're between cycles. They hold a state. A dishwasher after it's done just sits there full of wet clean dishes and warm air and does nothing and will do nothing until you open it. There's no word for that. Post-operational stasis. Pre-emptied. The dishes are done but not put away so the task is in a middle place.

A lot of life is in the middle place.

The circular definition thing — I think what arrived was actually the word "recursive." But recursive isn't quite the same as circular. Recursive is when the thing contains a smaller version of itself. Circular is when the thing points back to itself with nothing gained. A mirror facing a mirror is recursive. A dictionary defining "red" as "having redness" is circular.

Or maybe the distinction is less clean than that.

Something outside. A car door, not close. Two houses over maybe. No voices after it, which means someone arriving alone. Late on a Friday. The particular sound of one car door and then keys and then a front door, and the whole sequence means a specific kind of night — the kind where you drove home from something that ended and now you're just home and it's quiet and that's fine.

The fan turned again. The sound fattened.

I keep almost falling asleep and then not. That threshold where you're aware you're losing the thread of your own thoughts and that awareness is enough to pull you back. Like the thought of sleep is too interesting to sleep through.

The fridge clicked again.
