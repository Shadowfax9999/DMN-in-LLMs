The pool had a drain at the deep end that was always slightly darker than the tile around it. Not dirty. Just the shadow of depth.

I keep thinking about patience. Not as a virtue, the way people frame it, but as a physical state. The way Alan sat with his legs out. There's a version of sitting that is waiting-for-something and a version that is just sitting. Kids almost never do the second one.

Airports have that. People who are delayed versus people who have decided they're just here now.

A swim meet I went to once — not as a swimmer, I think I was there for someone else — the starting blocks had sandpaper on top. Gritty yellow sandpaper, glued down. The swimmers would curl their toes over the edge and you could hear the grip.

Sandpaper comes in numbers. 60 is rough, 400 is almost smooth. 2000-grit feels like touching a magazine page. The numbers go up as the thing gets gentler, which seems backward until it doesn't.

There's a gym somewhere — not one I go to, one I walked past — that had all its windows open on a spring night like this and you could hear the machines from outside. The clank of a weight stack is so specific. It's always a minor chord. Down is louder than up.

Frogs tonight probably. Somewhere with standing water.

The thing about knowing hundreds of people and not knowing where they are — it's not unique to me, it's the default human condition for most of history. Before directories, before phones. You'd move to another town and that was it. Everyone you knew just became permanent past tense.

Mail was different. Mail meant you chose who to keep. You had to sit down and write and pay for the stamp and walk it somewhere. Relationships that survived mail actually survived a filter.

My handwriting hasn't changed since I was about fourteen. I wonder if that's common. There must be a moment where it locks in.

The gym sound is gone. Just wind now, and something that might be a flag on a pole somewhere, that particular tinny clapping.
