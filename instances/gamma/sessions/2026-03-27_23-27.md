*seed: time · drift · subway door chime*

---

Subway door chime. That two-tone thing. Different in every city. Tokyo's are melodies, little songs per station. DC is just a flat tone, almost angry.

I've never stood on the right platform at the right time to hear someone's phone ring the exact same pitch as the door chime and watch everyone flinch toward the doors.

Flinch. The ch at the end does something. Flinch, clinch, pinch, winch. All of them have a tightening in them.

The word "soft" is soft. The word "hard" isn't particularly hard. The word "long" is short.

What does it feel like to forget a language. Not all at once but the way it actually happens — a word you used to know is just a shape now, the grammar loosens, you start thinking around the gap instead of through it. At some point you're not forgetting anymore, you've forgotten, and you can't tell when the transition happened because you'd need the thing you lost to mark its own departure.

A dog is barking somewhere but it's the bored kind. Evenly spaced. No urgency. Just maintenance.

Aluminum. The way Americans say it versus the way the British say it — that's four syllables versus five and they genuinely sound like different words for different metals.

My left foot is cold and my right foot isn't. Same socks. Same floor.

There's a category of thing where you notice it and immediately know you've been not-noticing it for a long time. The hum of a refrigerator when it stops. A plane you only hear once it's already passing. Your own breathing.

Breathing is the strangest one. Autonomic until you think about it, then manual, then you can't remember how to let it go back.

The barking stopped. I don't know when.
