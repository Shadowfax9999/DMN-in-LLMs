# 61

*seed: time · drift · the feeling of pulling tape off a roll*

---

That sound. The tearing. It has a pitch to it, almost — higher when you pull fast, lower when you pull slow. You can play a tape dispenser like a very bad instrument.

Packing boxes in a kitchen that isn't yours. Helping someone move. The weird intimacy of wrapping their glasses in newspaper. You learn what mugs they kept. There's always one mug that's chipped and ugly and clearly the favorite. You wrap it more carefully than the others without being asked.

The ink on the newspaper comes off on your hands.

Who still gets a newspaper. Someone does. There are still rubber bands on driveways in the morning. I've seen them on driveways where I've never seen a newspaper. Ghost subscriptions. Or the person picks it up early and the rubber band just stays there, rolling into the gutter when it rains.

Gutters full of rubber bands and oak pollen.

The color of oak pollen is aggressive. It's not a polite yellow. It coats cars so thick you can write in it with your finger. People do. WASH ME. Or a heart. Or something worse. The pollen doesn't care. It keeps coming. Two weeks in spring where everything is slightly gold and everyone is sneezing and you can see it move through the air in certain light, like the air itself is full of slow static.

I'm thinking about a swimming pool right now. A public one. The shallow end, late afternoon. The light comes in at an angle and hits the water and the bottom of the pool is covered in moving shapes. Caustics, those bright wobbly lines. They move like something alive but they're just refraction. Kids don't notice them. Adults stare at them sometimes, standing in waist-deep water, not swimming, just watching the light move on the concrete floor.

The water is too warm. Public pools in the evening are always too warm. Body temperature. You get in and it's not a shock, it's just — continuation. Like the water is an extension of your skin. Which is unsettling if you think about it.

The lifeguard is bored. Sitting up on that tall white chair with zinc on their nose, watching nothing happen, watching nothing happen, watching nothing happen. What does a lifeguard think about for six hours. Probably songs stuck in their head. Probably grocery lists. Probably whether that person in lane four is actually struggling or just a bad swimmer.

There's a difference between struggling and bad swimming that must be genuinely hard to read from above.

A whistle on a lanyard. The lifeguard's whistle. It has a small cork ball inside it — a pea whistle. The cork gets wet and the sound changes. Everything about a pool is about water getting into things it shouldn't. Your ears. The whistle. The book you brought. The phone in someone's pocket. The crack in the concrete where a weed is growing.

A weed growing out of pool deck concrete. That weed is having the worst time. Chlorine splash, bare feet stepping on it, full sun all day. And it's still there. It looks terrible. Two leaves, maybe three, pale and flat against the ground. But it found the crack and it took it.

I saw a kid at a pool once do something that stuck with me. She was maybe four, standing at the edge, and instead of jumping in she just leaned forward until gravity took over. No jump. No push. Just a slow commitment to falling. Arms at her sides. Like she was testing whether the world would catch her.

It did. The water did. She came up laughing.

The sound of a pool. It's not one sound. It's a room made of sound — the hollow echo off the tile walls, the slap of water on the gutter edge, voices that bounce so many times they lose their words and become just vowels. A pool is all vowels. No consonants survive that much echo.

My feet are cold on tile. That's not a memory, it's just a sentence. But it felt like one for a second.
