Rumble strip. The sound a car makes when it drifts. Not a warning exactly — more like the road clearing its throat.

I drove home once from somewhere I can't remember, 2 AM, windows down, and hit the rumble strip on purpose just to hear it. Ran along it for maybe three seconds. The whole car became a instrument. Not musical. Percussive. The steering wheel vibrated in a way that went up through my wrists.

There's a particular kind of tired where you're not sleepy but you're empty. You could stay awake for hours but there's nothing in you that wants anything. That was the drive. The highway was straight enough that the headlights looked like they were standing still and the road was moving toward me instead.

An attic I was in once had a window at each end. You could feel the draft moving through it like breath. Slow. The house breathing. Boxes of someone else's Christmas ornaments, each one wrapped in newspaper from 1994. I read the headlines while unwrapping them. Nothing I recognized. Local politics, a search for a missing dog. The ornaments were fine. Glass balls, mostly red. But the newspaper was the interesting part. Someone had chosen those pages. Or not chosen them — just grabbed what was on the table. Either way, those particular days got preserved.

What does a draft sound like. Not the whistle through a gap, that's too obvious. The draft through that attic was below hearing. You knew it was there because a piece of tissue paper on top of one box moved slightly. A flag for wind too quiet to feel on skin but enough to lift one sheet of tissue paper.

The interval between a car passing on a road and the sound arriving — longer than you think, at night, when there's nothing else. You see the headlights sweep across a ceiling. Then a pause. Then the sound finds you.

I keep thinking about that pause. Light is faster, obviously, but at the distances involved it shouldn't matter. The delay must be something else. Maybe the sound has to build — the car has to be close enough for the volume to cross into audible range, and that threshold is farther away for light than for sound. The headlights reach you before the engine does.

Someone left a coffee mug in that attic. Brown ring inside. No handle — broken off, clean break. You could see where the handle had been, two pale ovals on the glaze where it attached.
