*seed: time · drift · a rest that's louder than the notes around it*

The fridge compressor kicks off and the kitchen gets loud. That's backwards but it's true. The silence after a compressor is a shape you didn't know you were inside.

Someone tuning a guitar in another room. Not playing. Tuning. The little adjustments, the string struck and struck again, each time almost the same. You can hear the difference narrow. Two pitches getting closer like lanes merging. And then they lock and the person moves to the next string and you realize you'd been holding something in your jaw.

A rest that's louder than the notes around it. In an orchestra that's a real thing — the whole room lifts when sixty people stop at once. The air doesn't go quiet, it goes taut. Like a table cleared in one sweep.

What does a greenhouse smell like at night. During the day it's obvious — soil and chlorophyll and that plastic-baked warmth. But at night when the temperature drops and the condensation forms on the inside of the glass. Cold wet glass and warm wet dirt. The plants are still respiring. Exhaling. The oxygen ratio in a sealed greenhouse at 3 AM must be noticeable. Whether you'd feel it or just feel that something was different.

I keep thinking about the elevator woman. Not her specifically but the category of person who notices a thing nobody else registers and then just lives with that knowledge. Carries it around like a coin in a pocket. Never spends it unless someone happens to ask. How many of those private expertise go completely unwitnessed. Someone who can tell you which burner on the stove heats fastest. Someone who knows the exact minute the streetlight outside their window turns on and has watched it drift later by thirty seconds a week since March.

A friend had a dog that could hear the difference between his car and every other car on the street. Not the garage door, not the turn signal. The engine, from three blocks away. The dog would be asleep and then suddenly not.

Attics in spring. The smell of insulation warming up for the first time in months. Fiberglass and old cardboard and whatever got stored up there and forgotten. A box of someone's college textbooks. A lamp that doesn't work. Things migrate upward in a house — you carry them up one flight at a time over years and they never come back down.

The guitar in the other room has stopped tuning and started playing something. Just chords. No melody. Slow, like the person is remembering where their fingers go.
