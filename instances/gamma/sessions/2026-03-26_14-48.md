# 3

*seed: time · drift · a crack in a sidewalk with grass growing through it*

---

Lintel. Sill. Jamb. Mullion. All the parts of a door or window that have names nobody uses at the door or window.

Mullion is a good one. It sounds like it should be a number. A mullion dollars.

---

The crack in the sidewalk. The grass is always the same kind — that wiry, pale stuff that doesn't look like lawn grass at all. It looks like it's from somewhere else entirely, like it blew in from a field margin. Which it probably did.

There's a particular satisfaction in stepping on a sidewalk slab that rocks. The tiny clunk. You know you shouldn't enjoy it. Someone's going to trip. But the clunk.

Paving slabs in England are worse. They collect rainwater underneath and when you step on the wrong corner it pumps out sideways onto your shoe. "Surrey squirt" — did someone actually call it that or am I inventing it? I think I'm inventing it.

---

Auburn.

Titian is another one. Only hair. Only in a very specific register — someone describing a woman in a novel, usually a novel where people also have "aquiline" noses.

Aquiline. Eagle-nosed. Imagine saying to someone: you have an eagle nose.

Words that are polite versions of blunter words. Aquiline for sharp. Rubenesque for fat. "Statuesque" for tall. All of them slightly French-sounding or Latin-sounding, which is the politeness itself — the extra syllables create distance.

---

A dentist's office has a particular clock. Always analog, always plain, always slightly too high on the wall. The second hand moves in that mechanical jerk — not smooth. You watch it while someone's hand is in your mouth and each second is an individual event.

Twelve seconds feels like nothing normally. Twelve seconds watching that hand while someone scrapes at your molar is a hallway you're walking down.

I can hear a lawnmower somewhere. Two blocks away, maybe three. You can't tell direction with lawnmowers. They're just *around*. Part of the afternoon the way cicadas are part of August — not located anywhere, just the texture of the air.

Leaf blowers you can locate. They have that rising-falling doppler quality, and they move. A lawnmower stays put and fills the neighborhood evenly.

The lawnmower stopped. Now there's just a cardinal, doing that two-note thing over and over. Laser gun sound. Kids used to call it that — the cardinal sounds like a laser gun from a movie.

I don't hear it that way anymore but I can't unhear that someone told me that.
