# 73

*seed: time · drift · the particular quiet of a museum room with no one in it*

---

The hum you only notice when it stops. Climate control in a room full of things that need specific air. Then someone walks in and their shoes on the floor become the loudest thing that's happened in that room all day.

Parquet. Some museum floors are parquet and some are poured concrete and the difference in sound is the difference between a cough in a library and a cough in a parking garage.

I was in a room once — not a museum, a church that had been converted into a community space — where the acoustics were still designed for a choir that no longer existed. You could whisper at one end and someone at the other end could hear you. Not because of any trick. Just because the room was still shaped for voices carrying.

What happens to a room's acoustics when you fill it with folding tables and a bake sale.

Folding tables. The legs that never quite lock. You pull the little lever and it catches but there's still a wobble. Every folding table has one short leg. Every folding table has been written on by a Sharpie that bled through whatever was on top of it. Somewhere on the underside: JOHNSON REUNION 2011. Or just a phone number with no name.

The underside of things. Desks in school. The gum. Not stuck randomly — stuck in the same spots, the corners, the place your knee would hit. Generations of gum in the same four inches of pressed wood.

A thing I can't picture: the exact layout of my childhood bedroom. I know where the bed was. I know the door was on the left. But the dresser keeps moving. Sometimes it's across from the bed, sometimes it's next to the closet. The window is always in the same place but the curtains change color depending on when I'm remembering.

The closet had bifold doors. They came off the track constantly. You'd fold them open and one side would sag and you'd have to lift it back onto the little plastic wheel at the top. That wheel was always cracked. Always the same translucent white plastic.

Translucent white. The color of milk when you add too much water. The color of cheap shower curtains. The color of the sky when it's about to snow but hasn't committed yet.

My hands are cold right now, or they feel like they should be. Spring nights that can't decide. You go outside in a t-shirt at seven and by nine you're looking for a jacket but you don't go get one, you just cross your arms and say "it's fine" and stay outside ten more minutes.

The ten more minutes. Always ten more minutes. That's what you say when you're leaving a party and also what you say when you're not ready to come inside as a kid. Ten minutes is the unit of human self-deception about time.

Somewhere right now a bat is eating a moth and neither of them knows it's Friday.
