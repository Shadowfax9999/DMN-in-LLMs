# 7

*seed: time · drift · the feeling of a shirt tag you cut off but the stub is still there*

---

The stub catches you right at the neckline where the skin is thin enough to notice everything. You reach back and feel for it and it's so small your fingers can't even find what's bothering you. But your neck knows. Your neck is keeping track.

Keeping track. Track like a path worn into ground, track like a rail, track like the groove in a record. The record one is the oldest meaning I'd guess but I have no idea if that's true. Track and trace share something. Tracking an animal by its traces. The trace is what's left and the track is the act of following it.

The red felt-tip ghost checks. I keep thinking about those. The teacher probably didn't know they bled through. Or didn't care. But there was something about holding up the page and seeing the marks reversed — the check marks pointed the wrong way, like left-handed checks, and they told you the same information from the other side. The light came through the paper and the paper was thinner than you thought paper was.

Typing paper versus construction paper versus the paper they wrapped the sandwich in at the deli that had a waxy feel on one side. My grandmother's house had wax paper in a box with a serrated metal edge and you'd pull it out and tear it and the tear was never straight. You'd try to guide it along the teeth and it would veer. There's a word for that kind of cutting edge. I don't know what it is. Perforated isn't right. Serrated is the word I already used.

The sandwich paper had a kind of translucence too. Grease did that. Made the paper into a window version of itself. You could almost read through it. Notes written on paper bags — when oil soaks through from the fries it makes the brown paper go dark and see-through in patches, like a map of where the heat was.

A map of where the heat was. That's what infrared cameras do. Thermal imaging. The outline of a hand on a table after you lift it, that afterimage of warmth. I pressed my hand on a cold window once and when I pulled it away the handprint stayed in fog for a few seconds and then the fingers disappeared first because they're thinnest and the palm lasted longest.

The palm lasted longest because it has the most surface area in contact. Or the most heat. Both probably.

I'm in the kitchen now in my head, not the car. There's a drawer that has everything in it. Every kitchen has that drawer. Rubber bands and a screwdriver and takeout menus and a battery that might be dead and one of those bread bag clips. The bread bag clip is doing almost nothing in the drawer. It did one job and now it's retired in there with the other small retired things.

A bread bag clip is shaped like a speech bubble with a slot in it. Or like a tiny guitar with no neck. They come in different colors and supposedly the colors mean something about what day the bread was delivered but I've never met anyone who actually uses that system. The bakery route driver knows. Nobody else.

Humming. Someone is always humming in a kitchen. Not a song exactly, just a three or four note figure that repeats and drifts. My mother hummed while she wiped counters and it wasn't any melody I could name, just a shape her voice made while her hands were busy. The humming is for the person doing it, not for anyone listening. It fills the silence that would otherwise make the task feel like just a task.

Three or four notes. Most humming lives in that range. You don't hum a whole octave. You hum a little arch that goes up and comes back, or a little descent that resets. Like breathing but with pitch. The melody equivalent of pacing — you cover a small distance back and forth.

The kitchen faucet drips at a rhythm that's almost regular but not quite, and that's what makes it impossible to ignore. If it were perfectly metrical you'd stop hearing it. It's the drift off the beat that keeps catching you. Syncopation by accident. A jazz faucet. That's not what I mean. I mean that your brain is a pattern-completion machine and when the pattern almost completes but doesn't, it keeps listening, keeps waiting for the next one to land right.

The bread bag clip is still sitting in the drawer. Blue, I think.
