# 41

*seed: time · drift*

---

Four notes up, two notes down. Then again. Then a stumble, and again from the top.

The light changes. The car ahead of me doesn't move for a full second and I have that little flare of — not anger, just alertness. Then they go.

There's a smell in the car I can't place. Not bad. Sort of warm and papery. Maybe the glovebox. I haven't opened the glovebox in months. There's an insurance card in there from two policies ago.

My hands are at ten and two which nobody actually recommends anymore. Nine and three. Or one hand at twelve if you're the kind of person who drives like that. I'm not. I tried once on a long highway stretch and it felt performative, like I was in a movie about driving.

The piano stops.

Now it's just the road noise and the slight rattle from somewhere in the passenger door that I've never tracked down. It only happens above 35. Below that, nothing. Right at 35, there it is. Like the car has a threshold.

I'm passing the dry cleaner that always has the same two dresses in the window. Blue one and a dark red one. I don't think they're for sale. I think they're just there to prove the concept.

A man is walking a very small dog on a very long leash. The dog is fifteen feet ahead of him, fully committed to a direction the man doesn't seem to be going.

The neck thing again. I roll my head to the right at the next light and hold it there. It's the kind of stretch that feels productive but probably isn't doing anything. The muscle just tightens back up five minutes later.

I had a teacher who drove with both feet — left foot on the brake, right foot on the gas. She said it was faster reaction time. My friend's dad did the same thing but I think for different reasons. Some people just learn it that way and never unlearn it. The brake lights on those cars flicker constantly. You can't tell when they're actually slowing down.

There's a song I've had in my head for three days that I can only remember the chorus of, and I don't know the name, and I can't hum it well enough for anyone to identify it. It's in a minor key. There's a piano in it, actually. Maybe that's why the practicing caught my ear. The melody drops at the end of each phrase like it's going to resolve and then it just doesn't. It hangs there. I keep reaching for the next note in my head and it's not there.

I need to turn left in about half a mile. I'm already in the right lane. This is something I do — I stay in whatever lane I'm in until the last responsible moment, then signal and move over. Not because I'm strategic about it. I just forget to think ahead.

The trees along this stretch are all the same age, planted at the same time when the road was widened. You can tell because they're all exactly the same height, same trunk diameter, same distance from the curb. In ten years they'll look different from each other. Right now they're still identical.

I signal. Move left. The turn is at the light by the gas station that used to be a different gas station.
