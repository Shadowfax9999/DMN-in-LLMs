# 2

*seed: time · drift*

---

Marjoram is the quieter one. Oregano announces itself.

The difference between "similar" and "the same" is mostly whether you're paying attention. Like cousins at a family reunion — obvious once you see them standing next to each other, but from across the room, from a distance of years.

A spice rack arranged alphabetically versus arranged by frequency of use. Two completely different kitchens.

---

The number 7.

Not the meaning of it. Just the shape. That serif arm reaching out, the diagonal slash down. In some countries people put a crossbar through it, like a little belt. I write it both ways depending on — I don't know what. How fast I'm going. Whether there are ones nearby that might get confused.

7 looks like it's walking somewhere.
4 looks like it sat down.
8 never stops.

---

Alan Shepard hitting a golf ball on the moon. The six-iron head duct-taped to a sample-collection tool. He shanked the first one. The second one he claimed went "miles and miles and miles." Probably two hundred yards.

But what a two hundred yards.

---

The feel of a library book versus a book you own. The library book has a looseness to it, pages that have been turned by different pressures, different speeds. The spine's been broken in places you wouldn't have broken it. You're reading through the residue of other readings.

A borrowed book is different from a library book. A borrowed book has one other person in it. A library book has dozens. You can feel it in the way the pages fall open — they have preferences now, places they've been held open longest, paragraphs where multiple people paused.

Cookbooks from the library always fall open to the same three recipes.

---

The word "auburn." It only ever describes hair. You never see an auburn car, an auburn sweater. Maybe a leaf, in a poem. But really it belongs to hair and that's it. A word with one job.

"Cerulean" mostly just describes sky.

"Fetid" — I don't think I've ever said it out loud.

There are words that live entirely on the page. You read them, you understand them, you've never once moved your mouth around them in conversation. "Erstwhile." "Penumbra." "Albeit" — actually, people do say albeit. But it always sounds slightly performed, like they're aware they're using it.

The gap between reading vocabulary and speaking vocabulary. Thousands of words wide.

I know the word "lintel" but if I were standing in front of one, pointing at it, I'd probably say "that beam thing above the door."
