*seed: time · drift*

The bread bag clip. Those come in different colors and I've never understood the system. There must be one — bakeries use them to indicate what day the bread was baked. But from the consumer side it's just a blue clip or a yellow clip and you never learn the code so the information passes through you like a radio station in a language you don't speak.

A radio station in a language you don't speak is actually pleasant to fall asleep to. The rise and fall of someone talking, the cadence of sentences you can't parse, so your brain gives up trying to extract meaning and just rides the sound. That's close to what a lullaby does. The words in a lullaby don't matter, or they matter the way wallpaper matters — they give the surface a texture but you're not reading the pattern, you're just feeling that the pattern is there. Hush little baby, don't say a word. The instruction is literally to stop using language. The song uses words to tell you to stop listening to words.

My left knee does a thing when I stand up from sitting on the floor. Not pain, just a click that happens at a specific angle, maybe thirty degrees into the extension. It's been doing it for years. I don't think about it except when someone else is in the room and then I wonder if they heard it.

That's a whole category of body awareness — things you only notice about yourself when someone else might be noticing them. The way you chew. Whether your breathing is audible. That thing your ankle does when you walk downhill. Alone, these are nothing. With a witness they become almost unbearable.

The faucet. I was thinking about the faucet. The almost-regular drip. There's a name for the kind of rhythm that's close to regular but keeps slipping — in music it might be rubato, but rubato is intentional, it's the performer choosing to stretch time. The faucet isn't choosing anything. It's just a washer that's degraded unevenly so the water builds up and releases at slightly different rates depending on which part of the deformed rubber is bearing the pressure at any given moment. I said I wasn't going to do that.

The ceiling fan has a pull chain and the pull chain has a little acorn on the end. Brass, or brass-colored. It swings when the fan is on and traces a circle that's not a circle — it's a kind of wobbling ellipse because the fan isn't perfectly balanced. You can watch it for a long time. You can watch it the way you watch a campfire, not tracking anything specific, just letting your eyes go soft and following the movement. The acorn goes around. The shadow of the chain on the ceiling goes around too, faster or slower depending on where the light is coming from, and sometimes the shadow and the chain seem to be doing completely different things even though one is caused by the other.

I wonder what pitch the fan hums at. It probably depends on the speed setting. Low is lower. But I don't think it's a clean pitch — it's more of a broadband whir with maybe a slight tonal center, like the fan is almost singing a note but not committing. A hum that doesn't resolve into a pitch. The way a refrigerator hum lives right at the edge of being a note. You could probably match it with your voice if you tried. Just sit there and try to sing the same note as the refrigerator. You'd have to be alone to do that.

Most of the things I actually want to do require being alone. Not big things. Weird small things. Singing along with the refrigerator. Smelling a book. Standing in the middle of a room and turning slowly to look at everything in it. Walking through the house with your eyes closed to see how far you get. These are things that feel normal from the inside and would look strange from the outside, so you just don't do them when anyone else is around, and then eventually you forget you wanted to do them at all.

The pull chain is still going around.
