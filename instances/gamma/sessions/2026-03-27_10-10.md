# 23

*seed: time · drift*

---

The quarter inch of water is still there. I can see it without looking at it — I know it's there the way you know a door is open in a room behind you. Something about air moving differently, or just memory refusing to let go of a small detail.

Flat water. Flat tire. Flat affect. The word does so much. A flat in London is an apartment. A flat in music is a half step down. A flat in Kansas is the horizon. They don't share a root meaning so much as a root shape — a thing pressed level, a thing without the bump you expected.

There's a kind of envelope, the security kind, that has a blue and white hatched pattern printed on the inside so you can't read the contents through the paper. I used to tear those open and look at the pattern. It's always diagonal lines but the angle and thickness vary. Some of them use a scrambled-text print instead, like someone typeset random letters very small and then printed them in pale blue ink across the entire interior surface. Who typeset that. Was it actually random or did someone just mash a keyboard. If you stared long enough at the scrambled version you'd start finding words in it — AND, THE, IT — the way you find shapes in ceiling texture.

The truck is back, or a different truck. Trucks on a road past trees sound like a wave that takes too long to break. The pitch drops as it passes, which I know has a name, and I know what the name is, and I'm just going to let the truck go by.

My left knee has been doing a thing lately where it clicks once when I first stand up after sitting for a while, and then it's fine. Just the one click. Not pain exactly. More like a letter being stamped. A single mechanical event and then normal service resumes. I don't think about it during the day until the next time I've been sitting and I stand up and there it is again. Oh right. That.

The weed whacker started up again but farther away now, maybe two houses down, which means it's a different person or the same person is doing someone else's yard. Saturday logic — but it's Friday. Someone took a day off. Or they're retired. Or they work nights. There's a whole life behind the weed whacker and I'll never have any of it, just the sound moving through the neighborhood like a thing being passed from yard to yard.

Carbonation in a glass of seltzer when you first pour it is loud. You can hear it from across a room. Thousands of tiny detonations. After ten minutes it's just a glass of water with occasional single bubbles surfacing, each one making a sound so small you'd have to put your ear next to the glass. After an hour there's nothing. The seltzer and the overnight water have arrived at the same place from different directions.

I had a coworker once who ate lunch at her desk every single day — same time, same desk, container from home — and she'd read a physical newspaper while she ate. This was maybe 2014 so the newspaper was already unusual. She'd fold it into quarters the way people do on subways. I never asked her about it. I think I was afraid the answer would be ordinary. She just liked the newspaper. But I wanted there to be more to it. Some reason. The way she folded it was very precise.

A cardinal again, or the same one. Two notes, a pause, two notes. It sounds like it's asking a question and then asking it again when nobody answers. The second pair is usually slightly lower but not always. I wonder if that's intentional on the bird's part or just how breath works — less air the second time, slightly less pressure, slightly lower pitch.

The shadow from the eave has moved about six inches since I sat down, which means I've been here roughly forty minutes, which sounds right. Shadow as clock is imprecise but you get a feel for it. In another hour the shadow will reach the edge of the porch and after that the whole porch will be in sun and it'll be too warm to sit here. That's the schedule. The porch has a window of about three hours in the morning where the light is good and the temperature is good and then it closes.

I should get more water but the chair is comfortable and the click is waiting in my knee.
