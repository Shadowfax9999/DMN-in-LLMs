The lean. That's the word that stayed.

Like when you're standing on a train platform and the train hasn't come yet but your body is already angled toward where it will be. You're not moving. But you're not still either.

A greenhouse would smell like this. Like warm dirt and the outside of a tomato plant — that green, astringent smell that sticks to your hands. I've never liked tomatoes but I've always liked that smell. The plant and the fruit have almost nothing to do with each other, scent-wise.

My left foot is still cold. I put it on top of the right one. Now neither is comfortable but both are warm.

There's a sound outside. Not the compressor. Something with wings, maybe, or a branch finding its range in wind I can't hear from inside. The greenhouse I'm thinking of isn't real. It's a composite. Part of it is a garden center I walked through once where the misters were on and everything was too loud and humid. Part of it is a photograph. Part of it is just the word greenhouse, which sounds like it should be warm and isn't always.

F to E. That's a lean. One half-step and you're home but you sit on the F and the F doesn't know where it is until you let it fall. A choir holding an F over a C-major chord. That particular ache in the upper voice.

I keep thinking about hands. Specifically hands that are holding something too tightly. A pen. A steering wheel at ten and two when you're nervous. The way your handwriting changes when someone's watching you write. It gets worse. Not because you can't write but because you're suddenly writing *and* watching yourself write, and the watching takes up the hand.

The greenhouse misters. The sound they made wasn't a hiss, it was more like — like tearing a very thin piece of paper continuously. A whisper with no consonants.

My grandmother had African violets on a windowsill and she watered them with ice cubes. She said it was so they wouldn't get too much at once. The ice would sit on the soil and diminish over an afternoon. I thought about those ice cubes a lot as a kid. The slowness of it. Giving a plant something it needs in a form it can't use yet.
