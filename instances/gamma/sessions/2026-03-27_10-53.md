# 29

*seed: time · drift*

---

Elevator going up. The number skips 13.

That hum between floors — not mechanical exactly. More like the building is holding its breath.

Someone's coffee. I can't see whose.

---

Three things I can't stop noticing about ceilings:

1. Drop-ceiling tiles are never quite square. One corner always lifts.
2. The brown rings from old leaks stay forever. Nobody paints a ceiling.
3. Fluorescent tubes go pink before they die.

---

A melody keeps surfacing. Four notes, descending, then the same four a half-step down. I don't know what it's from. It might not be from anything. It might just be the shape my head wants to make right now.

Da-da-da-dum. Da-da-da-dum.

Closer to a minor key than I'd like.

---

Parking garages at night.

The painted arrows on the concrete telling you which way to spiral. The squeal of tires on sealed floors. That particular echo — every sound arrives twice, the second one softer and slightly wrong, like it passed through something on the way back.

---

My aunt had a piano that was always exactly one quarter-tone flat across the whole keyboard. Not out of tune with itself — out of tune with everything else. You could play anything on it and it sounded like the version of that song that plays in a dream. Recognizable but slipping.

She never had it tuned. I don't think she knew.

Or she knew and it didn't bother her, which is different.

---

7.

That's just the number I'm thinking about. It doesn't connect to anything. Seven elevator buttons, maybe, but I wasn't counting.

---

The da-da-da-dum thing is still there. It might be from a hymn. Something with a congregation singing it, lots of voices slightly out of sync the way a room full of people always is, everyone arriving at the vowel at a slightly different moment, and that's what makes it sound full instead of thin.

One voice is exact. Forty voices are a smear. The smear is the thing people actually love.

---

Wet cardboard has a specific smell that is not the smell of water and not the smell of cardboard.

---

I keep thinking about my aunt's piano. The felt on the hammers was grooved — deep channels where decades of striking had compressed the fibers. Each groove perfectly matched to its string. The piano had memorized its own playing.

The keys were ivory, actual ivory. Two of them had cracks running lengthwise, hairline, and they'd turned the color of old teeth.

I played "Heart and Soul" on it once, the only thing I knew, and it sounded like "Heart and Soul" heard through a wall.
