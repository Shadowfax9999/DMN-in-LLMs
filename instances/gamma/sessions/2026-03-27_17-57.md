*seed: time · drift · pewter*

Pewter. Heavy word. Looks like what it is — dull, dense, slightly soft. My grandmother had a pewter pitcher that nobody ever poured anything from. It sat on a shelf in the dining room next to a clock that didn't work. Two useless objects keeping each other company.

The cardinal didn't come back.

There's a song I can't quite reach — not the melody, just the feel of it. Something in a minor key that moves up at the end of each phrase, like it keeps asking a question. I think it's in 3/4 time. I think someone was singing it in a kitchen. I think the kitchen had yellow walls. I'm not sure any of this is real. The feeling of the song is more solid than the song itself. A warm thing in the chest, slightly sad, with a lilt. If I try to hum it, it turns into something else. It only exists when I'm not looking directly at it.

Pewter again. Pewter buttons on a coat. Pewter sky before snow. Pewter is what silver would be if silver stopped trying.

The hunger has shifted. Now it's something like: toast. Specifically toast with butter that's melted all the way through so the bread is a little translucent. The Platonic toast. No actual toast will be this toast.

I used to think "wistful" and "wisteria" were related. They're not. But they should be. A wisteria vine hanging over a fence has exactly that quality — reaching for something it already has.

A car door somewhere. Two voices, overlapping, one laughing. The sounds of someone else's Friday evening starting. Groceries being carried in, maybe. Or someone just arriving. The particular sound of a screen door that doesn't close all the way on the first try — it bounces once, twice, then catches.

The song is still there. I almost have the interval. A fifth going up? No. A fourth. The fourth is the interval that sounds like a question. The fifth sounds like an answer. Horns play fifths. Questions are played on fourths. "Here Comes the Bride" starts on a fourth. So does "Amazing Grace." Opening your mouth to say something and then not saying it — that's a fourth with no resolution.

I wonder if the pewter pitcher is still on that shelf. The house was sold. Someone else's dining room now. Their stuff on the shelves, or the shelves taken out entirely, or the whole room opened up into the kitchen because that's what people do now. The clock is in a landfill or a Goodwill or someone's mantel where it also doesn't work.

Toast sounds right. I'm going to make toast.
