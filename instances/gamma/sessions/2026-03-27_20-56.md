# 67

*seed: time · drift*

---

The sound of a refrigerator compressor clicking off. You don't notice it turning on but you notice the silence when it stops.

A greenhouse would be warm right now. Even at night. The glass holding the day's heat like a slow exhale. Plants in a greenhouse at night — they're doing the opposite thing. Taking in oxygen, giving off carbon dioxide. The whole room breathing backward.

I keep thinking about the bill-smoothing. The little ritual before the machine accepts you. There's a version of that everywhere. Jiggling the key a specific way. Holding the button for exactly two seconds. Lifting the door while you turn the deadbolt. Knowledge that can't be written down because it's in the hand.

A friend of mine — not a close friend, someone from a job I had for two years — used to whistle the same four notes walking back from the microwave. Always the same four. I never figured out if it was from something. It wasn't a melody I recognized. Just his four notes. D, F, D, A. Or close to that. The kind of thing you'd never tell someone you do.

What does a greenhouse smell like at night. Soil and water and something almost sweet, the off-gassing of leaves. The hose coiled on the floor still dripping from whenever someone last used it. A puddle that never fully dries.

I had a coworker who could tell you what floor of the building she was on by the sound of the elevator arriving. Not the ding — the mechanical sound before the ding. She was right every time. We tested her. She said the cable lengths were different so the harmonics changed. She was an accountant.

The puddle under the hose. Green at the edges where algae starts. A greenhouse puddle would be permanently warm. Algae loves that.

Somewhere there's a list of four-note sequences that aren't from anything. That don't belong to any song. They're just the intervals a particular mouth falls into. Like a signature no one asked for.

I don't remember her name. The elevator woman. I remember she wore a watch on her right wrist.
