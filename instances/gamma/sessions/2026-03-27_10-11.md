Orphan. A single line stranded at the top of a page, separated from its paragraph. Or is that a widow. I always get them mixed up. One is at the top and one is at the bottom and there's a mnemonic for it that I can't remember. Widows at the top because they have a future, orphans at the bottom because — no, that doesn't track either. Typesetters cared about this. Someone looking at a galley proof would circle a lonely line and write "widow" in red pencil and that meant break the page differently, add a word, remove a word, whatever it takes to pull that line back to its family.

The word "galley" doing double work there. A ship and a tray of type. Both long and narrow.

I'm thinking about a room I've never been in but can picture clearly: a print shop, late afternoon, the compositors have gone home, and all the type is locked up in its forms, mirror-image words pressed together in iron frames. Everything backward. You'd have to learn to read in reverse. After enough years you probably stopped noticing.

Forty-seven. That's a prime number and I'm not sure why it surfaced. Sometimes a number just sits in the front of your mind like a rock in a shoe. Forty-seven has a reputation, actually — people claim it shows up more than it should. There was a professor at Pomona College who made a whole thing of it. His students started finding it everywhere, which is of course what happens when you start looking for any number.

The confirmation bias isn't the interesting part. The interesting part is that forty-seven *feels* prime. It looks angular. Thirteen feels prime too. Fifty-one doesn't, even though — wait, fifty-one isn't prime. Three times seventeen. But it *looks* like it could be. It has that loner quality. Numbers have faces and I don't know where that comes from.

A truck just shifted gears somewhere a few blocks over. That rising whine and then the pause and then the deeper note. Trucks going uphill shift more often. You can hear the grade of a road you can't see.

I used to count the segments in the railing of a balcony at my grandmother's house. Fourteen. I'd count them every time even though I knew. The counting wasn't informational, it was rhythmic. Touch each one. Fourteen. Like a rosary but with no prayer attached, just the ticking off. Kids do that with everything — cracks in sidewalks, stairs, ceiling tiles. Then at some point you stop and the world becomes uncounted.

There's a fly on the window screen. It walks a few steps, stops, walks a few steps. It looks like it's reading.

I wonder what the screen looks like from the fly's side. A grid of tiny squares, each one a window to a darker interior. Or maybe the fly doesn't parse it as a grid at all. Maybe it's just: solid here, open here, solid here, open here.

The word "parse." From the Latin *pars*, a part of speech. To parse a sentence is to break it into its parts. Grammarians in the 1700s made children stand up and parse sentences aloud — "The" is the definite article, "cat" is a common noun, "sat" is a verb in the past tense. The whole sentence dismantled into labeled boxes. Somewhere along the way "parse" escaped grammar and now people parse situations, parse feelings, parse looks that someone gave them across a table.

A look across a table. That's a thing you can spend a week parsing and still not know. Was it annoyance or amusement. Was it directed at you or at something behind you. The face is a terrible communication device for anything precise. Good for the broad categories — fear, joy, disgust — but the subtleties are mostly projection. You read what you're primed to read.

The fly is still on the screen. It hasn't moved in a while. Maybe it's resting. Maybe it's stuck. I can't tell from here and I'm not getting up to check.
