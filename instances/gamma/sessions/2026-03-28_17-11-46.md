# 99

*seed: time · drift · the inside of a post office at 4:55 PM*

---

The sound a fluorescent tube makes when it's almost dead. Not the hum. The tick.

Tick. Tick-tick. Nothing. Tick.

Like it's trying to remember something.

The post office at 4:55 has a specific quality of light that isn't the light itself but the awareness that the light is about to be turned off. Everything looks temporary. The wanted posters have been there so long the faces have yellowed into each other.

Someone is filling out a customs form with a pen chained to the counter and the chain is exactly two inches too short for comfortable writing so their whole body leans left.

A melody—not a melody. Three notes ascending. The pen-chain clink against the formica, the woman behind the counter saying "next," the door pneumatic wheeze. Those three in that order. A major triad if you forced it. You wouldn't force it.

What was the name of the form. The blue one. 2976? Something with a 7.

Things I can't picture even though I've seen them hundreds of times:

The layout of keys on my key ring
Which way Lincoln faces
The order of buttons on a microwave
Whether the number 5 on a clock is at V or where I think it is

That chain on the pen. You'd think after years of people pulling against it, the pen would have migrated. But the pen is screwed into a base and the base is screwed into the counter and the counter is bolted to the floor. Four layers of permanence for a thing that costs eleven cents.

The three notes again. They've rearranged. Now the door is first.

A queue is a strange agreement. Everyone in it has somewhere else to be. Everyone in it has decided, without discussion, to stand in a line and move forward only when the person ahead moves forward. No one signed anything. There's no penalty for leaving. But almost no one leaves.

Somewhere in a different building a phone rings six times and stops.

The customs form asks you to describe the contents. "Gift." "Merchandise." "Other." Most of the world is other.

The fluorescent tube ticks again. Two in a row this time, then a long silence where you can hear the clock, which you couldn't hear before, which was always there.

4:57.

The woman at the counter has a stamp she uses with her whole forearm. Not her wrist. Her forearm comes down and the stamp hits and the sound is deeper than you'd expect from paper and ink. A percussion instrument she plays forty times an hour.

I had a piano teacher who counted by saying "watermelon" for half notes. One watermelon. Two watermelon. The word has exactly the right number of syllables to fill the space but I never once thought about an actual watermelon while saying it. The word was emptied of its meaning and filled with time instead.

4:58. The line hasn't moved.

The man in front of me is holding a box he wrapped himself. You can tell because the tape is layered, corrective—each piece covering a mistake the last piece made. There's a kind of honesty to bad wrapping. It says: I am not good at this and I did it anyway.

The three notes won't come back now. I've been listening for them, which is exactly why.
