# 34

*seed: time · drift*

Dogleg. It's a golf thing but also a hallway thing. The hallway in that apartment on Elm had a dogleg and you could stand at the bend and not be visible from either end. That felt like a secret even though it was just bad architecture. Or maybe good architecture. A place in a hallway where you're nowhere.

The water is still there. I'm looking at it. The glass has that faint ring where the water level was higher yesterday. A tidemark. Tidemarks in bathtubs, in coffee cups, in the rings inside tree stumps — no, those are something else. The glass ring is calcium maybe. Or just where the surface tension held the water against the glass and left behind whatever was dissolved in it. I'm not going to drink it. I'm also not going to get up and pour new water. This is a specific kind of inertia that only happens in bed on a morning when you don't have to be anywhere.

Friday. It does feel yellow. Saturday feels green, which makes no sense. Wednesday is red. These assignments feel permanent and non-negotiable even though I know they're arbitrary. Like the arrangement of furniture in a room you've lived in for years — technically you could move the dresser to the other wall but it would feel *wrong* in a way that has nothing to do with function.

The fitted sheet corner popped off again. You can see the elastic. The elastic in fitted sheets degrades before anything else does. The fabric is fine, the seams are fine, but the elastic gives up and then the whole sheet becomes a suggestion rather than an instruction. You're sleeping on a sheet that's trying to leave.

A car door outside. Not close — maybe the next street over. You can tell the distance of a car door by how much of the low end you lose. Close car doors have a thunk. Far ones have a tick. Someone's going somewhere at 11:22 on a Friday morning which means either they don't work a regular schedule or they're late or they're running an errand. Or they just got home. I'm assigning narratives to a sound that lasted less than a second.

The ceiling has a texture I've never been able to name. It's not popcorn exactly. It's more like someone pressed a sponge into wet plaster over and over. Each press slightly different. If you stare at ceiling texture long enough it starts to look like terrain, like you're flying over a white desert with tiny mesas. I've spent a lot of hours looking at this ceiling. Sick days, can't-sleep nights, Sunday mornings. The ceiling knows more about my supine face than any person does.

There's a bird doing a two-note call. High low. High low. Pause. High low. It's been going for a while actually, I just started noticing it. Which means it started before I started noticing it, which means there was a version of this morning where that sound was in the room and I wasn't hearing it. The sound was hitting my eardrums and everything. Just not registering.

The cream-versus-white thing is still bothering me. I think the sheet is the whiter one and the mattress pad is cream. But if I took the sheet to a paint store and held it up against actual white swatches it would probably look cream too. White is relative. Every white is cream next to a whiter white.

My left foot is outside the blanket. The air on it feels about 68 degrees. The rest of me is probably 85 under the covers. There's a 17-degree border running across my ankle. I'm a weather system. The foot is the cold front.

Dogleg. Still there. Sometimes a word just sits in your head like a marble in a bowl, rolling around, not going anywhere, not leaving. Dogleg dogleg dogleg. The more I think it the less it sounds like a word. It sounds like two separate words now — dog, leg — and I'm picturing a dog's leg, which is not what a dogleg is at all. The back leg of a dog bends the wrong way, or what looks like the wrong way. The joint that looks like a backwards knee is actually the ankle.

The bird stopped. I noticed it stopping, which means I was listening to it without knowing I was listening to it. Background attention is strange. It only announces itself when the thing it was tracking disappears.

I should get up. I'm not going to get up yet. There's a specific pleasure in knowing you should get up and not doing it. It's not laziness exactly. It's more like you're borrowing time from the version of you that's already up and making coffee. That version can wait.

The glass of water is catching a stripe of light from the window blinds. There's a bright line on the wall behind it, refracted through the water, slightly blue at one edge. It won't be there in twenty minutes. The sun moves fast enough that you can't see it move but you can see that it moved.

My phone is face down on the nightstand. I put it face down on purpose last night and I can't remember why. Probably so the screen wouldn't light up. It's still face down. It looks like a small black tile. A domino with no dots.

The fitted sheet corner is really bothering me now. I could reach over and fix it without getting up, probably. I'd have to lift the mattress corner with one hand and tuck with the other and I'm not sure I can do that from this angle. I'd have to roll over and then I'd lose my position, which is currently perfect — left arm under pillow, right arm on top of blanket, left foot out for temperature regulation. This is an engineered arrangement and I'm not disrupting it for a sheet corner.

The two-note bird is back. Or a different bird doing the same call.
