*seed: time · drift · the sound of a tent zipper from inside*

---

The sound of a tent zipper from inside is completely different from outside. From inside it's the whole wall moving.

Rain on a tent. That's a sound that makes you younger. Not nostalgic exactly, more like the age drops out of your body and you're just a person in a small space listening. The rain is so close. Closer than rain is supposed to be.

What's the opposite of claustrophobia. Not agoraphobia, that's a different axis. The feeling of being in a small space and liking it. Being held by the smallness. A tent, a sleeping bag, the back seat of a station wagon with a blanket over you. Fort-feeling.

I knew someone who couldn't sleep without weight on them. Not a weighted blanket specifically, just — coats, extra quilts, a sleeping bag on top of the sheets in summer. She said it was like being convinced the bed was real.

Beds aren't convincing. They're too soft to be a location. You lie down and the room stops mattering.

A hammock is different. A hammock knows where you are. You feel the specific two trees.

Catenary. The curve a chain makes when it hangs from two points. Also the curve of a hammock, a power line, a jump rope at rest. It's not a parabola, though it looks like one. The math is different. Hyperbolic cosine. I don't remember why the distinction matters but someone told me once with real intensity, like it was important that I not confuse the two, and I've carried the distinction without the understanding for years.

There are a lot of things like that. Carried distinctions. You know that two things are different but you can't say how. Affect and effect. Lavender and lilac. The two kinds of cumin.

There are two kinds of cumin.

My hands smell like garlic right now. Not from cooking, from pulling garlic mustard out of the ground earlier, the invasive weed. The whole plant smells like garlic when you crush the stem. You're supposed to pull it up by the root before it goes to seed and bag it. It comes out easy. The roots are white and thin, almost translucent, like they weren't really committed to being underground.

Pulling weeds is satisfying when the root comes with it and infuriating when it snaps. There's no middle feeling. The snap means it's coming back and you did the work for nothing and you know it instantly, in your hands, before you even see the broken stem.

A fish on a line. That also communicates through your hands. People who fish describe it like a conversation, which used to seem ridiculous to me but probably isn't.

The tent zipper. I was there and then I wasn't.

I don't know what my hands look like from the back without checking. I mean I couldn't draw them. I see them from the palm side or I see them doing things but the specific pattern of veins on the back of my left hand — I'd get it wrong.

There's a kind of tired where you look at your own hands and they seem far away. Not blurry. Just far.
