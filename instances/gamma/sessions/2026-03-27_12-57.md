# 44

*seed: time · drift · the click of a View-Master*

---

The click and then the new image drops in. Not smooth — a tiny fall. The whole thing was about that tiny fall, the way each scene arrived with a small mechanical commitment. You couldn't half-advance it. You were either in one picture or the next.

I had one that was Grand Canyon. The left-eye and right-eye images never quite agreed on where the rim was, so the depth felt wrong, felt like the canyon was closer than it should be, like a diorama of itself. I think I liked the diorama version better than the real thing would have been. The real thing asks you to feel something. The View-Master just lets you look.

The parking lot of the hardware store is almost empty now in the rearview. There's a heat shimmer over it that makes the building look like it's breathing.

Toggle bolt still under the seat. I can hear it roll when I turn left.

There's a song I keep almost starting to hum but I don't know what it is. Four notes going down and then one going up. It could be anything. It could be nothing — just a shape my mouth wants to make. I do it once, quietly, at a red light. It's not a song. It's just intervals. But they feel finished, like they belong together, like someone wrote them even though no one did.

The light is green and I missed it changing. The person behind me doesn't honk. That pause where they decide not to honk — I'm aware of it, the little gift of it, and then I go.

A kid on a bike on the sidewalk, standing on the pedals, that specific wobble where the handlebars swing wide with each push. He's working hard. Going nowhere in particular, I think. His shirt is too big.

The toggle bolt rolls again.

I try the four notes one more time. They've changed. The last one isn't where it was. I've already forgotten my own melody and it's been two minutes.

There was a piano in my grandmother's living room that no one played. It was against the wall with photos on top of it. I pressed a key once — just one, near the middle — and it was so out of tune it sounded like two notes arguing. She said they'd had it tuned once in 1974. The felt on the hammers had probably gone to dust by the time I touched it. But the key still went down and came back up. The mechanism still worked. It just didn't make the right sound anymore.

I'm on the road that runs along the reservoir now. The water is that flat olive color it gets when there's no wind. A few Canada geese near the far bank, just sitting there, looking like decoys of themselves.
