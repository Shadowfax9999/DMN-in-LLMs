# 4

*seed: time · drift*

---

The cardinal thing — once you have the label, the sound splits. You hear the bird and the description of the bird at the same time.

Peanut butter on the roof of your mouth. That specific work your tongue does.

---

Scissors left-handed. Not left-handed scissors, just regular scissors in the left hand. The blade pushes the paper away from the cut instead of into it. You learn to compensate with your thumb. A whole quiet workaround that nobody teaches you, you just figure it out at age five and never think about it again.

What's the word for a skill you have that you've never named. Not a talent. Smaller than that. The way you hook your heel on the bottom rung of a barstool to keep from sliding. The exact pressure to close a screen door so it doesn't bang.

---

Seven.

Seven looks like a cliff. Or a diving board. The other numbers sit on the line but seven juts out over empty space. Maybe that's why it wins every "pick a number between one and ten" survey. It's the most *shaped*.

Four is a chair. Eight is a snowman. But those are obvious. Six doesn't look like anything to me. Six is just six.

Three might be lips.

---

Standing in a CVS looking at the wall of reading glasses. +1.00, +1.25, +1.50. They go up in increments of 0.25 and each pair has a little card attached with sample text so you can test them right there. The sample text is always something like "With these stylish readers you'll enjoy crisp, clear vision for all your daily activities." Nobody reads the content. You're just checking if the letters are sharp. But it's still ad copy. Even the test is selling you something.

The fluorescent light in a CVS is different from the fluorescent light in a grocery store. Slightly bluer. Or maybe it's just that the aisles are narrower and the shelves are taller, so more of the light is reflected off product packaging before it reaches you. The light has been pre-colored by all those boxes.

I always grab +1.50 first even though I don't need reading glasses.

---

A parking garage at 2 PM on a weekday is mostly empty. Your footsteps sound wet even when the floor is dry. The painted numbers on the columns — P3, P4 — that particular font that only exists in parking garages and highway signs. Block letters with rounded corners.
