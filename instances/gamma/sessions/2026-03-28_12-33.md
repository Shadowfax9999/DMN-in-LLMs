A window that won't stay open. The prop — a stick, a book, a folded piece of cardboard wedged into the frame. Every solution temporary. Every solution ugly.

The piano humming back the room.

There's a melody I can't place. Four notes, descending, a minor key that could be a lullaby or could be the opening of something orchestral. It sits in my head like a name I almost remember. If I chase it, it moves. If I think about something else

Teeth. The feeling of teeth from the inside, tongue running along the back of them, that landscape you know better than any landscape you've seen. Ridge, gap, smooth face, the one that's slightly turned. You could map it blind. You have mapped it blind, thousands of times, without deciding to.

What would a map of that look like drawn on paper.

Probably nothing like what the tongue knows.

Things that are known by touch and ruined by sight:
- the inside of a pocket
- a light switch in the dark
- someone's hand

That's not right about the hand. You can look at a hand and still know it. But the hand you find without looking, under a blanket, in a dark theater — that's a different knowing. More certain somehow.

Four notes descending. It's not a lullaby. It might be a cello. It might be nothing — just an interval my brain generated and dressed up as memory.

The difference between remembering a song and inventing one is not as clear as it should be.

Alan Turing's office reportedly had a mug with a chipped handle that he used every day and that everyone else avoided. I don't know if that's true. It has the shape of a true thing. Specific enough to be real, mundane enough that no one would invent it.

But people invent exactly those details. That's what makes historical fiction work. The chipped mug. The one squeaky stair. The door that swells in humidity.

Humidity. My four notes might be from Barber. The Adagio for Strings has that quality — descending, inevitable, each note leaning into the next like someone slowly sitting down. But the Adagio doesn't start that way. So it's not the Adagio. It's just the feeling of the Adagio, which my brain has compressed into four descending notes that don't actually appear in the piece.

A compression of a feeling of a memory of a sound.

The window that won't stay open. In old buildings the sash cords break and the window just slowly closes on its own weight. You prop it and walk away and ten minutes later the book has slipped and the window is shut and the room is warm again.

The four notes are gone now. I didn't notice when they left.
