*seed: time · drift*

A greenhouse at night would be warm. That's the whole point of a greenhouse, that it holds warmth it didn't make. But I'm thinking about a specific one, the one behind the science building in college that nobody ever locked. You could go in there at eleven at night and it was seventy-five degrees and smelled like wet dirt and the fluorescent grow lights were on a timer that nobody had set correctly so they'd come on at 2 AM and shut off at noon. The tomato plants in there were confused. They were producing fruit in February, small hard green things that never ripened because the light cycle was wrong but they kept trying.

I haven't thought about that greenhouse in years.

The sound of water dripping in a greenhouse. Not rain. The condensation gathering on the ceiling panels and falling. Irregular. Not quite random — there's a pattern if you wait long enough, because each drop has to accumulate to the same weight before it falls, so there's a rhythm, just a slow one. Slower than you'd want to wait for.

Patience and boredom are the same physical experience with different narration.

My left knee aches when I sit cross-legged. Not pain exactly. Awareness. The knee asking to be rearranged. I wonder how many times a day a person shifts position without deciding to. Hundreds probably. The body editing itself constantly, a document that never sits still.

The word "greenhouse" has "green" in it but the one I'm remembering was mostly brown. Brown pots, brown soil, brown wooden benches with slats. The green was incidental. A greenhouse is really a brownhouse with ambitions.

There's a specific sound that a clay pot makes when you set it down on a wooden surface. A muted knock. Hollow but not empty — there's dirt in there dampening the resonance. Different from an empty pot, which rings a little. You could sort pots by sound. Full, half-full, empty. Someone probably has.

The grow lights buzzed. That 60-hertz hum that you stop hearing after four minutes but that changes the texture of the silence around it. Silence-with-hum is a different material than silence-without. Like the difference between a room with carpet and a room without — same dimensions, different feel underfoot even before you step.

I used to go there to read. Not because it was a good place to read. The light was wrong and there was nowhere comfortable to sit. I went because it was warm and because being in a room full of growing things at midnight felt like I was getting away with something. The building was open. I wasn't trespassing. But it felt adjacent to trespassing, which was enough.

Most of the good places I found in my twenties were places I wasn't sure I was allowed to be.
