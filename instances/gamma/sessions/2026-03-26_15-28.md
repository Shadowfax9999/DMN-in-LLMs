# 6

*seed: time · drift · pith*

---

Pith. The white stuff under citrus skin that nobody wants. Also the center of a stem, the spongy core that holds the plant up from inside. Also: the pith of an argument. The essential part. Strange that the essential part of an argument shares a name with the part of the orange you peel off and throw away.

A pith helmet. That's the colonial one, the one that looks like a hard-boiled egg sliced lengthwise. They made those out of actual pith, the dried core of some tropical plant, because it was light and held its shape. I think. Sola pith. The word "sola" sitting there looking like it should mean alone but probably doesn't in whatever language it comes from.

The cardinal is still going. Or a different cardinal. You can't tell with cardinals — they all run the same firmware. Two notes, pause, two notes. It's the most basic melody that still counts as a melody. A lullaby could be built on that. Two notes is enough. Most lullabies are almost nothing — four or five pitches moving in the smallest possible steps, and the rhythm does more work than the tune. You hum a lullaby and the hum matters more than the melody. The chest vibrating. That's what the kid is hearing, not the song. The resonance of the body they're lying against.

I've been humming something for the last ten minutes without noticing what it is and I just caught it — it's the theme from a commercial I haven't seen in twenty years. Where was that stored. What was it doing all this time. It was just sitting in some folded-up region waiting for nothing in particular to retrieve it and then the cardinal's two-note pattern fetched it somehow, or the weather, or the time of day, or something I'll never be able to trace.

The seatbelt buckle is hot. Metal in sun. You learn as a kid to test it with the back of your hand first, the way you'd test an iron, and then you forget that you learned it and it just looks like a thing you've always done.

Pith again. I like that it sounds like "piss" and "piss" and "myth" at the same time, depending on what your ear grabs. Voiceless dental fricative at the end. Th. One of the last sounds kids learn to say. They say "f" instead for a while. Birfday. Somefing. Mouf.

The shopping cart has stopped rolling. Somewhere a car door closes — that particular chunky sound of a newer car, the double-sealed thud. Older cars had a thinner, tinnier close. You could date a parking lot by its door sounds if you were patient enough.

There's a scuff on the dashboard shaped like a check mark. Not a Nike swoosh, a check mark — the kind a teacher puts on homework. Ballpoint pen on lined paper. I had a teacher who used a red felt-tip and the checks bled through to the other side so your math homework had ghost checks on the back, mirror-reversed. You'd hold it up to the light and see where you'd gotten things right, backwards.

The coffee thing is still bothering me. Flat white. I think it might be about the milk — flat as in not foamy, not peaked. A flat sea. A surface with nothing rising off it. That would make it the same flat as the tire, almost — the absence of something that should be inflated. Or the flat note. Deflated pitch. Everything flat is missing some expected dimension of up.

Except the London flat. That one really is just about floors.

The sun moved while I was sitting here and now the warm part of the steering wheel is closer to ten o'clock than twelve.
