*seed: time · drift · idiom*

Idiom. Not the linguistic kind. The word itself, sitting there like a stone in a shoe.

What does a flag clip sound like at distance. Not the sound but the way it reaches you thinned out, just the attack of it, no body. A ping that could be anything metallic. Could be a halyard. Could be a shopping cart nudging another shopping cart in a parking lot three blocks away.

I'm in a parking lot. Not the Walgreens one. A different one, almost empty, the kind that belongs to an office building after hours. One car besides mine. I always want to know whose. What are they doing in there at 7:30 on a Friday. Cleaning crew, probably. But I build a whole life for them anyway.

The asphalt has that shimmer where it was patched. Darker rectangle inside the lighter rectangle. Like a screen inside a screen.

Idiom again. Why did that word show up.

An idiom is a thing that means something other than what it says. But also: every instrument has an idiom. The things that fall naturally under the fingers. A piano idiom is different from a guitar idiom. You write differently for each one because each one has opinions about what's easy. The instrument pushes back and the pushing back becomes a style.

Typing has an idiom. Some words are easier to type than others and I wonder if anyone writes slightly different sentences because of where the letters sit.

The office building has that blue-green light on the second floor. Fluorescent, but the tint of the windows changes it. Every office building at night looks like it's underwater on the inside.

A thought about hands. Not a specific pair of hands. Just — the way hands look when someone is explaining something they care about. They stop being hands and become diagrams. They draw the shape of the idea in the air and then the shape dissolves and they have to draw it again.

My left knee aches when I sit in a car too long. That dull thing that isn't quite pain. More like the joint reminding you it's a joint. Most of the time a knee is invisible to the person who owns it. It only becomes a knee when something's slightly wrong.

The car in the other spot is a Camry. Silver. The most anonymous car. If you were trying to be forgotten by every parking lot camera in America you'd drive a silver Camry.

There's a bird I can't see making a sound I can't place. Two notes, close together, the second one lower. Over and over. Patient about it.

I locked my keys in a car once when I was nineteen. In a parking lot not unlike this one. The locksmith took forty-five minutes to arrive and popped the lock in maybe eight seconds. I remember the specific feeling of watching someone do something effortlessly that had been impossible for me. Not admiration exactly. More like the world rearranging itself around a new fact.

The bird again. Same two notes.
