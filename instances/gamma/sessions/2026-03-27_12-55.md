# 42

*seed: time · drift · a balcony you've never stepped onto before*

---

The gas station that used to be a different gas station still has the old curb cut at the wrong angle. You can feel it in the steering wheel when you turn in — the car dips left and then corrects. I'm not turning in. I'm turning at the light past it.

The melody is still stuck. Not stuck like an earworm, stuck like a sentence missing its verb. There's a place where it should go and it doesn't go there.

Balcony. I don't know why that word. But there's a hotel I stayed in once where the balcony door had a handle you had to lift and push at the same time, and I couldn't figure it out for an embarrassingly long time. I just stood there working the handle like it was a puzzle box. The curtain smelled like iron.

Green light. I turn.

This road is narrower than I expected. Not narrow, just narrower than the one I was on. The houses are closer to the street. Some of them have those metal awnings over the front door, the kind with the scalloped edge. Mint green, or that particular aluminum white that isn't quite white.

Someone is watering a lawn with a hose, not a sprinkler. Just standing there holding the hose with their thumb over the end. There's a specific posture people take when they do that. Weight on one leg. Looking at nothing in particular.

The melody tries again. Gets one note further than before and stops.

I'm going to the hardware store but I can't remember what for. I had it when I got in the car. It was a specific thing — not browsing, a specific thing I needed. A size of something. A replacement for something.

The person watering the lawn watches me pass. Not watching me. Their eyes happen to follow a moving thing.

A bracket. That's what it was. A shelf bracket, the L-shaped kind. The one in the hall closet pulled out of the drywall. I remember now because I remember looking at the hole it left, the small cone of white dust on the shelf below it, and thinking I should vacuum that up first before I forget, and then I forgot.

I'm at another light. Red. The car in front of me has a bumper sticker I can't read from here — just color, no words yet. I roll forward a few feet as the light stays red. Still can't read it. Blue background, white text. Could be anything.

A school bus crosses the intersection going the other way, completely empty. Just the driver. The windows are all down and you can see straight through it to the other side of the street, row after row of green seatbacks with no heads above them.

Light turns. The bumper sticker says something about a beach.

The hardware store is in a strip mall with a nail salon and a place that sells vacuum cleaners. The parking lot has those concrete wheel stops that are always slightly crooked, like each one was placed independently by someone who didn't look at the others. I pull into a spot and the engine ticks as it cools. The melody surfaces one more time, gets to the same place, and I realize it isn't missing a note. That's where it ends. It was finished the whole time and I kept waiting for more.

I sit there for a second with the key out.

The hardware store's front window has a hand-written sign about a sale on something. I can see the orange letters but not what they say. Everything in hardware stores is that same orange, or that same blue. I need a shelf bracket. I might also need the anchors, the kind that spread open behind the drywall like a little umbrella. I'll figure it out when I'm looking at them.
