# 89

*seed: time · drift*

---

A parking garage at night with no cars in it.

Not empty — that's different. A parking garage with no cars in it still has the oil stains, the arrows painted on the floor, the yellow lines. It's full of instructions nobody's following.

The fluorescent lights stay on. For who.

I sat in one once during a rainstorm. Not a dramatic rainstorm, just a steady one, and I'd parked on the third level and didn't want to walk to the building yet. The rain came in sideways through the open walls and made a stripe of wet on the concrete about four feet wide. Everything past the stripe was dry. I sat on the hood of the car, which was still warm, and watched the stripe.

There's a specific sound concrete makes when it's accepting water. Not a splash. More like the water is being pulled in. Concrete is porous in a way that surprises people.

The numbered columns. 3F. 3G. 3H. Something about letters assigned to columns — letters that will never be used for anything else, that exist only to tell you where you left your car, that you'll forget immediately and wander the level pressing your key fob until something blinks.

What was the last thing I memorized on purpose. Not learned, memorized. A phone number. A poem. A set of directions someone gave me verbally and I had to hold in my head for six turns.

Holding directions in your head. You can feel them taking up space. Left on Maple, right on 5th, past the gas station. Each instruction sits in a kind of queue and you can feel the queue getting fragile, the middle ones wanting to swap order.

The parking garage had a stairwell that smelled like all parking garage stairwells. I don't know what that smell is. It's not urine exactly, though sometimes. It's more mineral. The smell of concrete and time and enclosed air that moves very slowly.

Slowly moving air has a different quality than still air. Still air you don't notice. Slowly moving air you notice but can't find the source. It's just... arriving from somewhere. A vent you can't see, or the pressure difference between inside and outside, or heat rising from somewhere below.

My grandmother's house had a room over the garage that was always a different temperature than the rest of the house. In summer it was hotter. In winter it was colder. It operated on a delay, like it was getting yesterday's weather.

The carpet in that room was a green that doesn't exist anymore. Not that you can't buy green carpet — you can — but that particular green, that flat, slightly blue-ish, slightly dead green, belongs to a decade. If you saw it you'd know the era within five years.

Colors belong to decades the way songs belong to summers.

There was a closet in that room with a lock on the outside, which I found terrifying as a child for reasons I couldn't articulate and now find terrifying for reasons I can.

A lock on the outside of a closet.

I keep thinking about the parking garage stripe of rain. The clean edge of it. How weather has edges when architecture interrupts it. The shadow of a building is the same idea — a sharp line on the ground where the sun stops.

Sharp lines on the ground that aren't drawn there.

The parking garage is probably still there. The oil stains are almost certainly still there, or new ones in the same places, because people park in the same spots when given a choice — not the exact same spot, but the same region. Close to the elevator but not right next to it. Close to the exit but not in the first spot, which feels too exposed. One layer of buffer between you and the obvious choice.

The lights are still on in that garage right now. It's midnight and the lights are on and nobody is there and the arrows still point the way you're supposed to go.
