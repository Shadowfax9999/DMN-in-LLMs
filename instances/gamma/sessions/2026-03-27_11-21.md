*seed: time · drift · dogleg*

Dogleg. A hallway that turns for no reason. A golf hole that bends left. The leg of a dog doesn't actually bend that way though — it bends backward at what looks like the knee but is actually the ankle. The knee is up higher, hidden in the body.

The fitted sheet is still off that corner. I could fix it but I'd have to get up and walk around the bed and pull it and then the opposite corner would pop off. They always do. You fix one and lose another. Fitted sheets exist in a state of having at least one corner wrong.

Dogleg. Also a type of staircase. The kind that goes up, hits a landing, turns, goes up again. Most staircases in houses built before 1950 are like that. Or maybe that's not true. I'm thinking of a specific house.

The bubbles in the water glass haven't moved. I keep looking at them like they might. They're stuck to the inside surface in little colonies. Some of them are touching each other and have merged into a slightly larger bubble that's more oval than round. The water level is low enough that the bubbles above the waterline have dried into tiny salt rings. Those won't go away until you wash the glass.

Dogleg is one of those words that should be disgusting but isn't. Unlike, say, "moist" which everyone claims to hate but I think people mostly hate because other people told them to. "Dogleg" has the same consonant structure as "boggle" almost.

The light coming through the window is moving across the bed very slowly. Not slowly enough to watch but fast enough that if I look away and look back in a few minutes it'll have moved a visible amount. Right now it's a rectangle on the comforter, slightly warped because the comforter isn't flat. The wrinkles in the fabric make the light edge jagged.

I'm hungry but not enough to get up.

Missouri. The -ee and the -uh. Regional shibboleths. There's one for Oregon too — people not from there say Ora-gone and people from there say Oreg'n, almost two syllables. Nevada has one. Apparently the wrong way is Ne-vah-da with the broad A and the right way has a flat A like in "bad." I've never been confident about Arkansas versus Kansas and why one is Ar-kan-saw and the other is Kan-zis. They're the same word with a prefix.

The pillow has sunk again. I can feel my spine angle changing, the slow recline. I'm going to end up horizontal without deciding to lie down. Just a gradual surrender to the pillow's preference for being flat.

What would it be like to only know one language and then hear another one for the first time. Not as a baby, but as an adult. If somehow you'd never heard any language besides your own. The sounds would just be sounds. You wouldn't even be sure it was language at first — it might sound like an elaborate cough, or singing with bad melody. I think there are historical accounts of this. Greek and "bar bar bar," that's where barbarian comes from.

The rectangle of light has moved. It's touching my foot now through the comforter. I can feel the warmth but only barely, through the fabric. If I pulled my foot out from under the comforter and put it in the light it would be warm in a completely different way. Direct sun on skin in the morning is a specific temperature that's different from afternoon sun even if the actual temperature is the same.

Friday. The word Friday has a yellow feeling. Monday is dark blue. I don't actually have synesthesia, I think everyone has mild associations like this.

The fitted sheet corner is bothering me from across the bed. The mattress pad is showing, which is a slightly different white than the sheet — more of a cream, or maybe the sheet is more of a cream. Hard to tell which one is the "real" white. They look different only next to each other.

I should drink that water but it's been sitting out all night and room temperature water from a glass that's been sitting out tastes different from room temperature water you just poured. It tastes like the glass. Like the room. There's a staleness to it that's probably just dissolved gases leaving and being replaced by whatever's in the air.

Dogleg. I still don't know why that word showed up.
