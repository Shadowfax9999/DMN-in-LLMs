# 76

*seed: time · drift · tetherball*

---

Tetherball. The sound of it more than the game. That flat rubber slap and then the chain winding tighter, the pitch of the chain changing as it shortens. Higher. Like a spring compressing.

I haven't thought about tetherball in — how long. Playgrounds don't have them anymore. Or maybe they do and I just don't pass playgrounds.

The pole after the ball stops. Just a pole with a chain hanging from it. One of the loneliest objects.

A ferry at night. The engine underneath is a feeling more than a sound. Your feet know it. The deck vibrates at some frequency that makes your teeth feel loose, not painfully, just present. You become aware of your teeth as individual objects in your head. Which is unsettling. Then you stop noticing.

The water off the side of a ferry at night is black in a way that other dark things aren't. A field at night is dark but you can feel the ground. Water at night is just absence with texture. It moves. You can see the movement but not the thing that's moving.

Someone on the ferry is always leaning on the railing alone. There's a specific posture for it. Forearms flat, hands clasped or hanging, weight forward. Looking at nothing. The ferry version of a person is different from their land version. Slower. Willing to stand still for no reason.

The coffee from the ferry vending machine. Terrible. A kind of hot brown liquid that refers to coffee without being coffee. You drink it anyway because you're on a ferry and it's cold and holding a warm cup is the point, not the taste.

Inside the cabin the lights are fluorescent and too bright and everyone looks slightly ill. The contrast when you step back outside — your eyes take a full minute to adjust and in that minute the water is even blacker than before.

Somewhere across the water there's a light that might be a town or might be a single house. No way to tell from here. Scale disappears over water. A mountain could be a hill. A city could be a gas station.

The ferry horn. Two blasts when it leaves the dock. Low enough that it doesn't just enter your ears, it enters your chest. You feel it in your sternum. Then it stops and the silence after it is a different silence than before — it has the shape of the horn still in it, a low rectangular hole in the air that takes a few seconds to fill back in with wind and engine and water.

I wonder what key ferry horns are usually in.

The chain on the tetherball pole would rust by October. Orange rust that came off on your hands. The smell of rust is the smell of iron, which is the smell of blood, which is why you'd sometimes taste something metallic after gym class and not know if it was the equipment or your own lip.

The ferry is docking now. You can feel it in the way the engine changes. Not louder or quieter but a different kind of effort. The vibration in the deck shifts from steady to searching. Bumpers against the pilings make a sound like a huge shoe on a wet floor.

Everyone stands up before they should. The car deck isn't open yet. But people stand and collect their bags and move toward the stairs as if standing will make it faster. It won't, but sitting while everyone else stands feels like a statement, so you stand too.

The ramp drops with a metal clang and then the cars start and the exhaust collects under the low ceiling of the car deck and for thirty seconds you're breathing in a cloud of everyone's engine at once. Cold air from outside cuts through it in a diagonal line — you can actually see where the clean air meets the exhaust, a visible boundary, and you walk through it like walking through a wall made of nothing but temperature.

Then you're driving off the ramp and the tires make a different sound on the dock than they did on the ferry. Harder. More definite. Land again.

The headlights of the cars coming off the ferry scatter in all directions at the first intersection, and within two minutes you can't tell who was on the boat with you. Everyone absorbed back into regular traffic. The ferry version of them is already gone.

There's a stoplight at the top of the hill that's always red when you come off the ferry. Always. You sit there and the car still has that ferry feeling, that residual hum in the frame, and the heater is blowing and the red light is reflecting off the wet road and it looks like the road itself is warm.
