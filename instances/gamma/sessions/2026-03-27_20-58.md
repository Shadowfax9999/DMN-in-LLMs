The guitar chords through the wall have that quality where you can't tell if they're getting better or just getting more confident, which might be the same thing.

Spanish, maybe. Two voices overlapping in a way English doesn't. One rises while the other is still finishing. Not interrupting — just how it works. The rhythm of it carries more than the words. You could map the conversation without understanding any of it: agreement, disagreement, a story, the punchline, the protest, the concession. All from cadence.

Attics.

The thing about attics is the air is different. Not stale exactly. Held. Like it's been sitting at that temperature for six months waiting for someone to open the hatch. And then you do and it falls on you like a warm cloth.

My grandmother had a box of buttons. Not sorted, not for anything. Just buttons. Hundreds of them in a tin that used to hold Danish cookies. Every grandmother had that tin. The cookies were never in it. I don't know where the buttons came from — off of what shirts, what coats. Some were military-looking. Some were translucent pink. She never used them for anything I saw.

What happens to a box of buttons.

The conversation outside has moved. They're walking. The sound stretches as they go, the vowels pulling longer, and then it folds into traffic noise and the guitar is the only thing left.

Fingernails on nylon strings have a specific softness. Steel strings sound like they're being struck. Nylon strings sound like they're being asked.

There's a crack in the ceiling that's been there longer than I've noticed it. It runs from the light fixture toward the corner and then just stops, like it lost interest. Plaster does that. It commits to a direction and then doesn't follow through.

I had a teacher who could write on the chalkboard without turning around. She'd be talking to us, facing us, and her hand would just be going behind her, writing in this perfect slanted script. We never talked about it. It was just something she could do.

The person playing guitar has found a progression they like. They keep going back to the start of it. Four chords, maybe five. The last one doesn't resolve — it opens back into the first. A loop that could run forever if you let it.

Buttons in a cookie tin. The way they sounded when you ran your hand through them.
