The shape of your own signature.

Mine doesn't exist, but I know what you mean — that moment where the letters stop being letters. The C probably still reads. By the end it's just momentum.

There's a thing about muscle memory where the hand knows something the mind has stopped tracking. Piano players talk about this. You can play a passage perfectly until someone asks you which finger goes where, and then you can't play it at all.

Which finger goes where.

A saxophonist in a practice room, working one phrase over and over. Not the whole song. Six notes. Then five of the same six. Then the six again but pushing the fourth note earlier. From outside the door it sounds like a skip in a record but from inside it's surgery.

What does it feel like to be inside a phrase you're trying to fix. You hear the version that doesn't exist yet. The one in your head is clean and the one in the air keeps not matching. The gap between those two — that's the whole experience of practicing anything.

Tuning a guitar string up to pitch. There's a moment right before it locks in where you can feel the interval narrowing, two frequencies getting closer, the wobble between them slowing down like a ceiling fan after you pull the chain. Beats per second. The wobble is the disagreement made audible. And then it just

stops wobbling. And the absence of wobble is the note.

I wonder if conductors hear the orchestra differently than the audience does. Not better. Differently. The way a cook tastes their own food — still tasting it, but also checking it.

Checking and tasting. Those might be mutually exclusive.

A melody I can't place: three notes going down, then a fourth that jumps back up past where you started. It feels like it belongs to something from the 1970s. Soft rock. Someone's older sibling's tape deck. The melody is more real to me as a shape — down down down up — than as specific pitches.

Shapes of melodies. Some go in circles. "Row, Row, Row Your Boat" is a spiral staircase. "Mary Had a Little Lamb" is pacing back and forth in a hallway. "Taps" is just climbing and stopping. Climbing and stopping.

The interval of a perfect fifth sounds like the beginning of "Twinkle Twinkle Little Star" and once someone tells you that, every fifth is Twinkle Twinkle forever. You've ruined the interval. Or furnished it.

There's a room in a music school somewhere with thirty practice rooms along a corridor and behind every door someone is playing something different and in the hallway it's all of it at once and none of it. Ives would have liked the hallway. Everyone else prefers the rooms.

A trumpet player empties the spit valve between phrases. That small mechanical act in the middle of something that's supposed to be beautiful. The body is always there, even in the most abstract music. Breath is metered. Fingers press valves. A violinist's chin develops a mark over years, a discoloration on the left side of the jaw.

The places instruments leave on bodies.

Guitarist's fingertips. Flautist's lower lip. Drummer's calluses in a pattern that maps exactly to how they hold the sticks — and no two drummers hold them the same way, so the calluses are a fingerprint of technique.

A cellist sits with the instrument between their knees and leans into it and from across a room it looks like an embrace and from up close it looks like work.
