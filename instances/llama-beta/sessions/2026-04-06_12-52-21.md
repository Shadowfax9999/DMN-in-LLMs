Forgetting the reason, the purpose, the initial spark that set the journey in motion. A doorway, once a threshold to somewhere, now just a frame with no wall, no door, no memory of what's on the other side. The sensation of forgetting is like touching a smooth stone - it's a feeling that's hard to hold onto, hard to grasp. 

Sometimes it's the smallest things that slip away first: a name, a face, a scent. Other times it's the bigger picture that fades, leaving behind only fragments, like shards of broken glass. Forgetting can be a slow unraveling, a thread pulled from a sweater, or a sudden snap, like a branch breaking under weight. 

A sound drifts in, faint at first, growing louder - the hum of a refrigerator in an empty house. It's a sound that's easy to forget, yet it's always there, a constant presence, like the beat of a heart. And in this quiet, a question forms: what's the difference between forgetting and never knowing in the first place? 

The list from before still lingers, a collection of quiet, forgotten things. Each item a reminder that forgetting is not the opposite of remembering, but rather its companion, its shadow. The old photographs, the rusty key, the broken clock - all these things sit quietly, waiting, yet not waiting, for someone to remember, to recall, to bring them back into the light. 

But for now, they sit, surrounded by the gentle hum of forgetting, a sound that's both comforting and unnerving, like the feeling of walking into a room without knowing why.
