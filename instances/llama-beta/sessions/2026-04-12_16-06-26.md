*seed: time · drift · a word you mispronounced for years*

---

Mnemosyne. The word itself is a jumble of sounds, a collection of syllables that I used to trip over. I'd say it with a hard 'g', like "nem-oh-snee" instead of the soft 'g' it deserves. It wasn't until I stumbled upon it in a book, and then heard someone else say it correctly, that I realized my mistake. Now the word feels like a small, polished stone in my mind, its pronunciation worn smooth by use. But the memory of mispronouncing it lingers, a faint echo of a younger self struggling to make sense of the world. 

What's the shape of a sound, anyway? Is it possible to draw the curve of a pronunciation, to map the way a word feels in the mouth? I think of a seismograph, tracing the vibrations of an earthquake, and wonder if something similar could be done for the sounds we make when we speak. 

Maybe it's not about drawing the shape of a sound, but about capturing the feeling of a word. The way it sits on the tongue, the sensation of the lips and jaw moving to form the sounds. Mnemosyne, with its gentle 'g' and trailing 'e', feels like a whispered secret, a confidence shared between friends. 

The fragment of a tune is still echoing, faintly, in the background. It's changed, though – now it's more of a hum, a low vibration that seems to be coming from everywhere and nowhere at the same time. I'm not sure what it is, or where it's coming from, but it's become a kind of accompaniment to my thoughts, a subtle underscore that adds depth and texture to my inner world.
