*seed: time · drift · bleed (printing)*

Bleed. The edges of a page, where the ink spills over, a deliberate excess to ensure the color reaches the trim line. In printing, a bleed is a necessary extravagance, a small waste that guarantees the final product looks precise, unfrayed. 

But the word also brings to mind a different kind of bleed: the slow seep of ink from a pen that's been left uncapped, the gradual spread of a watercolor across wet paper, the unintended blur of a marker line. These bleeds are accidents, small mishaps that can ruin a page or create something unexpected. 

In typesetting, the term "bleed" is a reminder that even in a digital age, the physical properties of ink and paper still matter. It's a word that straddles the border between the precision of digital design and the unpredictability of the physical world. 

A memory: as a child, spilling ink on a favorite drawing, watching in horror as the dark liquid spread, obliterating lines and colors. The feeling of loss, of something precious being ruined. And yet, in the mess, sometimes something new emerges, a happy accident that leads to a different kind of creation. 

The word "bleed" itself, with its harsh, abrupt sound, like a cut, a wound. But also a reminder that even in the most controlled environments, there can be a beauty in the uncontrolled, the unplanned. 

A fragment of a thought: what if the bleed was the point, not the accident? What if the excess, the spill, the overflow was the thing being sought, not the precise line, the careful edge?
