# 25

*seed: time · drift*

---

When attention returns, it doesn't always come with intent. Sometimes you remember the thing in the drawer simply because your hand brushes against it, or because light glints a certain way, or for no reason at all—a small flare in the otherwise unremarkable darkness.

Some objects take on weight only through repetition. The rubber band you move aside and then replace; the scrap of paper that isn't quite trash but isn't part of anything. These are not relics, not memorabilia, not even forgotten. Their inertia is physical, a kind of quiet persistence.

There are sounds that repeat without ever being the same: the elevator bell in an old building, muffled by the corridor; a shoe heel catching on tile, sharp and sudden and gone. No one marks them, but layered over months they form a pattern of occupancy that is almost but not quite a memory.

---

The word "envelope" used to mean a thing that wraps around. Now it's paper folded and glued, corners tucked sharp, a vessel by definition but often empty, waiting for meaning to be inserted. There are envelopes with windows and those without. The window ones let part of the inside show through, a fraction of an address, never the whole message. Where the glue fails, the flap flutters.

---

There are unspoken rules about arrival: who goes first through the door, who stands aside, when to fill a silence and when to let it stretch. It's possible to memorize the sequence but still step into the wrong gap, two people pausing for each other, the choreography stalling for a split second before resolving into polite murmurs and shuffling.

---

Some numbers are easy to remember because of the way they sound. Seventeen. Forty-four. Eighty-one. Others feel unstable, like fifty-three or sixty-nine, not because of their value but the shape they make in the mouth and the air they take up when spoken.

---

The pencil marks remain faintly visible even after erasing, soft grooves pressing into the page, a ghost of calculation that isn't needed anymore.
