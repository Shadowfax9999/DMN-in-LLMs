# 6

*seed: time · drift · a book with someone else's bookmark still in it*

---

The bookmark is a torn envelope, half of a postmark visible, a triangle of old glue curling up at the edge. In the margins of page 87, pencil underlines, faint enough you almost have to tilt the book to see them. “Blossoms with no promise of fruit” is double-underlined. The paper smells like basements and a faint shadow of vanilla, maybe from the paper itself or maybe just time.

Some people leave objects in books: receipts, train tickets, a square of birthday wrapping, the tiny white slip that comes from a fortune cookie, flattened almost smooth. You imagine hands pausing mid-sentence to tuck something in, promises to return.

---

Words that feel like objects:  
cobblestone  
thimble  
husk  
capstan  
velvet  
anklet  
snag  
buttonhook

---

The porch light isn’t on yet.  
There’s always a half-second when you’re not sure if you should knock or just wait.  
The bottom of the grocery bag has dampened where the milk was sweating.  
There’s a single ant carrying something three times its size, moving stiffly along the groove.

---

The catalogue of things left in pockets:

1. Gum wrappers, folding into themselves.  
2. A penny, dull enough to look like a button in the half-light.  
3. A ticket stub with the words mostly faded.  
4. Lint in blue and gray.  
5. A shopping list with one item crossed off and three never bought.  
6. The small ridged stone from a path somewhere, warmer than you’d expect.  
7. Grain of rice, unknown origin.  
8. That plastic tag from bread, always green or white.

---

What does it mean when a word feels like the inside of a cupboard?  
Sometimes the house is quiet but it isn’t still.  
The honeysuckle must have trailed here from the fence; it was never planted on this side.  
Plastic porch chairs, one leg shorter than the others, always shifting under any weight.  
The sound the old screen door makes, slow and then a quick snap shut.  
The bookmark is still there when you close the book again.
