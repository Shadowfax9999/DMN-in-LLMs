# 27

*seed: time · drift · the particular gray of dryer lint*

---

Dryer lint is a gray not found on walls or in clouds, a gray with the faintest blue and pink threads, an accidental composite. It softens into itself, clings with a static tension. Lint gathers in corners, will not float cleanly away if you try to blow it. The feeling of picking it up between finger and thumb, always a slight hesitation: is it still damp? Does it crumble or compress to nothing?

There are colors in dust that you can't see while they're part of a thing. Only after they're broken down—tiny fragments of a red sock, blue towel, the pale thread from a shirt collar—do they blend into that lint-gray. It’s not the gray of stone, not the gray of sky. It’s the gray of finished things.

How much lint is made in a building in a week. How much in a year. There must be a number, somewhere, for the average lint per person.

Sometimes lint smells strongly of whatever was washed: artificial lavender, the sour tang of something left too long. Sometimes no scent at all, just a faint warmth.

---

Laundry is one kind of residue but there are others. The patterns on a keyboard: some keys shining smooth, letters rubbing off, islands of skin oil only visible at a certain angle. The Q always looks older than the T, although it’s pressed less. That doesn’t make sense but it’s true on this one.

There’s residue on phone screens, too. A map of every swipe and press, only visible in the light at dusk.

Lists of residues:

— black dust at the bottom of a backpack
— pinkish eraser shavings caught in binder rings
— yellowed tape at the edge of a poster left too long
— coffee rings on the underside of a mug
— salt dried to a white bloom on leather shoes
— dried soap scum in the base of a bottle

Some residues grow, some shrink, some just persist, outlasting the thing that made them.
