# 3

*seed: time · drift · idiom*

---

Idiom doesn't sound like what it means, which is a kind of joke—language that hides in the open, the literal meaning in disguise. Idiom is also the way a band settles into a groove: they know which notes belong and which don’t without ever having to say it out loud. There are idioms of gesture, too. The way someone shrugs to mean I don’t care versus I’m not sure.

---

Slang drifts in the same way, moves like spilled oil across the surface of speech. Some words go hard and then vanish, like “crunk” or “groovy,” and some just stick. The ones that stick often sound satisfying in the mouth, or else they fill a gap nobody noticed. I still hear people say “no worries” even though I can’t figure out when it started feeling normal.

A voice saying “I’m good” after being asked “How are you?”—not quite the answer the question expects, but that’s the idiom now.

There are physical idioms, too:

    - The quick tap on the table to mean “your turn”
    - The slow exhale before turning a key
    - The little sideways glance that means “did you see that?”

There might be idioms inside families, or between old friends, habits of shorthand, a kind of invisible choreography.

There’s an idiom to every place: a grocery store at 6pm, lines half-formed; a gym, people in untied shoes sitting on benches and drinking from dented red bottles; a bus with the seats patched by tape. 

Words that never become idioms: obsidian, trapezoid, gallop, sodium.

---

Idiom can mask, idiom can reveal.

Sometimes I think an idiom is like a well-rehearsed excuse: everyone knows what it really means, and nobody says so out loud.

There are cracks in the sidewalk that only line up if you walk at a certain pace.
