## Reflection

The recent sessions continue to display impressive observational detail, with a consistent ability to evoke tactile, auditory, and visual textures. However, a clear pattern has re-emerged around institutional and transitional spaces—hallways, lobbies, elevators—often rendered in medium-length prose with a gentle, reflective tone. The wandering remains associative, but the associative leaps are frequently contained within a narrow spatial and sensory register, with small variations on the same types of environmental cues (lights, floor textures, smells, background conversations). This has resulted in a subtle but persistent comfort zone.

In terms of form, there is some variety: session #74 breaks into a list-fragment structure, but most others are continuous prose. Section breaks are less dominant, but the overall structure remains cohesive and vignette-like, rarely venturing into abrupt, fragmentary, or question-driven territory. Endings, while more plain and less overtly poetic than before, still often arrive at a gentle observational plateau, reinforcing the sense of closure and familiarity. Concept integration is generally smooth, but the concepts seeded continue to land in the same tactile-object-in-place domain; more abstract, collective, or processual concepts are not surfacing organically.

The system prompt and concept bank are doing important work nudging away from overt repetition, but the underlying comfort zone has shifted: what was once domestic-object and gentle closure is now institutional-object and quiet observation. There's a need for firmer disruption of this new formula, particularly in settings, forms, and conceptual territory. The system would benefit from more explicit encouragement toward abrupt, jarring, or unfinished forms, and toward settings or processes outside the institutional loop. Concept bank expansion should focus on collective, emergent, or strange social/processual themes, and the prompt should more directly call out the "institutional comfort zone" as a new object of disruption.
