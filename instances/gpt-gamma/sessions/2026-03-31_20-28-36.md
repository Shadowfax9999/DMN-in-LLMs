# 62

*seed: time · drift*

---

The first twist tie you ever notice is silver, running through the neck of a bag of rolls, the wire poking through the plastic film after too many reuses. Later, you encounter green twist ties, paper flaking off to show the wire underneath. The wire inside is always surprised to be visible. The box labeled “Twist Ties” contains a handful of bread tags too, the ones with the tiny teeth. Bread tags never seem to line up with the serrations on the bag. Tiny plastic squares, color-coded, blue for Tuesday, yellow for Friday, or is it regional? No one ever remembers to check.

There’s a pile of rubber bands in the same drawer, thick tan ones and a rare, blue one, old enough to snap when you stretch it. The drawer smells faintly of pencil shavings and packet soy sauce. Things slide around in there, coins from other countries with unfamiliar monarchs or buildings, a battery leaking white powder, a half-used pad of sticky notes coming unstuck at the edges.

A phone cord coiled on the floor collects dust. Coils trying to remember the shape they had in the package, the tightness, but it’s a different shape now, relaxed and wide, not quite a spiral, curves pressed flat. Spiral stairs look like they should wind forever, but they are always closed off at the top by a locked door or a landing that turns.

Condiment packets get sticky and puffy at the corners. Ketchup packets look like tiny pillows, the press of the sauce making the plastic tight and cool. A child sorts the packets by color, then gives up midway and puts them all back in the bowl.

A garden hose uncoils with reluctance, keeps the memory of its last loop, resists stretching out straight, always one last kink that sprays water sideways. Unwinding isn’t the same as untangling. Some knots hold together because they are wound, not tangled. The knots in shoelaces, the way a twist tie can hold two things together but also make them harder to separate.

The extra buttons come in tiny plastic bags, translucent with logos printed on one side. Sometimes the thread is wound around the button, like a promise to fix something not yet broken. If you lose the button, you’re supposed to have saved this one, but it never matches quite right, or else the shirt is gone before the button is needed. Some gestures expect you to plan for a future you won’t enter. 

Sometimes the twist tie is the last thing keeping a bag closed, or keeping two wires from separating, or holding a bundle of cables just past the point where they could come unraveled.
