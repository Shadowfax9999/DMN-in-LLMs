# 68

*seed: time · drift · a fact you learned wrong and still think of first*

---

The Tropic of Capricorn is north of the Equator. That’s not right; it’s south, but the mistake is sticky, persistent, echoes up.  
The moon’s dark side is never seen from earth, and somehow my mind still pictures an actual half, black, hidden — not the slow rotation, always showing a face. The image is too neat, like a lid shut tight.  
Brussels sprouts: for too long just “brussel sprouts,” the 's' dropped, and even now typing it, it looks overgrown.  
Saturn’s rings are solid, like vinyl records, not vast discs of ice and dust — the old textbook drawing with bold, clean lines probably did that.  
Tomatoes, that memory: "Don’t refrigerate," but they arrive already cold, so what is it that gets lost, and at what point?  
A chameleon changes color to match any background — it’s camouflage, but not the kind from storybooks, bright to bright, flower to flower. They shift for mood, the stories say.  
The equinox isn’t when day and night are equal everywhere.  
Bats are blind, people say, but they aren’t. Blind as a bat: the phrase keeps flying.  
The tongue map: bitter, sweet, salty, sour, each in neat sections — still the first picture, but taste is everywhere, muddled, flooding all around.  
Lightning never strikes the same place twice.  

The shape of Greenland on most maps stays wrong, because of the way the world gets flattened and stretched, but in my mind it’s always huge, too big for the page.  
---

Does dust ever forget what it was?  
Who holds the rights to a word that means something else now, years after it’s slipped?  
What if the wrong fact is just quieter, waiting underneath all the times someone said it right?  
How many facts are just shapes that settled in before the rest of the world caught up?  
What do you do with all the little errors?
