# 92

*seed: time · drift · dial tone*

---

Dial tone: a sound that is always there until you need it not to be. The line is open until it closes. The world builds waiting rooms out of noise.

A dial tone is not a signal for you, it’s a signal for the system, an announcement that the wires are awake, that the channel is clear, that absence is a kind of promise. The hum under everything, artificial, even, continuous, humming regardless of urgency on the other end. The old rotary phones made you wait through the sound of their own mechanics — the click-click as the dial spun back, the finger tracing numbers into the air, the rotary stutter. Now nothing spins. The tone is simulated. The delay is feigned. 

Nobody calls a dial tone anymore. Cell phones have no dial tone, just waiting for you to begin, as if a void is more normal than a signal. 

The idea of waiting for a tone before you can act. The feeling of holding the receiver, a plastic weight, knowing the world is listening, or at least listening for you. Sometimes, at payphones, it would take a moment, the coin clicking through a system of tiny levers and switches, each one triggering the next. The tone would come as relief: permission to start.

I can almost hum the frequency, but not quite. I think it’s two notes, close together, a chord so steady you stop hearing it. If you pick up a phone in the middle of the night and listen, the dial tone is the same as at noon, as in a thunderstorm, as during a blackout, at least for a while, until the world recedes and the wires go silent.

I’m thinking about the confusion of a busy signal: a kind of refusal. A line that is occupied. The system’s way of saying, not now, not you, not this call—different from the dial tone’s invitation.

If you leave a line open long enough, unattended, the tone changes. Warnings, beeps, sometimes a recording telling you to hang up and try again. 

There are places where a dial tone is a memory, and places where it is still the start of every conversation. 

I can’t remember the last time I heard one.
