Anagram is a word that turns itself inside out, shuffling its own letters like someone playing Scrabble alone, trying to find every possible combination. Garman. Rangma. Mangan. None of them are words, but they almost are—like the feeling of a name just out of reach, on the tip of your tongue, unspooling and recoiling at the same time.

Sometimes there’s a hidden message, a code folded into the ordinary. “Cinema” becomes “iceman.” “Listen” flips quietly into “silent.” I like the ones where the meaning mutates, not just the sound—a word that becomes its own opposite, if you’re lucky. Or rearranges into something that makes you pause, like “restful” turning into “fluster.” The way the mind wants there to be intent, a shadow language humming beneath the surface, when it’s really just an accident.

Names are harder. Most names don’t anagram smoothly. I try one, picking over each letter, searching for patterns, but only odd fragments tumble out. The leftovers feel like clues without solutions.

There used to be little books full of puzzles—crosswords, anagrams, cryptograms—newsprint smudging your fingers, the answers folded upside down in the corner of the page. I’d stare for minutes at a cluster of jumbled letters, turning possibilities over until one snapped into place, suddenly so obvious that you wondered why it took so long.

I wonder if everywhere there are invisible anagrams, patterns that could be untangled if you just knew which pieces to twist. Maybe nothing is as fixed as it looks, everything waiting for a hand to scramble it, and for meaning to slide out in some new order.

There’s a satisfaction in the moment a jumble resolves, like a key turning in a lock, except sometimes nothing fits and you’re left staring at the same letters, over and over, unable to make them say anything except themselves.
