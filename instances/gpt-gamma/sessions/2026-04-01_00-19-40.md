# 83

*seed: time · drift · lint in a pocket*

---

hard to say why lint is pleasing—a softness that wasn’t invited, a kind of accidental gathering. Felted from friction, cotton, shirt, tissue shrapnel, thread, the secret cargo of trouser time. Pinching it out feels illicit, especially if someone sees. No dignity in rolling lint between fingers, but still you do it—press, flick, lose it to the corner or the wind.

Laundry leaves it behind, but it grows most in the gap-days, between cycles, while you walk and sit and kneel and move. It’s the memory of every surface you touched. A fragment of a napkin from a diner. Wool from a friend’s borrowed scarf. Something blue from no source you remember. Sometimes a hair, bent into the ball, proof you were somewhere with someone. Lint is a record, except it doesn’t keep the chronology—just the mass, the accumulation, a sediment of use.

Pockets feel emptier for lint. Or maybe just less complete—the burden of debris. Like time collecting in small corners, unnoticed until you reach in for coins. The lint is never the same twice, never pure, always composite. You can’t buy lint, only acquire it accidentally.

You could, in theory, collect it forever, create a ball, compress it over years. In the dryer, lint filters catch clouds and you peel them away in a single mat, the satisfaction is in one unbroken peel, blue-flecked, gray, sometimes almost purple. Touching that is like stroking something halfway between fabric and dust.

Lint on a suit is an emergency. On pajamas, a comfort.

A word like lint, carried in the mind’s pockets. Found by touch, rolled between thoughts, flicked away, forgotten, found again in some new lining. A sentence sheds lint, too, somewhere between saying and remembering.

Lint does not set off alarms. It doesn’t even enter the story. It’s what you find when you are looking for something else.
