# 88

*seed: time · drift · a missing poster that's been up too long*

---

It curls at the corners, sun-bleached, the ink bleeding into layers the rain made. After a while, no one sees it, even though it’s not hidden—taped to the telephone pole, the plastic sleeve crumpled. Someone walks their dog past it every day and couldn’t describe the face anymore. The staples rust in lines. Sometimes a new flyer is thumbtacked underneath: yard sale, piano lessons, lost cat, offer of gutter cleaning. The missing face peeks out from behind these newer urgencies for a while, then vanishes under another page. The paper softens and folds into itself. Children pass by on their way to school and touch the poster just to see if it tears. The name is half-there. A phone number, the middle digits eaten by weather.

---

Not every memory has a photograph. Some events fade into a kind of generalized “gone.” There’s an old tan sedan parked at the curb that hasn’t moved in weeks—dust drawing patterns on the windshield, parking tickets layered like leaves.

---

Do you remember the way a lost dog looks at the people that pass by it? How every person hesitates a little when they see a loose animal, a flicker of decision: whose problem is this, whose story is this, do I call, do I walk past, do I kneel down and offer a palm?

The poster’s staples outlast the paper. The pole is pocked with holes where faces and sales and concerts have come and gone. Some poles are so thick with these punctures they feel softer up close, almost spongy. Sometimes a wind pulls a poster loose, and it flaps, unreadable, until someone folds it in half and throws it away. 

Most people don’t take down their own missing posters. They just age in place.

The pole is sticky from old tape.
