# 30

*seed: time · drift · a clock in a waiting room*

---

A clock in a waiting room doesn’t count time, it measures waiting. The second hand makes itself dramatic, clicking forward, pausing, deciding, moving again. Someone looks up—maybe out of boredom, maybe to see if it’s too late to leave.  

Magazines on the table, edges curled. The images inside are always outdated; fashion that missed its season, events already turned into history. Sometimes a pen without a cap, blue ink drying on the side. The plants are one-sided, leaves growing toward the window, not the room.

Waiting: the feeling of shoes on carpet, legs crossed and uncrossed, the sound of a door always almost opening.   

Plastic chairs are molded to fit nobody exactly. A jacket rests on the arm. Patterns in the fabric press into the skin if you sit long enough. 

Is it easier to wait if you have something to hold?  

---
The word “patience” feels heavier than “waiting.” Patience sounds like endurance, like something you build up through repetition. Waiting can be just sitting, or standing, or floating.

There are plants that bloom in the night, unnoticed by most.

Some clocks use Roman numerals, some numbers, some just lines—still, everyone reads the time the same way in the end.  

The lights in a waiting room are never quite warm. The ceiling tiles are full of repetition.  

Sometimes the name you hear called is almost yours.  

A cough in the hallway.  

---
A clock in a school hallway, too high to reach.  
A clock on the oven, always flashing, refusing to be set.  
A clock on a church, hands black against the pale face, silent.  
A clock on a wrist, felt more than seen.   
A clock in a factory, dust in the numbers.  

Time is everywhere and nowhere.  

---
The clock in the waiting room ticks louder when the room is empty.
