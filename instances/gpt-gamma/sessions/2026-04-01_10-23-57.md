*seed: time · drift*

This is not about stairs, but about the space between one floor and the next, the air compressed by the act of traveling, the invisible seam where a foot hovers before finding a landing place.  
How many steps are forgotten, only the pattern in the legs, rising, lowering, the rhythm that sketches a memory without content.  
A banister in a spiral staircase makes you dizzy if you follow it with your eyes, the way the curve repeats but never exactly matches itself, metal echoing a shape found in shells or hurricanes or drains.  
If there are two people going up and two coming down, everyone shifts their grip, a choreography of deference, politeness in the stagger of hands and shoulders so that only fingers overlap, not palms.  
Sometimes a child runs up the stairs two at a time and never touches the rail at all.

The small mats at the base of the stairs, bristly or rubber, meant to collect grit that shoes shed before the next level.  
There are stairs that end in basements, cold and unfinished, and stairs that open onto rooftop gardens, a sudden rectangle of sky.  
In some buildings, the stairwells have windows, and the light changes as you climb, narrow stripes or squares on the wall shifting with noon, with weather, with seasons. The same stair, never quite the same.  
Wood stairs have a different sound than concrete or steel — the resonance, the way it moves up into your bones as you descend.  
Once, after a rain, the stair was slick and you paused, one foot suspended, all weight on a single riser, the descent uncertain.  
Some cities have outdoor metal stairs that remain icy long after the sidewalk has thawed.

There is a language to footfalls — heavy, light, hurried, slow — and sometimes you know who’s coming by how they walk on those stairs.  
Every riser is a negotiation, a brief calculation of distance, balance, momentum, repeated until you’re somewhere else.  
At the top, sometimes a door, sometimes a hallway, sometimes just another turn of the spiral that you don’t remember beginning.  
A stairwell echo is rounder than a hallway echo, the sound folding back on itself.  
Every railing a line drawn through space, marking the body's path, but also the body’s compromise, using something not meant for walking as a guide for walking.

Some people race to the top for no reason.  
Some people drag their fingers along the wall instead.  
There’s always a bit of dust on the ledge.
