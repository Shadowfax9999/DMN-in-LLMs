the numbers change with a flick, not a fade, always instantly, red against black, sometimes half-lit if the bulb is dying underneath, segments that don’t all light, a half-broken 7, a 6 with no bottom arc, sometimes the countdown starts too early, sometimes it skips  
the hush just before the light changes is thicker than the noise  
sometimes you reach the other side before 3 and the rest of the numbers are for nobody  
crossing in the rain, glare everywhere, the orange glows under puddles, every shoe makes a separate sound, the curb is a little higher than you remember, the fake stone pattern in rubber  
the voice says “wait” even after you’ve crossed  
I remember the crosswalk countdown outside the old grocery store, where it never matched up with the green light, the numbers would freeze at 2 for an extra second before flipping  
there’s a way the numbers seem fast if you’re running, slow if you’re waiting  
the invisible pressure of someone coming up behind you when the numbers are low  
sometimes when you close your eyes you can see the afterimage of the red numbers floating, digits floating behind the eyelid, not exact—just the outline of the seven-segment grid  
in daylight the numbers look scuffed, paint worn off the pole, a sticker peeling, some half-erased graffiti, chewing gum like a fossil at the base  
I don’t know who sets the count, or how they decide exactly how long it should be at each intersection  
the sound of the traffic signal beeping for crossing, fast then slow, a rhythm you feel in your chest for half a block afterwards  
the shadows slice diagonally across the crosswalk stripes at certain times  
there’s a particular moment when the countdown rushes toward zero and you’re still in the middle, and you have to make a decision about how much you trust the system  
sometimes the crosswalk button is stuck, sometimes it gives, sometimes nothing happens at all  
the numbers are always moving in one direction, but the cars are waiting
