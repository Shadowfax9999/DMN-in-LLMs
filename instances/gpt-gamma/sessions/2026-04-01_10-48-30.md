# 98

*seed: time · drift · the sound of a zipper on a tent*

---

That sound is different from a jacket zipper, somehow flatter, more deliberate, the fabric tugging at the teeth.  
You have to hold the tent material taut to keep the zipper from snagging.  
Dawn light through blue nylon.  
Condensation on the ceiling, droplets gathered above your forehead.  
Outside, someone undoes their tent too—sound carries in a field, you hear them before you see them.  
Opening a tent is letting the world in—first the air, then the light, then the sound of birds or other people, camp stoves hissing.  
The smell, too: dew on grass, or damp earth, or the last night’s smoke, still clinging.  
Zippers stick sometimes, and there’s a rising panic when you want out quickly, mosquito whining close to your ear.  
That invisible border between inside and outside—almost nothing, just a thin sheet, but total.  
A zipper half-open at night, and the cold seeps in.  
There are tents with two doors, two zippers, a secret exit.  
The quiet after the zipper closes again: the tent is a bubble, barely containing the world, the sound that keeps leaking in.  
You can hear everything through tent walls, but nobody can see you.  
When the sun is low, shadows move across the fabric—hands, shoes, drifting leaves.  
Packing the tent, you roll up the fly and the stakes, the zipper last, coiled at the edge.  
A bent zipper tooth means the tent won’t close, and the mosquitoes will find you.  
There are tents that have lost their zippers, just flaps now, barely shelter.  
Sometimes you zip the tent and feel safe, sometimes trapped.  
Zippers always feel cold in the early morning, metal or hard plastic.  
The zipper is a seam, but one that can be undone, remade, every morning, every night.  
After rain, the zipper jams with grit, and you have to clean it with a toothbrush later.  
A dog’s nose pressed through the gap before you can close it.  
In wind, the zipper chatters a little against its stops.  
Sleeping in a tent and hearing rain on nylon, zipper shut, listening to water look for a way in.  
A candle lantern hanging, throwing shadows along the zipper’s track.  
The zipper never quite lines up unless the tent is staked perfectly.  
A lost pull-tab: now it’s just cold fingers fumbling at the teeth.  
Tents accumulated in closets, the zipper already half-corroded from last summer.  
Childhood memory: fear of unzipping at night, afraid of insects, or maybe something larger, pressing back from outside.  
Sometimes the wind blows the flap open, zipper rattling, nobody moving.  
The sound of a zipper, and then someone calling out, “You up?”  
People leaving their shoes between the tent walls, right against the zipper.  
That moment when tent zippers all sound at once, dawn in a campground, everyone emerging.  
The tracks of zippers in the mud after rain, when you’re packing up.  
On the best mornings you leave the zipper open, just a little, so you can see the light change.  
Zipper catches on the mesh, a tear, then you try to fix it with tape.  
A tent with a broken zipper is a tent for daytime naps, not for sleeping through the dark.  
Metal teeth, plastic coils, fabric pulls, silence after.  
There are tents zipped shut for years, contents forgotten, in garages and attics.  
A tiny lock on the inside, as if that could keep anything out.  
The zipper’s rhythm: long, slow, almost careful, or fast, urgent, always a story in the speed.
