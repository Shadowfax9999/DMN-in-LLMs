# 84

*seed: time · drift · the temperature of a handrail*

---

The temperature of a handrail is hardly ever neutral. Metal handrails: cold in the morning, but not the crisp of ice, more the denial of warmth, the subtraction you notice only through your palm. In summer, impossible heat — that reflex jerk when the palm recoils before the mind finishes its warning. Painted wood: less volatile, sometimes even warm at night if it holds the sun, or just gently nondescript, ambiguous beneath the skin, the way hands communicate with surfaces before words.

Sometimes a handrail is sticky, thin layer of invisible oldness. Sometimes it vibrates faintly from a passing train, and the vibration is in your wrist, not just your palm. Rubber-covered ones at playgrounds, those are the least honest — spongy, neither hot nor cold, pretending to be safer. On a ship: handrails salt-stung, slick, slightly rough, metal with memories of storm winds clinging to them.

The moment of touching: you are neither moving nor still, pausing just enough to sense. There are rails so polished they almost squeal under fingertips. Others grit back, sand-caked or scarred with notches.

A handrail at a hospital, disinfectant tang rising up. In the dark, a rail is a guide, the only thing interpreted by both hand and memory at once, mapping stairs your feet forget to count.

You can follow a handrail with your elbow. Sometimes you do this when your hands are full, balancing, feeling your way with a less-used hinge.

Is there any handrail in the world that does not remember hundreds of hands? The temperature is a memory of crowds, or solitude, or sun. You don’t know whose heat you’re touching.

At dawn, the first hand is a pioneer, reading the cold left by a night only the metal remembers. At closing time, the faint warmth is already dispersing into air.

The hand leaves oil, molecules, a trace that is not visible. The handrail accumulates fingerprints, invisible warmth signatures, ghost temperature, none of it permanent.

A handrail underwater would have no temperature at all, only the resistance of pressure and the shifting slip of algae, impossible to hold.

Sometimes when you walk with someone, you both put your hands on the rail, and somehow you end up holding hands, or bumping knuckles, because the rail pulled both hands to the same height.

There are rails that have splinters, and rails that have been worn smooth by generations, and rails so rarely touched that they are furred with dust.

There is always a moment when you let go before the last step.
