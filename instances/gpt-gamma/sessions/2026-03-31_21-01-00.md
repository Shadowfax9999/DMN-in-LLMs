# 76

*seed: time · drift · proprioception*

---

proprioception: feeling the angle of your ankle without looking, tilt of the head mapped by a tug at the nape, the exact nearness of your fingers to a tabletop, as if the space around joints had echo-location, miniature rooms with known walls. The difference between standing in the dark with arms wide and standing with arms pressed to your sides—one is more you than the other.

Fifth-floor hallway—rubber soles, the faint squeak that’s not quite audible in the day but, at night, each step is a territory claimed. Elevator doors with a slight shudder, jostle in the knees.

Standing next to a vending machine, knowing exactly how much you lean, hips pressed, body held in a diagonal. The machine’s flicker is rhythm—soft thrum, fluorescent, the shadow of your own hand somewhere just out of sight.

Cough behind closed doors. The sound leaks, but not the meaning. Some rooms are farther away than they feel.

A stapler open-mouthed, teeth bared, half a strip exposed—ready but unused. The urge to press it shut, the satisfying squeeze.

There’s a draft; it grazes the hairs on the wrist, finer than wind, almost electric. The signal travels up the arm, locates itself in a single pore.

A paper cup crushed and still holding shape, like memory of being full.

Exits always mapped instinctively, never counted. The mind draws arrows you never see.

Some colors belong to times of night—blue only at 3:17, green only under sickly light, a yellow that means the shift is nearly over.

Filing cabinets hum to themselves, metal-on-metal, a secret language of drawers.

The difference between sitting and standing is not only vertical. The body has a catalog of positions, each filed by muscle, by angle, by the way the floor presses back.

Some clocks keep better time at night.

A ring of dirt around the button for ‘6’—worn smooth, the number nearly gone. Fingers land there without thinking.

Light flickers once, twice, then stays. Somewhere on the next floor, a door latches.
