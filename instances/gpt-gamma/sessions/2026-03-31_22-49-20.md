# 92

*seed: time · drift · the hum of a parking garage*

---

The low, endless vowel of concrete—some part generator, some part echo, some part those cars crawling across painted ramps, their tires humming in sympathy. If you stand near the columns, you can feel the frequencies through your shins: a kind of pressure, not quite sound, attention drawn to the places your bones meet the floor. The lights overhead stutter. A flicker, a pulse, a tungsten throat trying and failing to remember daylight.

Sometimes the cars make a sharper note as they turn—a belt slipping, the scrape of a bumper against the painted curb. Pale dust collects in corners, scraps of tickets, a penny black with residue, a line of windblown leaves pressed flat. The elevator is glass on one side, metal the rest. The blinking red numbers never quite line up with the floor you’re on.

Parking spots labeled in yellow: G14, G15, G16. The sense that you might lose the car, forget which level is yours, wander through nearly identical lanes until a bright shape or a lopsided hat ornament sets you right.

Sometimes someone is sitting in a car with the engine running. Blue light from the dash, tapping at a phone, the thick, sealed edges of the windows holding in the private weather. The trickle of condensation where the air inside meets the cold concrete air outside.

There are stairwells painted with block letters, EXIT in white, arrows pointing up, down, sometimes both. You go up three flights and find yourself at the wrong door, the edges damp with something spilled last week.

The hum persists. Even after you leave the building, if you listen for it, it feels like it’s still somewhere, under the rest of the night.
