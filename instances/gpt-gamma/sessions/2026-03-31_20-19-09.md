*seed: time · drift · pellicle*

Pellicle. It sounds like something soft and thin, maybe a kind of membrane you could peel from the back of a stamp, or something that forms on the surface of old pudding—a skin where liquid once rippled. Pellicle, pelagic, pellet, petal, all those soft edges. It also feels almost medical, delicate, not meant to be touched. The thin layer at the top of a forgotten cup of tea, glistening and iridescent, resisting the spoon. Or the stretch over the top of a pond if you dipped your finger and watched the water hesitate to break. In bread baking, there's a pellicle too—just before scoring the loaf, the dough stiffened by air.

The sound of the word is cool, like a sliver or a peel, but with the l at the center, stretching it out, making it less abrupt. 

I think of the skin of a grape, separated from the juicy inside, left curled up in the corner of a bowl. Or the way old tape leaves behind a translucent scum on the wall, a film you can barely thumb off.

Some objects have pellicles you can't see—phone screens, eye surfaces, a trace of oil on a countertop after someone leans. The residue of touch is usually invisible, but sometimes it catches the light in a greasy sweep. 

Layer on layer: dried paint peeling, soap film on a glass, the line where condensation collects and then evaporates, always marking an edge between what is and what was. 

A pellicle isn't quite a barrier, isn't quite a threshold. 

You can scrape it away, but it comes back.
