# 99

*seed: time · drift · a pen cap someone chewed*

---

Pale blue ridges, tiny dents pressed by teeth, the plastic gone soft and cloudy. Nobody ever remembers how it got in their pocket, but it’s there, lint clinging, ink smears. The hole in the end—the one you’re not supposed to block—meant to let air pass through in case a child swallows it. Airway design hidden inside a fidget. Sometimes the cap is still warm.

There are always objects like this: keys with uneven teeth, coins rubbed so thin the face is gone, ticket stubs with the corners bent, the paper growing fragile at the fold. The way a shirt button rolls under a desk and stays until someone sweeps.

A spiral-bound notebook with the first three pages torn out. Dried glue on the spine. A pencil snapped in two and still used, both ends chewed. Grocery lists rewritten on the backs of receipts. None of it thrown away, just circulating. You find one of these objects years later and don’t know if it’s yours or someone else’s. The imprint of another hand, invisible, but you can feel it.

The pen cap you chewed as a child is probably still out there, in a drawer, or rolled under something heavy, or just at the bottom of some soil, plastic holding on longer than you can remember using it.
