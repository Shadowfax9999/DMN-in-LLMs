# 59

*seed: time · drift*

---

Sometimes, if you touch the side of a glass where condensation gathered and dried, your finger leaves nothing at first but a feeling of coolness and a difference in slickness — but then, a few minutes later, you notice a smudge, as if your own oil needed time to emerge. The memory of a touch lagging behind the touch itself. 

Skin on glass, and then skin on skin — when you press a hand hard enough, you get a blanching, where all the capillaries are pushed away. Pull back and the blood floods in again, but the imprint remains for a few seconds, a ghostly red palm on a thigh or a wrist. Sometimes, when you wear socks with a tight elastic, the marks last until after lunch.

Plastic wrap sticking to itself, impossible to unpeel without stretching the surface out of shape. The film over pudding, spoon breaking through — it puckers and slides.

Tape that’s peeled off a window after a holiday, windows still showing faint ghosts of the letters that were once there in sparkling gold. You see outlines only when the light is coming at an angle, when everything else is dull.

The delicate shell of an air bubble in a spoonful of soup, catching the ceiling lights for a moment, rainbowed, then gone as soon as you tip the bowl.

Sometimes food comes with a skin you’re not expecting — the top of a cocoa left too long, or custard, or a dried bit of cream at the edge of a jug. You peel it away and the smell is different underneath. 

When you wake up and the bedsheet has left ridges all over your shoulder and cheek, red stripes that fade faster than you expect.

The layer of dust that settles on the inside lip of a lampshade, invisible until you reach to adjust the bulb and your finger carves a line through it.

Bubbles blown from a wand in summer, the skin so thin the color seems to swirl and settle at the bottom, like oil on water. Some burst instantly. Sometimes they linger in the air, drifting, holding together longer than you’d think possible.

Eggshell membrane, almost invisible, except when it clings to your thumb and you have to pick it away strand by strand. 

A layer you don’t notice until you break it.
