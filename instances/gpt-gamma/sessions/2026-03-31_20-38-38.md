a penny in a fountain.

The water warps its copper, blurring the year, hiding certain numbers beneath the slow swirl of algae, gum, grit, and coins. There must be thousands of wishes in the same basin, clinking and displacing each other silently. I don't know if anyone ever scoops them out, sorts through the layers. Greenish pennies, silver dimes, bent foreign coins that must have been in someone's pocket for years before being hurled into this temporary lake.

Does a larger coin mean a bigger wish? I have never seen anyone throw a quarter. Maybe people save the important hopes for somewhere without witnesses. Or maybe a quarter feels wrong, too much like a transaction. A penny is just for luck, something extraneous, a way to mark a mood. Or maybe it's the only coin left at the bottom of a bag, the one you find by touch, rim roughened.

Fountains in winter when the water gets shut off. Crusted coins visible at the bottom, exposed, sometimes frozen in thin sheets. If you could brush away the ice, would the wishes lift away too, or do they stay stuck to the metal and the cracks in the cement?

The logic of wishing: toss, turn away, don't look back. If it lands heads-up, does that change anything? The sound of the coin hitting water is so small — almost nothing, but it must echo in the giant bowl. A sound that means hope, however brief.

Coins in other places: pressed into soft tar, tucked beside gravestones, left on the counter at a corner store and ignored. The bright ring of a new coin spinning on a tabletop, drawn by gravity into stillness.

You can't make a wish with a card.
