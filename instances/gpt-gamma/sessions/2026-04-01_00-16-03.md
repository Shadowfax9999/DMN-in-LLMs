# 80

*seed: time · drift · crossing arms*

---

A crossing of arms can be protection, doubt, discomfort, cold, habit, focus, patience, refusal, waiting, impatience, or just a place to put hands when there’s nowhere for them. In some rooms, everyone does it and the tension knots in the crooks of elbows, a room of bodies folded in on themselves. Sometimes arms crossed feels like a barrier; other times it’s holding yourself, tucking in the stray edges, keeping the feeling close.

What if two people cross arms at exactly the same moment? Is it mirroring, or accident, or some social current that ripples through the group, invisible as a draft? In school assemblies, in auditoriums, all those bodies lined in chairs, arms folded, eyes forward, knees twitching — a symmetry of constraint.

A child crossing arms is defiance, or pout, or the boundary marked physically: I won’t, you can’t make me. At the bus stop, arms crossed for warmth, stillness, waiting. In the office, one person spills open, the other closed, the shapes of conversation shifting in the air above.

Sometimes the gesture goes unnoticed, an idle thing, muscle memory. Sometimes it’s too obvious, a signal: not now, stay back, or even just a pause before words arrive. Arms crossed, ankles crossed, eyes somewhere else.

Once someone crossed their arms and started to speak, and it sounded different, the voice tight, pinched, as if the chest were holding in more than just air.

Crossing arms as a way to wait for something: an answer, an apology, a door to open, a decision to be made somewhere else. The sound of fabric shifting, small friction.

At airports, midnight, people sleeping upright with arms hugged tight against their chests, inventing walls out of shoulders and sleeves.

There’s a way some people cross their arms and tuck their hands under their biceps, holding so tight it leaves red marks when the arms uncross. Others just drape loosely.

Crossing arms can also be a frame, a shape made in the middle of a sentence, punctuation without words.

Sometimes it’s just crossing arms. Sometimes it’s the only thing left.
