Vibrato is not a word I use much, but once I hear it, I can feel the sensation—the pitch quivering, the air moving around it, voice made into a ripple instead of a line. There are singers who use too much, like pouring water on sugar cubes. Other times, it’s only on the very last syllable, a permission to hesitate.

My mind tries to split vibrato from tremolo, but the difference always slides away. One bends pitch, one bends volume. In the dark you can only hear the difference if you lean in close, closer than you would in a room with lights on.

There’s a violinist practicing somewhere, and every time the note wobbles it feels alive, like something under the surface remembering to breathe. When I try to hum with vibrato, the muscles in my throat tense, but the effect is small, barely a shimmer.

In group singing, everyone’s vibrato cancels out, blending into a flat cloud of sound, no one allowed to shake too much. Soloists are allowed to break the surface.

The argument above has stopped; now it’s quiet except for the humming from the building’s pipes. There’s a quality to the silence, as if someone is waiting to start again. 

Some people walk as if sound follows them, and some try not to leave any trace, not even a print or an echo. The sneaker mark will fade as soon as someone passes.

A mailbox key turns just enough to let the latch slip.
