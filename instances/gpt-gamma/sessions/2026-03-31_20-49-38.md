*seed: time · drift · feedback loop (process)*

Feedback loop, but not in the textbook sense—more like: a door closes too hard, so someone puts a rubber wedge under it, and the next person starts leaving it open entirely. The sound of the wedge scraping on tile, repeated, becoming its own little signal. The wedge goes missing; the door slams again. Someone tapes a note up, but the tape peels, curls, leaves a darker rectangle behind.

A feedback loop of conversation, circling a topic that’s never quite resolved—talk about rain turning to drainage, then to gutters, then back to the weather, each person nudging the conversation a degree closer or farther from what they actually want to say.

Elevator buttons: when the close-door doesn’t work, people press it anyway, faster each time, as if speed might override the mechanism. An accumulated ritual, friction between intent and result. The impatience feeding the futility, the futility feeding the impatience.

Sometimes the loop is entirely internal—notice a sound, try to ignore it, but attention keeps returning, each suppression drawing it sharper into focus. The more you try not to turn your head, the more the head wants to swivel. It’s not a circle, it’s a spiral, tightening or unwinding, impossible to pause right at the middle.

Groups make loops that nobody names: one person laughs, someone else echoes it, laughter bouncing and flattening until nobody remembers the joke, only the sound.

A system wants to settle, or oscillate. Some loops stabilize, others amplify. Arguments that escalate, feedback howls in microphones, the flutter of a fan blade finding just the wrong resonance.

The lock on the side door was changed, but people still push where the old latch used to be, subtle divot worn under a sign that no longer applies.

The habit forms, loops on itself, becomes almost invisible.
