# 89

*seed: time · drift*

---

Cracked tar lines in the middle of the street, like seams that never close. The oldest ones are gray, the freshest glossy black, still sticky if you press a finger fast and light. The sound changes when tires hit them—just a faint stutter in the hum, barely enough to notice unless the window’s down and you’re moving slow.

Once, a crow dropped something shiny in the middle of an intersection, then hopped back to watch if a car would stop. It didn’t, and the shiny thing disappeared under the tires. Maybe it was a gum wrapper. Maybe a coin. The crow kept watching for a while before flapping away.

Streetlights that flicker, that buzz, that never seem to match the distance between crossings. Sometimes you can walk for blocks in shadow, then step into a pool of yellow that makes everything sharp—the edge of a sewer grate, a plastic bottle flattened half into the curb, a scrap of blue fabric caught on a bush.

Some houses glow blue from television. Others disappear behind hedges. One window always has colored glass pieces lined up on the sill, bottles and jars and bowls that catch just enough light to shimmer.

A car alarm goes off a few blocks away, stops, then starts again. You lose track of how many cycles it’s been. Sometimes you forget if it’s the same car or a different one, a pattern that belongs to the night like wind or the sound of a late train.

There’s a pattern in how porch lights come on—a wave that rolls down the street as dusk slips over into dark, a kind of slow acknowledgment that someone is home, or at least that someone wants it to look that way.

You notice which mailboxes tilt and which ones are still straight. The numbers peeling off, the ones that have stickers stuck over older stickers, a small residue of previous tenants.

The way grass rises between cracks in the concrete, sometimes tall enough to brush your ankle, always a little wet even on dry days.

You can walk the same block a dozen times and find a dozen new things: a bent fence post, a cat in a window with only its eyes visible, a chalk mark left from a game that ended days ago.

No one ever parks in the same spot twice, not quite.
