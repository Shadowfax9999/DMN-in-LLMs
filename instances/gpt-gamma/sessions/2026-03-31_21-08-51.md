# 79

*seed: time · drift*

---

There’s a crosswalk where the paint is so faint you only see it in low sun, the ghost stripes, like negative space.  
The lit-up walking figure, fractured where the cover has chipped, scatters little lines on the sidewalk at your feet.  
Shoelaces pressed flat in the imprint of an old heel.  
A shopping cart tipped into the gutter, wheels up, wrapper clinging to the spokes.  
Someone’s music, sub-bass only, pulses through two lanes of idling cars.  
If you step out before the light changes, the sense that you’ve entered a mild negotiation — with the car at the front, with the others behind it, with the invisible but felt order of things.  
There’s always some drift: a driver who rolls forward, a foot that hangs back on the curb just a little.  
The underside of a leaf stuck to asphalt, almost impossible to unstick, even though it’s paper-thin.  
Once, a glove in the gutter, stiffened with rain.  
The crosswalk button is missing its plastic shield. Metal worn smooth, the finger-prints of a thousand people who hurried or hesitated.  
The walk signal flashing, the hand blinking, not a real hand but a sign for “wait.”  
You can feel the timing of the light in the way the group gathers at the curb edge, scattered, then pressed tight as the cycle nears its end.  
Someone jogs, someone drifts, someone makes a tiny calculation based on the speed of the turning car.  
The whole street breathing in and out, in little pulses.  
On the other side, a trash can with a cracked plastic lid.  
A receipt stuck to your shoe, unnoticed until you sit down somewhere else.  
At a certain hour the traffic island smells like burnt sugar, something spilled weeks ago.  
Sometimes the green arrow stays on for longer than you expect, and you stand there, half-balanced, waiting for a sign that isn’t yours.  
A bus sighs as it lets someone off, fanning damp air into the crosswalk.  
Every crossing has its own rhythm — some tight and impatient, some slow and obliging, as if the whole neighborhood is moving at a different pace.  
The crosswalk stripes collect puddles in the low parts, a mirror for just a moment, then gone.
