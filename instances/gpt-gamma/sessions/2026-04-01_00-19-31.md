# 82

*seed: time · drift · the way a street looks different when it's wet*

---

Wet light, street dark. Headlights stutter, spread wide, then dizzy into the crossings. The white lines are whiter, the black darker, edges gone. A manhole cover glints, harsher than the rest — like it’s newer, but it isn’t. Gum spots on the sidewalk go pale and soft, blooming through water, chewed moons. The sound of tires is rounder, a hush, not so much a squeal as a brush. The air swings up something metallic, almost electric, before it settles back to damp stone. The puddles make a geography, shallow countries, the world’s lowest places all mapped for one evening. Streetlights hang doubled, orange pools, gold for no reason.

Somewhere on the block, a storm drain gulps and gurgles, as if the water is alive or hungry.  
People step around certain puddles but through others.  
The trees are heavy, sharper, loose strands of leaves stuck to the curb.  
You can see the footprints on the crosswalk for a minute after.  
Shop windows echo streetlights, neon drift mixing with reflections, layering sign over sign, as if the store is selling its own light.

Umbrellas open: blossom, pause, shut.  
Face under a hood, cheeks wet, head down, moving quick.  
The world feels simultaneously closer and less visible, boxed in by drops on glass and the blur.  
Water chases itself down a windshield, finds paths, draws lines, erases.  
Somewhere a bus grows louder and then softer, left behind in fog.

If you look at a puddle straight on it holds the sky just for a second.  
Later there’s the smell: not rain, but what the rain leaves behind, concrete, earth, the faint ghost of worms.  
People look up and down in the wet more than when it’s dry.

Sometimes wet pavement looks like oil, shifting colors, as if the surface has its own secret language.  
Wheels leave tracks that linger, then fade.  
Trash in the gutter goes soft, edges loosening, ink blurring to nothing.  
Shoe soles squeak against marble in an entryway; socks wet through.  
Even the city lights seem uncertain in the water.  
The taxi idles, fume curling into rain, brake lights flashing red on every slick surface.  
Steam rises from a grate, mixing with rain as if the street is exhaling.  
Somewhere, windows fog.  
The lamp above the door vibrates.  
A plastic bag flattens against the curb, stuck by the water.  
The street is wet and it doesn’t remember how it looked dry.  
A stop sign holds a droplet until it falls.  
There are streetlights farther away but they don’t look real.  
Someone walks past, shoes clapping, umbrella high and face hidden.  
A dog shakes its fur at the leash and the spray arcs in the air.  
The air tastes colder when you breathe in near the ground.  
There are reflections in the black of a car door, bent and running down in streaks.  
The street is wet and you can’t decide if the world is deeper or just thinner.
