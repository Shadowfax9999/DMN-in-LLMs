# 91

*seed: time · drift · antifreeze*

---

Antifreeze, green and viscous, not a natural color for a liquid—neon in its defiance of water’s clarity. Cooling and not freezing, a contradiction, a thing designed to prevent stasis by refusing ice—yet it sits, sometimes untouched, in enormous drums in garages, in sheds, in places where cold creeps under the door. The word itself is a safeguard, a guarantee that nothing stops moving, that water does not seize up in winter’s grip. Antifreeze is a guardian against winter, but also a poison in disguise, sweet-tasting to animals by accident, fatal in the smallest dose. The color is a warning, a compromise between utility and danger: don’t drink this, don’t touch this, keep it sealed. Yet the first instinct is to wonder who decided on green—why not blue, or red, or transparent. There are pinks, too, and oranges, depending on the car, the country, the chemical blend, the invisible system of codes under the hood.

There is something about the presence of antifreeze in a house, on a shelf, under the sink, that suggests a climate—regions where pipes can burst, where cars must adapt to the season, where forgetting is a risk. Lists on the side of the bottle: do not ingest, keep away from children, flush with water in case of contact—a litany of hazards in tiny print. Instructions for mixing ratios: one part antifreeze to one part water, or maybe something else entirely, depending on the make and model. The precise measurement becomes a threshold between running and frozen, between flow and rupture.

Sometimes the bottle sits, unused, for years, growing sticky at the cap, the plastic bottle sagging as the chemicals inside evaporate or shift. You find one at the back of the garage, half full, uncertain whether it’s still “good,” whether chemicals go stale. Time accumulates in the form of dust and spiderwebs around the neck.

The taste is not for you, but the myth persists—sweet and deadly, a paradox, like the idea of a thing that preserves motion by embodying a hidden threat. The word “antifreeze” is almost comical, given its lethal potential. 

Imagining the workshop where it’s poured into bottles, the conveyor belt, the caps being screwed down, the labels applied, batch numbers stamped, instructions translated into half a dozen languages. Or the first time it was mixed, in a beaker, someone wondering: what if water didn’t freeze at zero.

The idea that you can interrupt a process as basic as freezing—that you could cheat winter, slide past the laws of matter for a season, that maintenance is a form of disobedience. 

Antifreeze isn’t an end, it’s a condition—something in the middle, a state neither truly fluid nor truly safe, a substance meant to keep other substances from changing state. 

The bottle is never quite empty, always a residue at the bottom, pooling neon, refusing to evaporate. 

There are neighborhoods where the word “winterize” means nothing, and there are places where it’s a yearly ritual, a checkmark on a list, a bottle retrieved from storage, poured, measured, replaced, forgotten again.

A person standing in the cold, watching the liquid swirl as they pour it—hoping for another season of things not breaking.
