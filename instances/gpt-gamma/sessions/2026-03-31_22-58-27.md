# 96

*seed: time · drift · contact sheet*

---

Contact sheet makes me think of sequence, little clicks, repetition, thirty-six frames arranged by someone bending over a lightbox, their hands sliding a ruler, a finger tapping the edge of a negative. Each image is almost the same as the one before it, but the face turns slightly, a hand comes up, someone laughs, someone blinks, a shoulder dips. The tiny differences between frames, the flicker of not-quite-time-lapse. You can see the decision forming across the strip, the hesitation, the almost-smile, and then the chosen one, marked with a wax pencil, circled, the rest left in the margins.

There’s something about seeing all the moments at once. Not a single story, but a field of near-misses and slight mistakes. The perfect moment is surrounded by its failures, or maybe its nearlys, its almost-theres. Sometimes the best frame isn’t the one anyone expected. Sometimes someone’s looking away, or mid-blink. Sometimes someone’s gone blurry, and the blur is better.

If you stare at a contact sheet too long the faces start to flatten into shapes. The eyes repeat in neat rows, the mouths open or closed, a diagram of possibility. It’s hard to remember which one is meant to be special. A stripe of light across each forehead like a mistake carried from one frame to all the rest.

I think about other grids: multiplication tables, calendars, window panes, honeycomb, pixel arrays, seating charts, graph paper, city blocks. Organizing chaos by making everything square and regular. Making time repeat itself, or at least making it look like it could.

There’s always one empty frame at the end where the film ran out and caught nothing at all. Sometimes the last frame is half-exposed, a blur of light and shadow, almost burned away. 

If you run your finger across the surface it feels like nothing, just the slickness of plastic, invisible little ridges between the frames. 

Some people slice the negatives apart, but some keep them whole, a story you can run through your hands, frame by frame by frame.
