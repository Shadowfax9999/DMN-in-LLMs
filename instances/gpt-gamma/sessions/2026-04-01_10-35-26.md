*seed: time · drift · colophon*

---

colophon—at the back, the little note, technical, precise, proud of its fonts, its paper, sometimes a printer’s name, a year, a city you’ve never been to. The book’s signature, almost. Not the author’s—the builder’s. Every colophon is a mini-autobiography of the material, a résumé of itself, or just an apology for misprints, a clarification, a whisper: “Here’s how I was made. Here’s what I’m composed of, even if you only care for the words.” 

The thin line between information and ornament. Garnish at the end of a meal you’ve already finished. Do you ever check which font you’ve just consumed? Palatino, Garamond, Jenson, names that feel like family names, or cities. Garamond especially—an old friend in the margins. Sometimes an emblem, a publisher’s brand, a snail, a torch, a lyre, sometimes just words. As if the book wants you to notice you’re holding it. 

Other places where the mechanics peek through: the “about this app” section, the copyright statement, the “made on a Mac” back when that mattered, the etched logo under a laptop. The source code comments you only see if you dig. 

What’s the online equivalent of a colophon? Not the “about” page, not quite. Maybe the hover-text, the title attribute, the html comment: <!-- placeholder --> 

A footnote to the whole. 

Sometimes you find yourself reading an ancient book, and the colophon is in Latin, or Greek, or a language you can almost parse but not quite. A list of names, then a flourish, “excudit” or “fecit” or “impressit,” and you’re reminded this is an object, not just a text. Printed in Venice, 1542. A shape in time. 

How often do people write colophons about themselves, at the end of long periods—“these are my materials, these are the conditions of my making, these are the tools I used”? 

Maybe every résumé is a kind of colophon—typeset, formatted, a record of provenance, with a bit of flair if you have it, a watermark, a motto, a font you think says something about you. 

There’s a version of this for houses, too—plaques, inscriptions, “built by…,” “restored in…,” “this garden created in memory of…,” a little documentary sentence embedded in stone or wood. 

There’s a comfort in the record: an object announces, “Here’s how I came to be. Here’s what I’m made from. Someone bothered to record it.” 

But sometimes it’s just a single word, forgotten at the back, or a flourish, a leaf, a ghost. 

Colophon, colon, collapse, collate. The sound of “co-” at the front—togetherness, a joining of pieces—then the “-phon,” a sound, a voice, or perhaps just a trace, an echo, a sign-off. 

The ending isn’t really an ending, but a note on the process, a kind of afterlife.
