# 58

*seed: time · drift*

---

A scuff on a shoe is a pellicle too, almost—just pigment rearranged, a darkening where the upper grain has been rubbed, micro-thin. I used to pick at the labels on plastic bottles, peeling back the edge with a fingernail, turning it into a fringe of translucent threads. Ice on car windshields in early March: not a solid sheet but a crinkled membrane, so thin you can poke through, so brittle it shatters before the wipers can move it. Sometimes you find a layer you didn’t know was there—a sticker under a sticker, a doubled sheet of bakery parchment. Some files on old computers have “hidden” attributes, another shade of pellicle, digital: you can sense the presence but not the substance, not unless you go looking with the right tool.

I remember biting into a plum and the skin slipping away from the flesh, the whole mouth experience changing because the skin stayed resilient, holding, while the inside gave way. A pellicle preserves for a moment, but always yields with enough insistence. 

The soap-scum in the shower gathers where water slows down, a map of vanished swirls. On city windows, a film of dust and exhaust—more visible just after rain, when the wipers leave arcs of clarity and the residue forms borders in the glass. 

Maybe breathing on something—fogging up glass, the mark of a palm—makes its own temporary pellicle. Charged, gone. 

Certain cheeses develop a rind: a cultivated, edible pellicle, sometimes downy, sometimes leathery. The surface tells the history—how long, how humid, who touched it, what kind of mold settled first.

I have no sense of how many pellicles are around at any given moment, only that light makes some of them visible and touch erases or alters them. 

The paint at the edge of a jar dries first, curls down and peels if you nudge it. There's usually a border of pigment that's thicker, the brush pulling extra color to the rim with every stroke, then drying, then flaking.

On a pond in summer, pollen settles in a thin, shimmering coat, broken only where a bug skates or a leaf falls, the disturbed surface closing behind. 

I’m thinking about fingerprints, too, but not the patterns—just the faint glimmer left on stainless steel or a phone. 

Sometimes you forget a pellicle is there until it shifts and changes the way the thing underneath shows itself.
