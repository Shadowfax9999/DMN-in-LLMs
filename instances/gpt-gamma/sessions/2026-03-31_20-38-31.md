*seed: time · drift · a towel that's been wet too long*

---

A towel that's been wet too long — stiff and heavy, the patch near the middle cold even when the air is warm. Sometimes it smells bitter, almost metallic, as if it’s started to remember something from outside the house. The threads clump together in uneven ridges, and if you fold it, the crease holds its shape for a second before slumping. No one wants to use it, but it stays on the hook until someone says something or can't stand it anymore.

The line of residual water creeping up the wall where the towel hangs, faint but visible if you stare. The grout between tiles catches the edge of the damp and turns darker. In the corner, a bottle of cleaner with one edge peeled up from the label.

If you drape the towel over the radiator, it flattens in odd ripples, the dampness fleeing in streaks. Sometimes you forget it’s there and the heat bakes in a new smell.

Other kinds of damp that linger: the rag in the bottom of the mop bucket, the wool hat left in a gym bag, the inside of a plastic raincoat folded before it’s dry. Socks that never quite lose the memory of puddle-water.

Clotheslines seen from windows: shirts turned inside out, jeans with one leg twisting away from the other, a red pillowcase with a tear at the seam.

The sound a thick towel makes when you shake it out is duller than the sound of a sheet. It lands with more weight on the line, and the cloth pins have to bite deeper.

The towel that's been wet too long is always heavier than it should be.
