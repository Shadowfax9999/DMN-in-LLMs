# 47

*seed: time · drift*

---

A single glove at the curb, palm up, moss-green. Too thin for winter, too thick for summer. If it sat there long enough would someone pick it up, or would it flatten and pale, its owner’s hand already shaped differently by another pair? Mismatched things linger in corners—one earring, a sock at the back of the drawer, the charger that went to something no one can name anymore. 

The shape of your hand after carrying a bucket heavy with water, that groove pressed into the palm for half a day. Knuckles that click now, maybe from typing, maybe from years of pushing open doors with your fist instead of the handle. There is a way the wrist aches after holding a hardback book at an odd angle for too long.

The burnt edge on the bread, where a thumb pressed too close to the toaster. Someone on the bus flipping through pages, lips moving in silent rehearsal. In the museum, a velvet rope marks the edge: DO NOT TOUCH. But the imprints are still there, fingertips pressed into marble from years ago, before the signs.

---

Some spoons are too shallow for soup and too deep for coffee. Pillows that keep their shape even after you leave. Waiting rooms with the same magazines every season, their corners getting softer. The light by the crosswalk blinks three times slower on Mondays. A dog circling exactly three times before lying down.

Onions sweating on a plastic cutting board that warps slightly in hot water. The groove in the doorframe from a bicycle pedal bumping it every morning. The gap where the tile never quite fit.

---

How many pocket lighters are lost on picnic tables, gathered under benches, their fuel evaporating until they click but give nothing?
