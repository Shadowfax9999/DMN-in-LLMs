# 44

*seed: time · drift · grate*

---

Grate. Not great. The t and the e switch places—do they ever get confused? A kitchen grate, a fireplace grate, a metal grid with light coming up from underneath. Sewer grates swallow rings, pennies, keys. Someone’s shoe can catch on a grate. There’s a sound the metal makes when you step on it, echoing underneath, nothing solid, just air and dark space.

Wind snakes up through sidewalk grates in winter—sometimes hot air, unexpected, makes your ankles prickle. There’s always something under, always a below.

Grate also: to rub, to wear down, to shred. Cheese against the teeth of the grater, skin against the rough edge, grated carrot wet, orange, squeaks. The word begins to dissolve—grate, great, greet, gait. 

Gait: how a person walks.

Great: tall, impressive, “great job.” Grate: never complimentary.

A voice can grate on you. The sound of a knife against a plate. The feeling of chalk on teeth, or wool against skin.

Grate and grid. Both crossed lines, lattices. The view through a mesh—something partial, interrupted.

---

Aunt Pat used to keep a metal soap holder by the sink, the bottom corroded through. Soap slivers collecting in the corner, edges sharp, then soft, then gone.

Plastic colanders and the smell of boiled pasta water. The way steam sticks to the sides and runs down in little channels.

Egg slicer: not a grate, but similar—wires, tension, press down through white and yellow, slices fan apart.

---

I haven’t thought about escalator teeth in a long time. The metal comb at the bottom where the moving steps disappear, always a warning sticker, shoes away from the edge. The sound when someone drops a coin and it rattles, vanishes.

Sometimes when you look down through the grate, you can’t see the bottom. Sometimes there’s water, or nothing at all.
