# 86

*seed: time · drift · speakerphone in public*

---

A voice in the open, louder than conversation, flattened by distance, made for a room but poured into the air between strangers. The artificial clarity of a speakerphone, where every 's' hisses sharp, every breath amplified, so that the talk floats and slices, unclaimed.

Sometimes a tangle of words you can't quite assemble — "I'll be there in five—" "No, he said—" "Well, that's not—" — sentences cut by the world, not meant for you, but received.

People look up, or don't. The urge to glance, to mark the owner of the voice, but also to ignore, to pretend not to hear the logistics of someone’s coming appointment, the apology, the "can you pick up milk."

Shared space, but the conversation is not shared. Everything said on speakerphone feels like it hovers, wider than a mouth, belonging more to space than to person.

Sometimes laughter, abrupt and loud, the sound of an in-joke delivered to a thousand passing witnesses who will never be in on it. Sometimes anger, compressed and thin, the brief embarrassment of witnessing a stranger's argument.

The phone held out, never pressed to an ear, a gesture of opening but also of distance, as if the voice and the body are only loosely attached.

Children seem more at ease with it, shouting and wandering, the device moving through air, a parent saying "say hi" and a child's voice answering an invisible someone.

In trains, the sound is worse, bouncing off hard seats and windows, a private world leaking through the seams.

Sometimes the call ends and the world hushes for a second, a bubble burst, a return to footsteps and engine hum. The person checks the screen, pockets the phone, and for a moment the space feels private again, even if it never really was.
