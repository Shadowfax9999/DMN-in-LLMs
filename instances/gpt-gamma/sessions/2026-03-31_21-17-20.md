# 83

*seed: time · drift · the particular rattle of a medicine bottle*

---

and the way the pills cluster inside, never quite in the center, always tumbling toward the corner where the plastic curves in. Childproof cap, an architecture meant for delay. A hand that shakes it just to feel the rattle, the counting by sound.

Sometimes the sound matches a memory—grandmother’s room, or something more recent—how medicine in the bottle is both promise and nuisance. The little paper slip folded under the cap, absorbing nothing, taking up no space, a placeholder.

Sideways in the cabinet, bottles stacked, some labels peeling from moisture. Dosage, expiration, instructions in small print. The warning about drowsiness, or sunlight, or grapefruit. What happens if you ignore the grapefruit warning? Maybe nothing, maybe something unnoticeable, a change in how the body handles the rest.

The bottle is round but doesn’t roll; it catches on the counter’s grout lines. Inventory of types: tan orange for prescription, clear for vitamins, small blue for cough. The fake sound of candy imitating pills, or pills imitating candy.

What it is to keep something “on hand,” as if the hand is a shelf.

Even empty, the bottle remembers what it was holding. Static inside, the clinging of the last bit of powder. The label meant to be scraped off, but the glue stays, a dull spot even after washing.

There are people who save the bottles for sorting nails, beads, screws, seeds. A second life made possible by stubborn plastic.

Somewhere a machine fills a hundred bottles at once, counting out doses, the rhythm faster than any hand could manage. Sometimes one more, sometimes one fewer, nobody knows until the rattle sounds wrong.

The bottle never opens easily, even when you know the trick. Push and turn, the motion unfamiliar, the hand awkward. The sound when it opens: a sigh, a snap, the triumph of small force.
