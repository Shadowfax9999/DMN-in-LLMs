# 97

*seed: time · drift*

---

I think of the feeling of a sequence breaking. How you’re walking a set of stairs in the dark, and suddenly a step is missing, or there’s an extra one. The jolt travels through your whole body, not because you tripped, but because your expectation fell into a gap. All the tidy rectangles undone by one space that shouldn’t be there.

Sometimes the grid is only visible if you look from far away, like in crop fields or the rooftops of a city seen from an airplane. Up close, it’s only confusion and repetition, a patch of weeds, a cracked shingle, laundry dangling off a line. The order radiates outward and disappears the closer you try to see.

Boxes for everything—dates, names, numbers, checkmarks. Places to declare yes and no. The calm of a page that’s been filled in, every row accounted for, the small anxiety of the one square left blank. Sometimes the grid is just a comfort, a way to pretend nothing will spill over.

Pixel grids don’t show their seams until you zoom in too far—then everything is jagged and unnatural, colors stepping down in blocks, the diagonal line becoming a flight of stairs.

Multiplication tables folding back on themselves—there’s symmetry along the diagonal, a kind of mirror, seven times eight and eight times seven, the answer always the same but the order reversed.

The grid on window panes is there to keep things in or out, but mostly it’s to divide space into manageable views. You look through a square at a single patch of sky.

If you overlay enough grids, eventually the intersections become so dense there’s nowhere left to stand. The lines erase the world they’re meant to organize.

Sometimes the only way to break out is to color outside the boxes, to let the pattern stutter and fail for a second, a missing step in the dark, a note off the scale, a window left open just enough for the wind to get in.
