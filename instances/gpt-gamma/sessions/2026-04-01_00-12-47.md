Aquifer.  
Not a word for water, but for the spaces between.  
The dry lattice that houses what moves, the slow percolation, the silent drift downward, gravity finding gaps.  
No one sees an aquifer, they see only the suggestion: a pump that doesn’t run dry, the green patch that never browns, an old well the neighbors say is bottomless.  
No one trusts a bottomless thing.   
Maps draw blue veins under the land, cross-hatch for sand, stipple for gravel, a cartoon of something real but secret.  
Sometimes the aquifer collapses, the roof gives way, the ground becomes a bowl, a sinkhole that wasn’t there yesterday.  
If you listen, the water underfoot is moving, but it never sounds like water.  
There are laws about how much can be drawn, but the water doesn’t care.  
A drop pulled here is a ripple elsewhere, far away, under fences, under towns, under a silo or a playground, under a parking lot, under night or noon.  
Does the water remember the pressure, the darkness, the minerals it borrowed from each stone?  
If you fall through, do you enter water, or the memory of water?  
Aquifer: what sustains invisibly, what is always nearly gone, what moves without being seen.  

There’s a feeling, sometimes, that breath sits on top of something deeper —  
like words float on unsounded intention, speech fed by something lower, older, not spoken but soaked through layers, filtered, slow.  
The silence between hands in a game, the moment between call and reveal, the hush of a throat before it clears — all of it riding on an unspoken reservoir.  

Not every spring is visible.  
Some are just places the grass grows greener for reasons nobody can name.  
Some are just silence that feels full.  
A word arrives from nowhere: register
