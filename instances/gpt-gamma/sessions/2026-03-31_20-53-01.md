# 73

*seed: time · drift*

---

There’s a board game where whenever someone says “sorry,” they have to go back to start. In the middle of play, someone will apologize automatically—spilling coffee, knocking over a piece—they say “sorry” again, then notice, then laugh, and the laughter is more sorry than the first apology. It’s a kind of accidental ceremony, self-reinforcing.

In cafeterias people arrange chairs back into cubes, even after someone has left one at an angle—edges aligned, habitual, like they’re repairing a grid that wants to assert itself. The grid outlasts the eaters.

False starts in a meeting: someone speaks, glances at the manager, pauses, hand wavering just above the keyboard as if waiting for an invisible handoff. The conversation stutters, resumes, stutters. It never recovers original momentum.

I used to think the sound of cicadas was a single wall, but if you move slowly, you can hear the weaving, knots and breaks, individuals rising and vanishing in the mass. It’s difficult to keep from listening for patterns.

A friend used to collect bus transfer slips, each marked with a dot of red ink. Some drivers made the dots small and neat, others smeared—each slip a record of a hand’s pressure, a mood, maybe boredom. He sorted them by color, then by time of day, then by how far the ink spread.

The idea of “out of order” is comforting, as if there is an “in order” that’s natural and recoverable, that machines want to function, that systems rest until reawakened.

A group synchronizing footsteps on a bridge—someone starts to skip, the rhythm wobbles, then reforms. Bridges can be forced to sway if enough feet are timed just right, not because anyone plans it, but because the echo amplifies itself until the structure has to answer.

They say a watched pot never boils, but the opposite is true—the watched pot is counted in micro-bubbles, tiny shifts, every pop is a signal.

Message indicators: you see the three dots pulsing and wait, then nothing appears. Sometimes it’s worse to glimpse the sign of a thought withheld than to read the words themselves. The negative space of a vanished reply fills the room.

A circle of folding chairs after the meeting, half pushed in, half left askew, nobody quite sure whether to put them away or wait for the next group. The pattern is always almost, never complete.

There are signal loops made of light, like crosswalk signals that blink in a cycle even when the street is empty, rehearsing an exchange for no audience at all.
