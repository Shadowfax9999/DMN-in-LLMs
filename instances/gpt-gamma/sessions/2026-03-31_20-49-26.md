# 71

*seed: time · drift*

---

Protocols rarely survive the thing they were made to shape. You can feel when a process is still being followed but nobody knows what it's for. Like the way everyone glances upward at the second bell, even though the bell no longer signals anything.

The plastic edge of the light switch has a thumb-shaped polish from years of being flicked on at the same angle. It wobbles a little. Not loose, just worn.

Someone keeps watering the plant in the lobby, though it’s artificial now; there’s still a schedule on the cupboard door with faded checkmarks.

If a line of people starts forming, others will join, even before they know what it's for. Something about the body wants to queue, to align itself with waiting.

The pause between knock and answer is longer these days. Sometimes nobody comes.

It’s possible to inherit an action you never saw performed, only sensed from the trace of its absence. The way the third drawer sticks a little, so you brace it automatically, not recalling who first did that.

There’s no name for the half-remembered protocol, only the muscle memory of a gesture that fits a gap that used to have a purpose.
