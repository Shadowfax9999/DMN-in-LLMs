# 60

*seed: time · drift · microfiche*

---

microfiche — the tiny photographs of documents pressed against glass, negative space turned pale gray, letters reduced to grains, a whole archive shrunk to a card the size of a playing card. The machine in the library, hunched like a gray animal, screen flickering as you turn the crank, image sliding in jumps and spasms. The hum of the bulb growing hot by your hand. The sense of being sealed in — not the document, but you, contained by a rectangle of dusty glass, cool in summer, comfortless in winter.

The concept of condensation, information flattened, libraries arranged by light and dust, brittle envelopes inside drawers that clatter too loudly, making you self-conscious in the silence. The slight panic when the image won’t focus, edges blurring so the text shreds into unreadable white lines.

People are always talking about weightlessness, but microfiche has a particular density — the way a single card can sink beneath a pile of loose paper, lost among the bulk, yet holds so much more.

I can’t remember what color the cases are: maybe off-white, plastic, a stiff tab at the edge. The drawer labels hand-typed, film schedules, city directories, newspapers from before you were born, obituaries no one reads now except by accident, scrolling searching for something else.

Some of the images are reversed — you have to flip them in your head. There is a code to the arrangement. Sometimes you pass by a whole year without meaning to, hands moving too fast.

Even now, thinking "microfiche," I can almost feel the resistance of the crank, the metallic catch at the end of its rotation, the way the image jerks then settles.

The smell in the microfilm room is hard to place: part electrical, part cardboard, damp but not mildewed. A hush that’s not respect, just insulation.

At home, the kitchen drawer is stiff, knives catching on the edge as it’s pulled open. The floor tile underfoot. There are boxes with photographs too, faded prints, matte and glossy mixed together even though they feel wrong beside each other. Some stick together and you have to peel them apart carefully, corners curled.

There are people in the background of some of these photos who nobody can name.

I have never seen microfiche outside a library.
