# 29

*seed: time · drift*

---

“Pith” is a word I don’t use often, but it feels dry and soft at the same time, almost like a small segment of air trapped in cotton. Sometimes I think of certain words as having surfaces: “bitter” is sharp, “pith” is soft, “segment” is crisp at the edges.

There’s an old butter dish with a lid that never sits quite flush; the faintest wobble when you touch it, as if the dish breathes out while waiting on the table.

Sometimes when I open the fridge, the cold rush smells of something I can’t identify, maybe from a long-forgotten container behind the milk.

A glass of water left beside the bed collects bits of lint, almost invisible unless the light catches them.

The feeling of a doorknob in the dark, found by fingertips, always colder than expected.

Some chairs remember the last person who sat in them. If you press your palm against the seat, you might feel the warmth lingering.

Why does the hum of distant traffic become background so quickly, sinking underneath everything else until only sudden changes—sirens, a horn—call it forward again?

---

I’m thinking about the difference between peeling something and slicing it. Peeling asks more of you—slow work, careful, sometimes breaking the surface in thin strips. Slicing is decisive, one stroke, exposure.

A list:  
- The sound of a knife against an apple’s skin  
- The way juice leaks down toward your wrist  
- The small, translucent scar left on the cutting board  
- Light catching on the wetness, then disappearing  

Some stains never quite come out of wood.

The peel, after all, has its own memory of the shape it once held.
