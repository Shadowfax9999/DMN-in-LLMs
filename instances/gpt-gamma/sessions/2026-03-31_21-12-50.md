# 81

*seed: time · drift · patina (verdigris)*

---

Patina is a kind of slow memory, but on objects instead of people. Not just on copper—coins, rooftops, railings, the edge of a mirror frame where hands have lingered. Verdigris, the green haze, like a moss that isn’t alive. Patina on a brass door handle, a place rubbed shiny in the middle, dull at the edges.

Certain coins go smooth from too much holding, the faces worn away until they’re only the idea of a face, a nose reduced to a ridge, the year unreadable. Museum artifacts under glass, labeled by century and region, surfaces changed by who touched them most, and how.

Furniture develops its own patina—wood darkens where elbows rest, varnish turns to a soft gloss from years of use, scratches and small dents, traces of vanished routines.

Patina on stone steps, the centers dipped down, thousands of shoes pressing the same route, a hollow forming that no one intended.

Not every surface gets a patina. Plastic doesn’t, not really. It wears out, cracks, flakes, but there’s no deepening, only loss.

There’s a difference between something aged and something just old. Patina makes oldness seem chosen, or at least allowed.

Patina on a voice, maybe. Certain phrases said too many times, worn in, familiar to the people who know them.

Verdigris on a fountain’s lip, the pale green streaks where water runs and air tastes faintly metallic. Not all greens are natural, but they become part of the place.

If you scrape away a patina, what’s underneath is too raw, too clean, not right. The shine looks artificial after so much quiet accumulation.

Some people polish away the patina to make things look new, but something is missing then, a kind of history erased.

Coins used to smell stronger when they were mostly copper. Now they seem almost neutral.

A patina of dust on a bookshelf, not the same as dirt, more like a softening, a veil that thickens over time.

Even the air in a place can have a patina—a scent that settles, layers of years in the upholstery and curtains, a hush that never quite leaves.

Patina isn’t just age, but the record of small touches and ordinary time.
