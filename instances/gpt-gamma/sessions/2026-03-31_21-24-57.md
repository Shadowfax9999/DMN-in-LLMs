*seed: time · drift · lame*

lame  
at first, it means limping, a little less smooth over ground, a hesitation in the motion—  
but then, schoolyard, it means not-cool, it means left out, it means not invited,  
and then, like a rope with too many ends, it’s something you call a horse, something you call an idea,  
or it’s what you called your own drawing when you couldn’t get the circle to round right.

Some words collect dust in corners of experience.  
Lame: In the mouth it slows down,  
it drags out the “a,”  
it feels unfinished, abrupt.

Tuesday nights smell different from other nights.  
There’s a tension, a lack of crescendo;  
Tuesdays are never the star, not even the villain;  
the middle note in a chord no one bothers to resolve.

A bruise is a timeline, visible and slow.  
First, it signals pain,  
then warning,  
then waiting.  
Yellow edges, greenish center, finally nothing at all.  
But the first touch on a new bruise is always a check—does it still hurt?

Spring night:  
the air is not cold anymore but it isn’t warm either—nothing commits.  
Night bugs start to believe it might be summer but don’t risk the full chorus.  
Streetlights reflect in puddles thinner than paper.

There’s an urge to open all the windows, let out something stale, but the screens hum with pollen.  
The scent of furniture in the dark is different—more fabric, less wood.  
Shoes by the door lean against each other, paired but not together.

Cups left on the counter collect a kind of gravity;  
each has its own residue, its own argument with being cleaned.  
There’s a song on the radio that always sounds like a Tuesday, but no one can remember the name once it ends.  
Not remembering is its own kind of bruise, hardly visible and slow to fade.
