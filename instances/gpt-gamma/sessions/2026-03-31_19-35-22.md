# 36

*seed: time · drift · metronome*

---

Metronome — the word already ticks, two syllables like a slow clock. I picture the little silver pendulum swinging right, left, right, left, sometimes so quiet all you hear is the hinge.

Tick tick tick tick. I used to stare at that red wand, waiting for it to stop, but it never would unless you took off the lid and pinched the weighted needle still.

Mechanical metronomes smell like old wood and the insides of instrument cases. The felt lining with a cutout for rosin or a tuner.

It’s always a little hypnotic, being pulled into timing that isn’t quite the same as counting aloud. A memory of sitting at the piano with my foot pressed against the pedal, the felt sound of hammers with the metronome just behind.

Some people say you should practice with it, but it makes me tense after a while, like having someone snap their fingers beside your face.

There are digital ones now with numbers and a blinking light, but the old kind is more physical. You watch its little body lean, straightens up, leans again.

Back to the shoes: sometimes the clatter of footsteps on tiled hallways arranges itself into a simple rhythm, a phrase of steps repeated over and over. It’s easy to fall into step with the person ahead.

The difference between a metronome and walking is that bodies speed up and slow for corners, doorways, someone stepping aside. The metronome doesn’t care, it just keeps telling the same story.

Is the word Tuesday metronomic? It has two beats. Tues-day. But it doesn’t repeat in the same way.

Three coins stacked on a dashboard, sliding every time the car turns.

The feeling of walking past a vent in the sidewalk and catching an unexpected updraft. It sometimes smells of burnt dust.

The sound of a basketball hitting the rim is a sharp, metallic thud, different from the hollow echo off a backboard.

Pockets full of things that don’t belong: stones, tiny sticks, a bottle cap bent in half.

There’s a half-step between two doors in an old hallway, always feels like a trip hazard.

The pen you use for crossing things off a list never writes as well as the one you use to add things.
