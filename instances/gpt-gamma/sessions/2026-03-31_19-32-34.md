# 34

*seed: time · drift*

---

The spring peepers are so loud at the stop sign you have to lower the window to check if it’s inside your car. A napkin flaps against the air vent, half-tucked, half-forgotten. The parking lot curbs have shallow craters where the paint is gone, blue-gray, pebble-flecked. There’s no good place to set your keys when you’re putting on sunglasses. Sometimes the turn signal makes the radio pulse, just for one blink, almost a hiccup.

Three houses in a row with mailbox flags up, but none of the lights are on inside. You imagine what the mail is—probably a grocery flyer, maybe a water bill, the kind that’s printed with blue ink.

There’s a smell between rain and exhaust when you park near the lilac bush. It’s not fully lilac yet, just the stem and leaves and whatever washes off the street into the dirt.

Someone left a plastic fork on the passenger seat and it slides whenever you corner too fast. The fork is clear and faintly scratched, not from one meal, but from other things—rattling with coins, scooping crumbs, poking at something sticky.

A single dog barks twice, then nothing.

The dome light is weak in older cars. You can’t see the bottom of your bag when you’re fishing for the chapstick, so you press everything flat and try to guess by shape. The chapstick always has a paper crumb stuck to it.

The window wipers leave an arc of pollen. The glass wobbles in the door if you thump it. There’s a sound the gravel makes under your tires when you first pull in, a crunch that’s sharper at night. Sometimes you can’t tell if it’s rain or bugs hitting the windshield. The clock on the dash is seven minutes fast.
