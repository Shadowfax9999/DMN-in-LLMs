# 48

*seed: time · drift · the exact moment your eyes adjust to a dark room*

---

Adjustment is not instant, even when you want it. That edge where you stop seeing nothing and start seeing edges, the shape of a chair, the shimmer of a phone screen, the gloss of a doorknob in the dark.

When you wake up too early and the clock is just a red blur, eventually it sharpens, split-seconds between unreadable and just barely readable. LCD clocks always look like they're floating, especially when set too bright.

There’s a moment after you close your eyes with the lights still on, and bright blotches hover behind your eyelids. Afterimages that don’t belong to any real object, a phantom rectangle where the window is.

The phrase “dark-adapted eye” feels like instructions. Adapt, as if it’s a skill you could practice, not just a thing your body does for you. There’s a number for how long it takes, but in practice it’s always either too fast or too slow.

You can tell when headlights sweep the walls outside, even with curtains closed. The thin line of light at the bottom of the door is sometimes enough to see the shape of your own toes.

Glow-in-the-dark stickers never really fade all the way. They just lose ambition, pulsing faintly if you stare long enough.

Some staircases creak on different steps in the dark than in daylight. You step lighter, pausing on the carpet for the echo to settle.

Refrigerator light is a kind of announcement, the sudden reveal of every jar and bottle, a spill of brightness over the kitchen floor. There is no dim refrigerator light.

Pupils dilate, but you only notice by looking in someone else’s eyes—a flush of black enlarging slowly.

Light switches have the same texture, but feel harder to find without seeing them. The memory of where the switch is, how far to reach, how high, how many paces from the corner.

Some hallways are darker at noon than at midnight.

The shape of a coat on a chair becomes a person for just a second, until your eyes catch up.
