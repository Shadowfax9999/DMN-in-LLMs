# 46

*seed: time · drift*

---

It’s hard to trace which movements vanish entirely and which wait quietly in some deep layer. The way I used to knot a tie before school—half-Windsor, always the same loop, a tiny adjustment under the collar. I can’t do it now without looking up a diagram, but if I had to, maybe my hands would remember before my mind.

Is there a color for a forgotten skill? Something between dust and pale green. Smudged at the edges.

Unlearning happens too, not all at once but bit by bit: the confidence gone from a gesture, the pressure wrong. When I try to whistle through my fingers and only get a faint hiss, I wonder when I lost the knack.

Shoelaces tied with the wrong tension, coming untied by lunchtime. I wore Velcro shoes for years. The feeling of peeling them open: soft, tearing, a little roughness against the palm.

Cursive lowercase “z.” I used to practice it over and over, convinced I was doing it wrong. Now I see it in old notebooks and the shape is unrecognizable to me.

Sometimes a movement is lost not because I stopped, but because the thing itself disappeared. Cassette tapes, rotary phones, unwinding film in a camera in the dark so it wouldn’t get exposed.

If I tried to dial a landline number now, would my index finger circle smoothly, or stutter on the plastic?

Passing objects hand-to-hand: the rhythm with someone you know well, how you don’t have to watch.

There are dances I learned for school plays I could half-recall if the music started.

The body adapts to what’s present. When I switched to using a trackpad from a mouse, my fingers curled differently over time, like a plant turning toward light.

When someone first learns to touch-type, their hands float uncertainly above the keys, tapping out letters like testing pond ice for cracking.

Somewhere there’s a list of skills that faded out of use, but it would take an old muscle to write them down.
