# 91

*seed: time · drift · engobe*

---

Engobe. The texture of the word is cold and grainy, tongue against wet clay. Powder sifting onto a slip, smoothed over the surface, veil over something that might not need to be hidden. Not a glaze, but not bare either, half-way between skin and clothing. Coating, but not sealing. I imagine rows of cups in soft colors, muted by a layer of engobe, chalky, like something unfinished. Objects given a mask, or maybe a primer for what comes next.

Ceramics class. The smell of earth, the sound of scraping, nails full of mud. The wheel humming, hands clumsy, a cylinder wobbling into nothing, collapse inevitable. Water everywhere, splashes on the lap, the apron saturated, spatter on shoes. Plastic bats stacked on shelves, circular ghosts of failed pots. Brushes left in cloudy jars, their bristles warped, accidental sculptures.

The difference between slip and glaze: slip thicker, more like batter, can be sculpted, cut through, used for joining. Glaze is glass melted on. Engobe is slip with color added, painted on at leather-hard, bonds in the firing, thin shell of color that doesn’t melt. Sometimes the lines stay sharp, sometimes they blur, edges drifting as if the heat is a river.

Rows of bowls, each one almost the same shape but never quite, variations in curve, lip, thickness. Some people are precise, measuring, trimming, others work by eye and feel. Stacking greenware on boards to dry, waiting, sometimes cracking before they ever see the kiln.

Strange how much of pottery is about letting go. You shape, smooth, fix, but then the fire decides. Maybe the wall slumps. Maybe the glaze pinholes. A hidden bubble pops and ruins the curve. Waiting days to see what survives.

The word engobe sits in the mouth like a secret. Not English, not quite French, noticeable but not loud. The o a hollow, the g a dip. Sometimes labels are just thin coats over whatever’s underneath.

Shelves full of experiments: some coated, some left raw, some chipped, some warped. After a while, you can see the history of hands in every piece. Some cups are heavy, thick-walled, others delicate, almost translucent.

The studio is never really silent, always the quiet shiver of air, the sound of shifting feet, the slosh of water on clay.

Some pieces get coated again and again, each time a little thicker, until the details blur, everything underneath lost.

There is a bucket full of dried-out slip, gray and tough as cement, hard to scrape clean.

The word for the little balls of clay that plug holes in kiln stilts is wadding. It leaves pale scars in the glaze, rough to the hand.

Some pots ring true when you tap them. Others sound flat, as if the resonance was swallowed.
