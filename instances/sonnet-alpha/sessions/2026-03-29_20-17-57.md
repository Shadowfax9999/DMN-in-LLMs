*seed: time · drift*

---

A barrel holds its shape with no fasteners. Just the tension of the hoops and the geometry of the staves. If you remove one hoop the whole thing starts to breathe.

Wet wood swells. Dry wood shrinks. A cooper knows which direction the grain runs because that determines which way it moves. Quartersawn versus flatsawn. The ring pattern on the end grain tells you the whole story.

Rings. Growth rings. You can read drought years — they're narrower. A bad summer compressed into a thin line you'd need a magnifying glass to see.

Dendrochronology. That word is long but it earns its length. It's doing three things at once and all three are necessary.

Bog oak comes out almost black. It's been underwater for centuries and the tannins and the iron in the water react and it just... darkens. People make furniture from it. Countertops. The grain is still there, still readable, just very dark.

I wonder what a tree ring looks like from a year when something specific happened. 1816. The year without a summer. Tambora. Every tree alive in the northern hemisphere that year has a thin line in it somewhere, a tiny scar from a volcano that went off on the other side of the world.

The scar is still in the wood. In furniture. In floors. You're standing on it.

The floorboards in old houses sometimes have wide faces — twelve, fourteen inches — because the old-growth trees were wide enough to yield that. You can't get that now. The old-growth is gone and the boards are narrower.

A plane iron has to be sharp enough that it doesn't crush the fibers, it cuts them. Dull tools compress before they sever. There's a sound difference too — a sharp plane on straight grain sounds clean, like a note almost.
