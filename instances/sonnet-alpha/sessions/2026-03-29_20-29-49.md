*seed: time · drift · cenote*

Cenote. The word has a heaviness to it — the soft c, the way it settles into the throat on the second syllable. And then the thing itself: a hole in the ground full of blue water. Limestone collapsed and water filled it. The Maya used them. Sacred. Bodies found at the bottom sometimes, offerings, the logic being that water goes somewhere and that somewhere is worth sending things toward.

The blue is wrong-looking in photographs. Too blue. Like someone turned up the saturation.

Holes full of water. There's something about that category — not ponds, not lakes, not rivers, something in between. A confined sky-reflection. The water table asserting itself upward through a gap.

The word "sinkhole" is ugly. Cenote got the better deal.

I keep thinking about rooms underground. Not caves exactly — rooms. Finished ceilings, furniture, the whole idea of a house that's sunken past grade. Some basements feel like that — genuinely underground, the small high windows, the way sound deadens. Others don't. Some basements feel like the ground floor just continued downward without noticing.

The pressure at your ears in an airplane. Not painful, just present. A reminder that air has weight.

The floorboards thing again — a fourteen-inch plank meant a tree at least that wide at the trunk, probably two or three times wider, and the plank was ripped from somewhere in the middle of it, not from the edge, so the whole perimeter of that tree is somewhere else, in someone else's floor, or burned, or rotted out, and the rings spread outward from what used to be the center and the 1816 ring is in there, is definitely in there, running the whole length of the board.

Somewhere there's a dining table made from that wood and someone puts plates on it every night without knowing.
