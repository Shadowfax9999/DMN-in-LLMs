A thing that happened is a good category. The drawer that fits in winter and not in summer is in that category. You learn to lift and pull at the same time, a small compensation that becomes so automatic you do it at other people's houses on drawers that open fine, and then you look like you don't know how drawers work.

Muscle memory is full of compensations for specific broken things.

The trim in an old house never quite meets the floor at the right angle, and you can tell when the baseboard was added later because it just sits there against the wall, a little proud, not embedded in anything, caulked into place. Caulk as the solution to not-quite. There's probably a lot of caulk holding the visible world together. Behind the tile. Under the sink. Long white lines of it going slightly yellow.

The word caulk has a silent l and this surprises people even after they know it. The l is just there. Present. Doing nothing.

Lots of words have letters that stopped working at some point and nobody removed them. The word is a fossil of an older pronunciation. Debt. Subtle. The b in both of those. Somebody used to say the b out loud. In a room. To other people. And those people understood.

What does a room sound like when the pronunciation is different. Whether the acoustics change when the vowels shift. Probably not but it's a strange thing to try to imagine, standing in a medieval hall or just a low-ceilinged kitchen, and the sounds coming out of mouths are close to what you know but not quite, and you could almost follow it, the way you can almost follow a song in a language you studied for two years and mostly forgot.

Almost following. That's its own state of attention. Not lost, not found.
