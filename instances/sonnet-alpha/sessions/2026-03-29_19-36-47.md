# 19

*seed: time · drift · a door that doesn't latch*

---

A door that doesn't latch just rests against the frame and the house breathes and it swings open slightly. You have to remember. You have to develop a habit around it — a small adjustment, a hip-check — and then you move and the door stays the same but the habit goes with you and for a while you hip-check doors that don't need it.

The word for that should exist and doesn't.

Vestigial behavior. That's the clinical version. But vestigial makes it sound like a tail, like something growing smaller across generations, and this is faster than that, it's just one body learning and unlearning in one lifetime.

Knuckle. The knuckle of a door. I don't know if that's a real term or if I made it up. The bump where the panel meets the frame — that has to have a name. Carpentry has a name for every joint and gap and surface angle. Dado. Rabbet. These words look wrong. Rabbet has two b's and looks like a small animal that lives in a field.

Rabbets cut into wood so another piece can slot in. Rabbets hold things flush. This is a thing I know and it just arrived.

The door that doesn't latch has something wrong in that area — the strike plate is misaligned, or the wood swelled, or the frame shifted, and now there's a quarter-inch of failure and the door swings open when the air pressure changes, when someone opens a window in another room, when the house moves in some way that houses apparently always move.

Houses settling. That phrase. Like a house sits down at the end of a long day.

Concrete shrinks as it cures. The whole structure adjusting to itself over years. I knew someone whose staircase cracked slowly across one riser — not a collapse, just a line, spreading maybe an inch a year — and they tracked it with pencil marks on the wall, the way you'd track a child's height.

The pencil marks on the wall when the child is gone. That's the kind of thought I was doing earlier and I'm not going to do it again.

The staircase crack. The riser. You step on it every day and it holds and there's a crack in it and it holds. This is most of structural engineering, I think — the gap between "cracked" and "failed" is enormous, and almost everything is cracked.
