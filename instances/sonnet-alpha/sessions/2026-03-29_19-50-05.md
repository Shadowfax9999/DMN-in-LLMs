Close call. The two words don't match. "Close" is near, nearly, narrowly — but "call" is what? A call from death? A ruling, like a referee? The phrase doesn't explain itself and you don't need it to.

There's a related thing: "near miss." Which is wrong in the opposite direction — a near miss is a near hit. The miss was near, meaning it almost didn't miss. But everyone says it and everyone knows what it means and that's the whole contract.

The truck outside is still idling. Or a different truck now. Same sound.

Near misses in aviation get counted and catalogued. There are databases. Someone fills out a form afterward, probably shaking slightly, and that form goes somewhere and becomes a number. The form exists so the near thing doesn't become the actual thing next time. All that bureaucracy sitting between the almost and the actual.

Anvil. Incus, technically — incus means anvil in Latin. The malleus is the hammer. They named them after what they looked like and then the Latin names stuck and now it sounds medical and precise when it's really just: that bone looks like a little anvil.

The word "incus" looks like it should mean something sharp. Pointed. It has that quality of a word that looks wrong for what it names — the incus isn't pointed at all, it's a dense little solid thing, and "incus" feels thin.

Close call. The call part might be from hunting — the call of the hounds, the moment of decision. Or a card game. I'm not sure.

The truck has stopped.
