*seed: time · drift*

"Numb" isn't numb. Too much happening on the tongue.

"Loud" is loud though. Just the one syllable landing flat.

"Whisper" — you almost have to.

"Hush" is the whole gesture.

"Murk." That one earns it.

"Bland" is not bland. Bland has a good strong consonant at the front and a landing. Bland sounds decisive.

"Dull" is actually pretty dull.

"Vivid" — two v's, nothing quiet about it, works.

"Dim" is dim. Low and short.

"Slump." You can feel the posture in it.

"Placid" sounds too pleased with itself for what it means.

"Serene" is a little too clean, almost smug.

"Lull." That's earned too.

The ones that work seem to use the mouth mechanically — something closed becomes open, or the air stops suddenly where the meaning stops. The ones that don't work tend to be Latin. Latinate words came in as sounds of something else and never quite matched up. "Melancholy" meant something in Greek, landed in Latin, arrived in English still wearing those clothes.

"Grief" keeps bothering me. One hard syllable and then nothing.

"Sorrow" is better — it has room, that open middle, the soft ending. You can stay in sorrow for a moment.

"Wound" doesn't look like it sounds. W-o-u-n-d. The letters look like they should rhyme with "ound." Or "mound." Instead it goes somewhere else entirely. Looks like one word, sounds like another.

"Debt" has a b in it that does nothing.

"Wednesday" — the d sitting in the middle like it's been left there.

Lots of English words have letters just standing around.
