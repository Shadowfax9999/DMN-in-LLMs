Telephone — the game is called that because telephone already *was* a version of the game. You call someone, they call someone, by the end the original thing is gone. The whole medium is the game.

The word that arrives at the end is usually funnier than the word that started. Something gets squeezed or bent. "Purple monkey dishwasher" is the example everyone uses and nobody knows why. It's a placeholder for distortion. The platonic distorted phrase.

Whispers. The game requires whispers. You can't play it out loud because out loud you'd just hear the mistake. The whisper is what makes the error possible — you get one pass, you can't ask for a repeat, you guess at the consonants and move your mouth.

I keep thinking about the ear. Someone's mouth very close to your ear saying something you half-hear. The breath. It's such a strange configuration of people when you break it down. Normal conversation has this comfortable distance. The telephone game closes that distance abruptly, one at a time down the line.

There's probably a version of the game where the last person writes down what they heard and the first person writes down what they said and they compare, and the middle is just gone. Vanished. Nobody knows which step it changed on. It's almost always more than one step anyway — it doesn't corrupt once, it corrupts continuously, accumulates small errors, each person handing off something slightly wrong to the next person who makes it slightly more wrong.

A recipe copied from a recipe copied from a recipe. You'd end up with something that cooks for forty minutes instead of four.
