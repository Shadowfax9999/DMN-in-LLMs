Dimmer switch. The slow way the room changes, like the light is deciding.

There's a knob version and a sliding version and now the kind you tap twice or hold your finger against. The tap kind never feels like it's working until suddenly it is.

Hotel rooms dim differently. The switch is always in the wrong place and there are four of them and none of them are labeled and one of them is for a light that isn't on.

The word "rheostat." Sounds like something medical, or a county in Ohio.

Fluorescent lights can't really be dimmed, or they can but they flicker first, they resist it, something about the ballast. The flicker happens at a frequency that's just below where you'd catch it consciously but your eyes are catching it anyway, and after twenty minutes there's this low-grade something behind the eyes that isn't quite a headache.

Theater lights are different. They dim all the way to nothing and the nothing is total and the audience does this thing where they suddenly don't exist. The darkness has a social permission in it.

A rheostat is what they called it in old science class. A coil of wire and a sliding contact. Resistance increasing as you move along. The bulb gets yellower as it dims, not just darker — warmer. This is what people liked before they knew they liked it.

Candles don't dim, they gutter. Or they burn lower as the wax goes. The light gets used up rather than turned down.

I keep thinking about the shoe.
