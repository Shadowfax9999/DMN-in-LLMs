Compass rose — the name is doing something the object doesn't quite earn. A rose. Those thin lines radiating out don't look like a flower, they look like a starburst or a wheel or a crack in glass. But someone looked at it and thought: rose. Maybe the oldest ones were more elaborate, had more petals, the lines curved slightly.

N NE E SE S SW W NW — eight points, then sixteen, then thirty-two. At thirty-two the names get unwieldy. North-northeast. North by northeast, which is the Hitchcock film, which is nothing to do with compass navigation except the title is a direction that sounds vague enough to be a destination.

Cary Grant not knowing where he's going.

The word "bearing" — you take a bearing. You bear toward something. Bearing also means carrying. Also meaning: the metal ring inside a wheel that lets it turn. All from the same root, probably, the idea of holding something, supporting it, keeping it in relation to something else. A bearing tells you where you are in relation to north. The bearing in the wheel holds the axle in relation to the frame.

Magnetic north moves. It's been moving west across North America for a long time. Old maps are slightly wrong now because of it, their north drifted. A compass pointed at 1850 north is pointing at a place that isn't north anymore.

There's a feeling when you're turned around — genuinely turned around, not just momentarily confused — where north becomes a guess. You point and think: that way? And the certainty is fake, it's just the direction you've been facing longest. The body wants to have a north. It picks one.

The sun is usually enough. Late afternoon, shadows going east. Or which way the moss grows, though that's mostly wrong as navigation.

I keep thinking the word "azimuth" is more complicated than it is. It's just a horizontal angle. A direction measured from north, going clockwise. The azimuth of due east is 90 degrees. That's all it is. But the word sounds like it should require more.
