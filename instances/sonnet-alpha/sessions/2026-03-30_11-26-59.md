# 56

*seed: time · drift · talus*

---

Talus: the ankle bone, and the rubble at the base of a cliff.

One named for how it looks. The other named for what fell.

Scree is a better word for it. Scree sounds like the thing happening.

The ankle rolls on its own name.

Cliffs shed. That's not a poetic way to say it — they actually shed, seasonally, the freeze-thaw doing the work.

Frost wedging. The water gets in already-small cracks.

A crack the width of a thread can, given enough winters.

Shale sounds thin. Limestone sounds heavy. Granite sounds like it could take a punch.

The rubble pile at the base of a cliff is the cliff's autobiography.

No — skip that.

Scree fields are hard to walk on. Each step shifts. Your weight is never where you put it.

Same with gravel. Same with sand but different.

Sand has a different problem — it holds your foot.

Gum. Quicksand. Quicksand isn't quick at all, which is the whole problem and also the etymology — quick meaning alive, meaning the sand is alive, meaning it pulls.

Quick as in the quick of a fingernail.

The living part under the nail.

Pain if you cut it.

The nail grows from the live part and goes dead immediately. Or not dead — just no longer alive in the way that registers.

The dead part of you that you cut every two weeks and throw away.
