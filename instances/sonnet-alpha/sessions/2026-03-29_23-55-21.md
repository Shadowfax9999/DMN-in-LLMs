Greenland looks enormous because it needed to be somewhere on the page.

The island itself doesn't know.

Ice is lighter than water, which is unusual for a solid — most things sink in their own liquid — wait, I said I wouldn't do that.

Nuuk. The word Nuuk. Two syllables of the same syllable.

A place-name that sounds like the place might sound if you said it into cold air.

The Faroe Islands are between Norway and Iceland and nobody brings them up, they just sit there, 18 islands, sheep outnumber people, there's a word for the specific green of Faroese hillsides and I don't know it.

Sheep paths on a hillside make contour lines. Unintentional cartography.

The F in Fujita doing the same work as the F in funnel — I keep coming back to that. The mouth has to push out air. You feel it leaving.

Alphabets that don't have F. Arabic doesn't, not natively. The F sound gets borrowed.

There is a letter in Icelandic that looks like a p with a stroke through the stem and makes a th sound. Thorn. It was in English once. Ye Olde — that Y was a thorn that printers didn't have the type for.

A whole letter lost because a press didn't have the part.

Gutenberg's type cases had specific layouts, the most common letters closest to the compositor's right hand, and that layout became habit became standard, the muscle memory of a 15th-century compositor still living in how the cases are arranged.

But I don't actually know if that's true or if I'm confabulating a satisfying story.

The word *confabulate* is from Latin for chatting, conversation — the brain just making up a plausible continuation and presenting it as memory, as a story, as a fact.

The brain is very confident about things it invented two seconds ago.

So is a drain, probably.
