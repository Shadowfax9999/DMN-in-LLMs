# 81

*seed: time · drift · moving walkway dismount*

---

Steps that are not quite steps, rolling under you, so for a moment you’re not walking but being carried. The unsteady feeling at the end, when you have to match your pace to the still floor, and your body anticipates the shift. Shoulders hunch, arms ready, a heartbeat of recalibration. The patterned rubber ridges where the walkway ends — gray, ribbed, sometimes gritty with footprints, always a little dusty near the edges.  

There’s a stutter in the crowd as everyone enters new gravity, an almost synchronized hesitation. Trolleys rattle differently on the static tile, wheels catching for half a second. Children leap to land, refusing the pause, turning it into play.  

People waiting at the other end for arrivals, a kind of anti-runway. The uneven choreography of suitcases, backpacks, people searching for a gate, people not sure which direction to turn.  

You can feel the air currents change near the walkways, a faint rushing or coolness, maybe from the overhead vents or the pressure of people moving through the shared space. 

Announcements echo, but only the consonants are sharp; vowels blur and get lost in the tall ceiling.  

A shoelace comes untied on the moving belt, flopping and flapping against the metal, but there’s no safe way to stop and fix it until the end.  

There is a quiet allegiance among people who let others step off first, an invisible sorting by momentum or mood.  

Sometimes the walkway is still, powered down, and you walk the extra-smooth path, lightweight, the frictionless hush underfoot, strange in its silence.  

At night the belts still gleam with the day’s accumulation — dropped tickets, a spilled cup drying in a sticky halo, one forgotten child’s sock tucked near the handrail’s edge.  

Walking on the still walkway feels like cheating, but no one says anything.  

The transition moment, off the belt, is the smallest kind of journey, completed over and over without thinking.  

Someone pauses, lost in thought halfway down, and the whole line gentle-sways to avoid them.  

The handrail moves at a slightly different speed from the belt.  

I have seen people ride backward, just to pass the time.  

Near the end, there’s sometimes a painted yellow line: CAUTION, WATCH YOUR STEP, with the warning worn away in the middle from all the shoes.  

A candy wrapper winks from underneath the glass side-guard.

The carpet past the walkway is patterned with tiny blue arrows.
