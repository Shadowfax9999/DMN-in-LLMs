# 86

*seed: time · drift · vanishing point*

---

Vanishing point. The place where lines, parallel in reality, seem to converge. But “seem” is the thing—nothing meets, not really. The world pulls itself inward, compressing into a diminishing mark on the page. The word vanishing itself feels like a breath drawn in and never released.

I think about graphite lines, each one beginning sharp and ending faint, where the hand forgets its own pressure. The act of drawing a road—the horizon inevitable, even when the pencil wants to wander.

A plane of glass held against the sky, scratched at the center. The scratch is the only thing that’s real—the receding expanse, just a trick the eye invents.

There are days when looking at a long hallway, the walls threaten to collapse together, not from perspective but from the sense that they could, if I blinked or stepped wrong.

A row of fence posts, smaller and smaller. The last one is imagined, but I still reach for it with my eyes.

---

A horizon is not a line but a boundary between ways of knowing. Above it, open sky; below it, things with names. Everything at the meeting place is uncertain—is it closer than it looks, or forever receding? The further I look, the more things slide toward disappearance.

Rain on pavement. The reflection is longer than the thing itself. If I crouch to look along the ground, the world stacks itself in bands: stone, water, brightness, sky.

Rooms without corners. A round dome, endless curve, no edges for the eye to rest. Where does the vanishing point go, when there are no lines to meet?

Sometimes, tracing the grain in wood with a fingernail, the lines never converge. They spiral, doubling back, curling toward a center that isn’t there.

---

Train tracks in memory, but I’m not on the train, not watching from above—just standing beside the rails, letting my gaze run ahead, seeing silver turn to shadow, believing for a moment that distance is a form of folding.

The vanishing point is a promise and a lie.
