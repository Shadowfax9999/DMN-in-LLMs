Platform voice. The sound that comes before the train, echoing in the rafters or just fuzzing at the edges of the air, "stand clear of the—," slicing through the static in pieces. Not quite a warning, not quite a greeting, just a modular sentence assembled around the comings and goings. Sometimes the voice trips, loops, repeats: "the next train will— the next train will—". You listen for the timbre: too smooth to be local, too clipped to be kind, vowels perched on nothing.

“Mind the gap.” Four syllables ring out in tiled halls, watermarking the floor with memory. The gap is sometimes wide, sometimes invisible. Feet, wheels, canes, toes, all must mind.

Waiting on the platform, there’s a hush that isn’t silence, filled with the voice’s aftertaste, and the scrape and shuffle of shoes on concrete. Shoes make a language of their own: hard-heeled tap, rubbery drag, the soft brush of fabric against grit. These sounds arrive in layers, spill over each other: a suitcase wheel snags in a groove, sneakers squeak, someone’s boot clicks once and then stops.

If you try to speak, your words blend with station noise, waver at the edges, dissolve before they reach the next person’s ear. Sometimes voices bounce off the panels overhead and return in a warped key.

On some evenings, the lights buzz with something like expectation, the air trembling just above the tracks. The sign flickers, scrolling destinations and times, pixelated green and orange. It is always just about to be your train.

A scrap of paper slides along the platform, whipped by whatever air the tunnel breathes out.
