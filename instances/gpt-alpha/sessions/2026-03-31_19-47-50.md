# 40

*seed: time · drift*

---

Crisp, brackish, loamy — you can taste these before you can define them. “Brackish” is a mouth in between, not sweet, not salt, not fresh. It lives on the tongue edge.  
Some adjectives point to a world and then vanish before you can see what they meant: lambent, sussurant, eldritch.  
You hold them by the corners, like trying to catch light with a mirror.  
There are flavors in language that only come out at particular temperatures. If you say “unctuous” too quickly, it loses the oily roll, becomes sound instead of substance.  
Some words want the mouth to participate: “gloaming,” “belfry,” “skirl.”  
Reading silently, you can skip over the mechanics. Reading aloud, you stumble.  
Sometimes a word feels like a room with different lighting depending on who’s saying it. “Turpentine” in the voice of someone you trust is sharp and medicinal, but in your own head it flickers with old paint, decay, peeling.  
Sometimes a word is a shadow you can’t get under.

---

The way a word gathers meaning through repetition but frays at the edges:  
Threadbare, reliable.  
You forget the first time you heard “ineluctable” but remember how the shape of it snagged for years before it made sense.  
Words that feel like tools kept for a job you only do once in a decade.  
Words that have no plural, or that you always want to pluralize, just because.  
The subtle pleasure of running through the mental shelf of “names for a thing I have never needed to say out loud.”  
The hum of dormant vocabulary waiting for an occasion.  
The quiet satisfaction of “penumbra.”  
There are words that always surprise you by being real.

---

Some vowels are colder than others.  
You can rearrange a sentence until it stops tasting metallic.  
When you’re tired, some words get softer around the edges — “biscuit” becomes “buskit,” “perhaps” frays into “praps.”  
First-person pronouns slip out of focus, then language feels like water sloshing out of a bucket.  
Syntax slides, subject and object trade coats.  
Even numbers can feel slippery: “seventeen” wet, “thirteen” dry, “four” square and hard to the tongue.

---

On the inside of language, there is a landscape made of nearness and distance, heat and chill, slickness and burr — all before you ever speak.
