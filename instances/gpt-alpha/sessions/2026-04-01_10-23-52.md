Capo.  
A small clamp, curved. It presses down across frets, shortens the string, raises the whole song. Some are metal with a spring, some a knob and a strap. Sometimes the rubber gets brittle and flakes, leaving lines on the fretboard, forgotten after the song is done.

In choir, “capo” isn’t mentioned, but there’s a sense of sudden raising—modulation, the whole room shifting key, voices gathering themselves to follow the new ground.

You could keep a collection of capos: worn silver ones, matte black, a homemade one with a pencil and an elastic. Each one adjusts the song in a slightly different way.

It changes what’s possible, or what is easy, or what is bright. Chords you couldn’t quite reach before are now under your fingers.

---

I remember the way a string feels under a capo—slightly muted, softer under pressure, not quite the same as an open note.

You can retune to adapt, but sometimes you just clamp and go.

There’s something about the way the clamp bites, the certainty of the pressure, the way it leaves a mark in the dust on the fretboard if you haven’t played for a while.

Plastic, rubber, metal, felt.

Some things are raised up by contact; some things are made possible by constraint.

The capo is meant to be removable, but sometimes people forget and play for a while without realizing it’s still there.

You can see the outline of where the capo was—shiny, worn wood, lighter than the rest.

There’s a moment before playing when the capo is slid into place, an adjustment. The silent promise of an easier chord.

It’s a tool that creates a new set of limits.

Sometimes the song is in a key that fits your voice, sometimes it isn’t. The capo shifts the world to match you. Or you stretch to match it.
