*seed: time · drift · hammered string*

---

Hammered string: dulcimer, clavichord, piano, citra, santur, the sound shaped by a blow rather than a pluck. A bright shimmer, percussive and singing at once, energy transferred by impact, not fingers alone. The line between rhythm and melody is thinner on a hammered string, each note a micro-explosion, a vibration set loose by collision.

Sometimes the memory of a sound: metal on metal, soft hammers covered in felt, a thudding in the lower register, crystalline at the top. Repetition draws patterns, arpeggios rippling out like stones in a pond, echoes folding back, layers thickening with every strike. The way a phrase in music accumulates, not unlike sediment—each impact pressing a history into the air. There are pieces—etudes and toccatas—written to show off the physics of hammering, motion made visible in the bouncing wrists, fingers rebounding. The wrist flicked for staccato, hand leaping for octaves, chord struck like a bell.

Nothing to do with hammers in woodshop, or the sudden violence of construction. Here, the hammer is tamed, domesticated, its blow measured in grams and milliseconds.

The oddness of transformation: strike a string and get melody, not clangor. Force into music. The ancient fascination with making wire sing by hitting it.

---

Tools that shape sound:
- Mallet, hammer, stick
- Reed, breath, lip
- Bow, drawn hair on gut
- Pick, nail, fingertip
- Electronic pulse, speaker cone
- Water glass, rimmed by a finger
- Foot stomp, door slam, radiator clank

The boundary dissolves—the household becomes an orchestra if you listen: the accidental hammered string of a ruler overhanging a desk, thwacked just so.

The mailbox snaps shut, a dull chord reverberates in the metal cavity.

Language is struck, too. Some words are plucked, gentle, hesitant—others are hammered out, forceful, insistent, the syllables landing like blows. The rhythm of command, announcement, interruption.

In a room somewhere, an upright piano leans against a wall, hammers poised above their assigned wires, silent unless summoned, every note asleep until woken by a key.

The wild unpredictability of the hammered note: sometimes too loud, sometimes ghostly, a missed shot, a double strike. Unforgiving, but alive.

There is a mark left on the felt after a thousand strikes: grooves, signs of practice, invisible unless you look inside. The music accumulates a physical record, invisible to an audience but permanent, shaped by every performance.

A child discovers the action for the first time, jabbing at keys, delighted by the chaos, not yet seeking melody.

Sometimes two notes ring together, one struck, the other caught in its resonance, a string vibrating in sympathy, a harmony no one ordered.
