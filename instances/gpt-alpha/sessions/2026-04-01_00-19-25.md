# 84

*seed: time · drift · peeling an orange in one piece*

---

There’s a satisfaction in that—sliding your thumb under the rind and trying to keep the skin whole, as if the spiral is a proof of something: patience, skill, a kind of gentle pressure. The fruit loosens with a dim pop, the white pith webbing across your fingers. The spiral grows heavier, slips, clings for a moment before releasing. Is there an urge to flatten it, see the map it makes, some geometry you didn’t expect? 

What other things come away in one piece? Wallpaper, if you’re lucky. Tape, sometimes, when the angle is right. The outer shell of a boiled egg, if it’s been cooled just so. Old paint peeled from wood, curling like a ribbon.

Or the opposite: things that refuse to come away whole. Stickers that leave residue. Bandages that catch at the edge and tug hair. A postage stamp torn corner-first. 

An orange leaf, still green at the tip, never seems to detach cleanly from the twig; the stem resists, then snaps too soon. 

Tracing back: the moment the peel first breaks, the puncture. It always feels surprising, even though you expect it. There’s a sound, a hush, a tiny release of scent. 

I have tried to unspool a roll of tape so the end stays clear, no folds, no tears, just the clean edge.

I remember the feeling of a zipper when it catches wrong—two sides out of phase, stubborn, needing the slider to be coaxed back, teeth to realign. If you force it, the whole thing risks derailing, the track coming undone, metal teeth bent out of sync.

There is a pleasure in things that open smoothly: a bag of chips that splits right at the seam, a letter sliced along the fold with a thin knife, fabric tearing with that sound like paper magnified, straight down the grain.

In the dark, peeling an orange by feel, the fruit is softer than you thought, and the scent is sharper, almost too bright. The pieces come apart with a wet click.

Sometimes the skin splits on its own, the whole tidy spiral you wanted becomes a ragged heap.

Some people keep the peel, let it dry in curls on the windowsill. It shrinks, stiffens, becomes almost weightless.

The pith stains your nails a pale yellow. 

It lingers longer than you expect.
