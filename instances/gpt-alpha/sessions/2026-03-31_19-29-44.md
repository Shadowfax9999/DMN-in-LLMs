---

rock-paper-scissors

the sequence, the anticipation before the reveal — the moment you both hesitate, counting the rhythm in your bones, 1-2-3, and maybe cheating a little, letting an eye flicker, seeing the knuckles tense, trying to get ahead, but never quite sure. Why is paper enough to defeat rock? Paper is thin, rock is dense. Maybe it's the idea of wrapping, gentleness winning, but that's not what you think in the instant before you throw your hand forward.

There are other games decided in a gesture: coin-flip, odds and evens, thumb war, quick-draw. All of them small moments of contest, rules so simple you forget them as soon as the hand falls. Sometimes you just want the tie, both paper, both scissors, the mutual smirk.

The way children invent new gestures for the same game — "fire beats everything," "water soaks paper," "dynamite," the rules expanding, unstable, each neighborhood with its own order of the universe.

Hands speaking in code, faster than speech. Three shapes, all you need.

---

A corridor lined with mirrors — each reflection staggered a little, out of sync. At certain angles, the glass multiplies you, shoulder overlapping shoulder, never quite aligning. The mind doubles itself, watching from the side, wondering who will move first.

The air in the corridor feels charged, like a waiting room just before an appointment. You hear your own footsteps and for a flash, they're not yours.

Can you lose a game against yourself? The hand hesitates, lifts, falls. Rock, rock. Try again.

---

Somewhere else, a table where people fold napkins, stack glasses, the scrape of a chair as someone stands. Daffodils leaning toward the evening light, petals already softening at the edges. The window shows only the faintest reflection, barely enough to catch a face.

Three gestures. No winner this time.
