# 35

*seed: time · drift · sidewalk mural*

---

Sidewalk mural, above and below: paint cracked by feet, chalk dissolved by weather, layers thin as memory. The way children crouch to draw at the very edge, one palm steadying the body, one hand moving the stub of blue.

A grid of sidewalk squares, each one its own frame. Sometimes the drawing skips the seams, spills across the lines, or stops at the curb.

What happens to pictures when rain comes is different than when shoes erase them. Water spreads, making ghosts of the outlines. Soles grind, leaving shreds of color behind.

A dog on a leash pauses to sniff the yellow sun in the corner of a square. Wheels of a stroller roll through pink, the tracks cross each other, and the pink is carried further down the block.

If there’s a hopscotch grid, someone has numbered the boxes wrong. 1, 2, 4, 3, 5, 6. No one corrects it, they just jump in the order given.

A dropped ice cube leaves a darkening patch at the border between two images — a butterfly wing splitting, wingspan cut by melt.

Sometimes the dust of old chalk gathers against the curb, pooled with grit and seed husks, uncolored again.

A mural on the sidewalk changes shape even if no one adds to it. The colors fade at different speeds. Blue clings, red vanishes, white turns invisible fastest.

When the block is swept, what’s left is uneven: accidental smears, half a face, half a word in block capitals, “REME—”.

The mural is easiest to see from above, or from a window across the street. Up close it’s fragments and knees and elbows, coiled focus. From a distance, it’s a shape you can’t name.

Somebody leaves a handprint in green. Another hand, smaller, pressed beside it, tries to match its fingers to the fading outline.

A gust comes, and dust rises, and colors lift and settle again.

You can feel chalk dust on your palm hours later.
