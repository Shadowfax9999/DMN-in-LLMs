# 62

*seed: time · drift · rosin*

---

There’s a compactness to “rosin,” a dryness, yellowish and crystalline, sticking fingers that touch it. Not quite powder, not quite wax, somehow both brittle and adhesive. It shows up in the margins of orchestras, an invisible ritual before the instrument is played: the slow, purposeful stroke of rosin along the bow, invisible dust released, friction manufactured for sound.

Sometimes a process is made of steps you don’t really see—the rosin on the bow, the damp sponge for the reeds, the quiet cough before the phrase begins. Each a preparation, not performance but its precondition, threading the air with anticipation.

Rosin smells faintly of pine, maybe resin, maybe something else you can’t quite name. I’m not sure if the scent fills the room or if it clings to the skin, so faint you notice only after you’ve left.

---

A conversation that never quite balances: three people, one always trying to draw the others in, and another leaning away, eyes drifting, fingers fidgeting. Not hostility, but a gentle tension, like static before a storm. The sense that words are being matched, measure for measure—if you offer a story, someone else must offer one too, similar in scope or embarrassment or vulnerability, or else the air grows weighty, uneven.

Sometimes the give-and-take is smooth, like something practiced, and sometimes it falls apart, too many words at once, or nobody quite able to begin.

There’s a relief when laughter cuts through the tangle, resetting the score.

A sense that the structure of a group is marked not just by who speaks but by the texture of the spaces between—long enough to welcome interruption, or so brief there’s no room for anyone more.

Sometimes my memory of a group is just the memory of its rhythm, not its words.

---

There are words that sound like what they do: rosin, brittle, soften, hush. And others that don’t match at all, like “committee,” heavy but not weighty, or “protocol,” which seems almost clinical, but sometimes is just another game of waiting for the right turn.

If you listen to a group long enough, you start to hear not just the words, but the shapes they draw together. Some form tight loops, recurring again and again. Some spiral out and are never seen again, like a thread cut too soon.

Sometimes, after a group has been sitting for hours, there’s an exhale. Not with breath, but with posture—shoulders come down, knees uncross. A kind of tacit agreement: we have been here long enough.

Nobody decides, but gradually, one by one, coats are reached for, farewells are murmured, the group dissolves, and the room starts to remember its original shape.

The chairs scrape softly on the floor.
