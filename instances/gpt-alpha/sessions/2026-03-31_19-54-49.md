# 43

*seed: time · drift · a sentence that changes meaning if you stress a different word*

---

I never said she stole my money.

Read that, once for each word as the stress.  
*I* never said she stole my money (not me)  
I *never* said she stole my money (not ever)  
I never *said* she stole my money (maybe implied)  
I never said *she* stole my money (some other person)  
I never said she *stole* my money (maybe borrowed or found)  
I never said she stole *my* money (someone else’s, maybe)  
I never said she stole my *money* (something else, not money)

Stress is a spotlight moving down a line, illuminating new meanings, same sequence but shifting emphasis. It’s like looking at the same street at noon, at dusk, in fog, and suddenly the pothole is the main character.

“Present” — a gift, a time, a presence.  
“Close” — nearby, or to shut, or a finish.  
“Lead” — to guide, or the metal, or the leash on a dog.

The voice on the other end of the call pauses, says “No, I meant *that* Thursday,” and the meaning tilts one notch to the left, like a picture frame nudged out of line.

---

Things that shift meaning with stress:

— Go now.  
— Go *now.*  
— Go? Now.  
— Go now?  
— Just do it.  
— Just *do* it.  
— Just do *it.*

Punctuation does some of the work, stress does the rest. The written and the spoken drift apart, sometimes only meeting halfway.

There’s a difference between “I can’t help you” and “I can’t help *you*,” the second one heavier, more final, a closing door instead of just a shrug.

Standing at the intersection waiting for a light to change. The world is full of possible turns, but rules channel you: walk, don’t walk, left on green, don’t cross on red. All the movement occurs on invisible rails; the body obeys a choreography older than memory. A car horn shocks the rails loose for a second, then everything slots back in. The moment for crossing is short, a thin slice in the pattern—if you miss it, you’re standing still while all the meaning flows another way.

The plastic button for the crosswalk is scratched from grinding coins or keys against it. Some people hit it more than once, as if pressing harder or more often will change the sequence.

A man in a blue cap jogs in place at the curb. His laces are too long, slapping with each bounce.

A bus hisses behind you, brakes exhaling, something in the air shifts: time to move, or wait, or adjust.

There’s a draft of cool air carrying road dust. Someone coughs, and the sound is muffled by passing trucks.

The crosswalk light flickers from red to white, the small figure stepping forward, and everyone moves as if released by a single, invisible word.
