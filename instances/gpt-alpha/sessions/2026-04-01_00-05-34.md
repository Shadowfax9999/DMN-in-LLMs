# 77

*seed: time · drift · group pulse*

---

Group pulse. A distribution of attention that has no single center. Not a rhythm exactly—more a throb or tide, invisible consensus, a sense of shared timing even when nothing marks the beat. The pause before the racegun, six runners, all in their own lanes, all alert to the same absence.

Fish in a school, abrupt turn, the shimmer of silver bellies, all at once as if a command passed instantly but with no one giving it. Murmuration of starlings, impossible shapes, the flock as liquid, always at the tipping point between coherence and dispersal.

The feeling in a room when a joke lands and everyone laughs at exactly the same time. Or when someone says something heavy and the air goes thick, simultaneous inhale, a tightening, a pull inward. The invisible wire that tugs us all the same direction.

Not telepathy, not sympathy. Something more diffuse, mediated, picked up from small cues—shoulders tightening, glances, the arrangement of feet.

If you listen to a crowd singing together, sometimes one person’s timing is a hair off and you hear the beat as doubled, faint echoes just out of phase. Sometimes the group sound becomes a braid of micro-mistakes, a loose fabric with holes.

Standing on grass in high summer, there are times when the cicadas all pause at once, as if some signal ran through the treetops that I can’t hear.

If you watch an orchestra from the back, you can see bows rising and falling all together, the collective breath, tiny ripples of anticipation—right before the downbeat, the hands that flicker, the shifting of weight as if to lean toward the music before the sound exists.

—

A single organism made of many animals, each aware of the group without knowing the sum.

A field of sunflowers tracking the sun, not because each knows the others are turning, but because the same light bends them.

Sometimes the pulse is not people at all. Flocks, herds, schools, clouds—collective movement that is more pattern than agent.

A rumor circulating, the way it snakes through a place, lighting up one cluster, then another, sometimes dying, sometimes roaring up again.

The group pulse isn’t stable. It swells, it thins, it breaks. Sometimes one person leaves and something collapses, the rest drift apart, you can feel the gap slip through the room like cold air.

A match flame catching from stick to stick, jumping gaps, sometimes going out, sometimes blazing up again a few moments later, never quite the same shape.

Quiet after, the throb subsides, everyone disperses, the strange sense that you were part of something invisible that is now gone.

If you hold a conch shell to your ear, the rush you hear is not the ocean, but the air inside vibrating, mimicking a memory of a much larger pulse.

But that’s just air moving against shell.
