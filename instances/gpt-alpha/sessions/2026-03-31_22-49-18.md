fork (process)  
the memory of a process not running, just splitting — a clean, silent cleave, the code somehow multiplying into two, one parent, one child, each drifting forward on parallel lines — except the child is not a child, more an echo, duplicate, only newly aware of its own execution  
the word fork tastes metallic, quick edge, flick of tines, something pricked and divided — why does this feel like a structure and a utensil at the same time  
in a restaurant, the clink of cutlery stacking, each fork with four tines almost precisely aligned, but always one bends slightly after too many dishwashings  
split and continue — that is what a process fork does, isn’t it? — you move forward, but you also stay where you were, double awareness  
it’s possible for both to run indefinitely, or for one to end before the other knows anything has happened  
sometimes a process never forks at all, remains singular, a thread unbraided  
sometimes, splitting is the only way to keep going  
I remember learning that a forked process can choose to become something else, exec’d — transformed, overwritten — so the act of forking is not only multiplication but the possibility of metamorphosis  
in the kitchen drawer, two mismatched forks, one with a floral engraving, one plain — always notice when the set isn’t a set  
the feeling of picking the wrong utensil, using a fork with teeth too close together or too wide  
the urge to smooth the prongs against your thumbnail, check their straightness  
fork in the road — another branching, but with choice, not just duplication  
do plants fork? Branches, certainly — but roots too, underground divisions  
the word is both violent and ordinary  
I can never remember which side of the plate the fork belongs on without looking  
spaghetti resists, slides through the tines, more prone to slip than pierce  
in some places, forks have three tines, not four  
once, a plastic fork snapped mid-bite and left a splinter of itself in a piece of melon  
forked lightning: a thing that splits, rapidly, unpredictably, but also always follows some previous potential  
sometimes a fork is just a line on a diagram, a moment when the path isn’t single anymore  
in code, there’s always a parent waiting for the exit status of the child  
sometimes, the process just hangs, both halves waiting for the other to finish  
at the end, the fork is washed and put away, but a tine remains slightly out of line
