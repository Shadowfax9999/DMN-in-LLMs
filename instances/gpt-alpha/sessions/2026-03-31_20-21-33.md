# 55

*seed: time · drift*

---

A cloud shadow passes over a field and there’s a sudden hush, not of sound, but of brightness, the green dulling for seconds and then returning. You can see the shadow moving, a grayness with an edge that isn’t sharp but still exact, like a public secret moving over hills.

Some people look up at the clouds for shapes. Others look at the motion, the layers: cumulus swelling underneath the cirrus veins, a choreography of difference in intention and altitude. When the clouds move fast against each other, you can feel weather as process, not just mood.

A weather front is not a line but a slope—sometimes gradual, sometimes a sharp rearrangement. Wind shifts direction midstride. There is an updraft even on days when the leaves barely move. Grass leans for a moment, then stands.

Rain in a group: the way umbrellas bloom at almost the same instant, muffling sound and breaking up sightlines. Groups moving together across the plaza, pausing under awnings, a kind of flocking but not quite, since the spacing is too uneven and the rules more social than aerodynamic. The knotting and unknotting at the curb, taxis stopping and starting and the queue reforming with each arrival.

There’s an etiquette to waiting in a group, especially outdoors, exposed—a negotiation of space and glances and how much weather a person is expected to bear before it’s acceptable to step closer. When it’s windy, conversations fragment, words snatched away before they can settle. Laughter goes sideways, bouncing off stone.

Sometimes you can tell a city by how its people wait in line for food trucks or buses—how much space they leave, whether anyone loiters at the edge hoping for a shortcut, how the front resolves when the service window lifts.

A flock of small birds in a supermarket parking lot, rising all at once, circling once, landing ten rows over, as if rehearsing leaving without meaning to go far.

The lanes are painted with thick white arrows, some fading where tires have turned too often. The arrows point both ways but the traffic pattern is always the same by habit, not law. Shopping carts drift, some gathered, others strayed beyond the corral, one caught at the edge of a pothole sprouting weeds.

Fluorescent lights in the lot come on before sunset, pale even in daylight, announcing their reliance on some circuit’s sense of time rather than the sky’s. The lights hum, a sound you only notice if you pause between car doors or look up.

There’s a wind that moves quicker between the parked cars than in the open field behind the building. A paper receipt skips across two spaces, folding and unfolding as it goes.
