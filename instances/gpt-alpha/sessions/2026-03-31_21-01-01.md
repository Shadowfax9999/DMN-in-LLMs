# 73

*seed: time · drift*

---

The swell before the downbeat — the conductor not raising the baton, but the split second before, a pulse that runs through the orchestra, everyone simultaneously nowhere and poised everywhere, right at the mouth of the event. Sometimes the audience coughs in that gap, and even the cough is different; it’s part of the world before the sound.  

The first swirl in a cup of coffee with cream, not yet settled, clouds forming and dissolving, activity that is neither the pour nor the drink, a brief, contained weather. 

The huddle around a whiteboard, markers out, sleeves pushed back, someone says, “Okay, let’s get started,” and there is no clear line between before and after, but now the marker squeaks, and the board is no longer blank.  

Passing a ball back and forth, warming up, muscle memory twitching awake, easy, loose motions before the pattern sharpens, laughter not yet competitive, just wind and hands and the solid thud into a glove or palm.  

A computer’s fan spins up, a low whirr, screen lights up, colors bloom pixel by pixel. The login chime.  

Queue at the door, theater lobby, the slow shuffle as tickets are taken, murmurs and the rustle of coats, the sweep from casual conversation to the hush that says “here it comes,” the house lights dimming — not all at once, but in three slow fades.  

The ritual of turning a page in a brand new notebook, that first crease, hesitation, maybe a date written as an anchor — then the pen moves, and everything after has this first sentence underneath it, quietly supporting the rest, even if it’s crossed out.  

Clouds part for a moment and the day changes color; streetlights flick off, one after another, like dominoes set in reverse.

In a kitchen, water starts to boil, tiny beads forming before the full, rolling motion. Someone turns on the radio, volume just above the ambient noise, and the beginning is almost missed.  

Hands poised above keys, not yet pressing, only the intention of a word. Breath held, waiting for the downstroke.  

First click of a stopwatch, the tiniest jolt in the wrist.  

Some birds call out before dawn as if rehearsing the morning, soundless wings shifting in the dark, finding their place for the real song.
