# 81

*seed: time · drift · the way a phone screen lights up a dark room*

---

Backlit. The glow outlines a rectangle on the ceiling, faint blue spill. Not illumination, exactly—just a square of difference, a local contradiction in the dark. You don’t need to know what the message says to feel the shift; it’s the before/after that registers. Outline, then fade. The retina still records the ghost of it, an afterimage even when the light is gone. Maybe that’s all the mind does: register changes, small pulses, something there then not, or something not-there then suddenly present.

Sometimes a word is just a switch—“lamp,” “blink,” “pulse.” Names that thrum when you say them. The syllables almost flicker in the mouth. 

It’s strange, the comfort of screens in the dark. Not reading, just the fact of the surface humming. Like the low electrical promise of appliances left plugged in—an unspent surplus, a paused circuit. If you put your palm against old glass you could feel a faint warmth that never exactly vanishes, only gets overlooked.

The language for this is thin—“glimmer,” “faint,” “dim”—all adjectives of halfway, none getting at the small drama of a dark room breached by cheap LEDs. The feeling of being woken not by sound, but by a color.

Sometimes the light is enough to outline a shape on the quilt, to cast a shadow of the charging cord looping off the edge of the table. Sometimes it catches dust and the air looks momentarily solid.

There’s a silence in it, too. Not the silence of sleep, but the silence after the screen blips away, the brief surge of blue behind the eyelids.

Fold the phone back under the pillow or flip it face-down. No message, just the trace. The way the mind sits up for nothing.
