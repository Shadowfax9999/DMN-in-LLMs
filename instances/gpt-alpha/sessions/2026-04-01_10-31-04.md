# 91

*seed: time · drift · a thing you've memorized but never understood*

---

Mnemonic. The word tastes like metal, like mechanical tickings — mnemonic device, memory machine, a scaffolding for thoughts. I picture a tree diagram, words hung from its branches, the way you memorize something for a test by making it into a pattern. The days of the week in song, the order of taxonomy: Kingdom, Phylum, Class. I still remember “King Philip Came Over For Good Soup,” though I no longer remember what comes after family, or if the soup is meant to be literal. 

Mnemonic has a silent “m” at the front — why does it have that? The word is packed like a trunk, heavy with things tucked inside. Sometimes the devices are stranger: the lines on your knuckles for counting months, the way your fingers fold to remember the next number. Memory is physical, always mapped somewhere on the body, in the rhythm of repetition or the press of a pencil. 

What have I memorized that I never understood? The periodic table song, some prayer from a summer camp — mumbling phonemes whose meaning never caught. The colors of the rainbow organized by “ROYGBIV,” a word that isn’t a word but a map. There’s a comfort in saying it, automatic, as if repeating it is the same as knowing.

Mnemonics as colored beads, sliding back and forth on a string. Sometimes you memorize the shape of the answer rather than its content, the way a math problem looks filled out on a page, the rhythm of the solution. 

There are songs for geography, songs for anatomy, tricks for spelling “arithmetic”: A Rat In Tom’s House Might Eat Tommy’s Ice Cream. If I use someone else’s device, does it become mine? Or does it always sit slightly outside, a borrowed coat, never quite fitting?

Some mnemonics are social, learned from others, some invented in a hurry, a secret code that only makes sense to you. 

The word itself is hard to spell, hard to hold. 

I still remember “Please Excuse My Dear Aunt Sally,” but I don’t know if I’ve ever met anyone with that name.
