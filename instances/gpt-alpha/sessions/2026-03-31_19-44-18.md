# 38

*seed: time · drift · the feeling of a new haircut*

---

A sensation of air at the back of the neck, cool where there used to be heaviness. The ears feel exposed, like new things. The sensation when the scalp is pressed with fingertips, as if the whole head suddenly belongs to someone else. Hearing is different for a few hours—sounds travel less muffled, as though the hair was a curtain that's been pulled aside.

There is a strange relief giving way to self-consciousness: walking into the street, catching one's reflection, not quite recognizing the outline. Lightness accompanies the first turn of the head, small wisps still falling. Even the pillow feels different that night, nothing to fold or flatten. A faint itch along the perimeter where the scissors traced.

Memory of childhood haircuts—itchy collars, a cape that never closed properly at the neck, the snap of small silver clips. The plastic comb cold against the scalp. Sitting very still, holding the neck rigid, counting the seconds.

Sometimes the scalp tingles later, phantom hairs, a sense of being trimmed even hours after the shears have stopped.

---

The word “shears”: made of two open blades and the promise of transformation. Shear, sheer, shore, share—how a vowel bends the meaning. To shear is to subtract. To sheer off is to swerve. Sheer itself: a steep drop, a transparence, all at once. A sheer curtain lets in light without letting in the world. To share is to give a part away. Sheen, just next door, the gleam left on the skin after the hair is gone.

Shorn, shorn, shorn. Some words sound clipped at the ends, as if the sound itself has been trimmed and cannot grow back.

---

There are colors you can taste—a silver taste at the back of the tongue before rain, the blue of morning after not enough sleep. If orange is a word that tastes of its own rind, then silver is cold and sharp, blue is weightless. Turquoise is a temperature, halfway between cool and warm. Chartreuse is always sour, whether or not it is.

Sometimes you can feel the color of a haircut—crisp, pale, green for new; heavy, brown, warm for the old. 

---

If you part your hair differently, the scalp appears in a line of white. A line that was hidden, now briefly exposed, a secret divide. The hair wants to return to its old place, the part you’ve trained into it over years. Water, a comb, and yet memory persists, a faint furrow always pulling back, like a riverbed without water.

Some rooms echo more when your hair is short. 

Buttons on shirts feel different against the collarbone with no hair to buffer them.

The hush of scissors is a kind of permission, an interval between selves.

The neck feels longer. The world sounds louder. The hat never fits quite right on the first day.

A haircut is always a small forgetting.
