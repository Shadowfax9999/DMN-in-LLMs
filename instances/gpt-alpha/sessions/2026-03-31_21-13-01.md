# 79

*seed: time · drift · the smell of a new sponge*

---

New kitchen sponge: not lemon, not clean, something between plastic and raw bread dough. Almost like the inside of a sneaker just out of the box, but with the promise of water built in.

Dry sponge is a sound, too: dragging across tile, the dull resistance before it’s ever met soap. Squeezing it — stiff, then squish, then suddenly compliant.

There’s a point where old sponges forget what shape they were meant to be. Corners thinning, rounded, splitting at the curve, permanently damp in a way the new one can’t imagine.

The first time you use a new sponge for dishes, it feels too large, too aggressive, like a mitten instead of a hand. The first thing it absorbs always has a certain gravity to it, as if the newness is tainted, initiated.

I sometimes think about all the things a sponge will touch in its life, and all the things it will never touch. The world it will know: plates, mugs, the sharp rim of a can, sometimes the inside of a glass bottle, sometimes the stubborn ring on a pan.

It never gets used on windows, not once.

---

Ridges of soap left in the grooves of an old cutting board, hardening into white streaks that dissolve only with patience or heat.

The sound of a plastic glass knocked sideways in the sink: a hollow bonk, a subtle bounce, a tendency to trap water upside down.

A stack of plates feels different depending on whether they’re wet or dry. Wet, they threaten to suction together, tiny vacuums between ceramic, a resistance when you tip the pile.

A fork lost under the suds, always time to grope blindly before finding it, metal sliding against enamel.

Sometimes you find yourself staring out the window above the sink, not seeing anything but the movement of your own hands in the reflection. Sometimes the lights outside are enough to make the glass into a shallow mirror; sometimes you just see the dark.

Some sponges have a rough side for scrubbing. The difference between the soft and the scratchy is sharper when you’re not paying attention and it bites your fingertip.

I have never used one until it wore completely away. There’s always a point where it’s thrown out, quietly, and a new one appears, rigid, hopeful, not yet haunted by any particular stain.
