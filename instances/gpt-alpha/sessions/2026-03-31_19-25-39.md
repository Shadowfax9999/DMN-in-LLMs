# 27

*seed: time · drift · the way a cold room smells different from a warm one*

---

Cool dust, paper that’s never sunlit, quiet that’s thicker than in a warm room. The sense of corners. Maybe the molecules behave differently, or maybe it’s just the absence of heat’s permission. Cold makes even the soft things stiffer, sharper at the edges. You can feel silence pool in cold rooms, tangible as a draft across the ankles.

Noticing: The way you hold your breath before opening a door, the resistance in your palm as metal chills the handle. The feeling of stepping inside, the tiny shock of changing temperature climbing up your arms.

Ice on glass always looks shallower than it is.

---

List of temperatures:

- The coolness under the stairs
- The heat behind a refrigerator
- The tepid air in a hallway with shut doors
- The warm spill of sun on the tile, which disappears after noon
- The trapped closeness inside a parked car in summer
- The low, even chill of basements, unwavering

---

What does cold sound like? It’s a tighter kind of quiet, wires humming instead of voices, the refrigerator’s distant motor, a drip, a faint shuffle. Not quite silence, more like compression.

There’s also the way wool mittens muffle every gesture, hands moving through fabric’s memory. Gloves that keep warmth inside, trapping faint echoes of skin. It’s easy to disappear inside layers.

The voice of someone calling you from another room feels denser in winter, as if the air itself carries more weight.

---

Sometimes a room is cold because it’s been left alone for a long time. The dust organizes itself. The light is a thin layer peeled off the window, settling on everything in a film.

The coldest air you can breathe in a house is just before dawn, standing in a doorway as if waiting for something to happen.

Some doors are hard to open when the weather changes.
