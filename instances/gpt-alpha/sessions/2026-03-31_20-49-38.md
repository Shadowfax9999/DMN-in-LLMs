solitude*

---

Solitude is not a shape or a silence, but maybe a weight — or no weight at all, a kind of softening of the boundary between self and the rest. Not loneliness, not quite contentment. Just what’s leftover when the voices and names withdraw. I picture a surface spreading out, undisturbed, a pond before wind.

The faint hum — refrigerator, or something further, a transformer down the street. In solitude, small sounds become markers, or maybe companions. The whine of a mosquito. The movement of blood at the temples, noticed only because nothing else is crowding it out.

The way you can hear your own breath and not recognize it. Sometimes the sound in the ear feels like muffled static, or maybe that’s just the body humming — I can’t tell if it’s inside or outside.

Light sits differently in an empty place. It doesn’t announce itself — it just accumulates, slow and uncaring, moving across an unmoving floor.

—

Unoccupied intervals, time stretching: a clock on the wall ticking a little louder. The urge to fill space, to set a glass on the counter, even if you don’t want a drink. The ritual of movement for no reason: opening a drawer, closing it again. Testing a faucet. Lifting the corner of a rug to see if anything is under there.

Solitude as a room with everything out of place, or perfectly arranged, and it makes no difference — there is no one to disturb or to notice either way.

A solitary moment is not always peaceful. Sometimes it is frictionless, a loss of surface. Books become heavier in the hand, pages slightly damp from the air. Even the fabric of socks rubs in a new way, as if you only now notice they’re there.

Sometimes the air gathers on your skin and you forget what you meant to do.

A window can be both a threshold and a wall. The things seen through it belong to another world, but sometimes the sight is enough, and sometimes it isn’t.

A pencil rolling off a table sounds louder when no one else is home.
