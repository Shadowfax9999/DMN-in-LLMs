*seed: time · drift*

---

An edge in a sound wave, not a literal cliff, but a boundary where the shape of the vibration switches — crest, trough, crest, interruption. Patterns of interruption: a bird song half-swallowed by a passing truck, a voice splitting when it tries to rise too high, the way a violin's note turns, not evenly, but with a tiny hitch in every bow stroke.

Interruptions aren't always negative space. Sometimes the break permits something new: an overtone above the melody, a sympathetic vibration in the wood, the sound you only notice when silence lands right after. The hush after thunder — even dogs notice that.

When multiple frequencies meet, they create beats, pulses, phantom rhythms. Interference writes patterns in air — sometimes you can feel it in your teeth, the wobble of too-close frequencies, not quite canceling but layering, making a new thing.

Unison is rare. Even choirs, aiming for one voice, are always making a swarm, tiny differences forming a shimmer around the note. If you isolate a single voice in a recording, you can hear the shape of a person in the irregularities, the thing that keeps group sound from becoming just a test tone.

A coin rolls across a table and the sound modulates faster as it loses momentum, until it stalls and spins in place, rattling at higher and higher frequency before a sudden stop. The final sound is sharp, a break from the rhythm that came before.

A process that accelerates toward an endpoint sometimes ends with a whimper instead of a bang. Not collapse, just vanishing: a string ceases to vibrate, a spinning plate steadies and halts, the air clears.

Some signals are designed to fade — fade-out at the end of a song, the applause that dies down, the parade that keeps going long after the crowd has drifted away.
