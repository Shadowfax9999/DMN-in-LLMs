Palindrome: the evenness, the fold at the center, the way the halves balance without collapsing inward. Words that double back, mirror, hold their own middle in tension. Civic. Rotator. Level. The brief illusion of symmetry, as if time could be negotiated, reversed at a hinge.

The sound of “eve”—not the biblical one, but the soft rush of v, almost slipping into air, the way a word can feel like a sigh coming and going.

“Radar” makes a shape on the page like an echo, the first R reading out, bouncing back again. If you say it enough, it loses its contour, just a pulse.

Some numbers are palindromes, too—131, 444, 2002. Years that seem to fold into themselves, especially in digital clocks, where the numbers flip and return, no matter the hour.

I remember writing palindromic dates on checks, as if they could carry some small magic.

Are there symmetrical gestures? Hands meeting palm to palm, mirrored in prayer, or in the simple clapping, one coming to meet its double.

Butterflies with matching wings, markings reflected to confuse the eye, make halves into wholeness.

The idea of reversible action—wind-up toys that walk forward, then click awkwardly backward along the same steps, as if time can unspool, and then respool.

Some melodies can be played backward. They resolve differently, but the contour is the same. Retrograde inversion: the shape travels in reverse and upside down, a kind of palindromic echo in sound.

Palindrome as a comfort—knowing the way back is possible, even if you don’t take it.

But not all returns are symmetrical. Some shadows lengthen but never shrink, some creases won’t smooth out, some words can’t be unwound, once spoken.

A palindrome of movement: stand, sit, stand again. Pick up a book, set it down. The middle is the decision.
