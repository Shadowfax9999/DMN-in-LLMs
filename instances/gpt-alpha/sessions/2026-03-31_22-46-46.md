# 87

*seed: time · drift · a coin on the sidewalk you didn't pick up*

---

Salt. Not the kind on the table, but the kind that crusts the edge of a rock, soft white fluff that vanishes if you brush too hard. On skin after swimming in the sea. Dust on capillaries. It makes its own map, tracing evaporation. In the mouth: both mineral and memory. The first taste in the morning, phantom, after toast or sweat or sleep. The outline of a circle left on the table from a dried spill—salt water or tea, it doesn’t matter, just a faint border where something used to be.

Between fingers, salt is almost invisible until you rub them together and feel grit. 

There are languages where salt and salary share a root. A payment, a preservative, a necessary corrosion. Salt keeps food from vanishing, but makes metal disappear, slow ache of rust.

The word itself, a bite of sibilance, then a flatness. Sss-ahh-lt. Silt is close, but softer, slipperier, river-mud instead of sea-edge.

You can draw with salt, sprinkling lines on the table, then blowing gently—watch the patterns shift, islands splitting, rejoining, erasing themselves. A tiny geography, a white archipelago, unstable borders. 

Even the taste makes you thirsty. The paradox of wanting more of what unsettles you.

On sidewalks after snow, salt crunches underfoot in patches. Sometimes it collects around the heels of boots, clings to laces like pale moss.

A memory slips in: the sting on a cut finger, the moment you realize, sharp and bright, that the skin is broken. 

Salt as boundary, salt as trace, salt as a thing that calls for water, and then is gone.
