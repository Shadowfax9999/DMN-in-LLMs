# 72

*seed: time · drift · a map with no labels*

---

Sometimes a process is so slight you barely notice. The dough gathers itself under your hands, changing from sticky to smooth, and at some point it is a single thing and not scraps pressed together. Not transformation, just movement toward coherence.

A bus idling outside the terminal, filling slowly with people who drop their bags and then stand again, uncertain whether to sit yet. No one is in charge, but eventually the first person buckles in, and the rest follow, settling, the process like water finding its level.

Or the way a sprout splits the surface of the soil, neither announcing itself nor holding back, just the fact of green against brown, and after that, everything follows: the next leaf, a shadow on the windowsill, the decision to move the pot closer to the light.

---

There is a kind of group movement that doesn’t have a leader. In crowds at intersections, everyone steps forward together when the light changes, but there is that fractional pause when each person checks the others, a silent negotiation: now. In waves across a field at a concert, arms going up independently, converging until it’s a pattern.

A word repeated enough times loses meaning, becomes sound, becomes something to pass along—echo or ripple.

---

The soft click at the start of a recording, the hush of an audience before a first line, the moment of silent mutual agreement that things are beginning. Even the air seems to shift. One person inhales, and the rest follow.

When a zipper is closed at the start of a rainstorm, there’s a shiver that travels down the row of people, everyone tucking in, shoulders drawn up, movement in sequence.

Somewhere, a car engine turns over after a long winter, stutters, then settles, the beginning not a single point but a series of tiny thresholds.

The first time a song is played for someone else, there’s a tension in the space, as if the air is tuned to a new resonance, waiting to see if it will be received.

Plastic chairs in a circle, notebooks still closed, pens uncapped but unused.

The numbers above the elevator door start again from 1.
