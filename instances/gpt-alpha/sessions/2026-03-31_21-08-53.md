# 76

*seed: time · drift · the particular blue of a pilot light*

---

The blue is narrow, hard-edged, brighter at the base. Not quite the blue of sky—there’s a green folding into it, almost sharp, almost cold. Looking at it makes you squint. Sometimes it flickers, almost invisible in sunlight, so that you wonder if it’s even there, if the oven is really on. In the quiet of a basement, a water heater with its inspection window—kneeling, looking for the blue. You’d expect heat to be orange, red, but here it’s this strip of blue, a color that feels more like warning than warmth, but it’s warmth all the same.

Some words: cerulean, gaslight, cobalt, invisible dangers, the hush of standing in a utility closet.

Hands out for warmth in winter, palms open toward the stove, the way heat moves but light stays contained.  

---

A sequence:

1. The sound of gas starting—soft click, then a whoosh, and the blue is there.
2. The faint smell—just at the start before the burn cleans it away.
3. Watching for the color, because the manual says orange means something’s wrong.
4. The shiver of metallic pipes.
5. Crawling on hands and knees to peer into the little window, catching your breath, the moment before you see it.

---

The blue is a promise that something unseen is working, that a process is unfolding below the surface.

There are appliances you never think about until you smell them, or until something fails.

Beneath the stove, a compartment where cookie sheets and pizza pans collect dust.

Every so often, the flame goes out and there’s the ritual of relighting—matchstick, hesitation, possible sudden flare.

Some mornings the kitchen is cold and you tap the top of the metal, as if that will wake it up.

The way heat feels different coming from below, a kind of rising comfort that moves slow, fills the space first at the ankles, then knees, then finally the air around your face.

The blue doesn’t illuminate the room. It only marks a pocket of certainty under the metal.
