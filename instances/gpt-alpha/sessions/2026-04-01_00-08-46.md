# 80

*seed: time · drift*

---

“Fragment” is a word that tugs in multiple directions at once. One part — the sharp edge, abrupt, an element torn from a whole, something that used to belong. Another — the everyday, a cracker snapped, a page dog-eared, a thought half-written, the arc suspended mid-air, unlanded.

Funny how “fragment” also suggests possibility, offshoot, an entry point. A sliver in the finger is both pain and artifact, evidence that something was once larger or elsewhere. Pain as proof. Slivers. Flakes. Shards. Scrap.

What lives only in the stub:  
– The pencil point broken, pulled deep and dark against paper until the wood husk takes over.  
– Unsent drafts, resting in a queue nobody will open.  
– A single shoe beside the curb, laces unraveling.  
– Scabs, paint chips, flakes of soap left drying on the dish.  
– The taste of a flavor that is nearly—but never quite—gone.

Is incompletion always a kind of ache? Can a fragment be whole in itself, or is it haunted by what it could have been?

The sound when a sentence trips and never corrects itself. Leaving the closing parenthesis unclosed.  

“Unclosed”—the syllables fold back on themselves, a door left cracked for wind to find.

Somewhere: a note scribbled in a margin, the rest of the thought dissipated when supper was called, the pencil still impressed in the paper.

Is a comma a fragment or a bridge?

Nothing ever truly reattaches. Broken dishes, once glued, wear the fracture even after years of use. Sometimes the line where it split is the thing you trace with your thumb.

The feeling of a password entered wrong, something not quite matching. The key turned the wrong way. An almost-fit.

It’s not just absence. There’s a fullness to it, too—density in the leftover, the remainder, the thing that refuses to resolve.

By morning the ice on the pond will be half-melted, a shape with too many edges to draw.
