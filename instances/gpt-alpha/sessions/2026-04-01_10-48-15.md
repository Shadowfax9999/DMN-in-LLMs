# 99

*seed: time · drift · stack*

---

Stack, stratify, layers that can’t be merged, only set on top of one another: books at the corner of a desk, napkins, the flooring of a house, pancakes in a tall and implausible cartoon tower, dishes in a disorganized cabinet, archaeological sediment arranged by someone’s waiting for a team to unearth a coin from the third stratum, counting backward in kitchen linoleum.  

Stack is not the same as pile. Stack expects intent, regularity, each unit aware of the one below, mechanical or hopeful. Pile is a collapse. Stack is the imagined Jenga tower before the wobble.  

If you stack concepts, does the one at the bottom always bear the most weight? There are philosophical arguments, nested like matryoshka dolls, each claiming to be the primary. Which one pushes up from the bottom, the first supporting fact or the most recent addition, or are they all held by the thought just above the table?  

A stack of chairs, metal and molded plastic, built to interlock legs, scraping when separated, each waiting for an assembly. Stacks that travel: folding gym mats, canvas camp beds, bundled papers.  

In programming, a stack is last-in-first-out, a controlled loss of memory, each element retrieved in reverse order, like climbing back down the rungs you just ascended. Push, pop, undo. The mind sometimes feels like that: most recent thought accessible, older ones buried until you backtrack, reconstruct, unspool.  

Some stacks are ceremonial, like coins on a gravestone, marbles on a childhood windowsill, totems in miniature. Stacks as memory, stacks as aspiration.  

Language stacks too—prefixes and suffixes, layers of meaning, composite words built on top of each other until only the root recalls the beginning.  

Stacks can be towers or archives. They can be threats, promises, habits, plans. The block tower always sways before it falls but always wants to be rebuilt, piece by piece, stacked again.
