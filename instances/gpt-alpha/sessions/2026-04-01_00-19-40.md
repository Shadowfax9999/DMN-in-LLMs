# 85

*seed: time · drift*

---

Sometimes when you try to split something along its supposed seam—cardboard packaging, plastic wrap—it doesn’t yield where it’s meant to. Some lines are ornamental, weak perforations that feign helpfulness. The tab rips in the wrong direction, or halfway, and what you’re left with is something like a wound, rough-edged.

The cereal box opens with a crunch and a puff of cardboard dust that settles on your hands, invisible until you lick a finger and taste the paper.

There’s a difference between the sound of glass and the sound of plastic being forced. Glass resists, holds your intent at a distance, but plastic yields with a shudder, and sometimes, unpredictably, it snaps.

What is the exact moment something becomes broken, not just altered—when a zipper tooth bends, when a seal tears away from itself, when a page rips past the margin.

Tension in materials: the pause, the stretch before break. The invisible threshold you can’t see until you’ve gone past it.

---

There are words that sound like the things they mean, but only halfway: “snap,” “crack,” but “split” is silent, “seam” is soft, “cleave” goes both ways.

When a word divides into syllables beneath your tongue, each part feels like a bead on a string. Some words refuse to break; they are all one sound, impossible to separate.

If you try to hum a word instead of speaking it, the shape changes—softens, blurs, becomes something else entirely.

Some languages have more words for rupture, for sliver, for fracture. Some have only one.

---

After something splits, it’s often the edge that matters. Tactile aftermath. The peel, the torn strip, the fringe of fabric. Rasp of a torn jeans hem against skin. The sharp fuzz of paper torn against the grain—one side is always rougher than the other.

Edges accumulate dust. Things that are whole, unbroken, catch less.

A split creates two surfaces where there was one. The new faces are raw, unfamiliar. Touching them sometimes hurts, even if the break was intended. The underside of peel, the raggy cardboard, the sharp tender inside of a shell.

---

The word “seam” implies potential: for holding, or for bursting, or for being picked apart and resewn. Seams are not just closures, they are possibilities.

There is always a point where pushing harder stops being progress and starts being ruin.

Some things, once split, resist all attempts at reunion. Others can be stitched, pressed, glued, but the evidence remains—a scar, a seam, a faint ridge felt by fingertip even if not by eye.

A box with its lid forced never quite closes again.
