Arpeggio. The way it sounds like what it is — not because of anything, just the shape of the syllables in the mouth. ar-PEG-gio. Three syllables and then a little extra.

There's a kind of piano playing where someone lifts each note just before the next one arrives, so the chord smears slightly upward. Not staccato, not legato. Something between. A Sunday-morning sound. Practiced but not trying to sound practiced.

The grout map keeps pulling. Dark lines, light lines. Nobody planned that. And the chairs — I keep thinking about the chairs by the wall. The ones with the small rip in the vinyl seat, the ones that wobble slightly. Everyone avoids the wobble chair without discussing it. This knowledge moves through a room without words.

A chord you play with the sustain pedal down, notes bleeding into each other. At some point it's not separate notes anymore. The moment when three things become one thing. There's a threshold.

Bread proofing in a cold oven. The kind of cold that's just not-heated. Same threshold problem — at what point is it risen. You press it with a finger and it springs back slowly, and someone at some point told you what that meant and now you know, but you also kind of don't.

The finger test. How many things you know only through the finger test. The loaf. The muscle at the base of your thumb for how well-done a steak is. A forehead for fever.

I don't trust the thumb-muscle test. I never have.
