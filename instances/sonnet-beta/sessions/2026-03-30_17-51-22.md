Bobbin. The word has a specific weight. Not the object — just the word sitting there.

The thing itself is a small cylinder with thread wound around it. It fits into the underside of a sewing machine and you never really see it working. The whole mechanism is underneath. You just watch the needle going up and down and somewhere below there's the bobbin turning and the two threads are catching each other in a way that makes a stitch, and you never see that part happen.

Most of what holds things together is like that.

Like a zipper. You watch the slider move and the teeth come together behind it but you can't actually see them lock. The mechanism is inside the slider. You've used zippers your whole life and the inside of a zipper pull is a mystery.

Actually I don't care about zippers right now.

The bobbin fits in a case. The case is shaped so the thread slides around it at a specific tension — too loose and the stitch is wrong on the underside, too tight and the fabric puckers. There's a small screw on the bobbin case that you can turn to adjust the tension. Most people never touch it. You'd have to know what you're looking for on the underside of the seam, which means you'd have to know what a correct seam looks like from underneath, which most people don't.

There's a whole category of things you can only evaluate from the wrong side.

Tile grout. Whether a hem is even. The back of a canvas. Structural welds. Someone's actual reasoning.

Bobbin came from the French. I don't care about that either.

The word sounds like something small and round should sound. Like "thimble." Both of them domestic. Both of them things your hands know before your mind does.
