# 46

*seed: time · drift · silage*

---

Silage. The smell of it. That sharp-sweet rot that sits on the air for half a mile around a farm in August. You drive through it with the windows up and it still gets in.

Fermentation happening in a pile. The pile wrapped in black plastic, the plastic held down with old tires. Rows of tires on a hillside. That's a specific image. Not everywhere but somewhere specific in memory — Ohio, probably, or Indiana. The long flat fields with the silos.

A silo and silage aren't the same thing.

The word "silo" now means something else entirely. The organizational kind. People talk about working in silos. The grain silo became a metaphor before most people under thirty had stood next to one.

Standing next to one: they hum. In the wind. Or something inside shifts.

---

Black plastic held down with tires is such a specific solution to a problem. You just look at it and understand immediately. No diagram needed.

Baling wire. That also just makes sense when you see it. Holds the bale. Named for the thing it holds.

Twine, though — baling twine — that's different from wire. Orange or blue. Farms have lengths of it tied to everything. Gates, fence posts, a broken latch.

The color of baling twine is that specific orange. Not orange orange. Faded. Weathered to a kind of dusty salmon. Tied in a knot that someone tied quickly, in cold hands, probably.

The knot still there years later.
