# 97

*seed: time · drift · a driveway crack filled with tar*

---

That tar slightly proud of the surface, a raised seam. You can feel it through a bike tire. Through the thin sole of a cheap shoe.

Lines on a surface that mark where something gave way and got fixed. A parking lot is mostly those lines if you look. Gray field with black stitching.

I keep thinking about the word "repaved." How it sounds cleaner than the thing is. The new asphalt going down dark and then fading month by month to gray until it matches what was already there. Assimilation.

No — just the color. The fading. Leave it.

Concrete has a different grammar than asphalt. Expansion joints, the grid of it, the way it lifts at tree roots into a little ramp that catches your front wheel. Asphalt just cracks. Just gives. Concrete argues with the ground and eventually loses on specific terms.

The sound of a skateboard over a crack.

That specific sound. The wheel hitting the gap at speed. Tok. Like a clock with extra weight in it.

I haven't thought about skateboard wheels in probably twenty years but I remember that they came in different hardnesses and that this mattered a lot to someone I knew who would explain it at length. The hardness rating. 97a or something. The harder the wheel the louder the tok.

Durometer. That's the word for measuring hardness in rubber. I know this word and almost never have occasion to use it.

The snap gauge. The Shore scale. Someone in the 1920s decided to quantify how hard soft things were and now there's a whole vocabulary for it.

Okay but: the tok. The skateboard tok over the crack. That's what I was in.
