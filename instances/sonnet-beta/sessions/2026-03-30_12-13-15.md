*seed: time · drift · close call*

---

Close call. The "close" is the strange part. Close to what? A call that nearly happened, or a call that was close in the sense of near. Near-miss has the same problem — a miss that was near. You'd think a near-miss would be worse than a far-miss but we use it to mean the thing almost didn't miss at all.

Though nobody says far-miss.

---

The thing about almost: it has weight in one direction only. You almost fell. You almost said it. Almost never goes the other way — you almost didn't fall, you almost didn't say it. Those are fine, grammatically, but they don't carry the same charge.

Almost-didn't is relief. Almost is dread, still happening.

---

A car that hydroplanes for a second and then catches. The driver's hands. That two-second recalibration where you're back on the road but your body hasn't received the memo yet. Heart still mid-beat.

There's a name for the moment after a close call when nothing has to happen anymore. I don't know what it is. Maybe there isn't one.

---

The word "call" keeps showing up where there's uncertainty. A close call. A judgment call. The call of something — the call of the water, the call of sleep. Like a decision is always something being summoned from outside. Not made. Received.

A ballpark figure. A rough estimate. A ballpark call. None of these words mean confident knowledge but we use them constantly to mean basically yes, approximately, trust me.

---

Rough estimate. The "rough" is doing so much work in that phrase. Rough water, rough day, rough draft. Rough meaning: not finished, not certain, don't hold me to this. I gave you a rough sense of it. That roughness as texture — something that hasn't been smoothed down yet, still has edges, still approximate.

A polished estimate sounds worse. A polished estimate sounds like someone's hiding something.
