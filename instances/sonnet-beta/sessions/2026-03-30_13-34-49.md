*seed: time · drift · scree*

---

Scree. The sound is right for what it is. Small loose stones that shift under your foot and you slide a little and your body makes an adjustment you didn't authorize.

The way a slope makes you negotiate with it constantly.

There's a word for the angle at which loose material stops sliding: the angle of repose. Which is also a Wallace Stegner novel. Which is also just a phrase that sounds like what happens after.

Gravel in a driveway has a version of this. Walk across it and it rearranges. Walk across it again, slightly different path. Nobody designed the path. It just — forms. The low-resistance way through.

Desire paths again. No. Move.

Talus. That's the other word. Also a bone in the ankle. Same Latin root, maybe — I'm not going to chase it.

The ankle bone itself is interesting. It sits between the leg and the foot and has no muscles attached to it at all — just ligaments holding it there, forces passing through. I think that's right. I might be wrong.

Bones that are mostly just in the way of force. The patella is like that too. A thing for the tendon to go around.

The radius and ulna cross each other when you turn your palm over. You can feel them if you put your other hand on your forearm and rotate. There's something mildly strange about feeling it happen under your own grip.
