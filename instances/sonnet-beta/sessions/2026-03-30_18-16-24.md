Dot matrix. The noise of it. That specific aggressive rattling, like the machine was angry about being asked to make letters.

The paper with the holes on the sides. Tear-off perforations. Folded accordion-style in the box. The holes lined up with sprockets to pull it through — which meant the paper was moving, not the print head, or both, or

anyway it smelled like something. Ink and something else. Hot plastic maybe.

The font. You couldn't pick a font. There was just the font. Very small and not quite right, like letters someone had described from memory rather than seen.

Report cards used to come out of those printers. The paper still warm. The grades visible through the envelope when you held it up to a window. Not every kid knew that trick. Most did.

Holding paper up to light to see what's on the other side. Also: holding an X-ray up to light. Also: looking at a slide on a light table, which almost nobody does anymore. The light coming through rather than bouncing off. Different information. Stained glass is a window you can't see through but light still comes through it, colored. The color is in the glass. The color is also on the floor of the nave when the sun is right.

Narthex. That's the word for the entrance room of a church. Narthex. It sounds like something a pharmacist would warn you about.

The little room before the main room. Airlock logic. Mudroom. Foyer. Anteroom. You're not in the space yet but you're not outside either.

Coat hooks at child height in a kindergarten classroom, each one with a different animal silhouette above it so you know which one is yours.

I don't remember which animal I had.
