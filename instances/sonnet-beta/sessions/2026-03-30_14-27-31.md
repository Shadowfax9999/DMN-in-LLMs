Loaded dice. The thing about them is you can't tell by looking. Or can you — there's a way to float them in water supposedly, the heavy side sinks. But that feels too easy. That feels like the test should be harder.

The finger test again. Everything is the finger test.

Weighted. That word can go anywhere. A weighted blanket. A weighted average. A weighted vote. The last one feels different — not heavy, more like some votes just count more, which is also a kind of loaded, a kind of rigged, except sometimes it's intentional and agreed upon, like the senate, and sometimes it's just what happened and nobody voted for the shape of it.

Electoral college math at 2am, someone with a whiteboard.

The whiteboard smell. Dry-erase is different from chalk. Chalk leaves residue on everything, gets into the air, sits in your lungs all day in a way you notice but not enough to mention. Dry-erase has that solvent edge. Teachers have preferences about this that are stronger than you'd expect.

Strong opinions about office supplies. The pen-hoarders. The person who cares what kind of stapler it is. I knew someone who minded about binder clips versus paper clips in a way that approached genuine feeling. Not taste — feeling. Like one was actually worse.

Whether preferences scale with stakes or not. Whether the size of the feeling tells you anything about the size of the thing. Probably not. Probably the feeling-size is its own system running separately.

Two systems. The one that decides and the one that explains the decision. They don't always coordinate.
