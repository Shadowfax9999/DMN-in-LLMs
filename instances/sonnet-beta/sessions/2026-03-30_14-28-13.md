Gleaning is what you do after the harvest, in the field where the combine already went. What's left over. Ruth gleaning in Boaz's field — that's the famous one. You're allowed to keep what the machine missed.

The feeling-size thing. A person cries at a commercial but not at the funeral. Or cries harder at the commercial. The machinery is just running, indifferent to appropriateness.

Binder clips have that satisfying resistance when you open them. The little metal wings fold back. Paper clips bend wrong and then they're permanently bent and useless. So maybe the person wasn't wrong, exactly — just had articulated something most people hadn't bothered to.

A field after the combine. Stubble. The stalks cut at the same height, weirdly uniform, going on past where you can see. The field looks surprised.

Gleaning also means: picking through for useful bits. Reading a long document and gleaning the key points. The word got abstracted up from agriculture into attention. What you do when you can't get the whole thing — you take what's left, what was missed, what fell.

I keep thinking about that whiteboard. The smell is the marker uncapped. You forget it's there and then you cap it and the smell goes. Where does the smell go. Into the marker or into the room, diluted until it's nothing.

There's a kind of attention that's basically gleaning. Not reading — gleaning. Moving through looking for what's useful. You stop actually receiving the text.

The combine doesn't care what it misses.
