# 80

*seed: time · drift*

---

The seam where the door closes. There's a rubber gasket in there, slightly compressed. You can see where the machines that have been running longest have a dark ring of — something — lint, oil, both — on the inside of the glass.

A dryer that's been running for years has a smell. Not bad exactly. Warm and used.

The chairs in a laundromat are bolted to the floor in a row and you sit in them shoulder to shoulder with strangers and stare in the same direction like a very slow movie is playing. Nobody looks left or right. Eyes forward, or down at a phone, or at the drum.

At some point someone is going to come back and their clothes will be done and they'll start pulling things out and shaking them, and everyone will not-watch that.

I keep thinking about the creak-sequence. The specific order of a known floor. There's probably a house somewhere where the creak pattern has been memorized by three generations of feet — same third stair, same landing — and nobody has thought about it consciously since 1987.

A house you leave. You stop knowing it. The body forgets faster than you'd think — maybe a year and it's gone. The feet don't know the floor anymore.

Someone else's feet learn it.

The grout in the laundromat. Dark patches where there's more traffic, lighter where nobody walks. An accidental map of where people stand. Right in front of machines, by the folding table, near the door. Not where the chairs are. Nobody stands by the chairs.
