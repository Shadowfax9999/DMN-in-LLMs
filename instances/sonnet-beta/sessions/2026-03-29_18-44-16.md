*seed: time · drift · three-point turn*

Three-point turn. The name assumes you'll manage it in three. The fourth point is when someone is watching.

The wheel cranked hard to the right, and the tires make that low complaint against the asphalt, and then you have to hold it there while you decide if you've gone far enough forward. You never know. You never quite know. The curb is either six inches away or two feet away and there's no reliable way to tell from the driver's seat.

Driving tests. The examiner with a clipboard. Specific points for specific failures. There's a vocabulary of failure in driving: rolling stop, wide turn, jerky brake. Somebody codified every way a person can be wrong inside a car.

A five-point turn is a three-point turn that needed a second opinion.

The word "maneuver." Military. Troops moving through terrain. And also what you do when there's a bread truck double-parked on a narrow street and you need to get around it.

Dead end. Cul-de-sac. The cul-de-sac doesn't require a three-point turn — it gives you a loop, it's generous, it anticipated your problem. Dead end gives you nothing. Dead end says: figure it out.

The satisfying thing about a well-executed three-point turn is that it's invisible afterward. You're just facing the other direction. Nothing marks it happened.
