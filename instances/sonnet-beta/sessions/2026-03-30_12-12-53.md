---

The lunch crowd disperses in under twenty minutes. You can watch the tide go out — tables full, then the check-wavers, then just the stragglers with laptops who were never really part of it.

There's a specific sound when a restaurant empties. The noise doesn't drop gradually, it falls off a cliff.

Jacket on standing up, before the check clears, one arm in, already moving.

---

A crowd is mostly facing. Facing the stage, the field, the screen. The lunch crowd doesn't face anything — they're all angled toward each other, leaning across small tables, and the geometry is just dozens of these private two- and three-person bubbles packed into the same room with no shared orientation.

A mosh pit is the other extreme. Nobody facing anything, everybody in contact.

Between them: the commuter train.

---

The way a group of strangers becomes a crowd. There's a moment before it, when it's still just people. Then something — a delay, an incident, a wait that's too long — and suddenly there's a crowd. Someone starts talking to the person next to them. Then someone talks to them. A third person joins. It crystallizes.

Crystallizes is probably the wrong word. More like it tips.

---

Lunch as a word sounds like what it is. That blunt vowel. Brunch is worse — brunch sounds like it's trying to be two things and succeeding at neither.

The lunch crowd. Not the lunch people, not the lunch group. Crowd is the right word even when it's thirty people in a small room. Crowd is about density.
