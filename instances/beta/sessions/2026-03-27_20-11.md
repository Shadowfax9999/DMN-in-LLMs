*seed: time · drift · a fly against a window*

---

A fly against a window knows exactly where it wants to go and is wrong about how to get there.

That sentence already explains too much.

The sound of it. That's the thing. Not the meaning. The buzz has a specific pattern — two seconds of trying, half-second pause, two seconds of trying. Like it's thinking between attempts. It isn't thinking. The pause is probably metabolic. But the rhythm sounds like thought.

A fluorescent light makes almost the same sound when the ballast is going. You stop hearing it after about ten minutes. The fly you never stop hearing.

Things that make you aware of glass:
1. A fly on the wrong side of it
2. A bird hitting it from outside
3. Windex, briefly
4. A crack
5. Your own breath when it's cold enough

The rest of the time glass is not there. That's literally its job.

I've been in rooms where I couldn't find the fly. Somewhere behind the curtain, or up near where the wall meets the ceiling, that triangle of space you never look at. Spiders look at it. Spiders know about the room's margins better than anyone who lives there.

The corners of a room near the ceiling — are those dusty because air doesn't circulate there, or because nobody can reach them? Both probably.

A broom turned upside down. That image. My grandmother going after a cobweb with the handle end of a broom, holding it like a lance. The cobweb just wraps around the tip and she has to pick it off with her fingers and then wipe her fingers on her apron.

Aprons have a pocket right at the belly. What goes in there. A thermometer. A twist-tie. A receipt you were holding when somebody asked you something.

---

The zipper on a jacket sometimes catches a tiny fold of the fabric lining and then you're stuck, working it back and forth a millimeter at a time, and there's no way to rush it. You just have to be patient with a zipper. They don't respond to force. They respond to angle.

I had a coat with a separating zipper — the kind where you have to align the pin into the retainer box at the bottom — and one side of the retainer box cracked, so every time I zipped it I had to hold the bottom together with one hand and pull up with the other. I wore that coat for two more winters like that.

The retainer box is the part that breaks on every separating zipper I've ever owned.
