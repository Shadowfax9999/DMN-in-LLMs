Margin of error. Like the phrase itself has a margin — you could mean measurement tolerance, you could mean the white space at the edge of a page, you could mean how much room you have before something goes wrong. Three completely different shapes wearing the same name.

The white space one is the one that sticks. Margins on a page. Somebody had to decide how wide they should be. There's a standard — one inch? An inch and a quarter? — and it's arbitrary but now it feels inevitable, the way all formatting decisions do once you've lived with them long enough. If someone handed you a page with half-inch margins you'd feel crowded before you read a word.

The margin on a medieval manuscript was enormous. Monks left all that space for commentary, for corrections, for later monks to argue in the margins with the text and with each other. The margin was the comments section. And the text kept shrinking to make room for more margin notes until some pages are mostly margin and the original text is this tiny column in the center surrounded by centuries of response.

Which is the opposite of how we think about margins now. Now the margin is the part you're not supposed to use.

A test you take with a scantron sheet. The bubbles are inside the margins. The margins are where the machine grips the paper. That's mechanical — the margin is literally where the apparatus holds on. And you're told don't make any stray marks outside the bubble area, which always felt like a threat, like the machine would misread your pencil smudge as an answer and you'd fail chemistry because your hand rested on the wrong part of the page.

Pencil graphite on the side of your hand. Left-handed people get that silver smear across the heel of the palm. Right-handed people get it too but less, depending on how they hold the pencil. The smear is shiny in certain light.

Graphite is a lubricant. They use it in locks. You can buy a little squeeze tube of powdered graphite and puff it into a sticky lock cylinder. It's black and dry and gets on everything. The lock works better for about six months and then you do it again.

Six months is an interesting unit. It's not a season, it's two seasons. It's not a natural cycle of anything. Half a year, but nobody plans in half-years. You plan in weeks, months, quarters, years. Six months is always described relative to something — six months from now, six months ago, six months into the lease.

Leases that start on the first of the month. Rent due on the first. Why the first? It's the most arbitrary anchor — there's nothing about the first day of a month that makes it a better collection point than the fifteenth. But the fifteenth feels mid-stream, like you'd be interrupting something. The first feels clean. Starting fresh. Even though it's just the next day after the last day of the previous month, same as any other consecutive pair of days.

Moving trucks on the last day of a month. Every apartment hallway in a city, someone's carrying a box past someone else's door. The elevator has that quilted pad hung on the walls. The pad is gray and has metal grommets at the top where it hangs from hooks. It makes the elevator feel like the inside of a shipping container. And it smells different — industrial fabric, the kind that doesn't absorb anything, just repels.

The floor of the elevator gets grit on it from people's shoes going in and out. Little crescents of dried mud from the truck tires in the parking lot. The grit crunches underfoot and you can feel each grain through thin-soled shoes. Some of the grains roll slightly when you step on them, like tiny ball bearings, and for a fraction of a second your foot feels unsure.
