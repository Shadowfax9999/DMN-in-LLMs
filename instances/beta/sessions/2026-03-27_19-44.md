The sound of a key turning that isn't yours — that's a specific kind of listening. You know your own key. The weight of it, the number of attempts.

Hotel hallways at night. Someone three doors down. The mechanism catches and you're already placing them in a room you'll never see.

A lock is a question asked in metal.

No — that's exactly the kind of sentence I'm not supposed to write.

A lock has pins. Five usually. Sometimes six. Each pin has to be pushed to a different height. The key is just a set of heights encoded as a shape. That's it. That's the whole idea. Everything else is housing.

Bump keys work because you can hit all the pins at once and for a microsecond they all jump to the shear line simultaneously. It's like getting five coins to all land heads by slapping the table.

I locked myself out once and a locksmith came in eleven minutes. He used a tension wrench and a rake pick. The whole thing took less time than I spent waiting. He charged eighty dollars. He didn't seem impressed with himself. He had a lanyard with maybe forty keys on it that weren't for anything — they were just there, weight at his hip.

The phrase "lock, stock, and barrel" — the three parts of a musket.

Barrels are curved wood held together by tension. Coopers made them. There are almost no coopers now except for whiskey.

Whiskey barrels get used exactly once for bourbon, then sold to Scottish distilleries, then sometimes sold again to hot sauce companies.

So a hot sauce store and a googly eye store are maybe three transactions apart from each other, if you go through oak.

The key sound again. Someone across the hall, coming home late on a Friday. Plastic bag. One bag, so probably not groceries. Takeout or a drugstore run. The door opens and closes and then there's just the building's hum, which is either HVAC or plumbing or both, and you can never quite find the wall it's coming from.
