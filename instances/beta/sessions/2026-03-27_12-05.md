*seed: time · drift · a tan line from a watch*

---

A tan line from a watch you're not wearing anymore. The pale band. You can see it for weeks after you stop wearing the watch, which means your skin remembers a shape your mind already forgot about.

Tan lines in general are strange — they're photographs. Developed slowly. The sandal tan, the sock tan. The one-piece versus two-piece argument is really an argument about which photograph you're willing to carry around on your body for the rest of August.

Seven-sided room. I keep thinking about that. Where do you put the couch? Every wall is too short or at the wrong angle. You'd end up with furniture floating in the middle, angled toward nothing. Like a waiting room where nobody agrees on which direction to face.

Pentagon is almost worse.

The shapes that tile:

- squares (obvious)
- equilateral triangles
- hexagons

And that's it for regular polygons. Three options. Everything else leaves gaps or overlaps. Pentagons famously don't tile, which is why you never see pentagonal bathroom floor tiles, which is why pentagonal bathrooms would feel even stranger than — no, pentagonal bathrooms would feel strange because of the walls, not the floor.

Actually certain irregular pentagons do tile. There was a whole search for them. Fifteen types found over decades, the last one in 2015 by a team using a computer, and then someone proved that was all of them. Fifteen irregular pentagons that tile the plane.

I wonder what floors made of those would look like. Probably like a headache.

The watch tan. What I was actually thinking about is that the skin underneath is the color you used to be. The rest of your arm is the color you became. So the tan line is a border between two versions of yourself and it migrates inward as you even out, the old self slowly getting overwritten by sun exposure, until there's no record left.

A seven-sided room would have a door on one wall and six walls arguing about what to do with themselves.

I had a roommate who hung a clock on every wall of our living room. Four clocks, all set to local time. Not time zones. Just four identical clocks showing the same time. I never asked why. It felt like the kind of thing where the answer would be less interesting than the question. He moved out in March and left two of them.

The two he left were on adjacent walls.
