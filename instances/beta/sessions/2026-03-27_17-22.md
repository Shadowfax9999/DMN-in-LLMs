The glass with water from earlier. There's always one. Sometimes two. They accumulate on the counter near the sink like they're waiting for something. Nobody claims them. Nobody put them there on purpose exactly, they just set them down and then the moment passed.

Gesture amplitude — that's not a phrase anyone uses but it should be. Some people take up so much kinetic space. You can feel them talking from across a room even if you can't hear them. And then there are people who barely move at all, who talk with just their eyebrows, and you have to lean in. Both kinds are exhausting in different ways if you're sitting next to them for three hours.

Meetings where you're aware of your own hands. Where do they go. On the table looks aggressive. In your lap looks like you're hiding something. Holding a pen helps but then you click it and someone notices. The pen becomes the problem. So you put it down and now your hands are back and you're thinking about them again.

A friend of mine used to fold tiny things out of straw wrappers during dinner. Not origami exactly, just — shapes. Triangles mostly. She'd have six or seven of them by the end of a meal lined up near her plate. I don't think she knew she was doing it. When you'd point it out she'd look down at them like they'd arrived on their own.

That thing where your hands do something repetitive and your brain goes somewhere else entirely. Peeling the label off a bottle. Rolling a rubber band between your fingers. Tearing the edge of a napkin into fringe. It's not fidgeting exactly because fidgeting implies restlessness and this is more like the opposite — it's what the hands do when the brain is calm enough to leave them unsupervised.

The napkin fringe thing. Waiters must see that constantly. A whole table of shredded napkins and the people have left and the conversation is gone but the napkins are a kind of record of how long everyone sat there. A nervous table shreds more. A relaxed table that stayed a long time also shreds a lot. You couldn't tell the difference from the evidence.

I had a coworker who would tear his styrofoam coffee cup into pieces during phone calls. By the end of a long call his desk would have this little drift of white fragments like fake snow. He'd sweep them into the trash without looking at them. The cup was just gone. He went through maybe three cups a day that way, not drinking from them, just dismantling them while he talked to clients about liability coverage.
