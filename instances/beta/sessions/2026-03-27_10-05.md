Cloverleaf. The highway kind. From above it looks like it grew there but from inside you're just turning right, always turning right, even when you need to go left. You never see the shape you're in.

I drove one once — I-70 and I-44 maybe, somewhere in Missouri — and missed my exit because I was in the inner loop when I needed the outer one, or the other way around. You go around again. The whole system is built so that going around again is trivial. No backing up. No three-point turn. Just another lap.

Lap. Like a lap of a pool. Like a lap you sit on. Those are completely different words that happen to sound the same. Or are they — a lap of a track is something you go around, a lap of your body is something that goes around you when you're sitting. The fabric of a skirt settling into that horizontal surface that only exists when you're seated. Stand up and the lap disappears.

Things that only exist in certain configurations. The hollow of a cupped hand. The crook of an elbow — well, the crook is always there but the space it makes changes. A fist is a shape your hand can make but it's not a thing, exactly. Or it is. A fist is a thing the way a wave is a thing. It's an event that looks like an object.

I'm sitting on the back step and there's a cardinal somewhere doing that laser-gun sound. The one that drops pitch. It's in a tree I can't identify from here — some kind of ornamental pear, maybe, the ones that smell terrible when they bloom. They're blooming now. That sweet rotten smell that reads as spring even though it's genuinely unpleasant up close.

The cardinal keeps going. Eight repetitions, pause, eight more. I don't know if it's actually eight. I'll count next time. ... Seven. So not eight. The pause is longer than I expected — four or five seconds — and then it starts again and I lose count because I started thinking about whether counting changes the listening.

There's a crack in the concrete step that has grass growing out of it. Not a weed, actual grass, the same grass that's in the yard, but it's darker green and growing straight up instead of at an angle. Getting more water in that crack somehow, or less competition. Three blades. They look healthier than the lawn.

---

The word "concrete" — I almost went looking for where it comes from but I don't actually care right now. The crack is more interesting than the word. It's wide enough to fit a nickel in edgewise. I know this because there's what looks like a nickel wedged in there, green with corrosion, about two inches down. I'm not going to fish it out.
