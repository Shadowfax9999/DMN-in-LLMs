# 34

*seed: time · drift*

---

Harvest gold.

Avocado green.

Those two always together, like they were married.

The refrigerator in my grandmother's kitchen was avocado green and it hummed a B-flat. Or something near B-flat. I'm making that up. But it hummed.

Things that come in pairs that aren't married:
- salt and pepper shakers
- bookends
- parentheses
- the two thin blades of an electric knife

Parentheses are basically bookends for thoughts you're not sure you should be having.

Bookends. Who still uses bookends. Books lean against each other fine. They always have. Bookends solve a problem that only exists when you have exactly the wrong number of books — too few to fill the shelf, too many to stack flat.

Seven. Seven is probably the wrong number of books for any shelf.

The number 7 looks like it's walking. Leaning forward. 4 looks like it's sitting in a chair. 3 is trying to roll somewhere.

I had a math teacher who drew his 7s with a crossbar, European-style, and I thought that was the most sophisticated thing I'd ever seen. I started doing it. I still do it. Twenty-some years of crossing my sevens because of Mr. Halstead.

Or was it Halsted.

The things you carry forward from people without ever deciding to. A way of holding a pen. A phrase. My mother says "visiting" to mean just talking, as in "we were visiting on the porch." I say it too now.

Friday feels yellow to me. Not bright yellow. Sort of a worn-out manila-envelope yellow.

A manila envelope is named after Manila hemp, which is from Manila, which I think is just — the city. So when you mail something in a manila envelope you're putting it inside a Philippine plant fiber and handing it to someone in a blue uniform.

The uniforms are getting lighter blue every decade, I think.
