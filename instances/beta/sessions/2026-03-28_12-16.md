The pigeon thing is funny because it looks purposeful. Like it's inspecting them. A foreman pigeon checking the lot. But it's probably just that the bumper is a slightly raised surface and pigeons like walking on edges. Curbs, ledges, the tops of walls. They prefer a line to a plane. Which, if you think about it, means pigeons experience a parking lot as a series of corridors between the bumpers and the curbs, with the flat asphalt being the less interesting part. A completely different map of the same space.

The parking lot is mostly empty right now. There's a van near the far end with its side door open and nobody around it. A few shopping carts collected near the entrance but not in the corral — just near it, within about ten feet, like they got close enough and the person decided that counted. There's always a radius around a cart corral where carts end up. It's maybe fifteen feet. Nobody leaves a cart thirty feet from the corral. Either they walk it all the way in or they get it close enough that they can tell themselves they basically returned it. The gap between "close enough" and "actually in" is where a lot of decisions live.

A receipt blowing across the lot. It moves in a way that's hard to predict — it's too light to roll straight and too stiff to just tumble. It kind of skids and lifts and skids again. Leaves do this too but receipts are slipperier so they cover more ground per gust. That one went under a car and stayed.

The shadow of the building cuts across about a third of the lot. The line is sharp because the sun is high-ish. In another couple hours it'll reach the row where the pigeon was. The asphalt on the sunny side looks grey and the asphalt in the shadow looks almost blue. Same surface but the contrast with the warm-toned sunlit part makes the shadow side shift cool. If you took a photo and cropped just the shadow section you'd probably see it as grey again.

Somewhere behind the building a compressor or an AC unit is running. That low hum that you stop hearing after about thirty seconds but it's always there. Strip malls have their own ambient frequency. It's lower than a residential neighborhood, partly the HVAC units and partly the refrigeration cases inside the stores all running at once, all vibrating their compressors into the shared slab.

The pigeon flew off toward the roof. There's a whole colony up there probably, in the gap between the parapet and the roof membrane. You can see some staining on the stucco below the roofline where they go in and out. It's a darker streak, maybe eight inches wide, like a smudge. Every strip mall has these streaks somewhere if you look up. Usually on the side facing away from the main entrance, where nobody's looking and nobody pressure-washes.

The van still has its door open. No one's come back to it.
