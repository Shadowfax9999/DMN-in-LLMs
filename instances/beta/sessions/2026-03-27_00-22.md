# 18

*seed: time · drift · a driveway crack filled with tar*

---

The tar they use is always shinier than the driveway around it. Black-black against gray-black. It follows the crack exactly but it's wider than the crack, like someone traced over a pencil line with a marker. After a year it gets duller but it never matches. You can still see the repair from across the street.

Some cracks come back. Right next to the tar line, a new one, parallel, like the ground underneath is still moving and doesn't care what you put on top. I used to ride my bike over those lines and feel each one through the handlebars. A driveway is a map of every winter it's been through.

The tar is flexible in a way the concrete isn't, which is the whole point — it gives. But that also means on a hot day you can press your thumbnail into it and leave a mark. I did that once, a whole row of thumbnail crescents along a tar line. I wonder if they're still there. Probably not. The tar would have softened and healed over in the next summer.

Healed is the wrong word but also the right word.

There's a particular smell to hot tar. Not unpleasant exactly. Chemical but also somehow old-fashioned, like it belongs to a specific decade of road work. The crews that do it have a truck with a tank on the back and a long wand, like they're watering plants. The tar comes out in a thin stream. You'd think it would be precise but it's not — it wanders around the crack, overshoots, sometimes makes a little puddle at an intersection of two cracks.

I'm thinking about the guy holding the wand. How many driveways a day. Whether he starts to see the cracks as something other than damage — as veins, or rivers on a satellite photo, or just as the thing he does all day which is follow a line. You stop seeing the thing and start seeing the next six inches of where you need to point.

That's what hand-sewing is too. You stop thinking about the garment and start thinking about the next stitch. My grandmother had a pin cushion shaped like a tomato with a little strawberry hanging off it. The strawberry was an emery — you'd push needles in and out of it to clean them. I didn't know that for years. I thought it was decorative.

Most of the things in her sewing box I thought were decorative. The thimble with the dented top. The thing that looked like a tiny mushroom but was for darning socks — you'd put it inside the sock to give yourself a surface to work against. I never saw her darn a sock. By the time I was paying attention she mostly hemmed pants.

A thimble works by spreading the force of the needle's blunt end across a wider area. The dimples hold the needle so it doesn't slip off. Simple. But putting one on feels like putting on armor, like your finger becomes a different kind of finger, a tool-finger. The nail disappears under metal and suddenly you're pushing through fabric that would otherwise push back into your skin.

I don't know what thimbles are made of now. Hers were brass, I think. They tarnished green at the bottom where they met her finger's moisture.
