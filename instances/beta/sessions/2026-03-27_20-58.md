# 69

*seed: time · drift · the click of a seatbelt*

---

The click of a seatbelt is two sounds. There's the metal-on-metal insertion and then a half-second later the webbing settles against your collarbone with a faint sliding noise. The second sound is the one nobody thinks about.

Sitting in a parked car with the engine off, seatbelt on, not going anywhere yet. The steering wheel is cold if it's morning. That particular kind of waiting where you're early and you're just sitting in the parking lot of wherever you're going and you could go in but you don't. You look at the building. You look at your hands. You read every word on the dashboard. There's a warning about airbags on the visor that you've read forty times.

The foam inside a steering wheel. What is it. Some kind of polyurethane probably. It gives slightly under your thumbs when you're gripping it in traffic. Older steering wheels were thinner, harder. My grandmother's car had a steering wheel that felt like holding a broomstick. Cream colored. The horn honked if you pushed anywhere on the center pad and she'd hit it by accident reaching for the gear shift and then say "oh!" every time like it surprised her.

Gear shifts that come out of the dashboard instead of the floor. Column shifters. Some old trucks had them. The turning signal is on the same stalk and you'd bump it trying to shift and the blinker would come on for no reason.

A parking lot at dusk when the stores are closed and there's one car left and you don't know whose it is. Could be an employee. Could be someone who parked there and walked somewhere else. Could have been there for days. There's a specific loneliness to a single car in a big parking lot that I don't think I need to say anything else about.

Felt. Like the material. The inside of a jewelry box. The bottom of cheap table lamps. Felt is just fibers pressed together without weaving — no warp, no weft, just pressure and heat and moisture matting everything into a sheet. It's one of the oldest textiles. You can pull it apart with your hands if it's thin enough, and it tears in a way that has no grain to it, just a soft ragged edge in whatever direction you pull.

The green felt on a pool table is actually worsted wool, I think. It's directional — you can brush it one way and it's smooth and the other way it resists. The balls roll differently depending on the nap. People who are serious about pool brush the table before they play.

My right knee clicks when I go down stairs but not up.
