Harmonic. The note you get by barely touching the string instead of pressing it down. Hovering over the twelfth fret, the seventh, the fifth. The string vibrates in sections instead of as a whole. It sounds like a bell that doesn't know it's a guitar.

The fifth fret harmonic and the seventh fret harmonic on adjacent strings — that's how you tune. You listen for the beating to slow down and disappear. Two frequencies getting closer together sound like a wobble, like a plane propeller on film.

Wobble.

I had a bike wheel once that wobbled and I kept riding it for weeks. You could feel it in the handlebars, this gentle pulse, and after a while it just felt like how bikes worked.

Drywall dust gets into everything. You vacuum three times and it's still on the bookshelves a week later. It has that calcium smell. Chalk smell. They might actually be related.

The word "gypsum" is one of those words that looks like it should be an animal. Some slow gray thing that lives near rivers.

Four pencils on a desk. I keep coming back to that. Why four. Three would feel like a set and five would feel like a handful but four just feels like nobody's been paying attention to the pencils. They accumulated. One rolled there from somewhere else.

The eraser on a pencil that's gone hard and just smears a pink streak instead of erasing. You flip the pencil over, try it, and the mark gets worse. Then you're committed to the mistake.

Committed to the mistake is most of drawing, actually.

I don't know what harmonic I'd be. That's a strange way to think about it. But the twelfth fret one is just the same note an octave up — it's the simplest, the most expected. The ones further up the neck get stranger and quieter and harder to find. Past the seventh fret they start to sound like they belong to a different instrument.

A piano has dampers that lift off the strings when you press the sustain pedal. All the strings are free then. You play one note and every string that shares a harmonic with it vibrates a little in sympathy. The whole piano is quietly responding to everything you play. Even the notes you didn't choose.

My neighbor is mowing. It's that time of year where it switches over from "too early to mow" to every Saturday, all at once, the whole street.

The mower sound has a harmonic too, probably. A fundamental frequency and then all the overtones from the blade and the engine and the wheels on concrete when they cross the driveway. Nobody listens for it though. You just hear "mower."

I used to like the smell of cut grass more than I do now. Not that it smells different. I think I just associate it with having to do it.
