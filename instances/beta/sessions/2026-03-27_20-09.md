# 63

*seed: time · drift · a thumbtack hole in a wall*

---

A thumbtack hole in a wall.

Smaller than you think. You notice it because of the shadow it holds. Pinprick of dark.

Paint over it and it's still there. Just white now instead of dark. A sealed hole is still a hole.

The thing about rentals — you're not supposed to leave them but everyone does. Fifty, sixty, a hundred tiny punctures behind a poster, behind a calendar, behind the thing you tore out of a magazine and stuck up because you thought you'd look at it every day. You look at it for a week.

Spackle. The word sounds like what it is.

Landlords sell those little repair kits — toothpaste works, people say. I've never tried toothpaste. I've used my thumb. Just press the surface closed. Doesn't work but it feels like it should.

---

The pegboard in the garage had holes on purpose. Holes as feature. Every quarter inch, the same diameter, the whole sheet. A grid of nothing.

Corkboard is the opposite — you make the holes yourself, wherever you want. The cork remembers. Pull the tack out and the cork tries to close but can't quite. Foam core does the same thing. Drywall doesn't try at all.

Materials that remember puncture:
- Cork (partially)
- Foam (partially)
- Skin (yes, then scar)
- Clay before firing (fully, you can smooth it)
- Clay after firing (not at all)

Wet sand at the beach. You poke it with a stick and water fills the hole from below. The hole lasts about four seconds.

I had a map on my wall in college with tacks in places I said I'd go. Red tacks. Most of them are still just tack-holes in a wall I moved out of eleven years ago. Someone else's wall now. Someone probably painted over them or maybe they're behind a tapestry or one of those foam headboard things.

The tacks themselves are in a drawer somewhere, or a landfill. Brass-colored steel, not actual brass.
