Things I can hear if I hold still right now (imagined):

- Refrigerator compressor
- Something ticking that isn't a clock
- My own swallowing
- A car with one window down, passing

Things that are held together only by tension:

- Barrels
- Knots
- Dry stone walls
- A conversation at a dinner party where someone just said something political

Things I've never seen the inside of:

- A parking meter
- A fire hydrant
- A golf ball (actually I have — someone cut one open in middle school and there was rubber band wound tight around a rubber core, or maybe that was an old design)
- A telephone pole. What's in there? Just wood all the way through? Creosote-soaked wood.

Who decides how tall a telephone pole is? There must be a spec. Forty feet? Some of them look taller than others but that might just be the hill.

Things that come in exactly one size and things that come in many sizes. Manhole covers: one size. Envelopes: many sizes but you only ever use two. Eggs come in sizes but I've never once bought anything other than large.

A question: has anyone ever, in a grocery store, reached past the large eggs to buy medium eggs on purpose?

Another question: why is the small size at coffee shops called "tall"

These are not interesting questions. They're the questions you ask when you're nineteen and think observations about consumer culture are insights.

Plywood comes in one thickness. No — plywood comes in many thicknesses but I only ever think of three-quarter inch. My dad called it "three-quarter ply" which I don't think is the right term but I knew what he meant. He also called adjustable pliers "channel-locks" which is a brand name. And adhesive bandages "Band-Aids." And petroleum jelly "Vaseline." Genericized trademarks. Thermos. Jacuzzi. Dumpster — that one surprises people.

I keep almost starting to explain how genericized trademarks work. I know how they work. That's not the point. The point is my dad standing in the garage saying "hand me the channel-locks" and me not knowing which thing he meant because there were four kinds of pliers on the pegboard.

The pegboard had outlines drawn around each tool in black marker. Like a crime scene for wrenches.

Friday evening and the light is doing the thing where it comes in almost horizontal through west-facing windows. Everything on a shelf casts a shadow twice its length.
