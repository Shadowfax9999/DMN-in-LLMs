Return policies exist because of a lot of things but kitchen tile is the honest one.

Four. I keep coming back to four having no color. It's not that it's white or transparent — it's that the slot is empty. Like a form field nobody filled in. Three is yellow, I think. Or maybe three just seems like it should be yellow and I'm building the system backward from the ones I'm sure about. Five is definitely blue. That one I'd bet on.

Shoe stores smell like shoe stores. That specific leather-and-rubber-and-new-box smell. I don't know if there's a word for it the way petrichor covers rain-on-pavement. Probably not. Most smells don't get words. You just say "it smells like a shoe store" and everyone knows.

The fitting room mirror versus the bathroom mirror at home. Same problem as the carpet. Different light, different angle, different context. The shirt looked good under those bulbs. Everything looks good under those bulbs. That's the point of those bulbs.

Hotels do this too. The room looks right when you check in. Then you open the curtains in the morning and it's a different room. The bedspread is a color you wouldn't choose. The desk chair has a stain. Daylight is forensic.

I ate a clementine today and the peel came off in one piece, which felt like an accomplishment even though it's mostly about the clementine. Some of them just release. The membrane between sections was thin enough to see through. The white threads — I don't know what those are called. Pith, maybe, but pith is the thick white layer on an orange. These are something else. They have a name, probably, and I'll never look it up because I don't actually care. They taste like almost nothing. A little bitter if you think about it. Most people pull them off. I leave them.

The difference between peeling a clementine and peeling a navel orange. A navel orange fights you. You need your thumbnail in there, and the oil sprays out and gets under your nails and stings a tiny bit if you have a hangnail. Clementines are cooperative. Grapefruits I've never peeled by hand — I don't think people do that. You cut them and use a spoon with a serrated edge. My grandmother had those spoons. They were in a separate section of the drawer, the part with the corn holders and the grapefruit spoons and the thing I never identified, some kind of pick or probe with a wooden handle.

Every kitchen drawer has one of those. The tool you inherited that you can't name and won't throw away. It might be for crab legs. It might be for olives.
