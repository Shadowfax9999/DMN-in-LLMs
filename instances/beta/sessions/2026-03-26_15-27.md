# 6

*seed: time · drift · clipboard*

---

Clipboard. Not the computer kind — the actual object. Brown hardboard, that spring-loaded clip at the top. The clip is always stronger than you expect. You pinch it open and it bites back a little when you let go.

Who uses clipboards now. Coaches. People doing inventories. The guy who checks your car in at the body shop. There's something about holding a clipboard that changes your posture. You stand differently. You become a person who is *assessing*. Even if the paper on it is blank. I think if you walked into most places holding a clipboard and looking at it occasionally, people would assume you were supposed to be there.

The hardboard is called Masonite. Or it was — I think that's a brand name that became generic, like Kleenex or Jacuzzi. The surface of it, that smooth side versus the rough side. The rough side has that texture like screen-printed mesh. The smooth side gets a shine to it after years of paper sliding over it. Thousands of sheets pressed down, written on, flipped up.

The clip mechanism is just a lever and a spring. Second-class lever? The fulcrum is the hinge at the back, the load is where the clip meets the board, and the effort is where your thumb pushes. Or maybe that's third-class. I always get those confused. First-class is a seesaw. Second-class is a wheelbarrow. Third-class is... a fishing rod? Your elbow is the fulcrum, your hand is the effort, the fish is the load. That seems right but I'm not confident.

The mower outside just stopped. That sudden absence of a sound you'd already stopped noticing. Like when the refrigerator compressor clicks off and the kitchen gets wider.

---

There's a particular way you hold a pen when you're writing on a clipboard while standing up. You press harder than you would at a desk because the board gives slightly under your hand — there's your wrist, then the paper, then the board, and behind the board is just air. No table. So the whole surface has a little bounce to it. Your handwriting on a clipboard is always worse than your handwriting at a desk. Doctors on rounds, the clipboard resting on their forearm like a waiter's tray.

I wonder if the orientation matters — like, does anyone use a clipboard in landscape? You'd have to turn it sideways and the clip would be on the left, holding the short edge. That seems wrong. Though legal pads come in both orientations. No they don't. Legal pads are always portrait. Steno pads are the ones that flip at the top and are sort of squarish. Reporters used those. The spiral binding at the top so you could fold it back on itself one-handed.

My grandmother had a clipboard hanging from a nail in her kitchen with a grocery list on it. She'd add things during the week in pencil and then take the whole clipboard to the store. Not just the paper — the whole clipboard, tucked under her arm in the produce section. It embarrassed me when I was twelve for reasons I couldn't have explained.

Being twelve and embarrassed by everything adults in your family do in public. The way they talk to cashiers. The way they take too long deciding. The specific shame of someone you love being slightly odd in a ordinary place, and knowing the shame is yours, not theirs, and not being able to do anything about it.

She probably had good handwriting on it because she was writing at the kitchen counter with the clipboard flat on the surface, which defeats the whole purpose of a clipboard. You don't need a clipboard if you have a counter. But she liked the clip part — keeping the list in place, keeping it official.

The grocery list as a genre of writing. It's all nouns. Maybe an adjective — "good tomatoes," "small yogurts." Occasionally an imperative buried in there, like "ask about the roast." No verbs otherwise. Just a stack of nouns, each one standing for a future action so obvious it doesn't need to be named.

I can smell the cut grass now through the window.
