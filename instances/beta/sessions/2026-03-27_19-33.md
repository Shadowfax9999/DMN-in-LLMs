Sand in the grooves of a aluminum canoe is almost geological if you leave it long enough.

The hammered texture — why hammer aluminum from the inside? Something about strength. Corrugation does the same thing to cardboard.

A canoe stored upside down on sawhorses collects leaves on its hull. From underneath it looks like a ceiling.

Dichloromethane. That's also in paint stripper. The smell that makes your eyes water before you realize you're smelling anything.

Friday evening and somewhere a lawnmower is running late. Not here. Somewhere in the concept of Friday evening.

The drinking bird never stops unless you take away its water glass. Which feels like cheating. The whole toy is about perpetual motion that isn't perpetual motion, and the way you prove it's not perpetual is by removing the thing it depends on. But you could say that about anything alive.

No — skip that.

A lock chamber fills from the bottom. I think. Or from sluices in the gates. The water doesn't pour over the top, it seeps up, and the boat just rises like it's on an elevator that happens to be made of river.

Googly eyes on a lock gate would be enormous. Each eye the size of a dinner plate.

There's a store somewhere that sells only googly eyes in different sizes. There has to be. The economy supports a store that sells only hot sauce, a store that sells only left-handed things, a store that sells only magnets. Googly eyes seems viable.

The smallest googly eyes are maybe 4 millimeters across and people put them on shrimp.

The pupil in a googly eye is just a black disk sitting on a concave transparent lens, held in by friction and gravity. It's one moving part. Less than a drinking bird. Less than almost any toy. And it's funnier than most of them.

The black disk slides around on a film of air, basically.

I've never actually taken one apart to check.
