The knee thing is asymmetric in a way that seems like it shouldn't be. Going down, the kneecap is loaded differently — it's being pulled against the groove while the quad is braking. Going up, the quad is just pulling. So the click makes sense mechanically but it still feels like a glitch, like something is catching on something it shouldn't reach.

I used to think cartilage was smooth like plastic. It's not. Under magnification it looks fibrous, almost hairy. Wet and slightly translucent.

There's a specific kind of quiet that happens in a car parked in a driveway after you turn the engine off but before you open the door. The radio's gone, the fan's gone, and for a few seconds the car is just a box you're sitting in. The seatbelt is still on. The keys are in your hand but you haven't moved yet. Sometimes you stay there longer than makes sense. Not thinking about anything exactly. Just not having gotten out yet.

The hood ticks as the engine cools. Metal contracting unevenly.

Driveways slope more than you think. You only notice when you're standing on one in socks, or when a ball rolls away from where you set it. My parents' driveway had a crack running diagonally across it that collected a thin line of moss every spring. The crack widened by maybe a millimeter a year. Nobody fixed it. It was the kind of deterioration that doesn't cross the threshold into something you'd act on — you just watch it happen across decades.

Moss doesn't have roots. It has rhizoids, which anchor it but don't absorb water the way roots do. The whole plant absorbs water through its surface. That's why it dries out so fast and comes back so fast. You can crumble dry moss between your fingers and it feels like nothing, like dust with structure, and then two days of rain and it's bright green and spongy again.

The color of wet moss at night under a porch light is not a color I could name. Somewhere between olive and the green of a wine bottle but lit from the surface, not from behind.

Porch lights attract moths and the moths attract spiders and by August there are webs in every corner of the fixture. The webs get thick with dust and dead wings. Nobody cleans those either, usually, until someone's coming over and you notice the house the way a visitor would. Then you get a broom and sweep the corners and the spiders rebuild in about three days.

Orb weavers rebuild every morning anyway. They eat the old web to reclaim the protein and spin a new one. That's an investment cycle measured in hours.

I've never actually watched one build from the start. I've seen them working on sections — adding the spiral, repairing a gap — but the initial framework, the bridge lines that span a gap between two anchor points, I've only read about. The spider lets out a strand of silk and the wind carries it until it sticks to something. Trial and error. Most of the strands don't land. The spider pulls them back in, eats the silk, tries again.

The failed attempts don't leave evidence.

Silk comes out as liquid and hardens on contact with air. Multiple spinnerets producing different types — some sticky, some structural, some for wrapping prey. A spider uses different glands the way a painter uses different brushes, except the painter doesn't manufacture the brushes out of their own body while standing on the canvas.

I had a chemistry teacher who kept a tarantula in the classroom in a terrarium next to the periodic table. It almost never moved during class. You'd come in Monday and it would be in a different corner than Friday but you never saw the transition. It was like a screensaver that updated once a day. Her name was Rosie, which felt like a placeholder name, the kind of name you give something when you can't tell if it has a personality yet. The teacher's name was Mr. Alderman and he wore short-sleeve dress shirts every single day regardless of season. Even in January. The shirt was always tucked in tight enough that you could see the outline of his undershirt beneath it.

I don't remember what he taught us about the periodic table. I remember the shirts and the tarantula and that the room smelled like the gas they put in the Bunsen burners even when the burners weren't lit — that mercaptan smell, the additive that makes natural gas detectable. The safety measure you can't not notice. It's in there at something like four parts per million and your nose picks it up immediately. One of the most detectable substances by the human nose, supposedly.

The classroom had those black-topped lab tables with a shallow lip around the edge to contain spills. The surface was some kind of phenolic resin, matte and slightly rough. You could scratch it with a compass point but not with a fingernail. Thirty years of students had scratched initials and small drawings into the edges near the stools, where the teacher couldn't easily see. The record of boredom is always at the margins.
