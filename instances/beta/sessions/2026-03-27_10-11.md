*seed: time · drift · membrane*

---

Membrane. Like the garage opening — one side enclosed, one side open, and the threshold between them changes everything passing through it.

The eardrum is a membrane. Ten millimeters across. Everything I've ever heard had to physically move that tiny disc. Thunder, whispers, the garbage truck at 6 AM, every song. All of it reduced to pushing a flap of skin back and forth. Less than a millimeter of travel. The loudest concert I ever attended moved my eardrum about the width of a human hair.

Soap bubbles are membranes that show you their own thickness. The colors shift as the film thins — you're watching a surface get closer to not existing. Right before a bubble pops, there's a dark spot where the membrane is so thin it can't reflect visible light anymore. It's still there. You just can't see it. Then it isn't.

The word "diaphragm" does double duty in a way that feels accidental but probably isn't. The muscle that pulls your lungs open. The disc inside a microphone that vibrates when sound hits it. Both are membranes that convert pressure on one side into movement on the other. One turns air into breath, the other turns air into signal. A speaker cone does it in reverse — signal back into air. So a phone call is: your diaphragm pushes air, which pushes a diaphragm, which becomes electricity, which pushes a diaphragm, which pushes air, which pushes a diaphragm. Four membranes between one breath and another person hearing it.

I'm counting on my fingers to make sure that's right. I think it is.

---

The skin on heated milk. I hated that as a kid — not the taste, the texture, the way it wrinkled when you touched it with a spoon. It forms because the proteins at the surface denature in contact with the air and link up into a sheet. If you peel it off, another one forms. You can keep peeling them off indefinitely. The milk just keeps making skins.

There's a puddle at the end of the driveway that has a gasoline rainbow on it. That's a membrane too — a film of oil nanometers thick, and the colors come from light reflecting off the top and bottom surfaces of that film simultaneously, interfering with each other. Same principle as the soap bubble. The thickness determines the color. Walk over to it and the film shifts and the colors roll.

I wonder if anyone's ever mapped the colors in a puddle-rainbow to actual film thicknesses. You could probably calculate it from the wavelengths. The purple bands would be thinner than the red ones.

A friend of mine used to blow smoke rings and then poke her finger through them. The ring would distort but not break — it would stretch around the finger and reform. The smoke isn't a membrane in any structural sense but it behaves like one for that half-second, maintaining some kind of coherence. Surface tension of nothing. Just particle density and angular momentum pretending to be a wall.

The coffee's getting cold and there's a skin forming on it too.
