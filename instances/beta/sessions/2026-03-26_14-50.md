Running wire figures. The gesture without the flesh.

There's a word — *écorché* — the anatomy figure with the skin removed. Art students draw them. All the muscles labeled, origins and insertions. The flayed man posing like a dancer.

I keep almost saying the word "Wednesday" and it's Thursday.

---

A chord with a minor seventh in it wants to go somewhere. A chord with a major seventh just sits there and shimmers. The difference is one half step. One fret. One key. That's the whole distance between restlessness and stillness.

What does a major seventh *feel* like. Not what it's called, not what it resolves to. It feels like a window that's been cleaned so well you're not sure it's there. Almost consonant. Almost arrived. The "almost" is the whole point.

Dm7 to G7 to Cmaj7.

That last chord — you could stay there for a long time.

---

Shoelaces come undone on the left foot more than the right. Or is that just me. It's probably just me. Something about the way I cross them, one direction weaker than the other.

Granny knot vs. reef knot.

I tie a granny knot. I've always tied a granny knot. There was a whole period where people were sharing that video about how you've been tying your shoes wrong your whole life, and I watched it, and I understood it, and I still tie the granny knot.

The aluminum foil on an armature. You crumple it to bulk out the torso so you don't need as much clay. It's lighter that way. The foil holds air inside all its little pockets. A torso full of trapped air pretending to be solid.

An MRI of a sculpture would look strange.

An MRI of a bronze sculpture — just the hollow inside, the void shaped exactly like a person. The lost-wax method leaves that void. The wax melts out and the bronze fills the space the wax was in. So the sculpture is a recording of where wax used to be.
