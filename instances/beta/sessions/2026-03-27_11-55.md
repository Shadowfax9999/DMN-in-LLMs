The desk thing is real. I have a zone where pens go and I never chose it. Left side, slightly back. If someone moved them to the right side I'd move them back without thinking about it, the way you'd scratch an itch.

Heptagons. I was wrong about the 50p — I think it's actually seven-sided. No, nine? It's some odd number because odd-sided coins roll smoothly in vending machines. Something about the width being constant no matter how you rotate them. A Reuleaux triangle does that too. Those are the shapes that look like rounded triangles, and they can rotate inside a square, which means you can drill a square hole. That seems like it shouldn't work.

Drilling a square hole. The bit itself isn't square. It's the rounded triangle spinning off-axis inside a template, and the corners trace out something very close to square. Not perfectly square — there's a tiny radius at each corner. I wonder if anyone has ever looked at a square hole drilled that way and been bothered by the corners.

The arrangement on a desk. There's a study — or I think there's a study, or maybe someone just told me this — about how people in offices gradually rotate their monitors over months so the screen faces slightly away from the door. Privacy drift. The angle changes by fractions of a degree and nobody notices until one day the IT person comes to fix something and has to walk around to see the screen.

Privacy drift would be a good band name. No it wouldn't.

My handwritten 7 has a crossbar through it. I picked that up from a math teacher in — I don't remember which year. She crossed her 7s and her Zs, and I adopted the 7 but not the Z, and I've never thought about why one stuck and the other didn't. Maybe because I write more 7s than Zs. Or maybe the crossed Z looked too European and I was thirteen and self-conscious. The crossed 7 just looked like I knew what I was doing.

There's a grocery store receipt in my pocket right now. There isn't, but I can feel the ghost of one — that specific crumple.

Constant-width curves. The 50p. Manhole covers are circles because circles can't fall through their own hole, since the diameter is constant in every direction. But a Reuleaux triangle manhole cover wouldn't fall through either, for the same reason. So why aren't manhole covers triangular? Probably just easier to manufacture. Or tradition. Circles don't have an orientation, so you can't put them back wrong.

You can't put a circle back wrong. That's also why most pill bottles have round caps — no, pill bottles have round caps because of the threading. That's different.

A seven-sided room would be deeply unpleasant to furnish.
