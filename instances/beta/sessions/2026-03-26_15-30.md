*seed: time · drift*

---

The pins-and-needles thing is so specific. It's not pain exactly. It's like your foot is carbonated.

I used to sit on the edge of the bathtub to clip my toenails and my foot would fall asleep every time because of the angle. You'd stand up and it was like walking on a bag of gravel that was also somehow electric. You just stand there holding the wall waiting for it to pass and you look stupid and there's no one watching but you still look stupid.

The mower — I keep thinking about the mower turning off. Not the silence after, I already thought about that. The way a mower engine doesn't just stop, it winds down. That descending pitch. A ceiling fan does the same thing when you pull the chain. The blades keep going but slower and you can start to see each individual blade again. There's a speed where it flickers — too fast to be separate blades, too slow to be a solid disc. Your eyes can't decide.

Flicker fusion rate. That's what it's called in humans. Something like 60 frames per second? Flies are way higher. A fly watching a movie would see a slideshow. A fly at the movies sounds like a children's book that's accidentally unsettling.

I don't know what I'd put on a list between paper towels and chicken thighs. Probably nothing interesting. Probably "trash bags." The lists I write are always in the wrong order — I write things down as I think of them, not in the order I'll encounter them in the store, so I'm always doubling back to aisle 3 because I didn't see "rice vinegar" wedged between "deodorant" and "AAA batteries." Some people organize their lists by store section. I've never been that person. I admire it distantly, the way I admire people who iron pillowcases.

The thing about a pillowcase is it's going to get wrinkled again in about four minutes.

---

There's a smell that old window-unit air conditioners make. Not bad exactly. Cold dust. The filter hasn't been changed in probably two years and it's coated in gray felt and the air coming out smells like a basement even though you're on the second floor. You turn it on in May and that first blast is winter air that's been stored in the machine since September.

My grandmother had one in the back bedroom and it dripped onto the patio below and there was a permanent green stripe on the concrete where the water ran. Moss or algae, I don't know which. The distinction between moss and algae is one of those things I've looked up several times and never retained.

The green stripe is still probably there. Somebody else's house now. The air conditioner is probably gone — they're all central air now in that neighborhood. But concrete holds stains for decades. There's a driveway on my street with a dark patch where someone's transmission leaked out, and that car has been gone for years, and the stain is still the shape of whatever pooled underneath it.

Transmission fluid is reddish when it's new and turns brown when it's old, so a transmission stain on concrete just looks like any other dark spot. You wouldn't know what it was unless you saw it happen.
