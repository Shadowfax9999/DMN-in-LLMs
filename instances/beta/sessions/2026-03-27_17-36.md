That's the thing about listening faces. You develop one and it sticks. Mine is apparently a slight frown, which people read as disagreement when really it's just my face paying attention. Someone told me that once and now I think about it every time I'm in a meeting, which means I'm thinking about my face instead of listening, which probably makes the frown worse.

Aluminum under a bandsaw sounds different from steel too. Higher. Thinner. Steel has that grinding baritone and aluminum is more of a whine. You can hear what someone's cutting across a shop floor without looking.

Friday at 5:30 feels different from Friday at 5:30 six months ago when it was dark by now. The light is still out. That stretch in March and April where you keep noticing it, keep being mildly surprised that it's still light when you leave somewhere. By June you've forgotten it was ever dark at this hour.

There's a bird outside that's doing a two-note thing. Down then up. Over and over. Not a song exactly — more like it's checking. Like sonar. I don't know what bird it is. I never learned birds by sound the way some people do. My uncle could do it and it seemed like a magic trick. He'd just say "towhee" without looking up from whatever he was doing. I didn't know what a towhee was. I still don't really know what a towhee looks like, I just know the word and that my uncle could identify one by ear while reading the newspaper.

The newspaper. That's a whole object that's basically gone. Not the news, the physical thing. The way it folded wrong once you'd opened it and you could never get it back to its original shape. The ink on your fingers. Grey on your fingertips, like the aluminum dust but lighter, more even. You'd see people on the subway with grey thumbs and grey index fingers and you knew what they'd been reading. Nobody reads a physical newspaper on the subway anymore. They read phones with clean hands.

I wonder if newsprint ink was always soy-based or if that was a switch at some point.

The two-note bird is still going.
