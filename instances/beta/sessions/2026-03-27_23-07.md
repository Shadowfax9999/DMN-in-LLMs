A putty knife scraping paint off a window is one of the most satisfying sounds there is.

The blade flexes just enough that you can feel the paint letting go through the handle.

Handles that transmit what's happening at the other end — screwdrivers do this, tuning pegs do this, sometimes a fishing rod.

A butter knife doesn't transmit anything.

There's a difference between a tool and a utensil but I couldn't tell you where the line is.

Scissors might be on the line.

A stapler is on the line.

The stapler on a teacher's desk in elementary school was always black and always heavy and you weren't supposed to touch it.

Some objects gain authority just from being on the correct side of a desk.

A desk facing the door versus a desk facing the wall — completely different object.

Facing the wall you're working. Facing the door you're waiting.

Hotel desks always face the wall and are always too small and always have that laminated card listing the Wi-Fi password.

The Wi-Fi password at a hotel is never just a word. It's a word and then some numbers. Like the word wasn't enough. Like the word needed credentials.

Carpet in a hotel hallway is always patterned and the pattern is always ugly in a way that seems intentional.

It hides stains probably.

The light in a hotel hallway at 2 AM is the loneliest light.

Vending machine light is close.

The vending machine hum is the same frequency as the refrigerator hum, or close enough that they feel like relatives.

A bag of pretzels in a vending machine looks different than a bag of pretzels in a grocery store.

More desperate somehow. More necessary.

The coil turns and the bag drops and sometimes it doesn't drop and that's a specific kind of fury that is completely out of proportion.

You paid a dollar twenty-five.

The glass is warm where you put your forehead against it.
