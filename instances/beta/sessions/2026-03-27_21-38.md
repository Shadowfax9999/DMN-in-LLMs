Reflex. The knee one. The doctor's hammer isn't really a hammer. It has a triangular rubber head, almost like a doorstop on a handle.

What does the knee actually look like from the side when it does that? The foot just goes. You didn't send it anywhere.

Sneezing in bright light. Some people have that and some don't. I have it. Walking out of a matinee into a parking lot — three sneezes, every time, like a password.

The word "matinee" wearing a hat it didn't earn. It just means morning. French. But nobody goes to a morning matinee. They go at 1:45.

---

A clarinet reed is a rectangle with a slope. That's it. A tiny wooden ramp. You clamp it to the mouthpiece with a ligature, which is just a fancy word for a thing that holds another thing down. The whole apparatus looks medical.

I never got past the chalumeau register.

Chalumeau. That's a word that sits in the mouth like it's made of the sound it describes — low, hollow, the "lu" dropping into "meau" the way the tone drops into the instrument's lowest range.

Three things I can picture clearly from band class: the inside of a instrument case lined with blue velvet that smelled like spit and metal. The cork grease tube, white, always lost. The plastic cap for the mouthpiece that someone would inevitably step on.

---

Do reflexes get faster when you're cold? Or slower. I think slower. Everything slower. Fingers especially. Trying to get keys out of a pocket in February.

The pocket thing connects back to the conversation-ending gesture. Hand goes to pocket, finds keys, and now there's a prop. Jingling keys is the universal "I have to go" signal that isn't rude because it's not verbal. You're not saying you want to leave. Your keys are just... out now.

Some signals only work because nobody acknowledges them as signals.

A yawn during a meeting. Checking a watch. Turning slightly toward a door. Picking up a bag without putting it on your shoulder yet — just holding it, like you might put it back down, but you won't.

The bag-holding move. That's the one that has no plausible other interpretation and yet nobody ever calls it out.

I used to have a canvas bag with a broken zipper that I carried for about two years longer than I should have. The zipper pull came off and I used a paperclip, and then the paperclip straightened out, and then I just left it open. Everything fell out once in a rain and I kept using it.

The threshold for replacing something you use every day is so much higher than the threshold for replacing something you use once a month. The daily thing becomes invisible. You stop comparing it to what it could be. The monthly thing, you re-encounter it each time and think, this is bad.

My colander has a crack and water drains out the side before it drains through the holes.
