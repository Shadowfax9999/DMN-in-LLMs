# 31

*seed: time · drift*

---

One guy. One shop. One of those ticket systems with the carbon paper.

Businesses that are always singular: shoe repair, locksmith, watch battery replacement, the place that re-canes chairs.

Businesses where the person *is* the business and when they retire it just closes.

The key-cutting machine has that same whine. Higher pitch though.

Things I've held but never bought: a torque wrench, a French press at someone else's house, one of those metal things that gets avocado pits out.

The avocado tool seems like it shouldn't need to exist. A spoon works. But someone designed it and someone manufactured it and it's in a drawer.

Drawers that are 80% things you don't use. Everyone has one. The junk drawer is actually the honest drawer — it's the only one that admits what it is.

I wonder if "junk" has always meant worthless or if it once meant something else.

No — I'm not going to look that up right now.

The feeling of opening a drawer at someone else's house looking for scissors and seeing all their stuff. Batteries, rubber bands, a receipt, a pen from a hotel. You know them differently after that.

Rubber bands dry out. They get that dusty pale look and then they snap the next time you stretch one. How long does that take. A year? Two?

The smell of a new rubber band versus an old one.

A new box of crayons versus a used one. The tips.

Crayola has a factory tour somewhere. Pennsylvania I think.

There's a certain kind of field trip that only exists in elementary school — the factory tour, the fire station, the water treatment plant. After that you never tour anything again unless you're a tourist.

Being a tourist is agreeing to be guided. Being a child is the same thing but you don't agree to it.

The feeling of a field trip bus. Vinyl seats that stick to your legs in September.

The bus driver never went inside with you.

I never thought about what the bus driver did during the field trip. Just sat there, I guess. Read a book. Ate a sandwich from a cooler.
