Water finding level isn't fast. In a lock chamber it takes minutes. You can hear it.

The pencils at mini golf courses are the same as the IKEA ones. Somehow worse. The graphite is chalky.

A lock chamber is just a room that fills. The boat doesn't move. The water moves and the boat is on the water. You rise without doing anything.

I sat in a canoe once in completely still water and couldn't tell I was floating. The surface looked solid. Then someone threw a stick and the ripple reached me and the whole canoe remembered it was a boat.

Level isn't flat. Level follows the curve of the earth. A long enough lake is curved.

Spring evenings have a particular fifteen minutes where it's warm enough to stay outside but the light is going and you can feel both things at once. The warmth saying stay, the dark saying go. Nobody talks about it but everybody adjusts — someone goes in for a sweatshirt, someone else moves to a chair that's still in the last sun.

Googly eyes on a lock chamber. Two big ones on the upstream gate.

The wavy drinking bird works by evaporation cooling the head, which changes the vapor pressure in the tube, which draws the fluid up, which tips the bird forward. I think. The fluid is dichloromethane maybe. It's one of those explanations I've heard three times and never retained past the conversation.

There's a particular frustration of knowing you've known something. Not forgetting — that's different. This is remembering that you once understood it clearly, in a specific room, someone explaining it, and now you have the shape of the explanation but not the content. Like a box that's the right size but empty.

The canoe was green. Aluminum, painted green, with a sort of hammered texture on the inside that collected sand no matter how much you rinsed it.
