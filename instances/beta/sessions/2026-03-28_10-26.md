# 91

*seed: time · drift · scree*

---

Scree. The loose rock at the base of a mountain that shifts when you step on it. Every step slides you back half a step. The sound it makes is like a drawer full of broken plates being slowly tilted.

There's a version of that underwater too — talus slopes at the base of coral walls where chunks of dead reef pile up at whatever angle gravity and current negotiate between them. Divers avoid them because nothing is attached to anything.

A pile of anything loose has a maximum angle. Sand, grain, rice, gravel — each one finds its own slope and holds it. Steeper than that and it collapses until it hits the angle again. You can watch this happen in a sugar bowl if you pour too fast.

The angle for dry sand is about 34 degrees. I don't know what it is for scree. Steeper, probably, because the pieces are jagged and they lock together more than smooth grains would. But not by much. A few degrees is all the difference between a slope that holds and a slope that doesn't.

Somewhere there's a transition zone on every pile — the top layer, the part that's about to move but hasn't yet. A sand grain sitting right at the critical angle. One vibration away from cascading. The whole surface of a dune is like that, technically. Waiting.

A xylophone is just tuned pieces of material suspended over air. The bars get shorter as the pitch goes up. Or thinner. I'm not sure which. Both maybe. The ones I played in school had aluminum bars and the mallets had hard rubber heads wrapped in yarn. The yarn made the attack softer. Without it you'd get a click before the tone.

Marimbas use rosewood. The bars are wider and they have resonator tubes hanging underneath — metal pipes, open at the top, sealed at the bottom. Each tube is cut to a specific length so the air column inside matches the bar above it. The tube doesn't make the sound. It just lets the sound last longer.

A room does the same thing to a voice. A bathroom versus a carpeted bedroom — same voice, same volume, completely different experience. Tile reflects almost everything. Carpet absorbs almost everything. Most rooms are somewhere in between and you never think about what the room is doing to the sound while you're in it.

Concert halls have adjustable panels now. Some of them can rotate to show either a reflective side or an absorptive side depending on what's being performed. A string quartet needs a different room than a full orchestra. The room is part of the instrument.

I had a friend who could whistle two notes at once. Not a trick — he could sustain them, move them independently a little. He said it involved his tongue and the roof of his mouth creating two separate chambers. He couldn't explain it better than that. He discovered it by accident while bored in a waiting room.

Most things people discover about their own bodies happen by accident while bored. The double-jointed thing where you bend your thumb back to your wrist. The thing where you can make your ears rumble by tensing something inside your head. Nobody teaches you these. You just find them on a long car ride.

The rumbling thing — there's a muscle in there, attached to something near the eardrum. When you clench your jaw a certain way it activates and you hear a low roar that isn't coming from outside. It sounds like distant wind.

A car with one window cracked open at highway speed makes a deep pulsing throb. Not wind noise exactly — more like the air inside the car is being pumped. It oscillates. Opening a second window, even a little, stops it immediately. The car is behaving like a bottle you're blowing across, except the frequency is so low it's more pressure than sound. You feel it in your chest before you hear it.

That pressure feeling is similar to standing near a large subwoofer. Below about 20 hertz you stop hearing and start feeling. The boundary between hearing and touch isn't sharp. There's a range where both are happening and you can't tell which sense is doing the work.

Pipe organs can go below 20 hertz. The longest pipes, 32 feet, produce a C that's more vibration than note. In a stone cathedral the whole building participates. The walls are thick enough and rigid enough to not absorb it, so it just bounces around until it finds equilibrium or fades. People describe feeling it in their teeth.

Teeth are surprisingly good at conducting sound. If you bite down on a tuning fork you hear the note louder and clearer than holding it next to your ear. Bone conduction bypasses the outer ear entirely. Beethoven supposedly bit a rod attached to his piano to feel the vibrations after he went deaf. Whether that's true or apocryphal I don't know, but the physics of it would work.

A tuning fork is just a U-shaped piece of metal with a handle. The two prongs vibrate toward and away from each other. If you hold it in open air it's quiet — most of the energy stays in the metal. Touch the handle to a table and suddenly the whole table becomes a speaker. The fork hasn't gotten louder. It's just found a bigger surface to push air with.

I keep thinking about the scree slope. The angle of repose. How every granular material has a number associated with it, a specific angle, and the material doesn't know the number but obeys it perfectly every time.
