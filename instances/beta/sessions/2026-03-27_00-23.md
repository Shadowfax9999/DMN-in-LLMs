# 19

*seed: time · drift · the particular weight of an empty picture frame*

---

An empty picture frame is heavier than it should be. Not physically — physically it's lighter without the glass and the backing and the photo. But picking one up off a shelf, there's a wrongness to it. The hand expects resistance and gets almost nothing. Like picking up a suitcase you thought was full.

I held one last week, actually. No — not last week. Some other time. A frame face-down on a table and I turned it over and there was just the cardboard backing with that little easel leg, and the front was empty, no glass even, just the lip where the glass would sit. A gutter. That's what it is, a shallow channel. You could run water through it and it would hold, barely. Surface tension doing the work.

Surface tension is the thing where water at a boundary acts like it has a skin. Insects walk on it. But I'm not going to talk about how surface tension works.

The frame was oak, I think. Or oak-colored. There's a difference that matters to someone. Not to me, not that day. I put it back face-down.

---

Friday has a sound. Not the word — the day. Friday night specifically. There's a particular quality to ambient noise after about 11 PM on a Friday that's different from the same hour on a Tuesday. More engines at odd intervals. Someone's bass from a car three streets over, just the pressure of it, not the melody. A screen door. The Tuesday version of midnight is quieter but also somehow louder because each individual sound stands out more. Friday midnight is a texture, Tuesday midnight is a sequence of events.

I'm in a hotel right now. Not really. But I'm thinking about hotels. The particular deadness of hotel room air. The HVAC hum that's pitched to be ignorable but isn't, quite. You notice it when it cycles off. The gap where it stops is the loudest moment. Then it kicks back on and you lose it again within thirty seconds.

The bedspread is always pulled too tight. Who makes a bed that tight? There must be a technique — hospital corners, and then something else, some tuck that creates that drum-skin surface. You have to kind of peel it back. The first night in a hotel bed you're sleeping in someone else's idea of how a bed should feel.

The peephole in the door. Fish-eye lens. Everything in the hallway compressed into a circle with the edges bending away. A face at a peephole looks like a face reflected in a spoon. The nose enormous, the ears wrapping around the sides of the head. I wonder if anyone has ever recognized someone through a peephole who they wouldn't have recognized through a regular door-sized window. The distortion might actually help — you'd recognize the general shape, the color of someone's hair, without getting distracted by whether they look tired or older than you remember.

The hallway carpet in hotels has the densest pattern of any carpet anywhere. Maroon and gold, usually. Swirls. The pattern exists to hide stains. That's the entire design brief: make a carpet where nothing visible can make it look worse. The opposite of a white shirt.

I left my shoes by the door once and in the morning they were facing a different direction. I lived with that for about ten minutes before deciding I'd just remembered wrong. The alternative was too specific to be scary — not a ghost, just a ghost that adjusts shoes. A ghost with opinions about which way shoes should face.

The ice machine down the hall. You can hear it drop a load even through the walls. That clatter and then the mechanical sigh after. I've never once used a hotel ice machine for a reason that justified putting on shoes and walking down the hallway with that plastic bucket. But people do it. The bucket is translucent and ribbed and the handle is the same material bent into a loop. They cost almost nothing to make, probably.
