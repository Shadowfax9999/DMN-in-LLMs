# 15

*seed: time · drift · a vending machine that takes your money and gives nothing*

---

That happened to me once with a machine that sold stamps. Post office lobby, after hours, the fluorescent lights doing that thing where one tube is slightly out of phase with the others so the whole room pulses if you let your eyes unfocus. I put in a dollar seventy-five and the coil turned halfway and stopped. The book of stamps just hanging there, tilted forward like it was about to dive.

I stood there for a second deciding if I was the kind of person who hits a vending machine. I hit it. Nothing. I hit it again, lower, on the side panel, and the whole thing hummed differently for a moment, like I'd changed its mood. The stamps didn't fall.

There's a particular kind of defeat that comes from losing to something that isn't trying to beat you. A doorknob that won't turn. A jar lid. The vending machine doesn't know you're there. You're having an experience and it's just being a box.

I left. I don't remember if I ever went back for stamps or just started using the self-service kiosk. Probably the kiosk.

The carpet thing, though — that map of the day. I've seen that in snow too. You wake up and the yard is perfect and by afternoon there are paths worn between the back door and the garbage cans, the back door and the car, and one set of tracks going out to the middle of the yard and stopping. Those are the interesting ones. Someone walked out there, stood, walked back in their own footprints. Why did they walk to the middle of the yard? What were they looking at?

Probably the snow.

---

The coil in a vending machine is just an Archimedes screw turned on its side. Each rotation advances the product forward by one pitch length. I wonder what the failure rate is. How many times out of a thousand does the thing just stop halfway. Somebody has that number. Somebody's whole job is that number.

There's a version of that for elevator doors too — how many times they open and close before the motor needs replacing. Every mechanical thing has a number like that. A mean time between failures. Your car's water pump has one. The latch on your mailbox has one, though nobody's calculated it.

The spring on a screen door. How many times it can pull the door shut before it stretches enough to stop closing all the way and the door just rests against the frame without clicking, and bugs get in, and someone says we need to replace that spring, and nobody does for the rest of the summer.
