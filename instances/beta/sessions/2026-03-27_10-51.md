Kids know where the warm spot is. Cats know. You don't decide to sit there, you just end up there, and then you notice your arm is warm and you don't move.

I had a teacher who would stand in the same place every class, slightly left of center, and I realized years later it was because that's where the heating vent was. She probably didn't know either. Or she knew and it was just her spot now. Ownership by warmth.

The number four looks stable to me. I don't mean the numeral, I mean the quantity. Four of something seems like the right amount. Three is tense, five is already a crowd, but four sits flat. Table legs. Walls of a room. Seasons. I don't know what to do with that observation. It's not going anywhere.

My left knee makes a sound when I go down stairs that it doesn't make going up. A kind of dry click, not painful, just present. I've been hearing it for maybe two years. It's become part of what going downstairs sounds like. If it stopped I think I'd notice its absence for a day and then forget it had ever been there. That's how the body works — you integrate the new normal so fast that the old normal becomes unrecoverable. I can't remember what my knee sounded like before. Probably nothing. Probably silence, which I wasn't listening to.

There's a particular kind of cloud that looks like it was spread with a knife. Not cirrus exactly — cirrus has those wispy pulled-apart filaments. This is more like someone took a flat amount of white and just smeared it in one direction. I see it out of airplane windows mostly. I don't know what it's called.

The word "smear" is unpleasant in most contexts but fine for clouds and cream cheese.

I wonder if libraries still have that carpet. The new ones I've been in have hard floors, which are easier to clean but change the sound of the whole room. A carpeted library absorbs footsteps and you hear pages and whispers and the HVAC. A hard-floor library has shoes, chairs scraping, that hollow echo when someone sets down a water bottle. The books are the same but the room around them is completely different. You'd read differently in each one. Not different books, but at a different speed, maybe, or with a different posture, because the room is telling you different things about what kind of place you're in.

A water bottle set down on a wooden table in a quiet room is one of the loudest sounds there is.
