# 7

*seed: time · drift*

---

Cut grass is one of those smells that arrives before you notice it arriving. You're already in it by the time you think "grass."

Small yogurts. That's a strange modifier if you think about it — she means the individual cups, not the big tub, but "small" is doing all that work without specifying any of it. Grocery lists rely on a shared decoder. Someone else reading "small yogurts" would buy the wrong thing. The list is a language between two people, or between a person and their future self, which might be worse because your future self in the store is distractible and will absolutely forget what "the good bread" meant.

I used to think the clipboard was a medical instrument. Not literally, but the association was so strong — clipboard equals doctor's office, equals the moment before someone calls your name and mispronounces it. The way you half-stand when they get close to yours. That anticipatory crouch.

The mower is getting closer. You can tell by the pitch change, which isn't really Doppler at that speed, it's just volume, but it feels like pitch because louder and higher get confused somewhere in the processing. Or maybe the engine actually does rev differently on the turns.

There's a particular kind of afternoon where nothing is required of you and the nothing feels heavy instead of light. Not boredom exactly. More like the specific weight of hours you chose not to fill. Thursday has that quality sometimes. Weekend is too far to plan for, the week's momentum is spent.

A clipboard without paper on it. Just the brown pressboard and the silver clip open like a mouth. I had one I used as a drawing surface when I was maybe ten, sitting in the back seat on a long drive. The clip kept catching my sleeve. I remember being annoyed by it in a way that felt enormous at the time, the way small physical frustrations do when you're a kid in a car and your legs are too short to reach the floor so they just hang there and your feet go to sleep.

The mower turned off. That sudden silence after a loud machine is its own kind of sound — the neighborhood rushes in to fill the space. Birds were singing the whole time, apparently.

I wonder if she ever wrote anything on those lists that wasn't groceries. A reminder to herself tucked between "paper towels" and "chicken thighs." I'll never know because I wasn't reading carefully, I was just carrying the list in my back pocket and pulling it out under the fluorescent lights and trying to find the small yogurts.

The fluorescent lights in grocery stores hum at 60 hertz, or 120, I can't remember which. Some people can hear it and some can't, and the ones who can't think the ones who can are being dramatic.

My left foot is asleep right now, actually.
