*seed: time · drift · half-life*

---

Half-life. Not the decay — or yes, the decay, but the feeling of it. The way something can be half-gone and still fully present. A smell in a room after the person wearing it has left. You walk in and the perfume is there and the person isn't and for a second the room is more them than they are.

Fifteen pulses per second. A ceiling fan is almost a note. And a note is almost a ceiling fan if you slow it down enough. A bass string vibrating at 30 Hz — you can almost see the individual swings. Speed it up and it blurs into pitch. Slow a pitch down enough and it becomes rhythm. Slow rhythm down enough and it becomes waiting.

I'm thinking about the word *threshold*. Not what it means now but what it is physically — the board at the bottom of a doorway. You step on it going in and out. Every room you've ever entered, you crossed one. Most of them wood. Some of them metal, that little strip where carpet meets tile. The T-shaped ones. I've never understood how those stay down. There must be a nail or a screw hidden under the flange but I've never looked.

The flange. A word that sounds exactly like what it is. Flat, wide, metal.

Friday at midnight is a particular kind of time. Not the Friday-night-out midnight, which is 11 PM wearing a different name. The staying-in midnight. The one where you realize you've been reading the same paragraph for ten minutes and the house is making sounds you don't hear during the day. The fridge compressor. Something in the walls that might be pipes or might be the house shifting on its foundation by some amount too small to measure but large enough to click.

Houses click. I don't know why that seems strange but it does. Like they're counting something.

A half-life means you never actually reach zero. You just keep halving. The perfume in the room gets fainter and fainter but mathematically never disappears. Which is obviously wrong in practice — at some point there's one molecule left and then there's none. The math says infinite recession, the physics says there's a floor. There's always a floor. Zeno knew this was a problem and didn't care, or cared differently than we do.

---

The feeling of being in a car that's parked but still running. Engine on, headlights off, seatbelt still buckled. You're not going anywhere but you haven't committed to staying. The dashboard lights make your hands look blue-green. The radio is on low enough that you can't tell if it's music or talking. You could put it in gear. You could turn it off. Neither thing is happening yet.

That's a specific kind of comfort. The uncommitted minute. I think people sit in their cars in driveways longer than they'd ever admit. Not on the phone, not listening to a song, just sitting there with the engine doing its thing, in the space between where you were and where you're about to be.

The gear selector in an automatic has that button you press with your thumb. On some cars it's on the side, on some it's on top. The ones on top feel more deliberate — you're pressing down into the shift instead of squeezing it from the side. I learned to drive on a car where it was on the side and the first time I drove one where it was on top I couldn't find it for an embarrassing number of seconds.

My left foot still searches for a clutch sometimes even though I haven't driven a manual in years.
