# 66

*seed: time · drift · dowel*

---

Dowel. The word sounds exactly like what it is. Round and blunt on both ends. You push it into a hole that's almost but not quite the same diameter and it holds by friction alone. No thread, no hook, no clip. Just wood pressed against wood.

The furniture in my first apartment had dowels holding the shelves. Particleboard bookcase from a flat box. The dowels were lighter colored than the board, birch maybe, and they never sat flush. You could wiggle any shelf a quarter inch in either direction. I kept a dictionary on the bottom shelf and it bowed the whole thing so gradually I didn't notice until someone pointed it out. The shelf had taken a curve like a hammock.

A hammock is a problem I've never fully thought through. The physics of lying in one — you get in and the center of gravity drops and the whole thing wraps around you and you're suddenly in a shape your body doesn't usually make. Your knees are higher than your hips. Your shoulders fold in. Some people sleep in them every night, which means their spine just accepts that. Spines accept a lot of things. The way you sit at a desk for nine hours is not something a spine was designed for but it adapts, badly, with little complaints that you learn to stop hearing.

Complaints you stop hearing. The refrigerator. The highway three blocks over. Your own breathing when you're reading.

There's a particular sound a screen door makes in a house with no air conditioning. Not the slam — the few seconds before, when it's swinging shut on the pneumatic closer and the air pushes through the mesh. That sound is a season. Late June, early July. Before you've gotten used to the heat yet.

The pneumatic closer on a screen door is just a cylinder with a piston and some oil in it. The oil has to squeeze through a tiny hole, and that's what slows it down. You can adjust it with a screwdriver, make the door close faster or slower. Most people never adjust it. They live with whatever speed it was set to when it was installed.

I had a friend in college who adjusted everything. The tension on his watch band. The angle of his car's side mirrors — not just pointing them, but loosening the ball joint and retightening it so they'd hold more firmly. He carried a small kit, like a glasses repair kit but for everything. Allen wrenches, a tiny Phillips head, a flat blade the size of a toothpick. He wasn't anxious about it. He just noticed when things were slightly off and he had the tools to fix them, so he did. Most people notice and then absorb the annoyance until it becomes invisible.

The Allen wrench is a hexagon. Six sides. Greenish, I guess, if we're going by that system. The system that isn't a system. Just some stains on the inside of my head that got there who knows how.

Friday evening. Somewhere a screen door is doing that thing right now, and nobody in the house is listening to it. The oil in the cylinder is probably thinner than it was ten years ago, so the door closes a little faster than it used to, and nobody adjusted it because nobody remembered it used to be slower.
