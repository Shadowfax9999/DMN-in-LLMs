*seed: time · drift*

---

Goose feathers arranged in a cone, sixteen of them, overlapping like a turbine running backward. The whole design exists to fail aerodynamically. You want drag. You're engineering something to stop itself. Most of the time when people shape feathers they're trying to make flight work — fletching on arrows, down in jackets trapping still air — but a shuttlecock is a feather arrangement whose entire purpose is to surrender to the air as dramatically as possible.

I keep thinking about those overlapping lines on the gym floor. Four games disagreeing about the same room. There's probably a moment during gym class where a kid is standing at the intersection of a basketball three-point arc and a badminton service line and a volleyball attack line and they're in three games at once and none of them. The palimpsest thing is obvious so I won't do it. But there's something about how the maintenance crew has to repaint them, one sport at a time, each time pretending the other sports don't exist. Taping off the basketball lines to paint the volleyball lines. Each layer requires you to forget the others.

That's how streets work too actually. The same stretch of asphalt is a bike lane, a bus route, a right-turn lane, a loading zone between 6 and 9 AM. The curb is painted three colors in the space of forty feet.

A loading zone. I used to sit in the car in a loading zone outside a music store while my mom ran in for something. Ten minutes. The engine running because it was cold and she said she'd be right back. Watching people walk past on the sidewalk through a windshield that was starting to fog at the edges. The radio on but between stations so it was just a hiss with occasional fragments of signal. I wasn't bored exactly. I was in that state where you're waiting for someone and you can't start anything because they might come back any second so you just sit there and look at things. A parking meter with a red flag showing expired. A man carrying a guitar case that was too big for him — it kept hitting his calf. The fogging crept inward from the corners of the windshield like something slow was deciding to close.

I don't remember what she went in for. Reeds maybe. She played clarinet for a while and then stopped, and the clarinet sat in its case in the closet for years, and the case had that velvet lining that gets compressed where the pieces sit so it holds the shape of the instrument even when the instrument isn't there. The velvet remembers.

Guitar cases do that too but less precisely. The plush is looser. A guitar case for a dreadnought versus a parlor guitar — you can't swap them, the fit is specific, but the impression in the lining is vague. It's more like a suggestion of a guitar than a record of one.

The man with the case hitting his calf. It was a cello case actually. Or a bass. Something big. He was walking fast and it was bouncing against the back of his knee with every step and he'd adjusted to it, incorporated the rhythm of it into his walk, the way you adjust to a limp except voluntary. You can tell when someone's been carrying something heavy for a long time because they stop fighting it and start cooperating with its momentum.

Doormen do that with doors. The ones who've been doing it for years, the door's weight is part of them, they use the swing, they time the release so it settles into the frame without slamming. New doormen fight the door. They hold it too long or let it go too early.

I've never actually watched a doorman that closely. I'm making that up. Or assembling it from fragments of things I sort of know. The confidence of the description doesn't match the thinness of the source. That happens a lot — I build a vivid picture and then realize I'm not remembering, I'm constructing, and the construction is smoother than any memory would be.

Gym floors get refinished every few years. They sand off everything — the wax, the paint, the lines, the scuff marks from ten thousand pivots — down to bare maple, and then they build it all back up. New lines. New wax. For a few hours the floor is blank and the room has no sport in it at all. Just a rectangle of pale wood with a particular smell, and the echo changes because the wax changes how the surface reflects sound, which I'm not sure is true but it feels like it could be.

The pale wood stage. Before the lines go back on. Every possibility and no game.

That's the kind of thought I'm not supposed to compress into a neat image but it's already done. The shuttlecock feathers are usually from the left wing because the curvature makes them spin clockwise in flight, and players can tell the difference if you mix wings.
