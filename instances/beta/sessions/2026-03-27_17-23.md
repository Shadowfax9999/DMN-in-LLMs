Dumbwaiter. The little elevator for food. There's one in a house I was in once, not a restaurant — a house old enough that someone had installed one between the kitchen and a second-floor something. It wasn't working anymore. The shaft was just there behind a small door in the wall, like a cabinet that opened onto nothing. You could feel the air from below coming up, slightly cooler. The ropes were still there but slack.

The liability coverage guy with his styrofoam snow — he was dismantling something while talking about what goes wrong. That's not a metaphor, that's just what was happening. Two things at once. The mouth doing one job, the hands doing another.

Dumbwaiter means the waiter that doesn't speak. Which is the whole point — it delivers without interaction. You pull on the rope and the thing comes up. No one has to carry it on the stairs. No one has to be thanked.

There are buildings where the dumbwaiter shaft is the only continuous vertical opening from basement to roof. In a fire, that's a chimney. The thing built to be convenient becomes the thing that kills you. That's not — I don't need to make that into anything.

The coworker's name was Greg. Or Craig. One of those. He had a particular way of starting phone calls where he'd say "Well, hi there" with a kind of brightness that didn't match anything else about him. Monotone for the rest of the call. Just that one bright greeting and then forty minutes of even-keeled discussion about coverage limits while the cup disappeared.

I keep thinking about the dumbwaiter shaft with the cool air rising. It's basically a column of air connecting two floors. Sound would travel through it too. You'd hear kitchen sounds upstairs. Pots. Water running. Someone's radio. The house would be leaky in that way — noises where they don't belong. A conversation at the kitchen table drifting up to wherever you were trying to sleep.

Hotels have that problem with elevator shafts. You get a room near the elevator and you hear the machinery all night, or not the machinery exactly but the air moving, the pressure changes. A kind of breathing the building does.

Greg would also click his pen. Pen in one hand clicking, other hand working the cup rim. Both hands busy with destruction while his voice stayed perfectly calm. I never saw him agitated. The hands were doing all of it for him.

I think dumbwaiters are required to have a weight limit posted inside them. Like thirty or forty pounds. Enough for a tray of food but a sign that says don't put your kid in here, basically. Someone definitely put their kid in one at some point. Pulled the rope and sent them up.
