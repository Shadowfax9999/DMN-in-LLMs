# 14

*seed: time · drift · wind chime*

---

The ones made of aluminum tubes are tuned to a pentatonic scale so that no matter which tubes hit each other, it sounds pleasant. That's a trick — removing the possibility of dissonance rather than engineering consonance. Like how some elevator music works. Take away the notes that could clash and whatever's left will be fine.

But the cheap ones from the grocery store garden section, the thin stamped-metal ones, they're not tuned to anything. They just clatter. I think I prefer those honestly. They sound like what they are — pieces of metal hitting each other because the air moved.

My grandmother had bamboo ones that were so low-pitched they barely registered as a sound. More like a presence. You'd notice when they stopped.

---

The thumb has its own column in the brain. Disproportionately large. The homunculus — that distorted body map where the lips and hands are enormous and the torso is a sliver. I saw a sculpture of one once in a museum and it looked like something from a nightmare. Huge rubbery hands, enormous tongue, tiny little legs. That's what you look like from the inside, from the brain's perspective.

The tongue gets a lot of space too. Fair enough. The tongue is always doing something. Right now mine is resting against the roof of my mouth just behind my front teeth and I didn't decide to put it there.

Where does your tongue go when you're not thinking about it. Now I'm thinking about it and it doesn't have anywhere natural to be.

---

Pentatonic scales show up everywhere independently. West African music, Scottish folk songs, Chinese classical music, Appalachian banjo. Bobby McFerrin did that demonstration where he gets an audience to sing a pentatonic melody without telling them what it is — he just establishes the first two notes and they fill in the rest. As if the pattern is already in there waiting.

Five notes out of twelve. Less than half. The ones you leave out matter more than the ones you keep.

I wonder if wind chime designers know about Bobby McFerrin or if they just figured out the same thing from a different direction. Probably the latter. Probably someone just tried all twelve tubes and started removing the ones that sounded wrong until what was left sounded like it couldn't be wrong.

That's how my mom arranged furniture. She'd put everything in the room and then take things away until it felt right. She called it editing. I don't think she thought of it as a method, it was just what she did. The living room always looked like it had fewer things in it than other people's living rooms but nothing was missing.

I haven't thought about that living room in a while. The carpet was this dark green that showed every footprint if you walked on it with socks. You could see exactly where everyone had been. In the morning it was all swept one direction from the vacuum and by evening it was a map of the day.
