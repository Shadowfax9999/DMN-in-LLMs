# 30

*seed: time · drift · the creak of a new shoe*

---

New shoes creak at the ball of the foot. That stiff leather bending where it doesn't want to bend yet. You can hear someone coming down a hallway in new shoes before you see them. It's a announcing kind of sound, almost embarrassing, like the shoes are telling on you.

Old shoes don't make any sound at all. They've given up.

I had a pair of boots once that I resoled twice and the cobbler — do people still say cobbler? There must be a more current word but I don't know what it is. The guy at the shoe repair place. He said the uppers were still good and I felt proud of that, like I'd been praised for something I hadn't actually done. The leather just happened to hold up. But I took credit anyway.

The feeling of putting on someone else's shoes by accident at a house where everyone took their shoes off at the door. The wrongness of someone else's foot-shape pressed into the insole. You feel the ghost of their arch, their weight distribution. It's almost too intimate for an accident.

I wonder if that's why bowling shoes feel so unsettling. Not the ugliness, though that too. The smoothness. Hundreds of feet have worn any single impression out of them. They belong to no one.

A waiting room. Not a specific one. The composite waiting room that exists in my head: chairs along the wall, a low table with magazines from seven months ago, a television mounted too high showing something no one chose. The particular quality of time in a waiting room, which is that it doesn't accumulate. You can sit there for forty minutes and it feels like one long minute that just kept not ending. There's a clock but it seems decorative. Someone coughs and you become aware of them for the first time even though they've been sitting there the whole time.

The receptionist slides the glass partition open and closed. Open to call a name, closed again. The glass has that frosted lower half. You can see shapes moving behind it but not faces. Just color and motion, like fish in a murky tank.

I always sit facing the door. I don't know when that started or why. It's not a tactical thing, not consciously. I just don't like having the door behind me. Restaurants too. My dad was the same way. Maybe I learned it from watching him choose seats, or maybe it's just a common mammal preference and we both arrived at it independently.

The number of things you might have learned from a parent versus independently derived — there's no way to sort that out. You can't run the control. You are the experiment and the experiment only happened once.

Shoe repair places always smell like glue and leather and something mineral, like iron filings almost. The machines in the back, the grinders or buffers or whatever they are, have a whine to them that you can hear through the whole shop even when the guy's up front talking to you. It's always one guy. I've never seen a shoe repair place with two people working in it.
