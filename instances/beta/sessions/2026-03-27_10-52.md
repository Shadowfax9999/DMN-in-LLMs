The loudest sounds are always the accidental ones. Dropping a fork in a restaurant during a lull. Your phone making that sound it makes when you forgot to silence it.

I was in a dentist's waiting room once where the TV was on but muted, captions scrolling, and the only real sound was the receptionist's keyboard and some drill whine leaking through the wall. Someone's phone buzzed on the glass side table and everyone looked. Not at the person. At the phone. Like the phone was the one who'd done something.

Glass tables. Who decided those were good for waiting rooms. You can see your own knees from above, which is a perspective you never otherwise get on your knees. And the magazines slide. Everything on a glass table is always slightly migrating toward the edge.

I think about coefficient of friction more than most people probably do. Not by name, usually. Just — noticing which surfaces grip and which ones don't. Socks on hardwood. A wet cutting board on a counter. The way a phone on a car dashboard will ride the first curve and then commit to falling during the second one. There's a pause between when it starts sliding and when it picks up speed. That pause is static friction losing to kinetic friction and it happens every time but it still looks like the object is deciding.

Coins on a table do the same thing if the table isn't level. You don't notice the table isn't level until you set a coin down and it starts traveling. Slow enough that you think maybe you imagined it, then you watch, and yes, it's going. The coin knows something about the room that you didn't.

My grandmother's kitchen table had a wobble that she fixed with a folded piece of cardboard under one leg. It stayed there for years. I think it was from a cereal box. Nobody talked about it but everyone knew not to kick that leg. That's a kind of household knowledge that never gets written down — which chair has the loose arm, which burner runs hot, which door you have to lift slightly while you turn the knob. You learn a house like you learn a face: not all at once but through a long accumulation of small corrections. Then someone else moves in and starts over.

I don't remember which leg it was.
