# 68

*seed: time · drift*

---

Fountains in malls are almost all gone now. The ones that are left have that weird bright blue dye in the water that makes the coins underneath look like they're embedded in glass. I sat next to one once in a food court waiting for someone and the pump was loud enough that you couldn't hear the water falling. Just a mechanical hum with wet on top of it.

The food court had a Sbarro. Sbarro always looked like it was about to close. Even in 1997 it looked like it was about to close. The pizza sat under heat lamps on those tilted metal shelves and the cheese had that skin on it. I ate it anyway. Everyone ate it anyway. You didn't go to Sbarro because you wanted pizza. You went because it was the closest thing to the table your friend picked.

Seating in food courts is genuinely strange if you think about how it works. The tables don't belong to any restaurant. You buy from one place and sit in the commons. It's a public square inside a private building. The chairs are bolted to the tables so nobody rearranges them. That bolting — that's a decision someone made because the alternative was people dragging chairs into clusters of nine and leaving other tables empty. The bolted chair says: groups of four. That's the unit. If your group is five, figure it out.

My left knee has been making a clicking sound when I go down stairs. Not up. Just down. It doesn't hurt. It's just there, like a metronome set to the rhythm of descent. I keep meaning to ask someone about it and then forgetting because it doesn't hurt.

The clicking is in the kneecap, I think. The kneecap floats. It's not attached to the other bones directly — it's held in place by tendons, like a button sewn onto fabric. The whole structure relies on tension. If the tension changes, the kneecap tracks differently in its groove and you get the click. Or maybe that's wrong. I'm not sure the groove is the right word.

A groove in a record. A groove in wood where you're going to set a shelf. A groove meaning you've found the right rhythm. All of those are the same shape — a channel that something else follows.

Friday night and I can hear someone's bass through the wall. Not the song. Just the bass. The melody is someone else's problem. From here it's just a pulse that speeds up and slows down and I can't tell if it's the same song or different songs. The wall filters everything above a certain frequency and what's left is just the skeleton of whatever they're listening to. It could be anything. Country, hip-hop, classical if there's a double bass passage. From here it's all the same genre: bass through drywall.

The drywall in this apartment has a hairline crack that runs from the corner of the window to about two feet up toward the ceiling. It's been there since I noticed it and probably before. Buildings settle. The framing shifts a sixteenth of an inch and the drywall, which can't flex, cracks. It's not structural. It's cosmetic. But I look at it sometimes and think about the fact that the building moved and this is the only visible evidence.

There's a cobweb in the top corner of the same window. Not a web — a cobweb. The difference being that nobody lives there anymore. It catches dust and sways when the air kicks on. I should clean it and I won't. Not because I've decided not to but because I'll keep not being in the right position with the right thing in my hand at the right moment. You need a paper towel and a chair and the intention all at once and they never line up.

The bass through the wall just stopped. Either the song ended or they left the room or turned it down. There's a gap now where the pulse was and my ear keeps reaching for it. Not hearing something you were hearing — it takes a second to recalibrate. Like when the power goes out and you suddenly hear the refrigerator by its absence, except you realize you'd stopped hearing it hours ago.

The fridge in this apartment has a rattle at a specific RPM of the compressor — it hits some resonant frequency of the shelf inside and the shelf buzzes. If I open the door and press the shelf with one finger, it stops. I've considered putting a piece of tape on it or a folded bit of paper but instead I just press the shelf when it bothers me. The temporary fix has become permanent through repetition.

There's a streetlight outside that's the orange sodium kind, not the new white LEDs they've been replacing them with. It makes the parked cars look like they're all the same color.
