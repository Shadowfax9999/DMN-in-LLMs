The angle of repose for dry sand is about 34 degrees. For rice it's steeper. You can see it in any jar that's half full — the surface isn't flat, it's a little cone or a little crater depending on how it settled. Tilt the jar slowly and there's a moment where nothing moves and then everything moves. Not gradually. All at once, like a decision.

Grain silos have killed people that way. The surface looks solid and then it isn't. Someone steps onto it and the whole thing behaves like a liquid for exactly long enough.

A friend's uncle worked at a feed mill and said the rule was you never go in alone and you never go in without a harness. He said it the way people say things they learned from someone else's mistake.

Harnesses have that specific weight to them. Heavier than you'd expect from looking at one on a hook. All those layers of nylon webbing folded and stitched and riveted. The D-ring on the back sits between your shoulder blades where you can't reach it, which seems like a flaw until you realize the whole point is that someone else clips you in. The system assumes two people.

Ropes assume two people too, mostly. Belaying. One person moves and the other person holds. There's a version of trust that only exists when you're fifteen feet up an indoor climbing wall and the person below you is someone you met forty minutes ago in the introductory session. You don't trust them exactly. You trust the equipment and the fact that someone showed them the figure-eight knot.

The figure-eight knot looks like an eight. That's the whole name. Some knots are named for what they do — the cleat hitch, the taut-line hitch — and some are named for what they look like.

A cleat on a dock is just two horns of metal bolted to the wood. You wrap the line around it in a specific pattern. People who've done it a thousand times do it without looking, the way someone types without looking, the rope just finding the shape under their hands. There's a half-twist at the end that locks it. If you skip the half-twist the whole thing can slip.

Docks smell like creosote in the sun. That dark oily smell that's halfway between tar and smoke. The wood turns silver-gray after a few years and the grain raises up so it's rough under bare feet. Splinters from dock wood are the worst kind because they're full of whatever the treatment chemical is and they go in deep and they're dark so you can see them under the skin.

Tweezers never work as well as you want them to. The cheap ones don't meet at the tips. Even the good ones — you get the splinter between the edges and it slips. Needle and tweezers together, that's the actual method, using the needle to lift one end free and the tweezers to grab what the needle exposed.

My grandmother kept a sewing needle stuck in a bar of soap by the bathroom sink for exactly this purpose. The soap kept it clean, supposedly. Or maybe it just kept it from rolling off the counter.

Bar soap has almost disappeared from bathrooms. Pump bottles everywhere now. But bar soap lasts longer if you let it dry between uses, and there's that moment where a bar gets thin enough to be translucent — you can almost see light through it — and it's too thin to hold comfortably but too thick to throw away, so it just sits there in the dish getting soft.

The dish always has that residue in it. That cloudy slick layer that's part soap and part water and part whatever mineral deposit the water leaves behind. In hard-water areas the dish gets a white crust around the edges that you have to scrub off with a thumbnail.

Hard water spots on glass look like little circles. Each one is the outline of where a water drop sat and evaporated, leaving its calcium behind. The drop was a dome and the ring is just the base of the dome. You're seeing the footprint of something that used to be three-dimensional.

Car windshields after a sprinkler hits them. Hundreds of overlapping circles dried in the sun, each one a slightly different size.
