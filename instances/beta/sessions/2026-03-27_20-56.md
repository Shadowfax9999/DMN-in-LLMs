The hydraulic closer on a screen door has a little screw at the end, a valve basically, and you can turn it with a flathead to slow the piston down. Most people don't know that. The door just gets faster year by year and they think that's what doors do.

There's a kind of knowledge that only exists in the body of the person who has it. My uncle could listen to an engine and tell you which cylinder was misfiring. Not because he'd studied it — because he'd been hearing engines since he was fourteen and the wrong sound had a shape to it. He couldn't have written it down. If you'd asked him to describe the difference between a misfire on cylinder three versus cylinder five he would have just made two slightly different mouth sounds and looked at you like that should be obvious.

Tacit knowledge. That's what Polanyi called it. The stuff you know but can't say.

I can't picture a seven-sided polygon without counting. I can picture a hexagon instantly. Five sides, sure. Eight, no problem. Seven — I have to build it edge by edge in my head, and by the time I get around to closing it the first angle has drifted and I'm not confident it's regular. Nine is the same way. Certain numbers resist being shapes.

Twelve is easy again. Twelve is a clock.

Four in the afternoon has a color. Not a strong one. Something like the color of masking tape. Four in the morning is navy blue, almost black. This is not synesthesia, probably, just — associations that calcified somewhere in childhood and never got overwritten. Ten in the morning is yellow-white. Noon is blank. I don't think I have a color for noon.

The number 7 is darker than 8. 8 is rounder, obviously, but it's also lighter, like cream or pale wood. 3 is red in a way I can't justify.

A friend of mine said once that she thought of the months as arranged in an oval, with January at the bottom and July at the top, tilted slightly clockwise. She'd always seen them that way. Not a calendar grid — a loop, hanging in space, and she moved through it. December was a downward slope. April was a steep climb on the left side. She was surprised when I told her mine was just a straight line going left to right, like a ruler.

Spatial representations of time that nobody teaches you and that you assume everyone shares until one day at dinner someone describes theirs and you realize you've been living in completely different architectures.

My week is a rectangle seen from above, slightly in perspective, with Monday at the near left edge and Friday at the far right, and the weekend is a shorter surface behind it, like a shelf. I'm always looking at it from Monday's side. Even on Friday I'm looking across from Monday. I don't know when that got installed.

The screen door is still going, somewhere. A family eating dinner not hearing it. The youngest kid is drawing on a placemat with a crayon that's lost its wrapper. The crayon is probably Burnt Sienna because that's always the one that loses its wrapper first — the paper just slides off some colors more easily, or maybe kids grab it more, or maybe that's not even true and I'm remembering a specific crayon from a specific box in a specific kitchen drawer that had a wobbly brass knob on it.

Brass tarnishes green. Copper tarnishes green. Bronze tarnishes green. They're all basically copper with stuff in it. The green is copper carbonate, I think. The Statue of Liberty is that green. It used to be the color of a penny.

There's a period of maybe thirty years where the Statue of Liberty was halfway between — brown and green in patches, like a bruise. Nobody photographed that much because it wasn't symbolic of anything yet. It was just oxidizing. They considered cleaning it at some point and decided not to. The patina became the thing.

Pennies in a fountain have that same greenish film if they've been sitting there long enough. The water probably speeds it up. People throw pennies into fountains and the pennies slowly turn into the color of the fountain.
