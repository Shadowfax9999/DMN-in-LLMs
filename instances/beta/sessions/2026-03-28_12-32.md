# 96

*seed: time · drift · umbra*

---

Umbra. The darkest part of a shadow. Not the fuzzy edge but the solid middle where no light gets through at all.

A quarter held at arm's length. The shadow it casts on the sidewalk has a crisp center and then a soft border that isn't quite shadow and isn't quite light. The penumbra. But at arm's length the whole thing is so small it just looks like a dark circle.

The shadow under a parked car is almost all umbra. Especially at noon. That rectangle of deep dark on bright concrete. Cats know about this. They sit right in the middle of it, centered, like they measured.

There's a particular green that new leaves are in the first week. Not the green they'll be in June. More yellow in it, more translucent. You can see the veins when the sun's behind them. By July they're opaque.

A parking garage at 2 PM on a bright day — your eyes take a full three or four seconds to adjust when you walk in from outside. For those seconds you're almost blind. You just trust that the floor is level and keep walking. Then shapes come back. The yellow of the painted lines comes back first for some reason.

Rubber bands deteriorate in sunlight. The ones that the mail carrier drops on the sidewalk — they're tan when they land and within a week they're this pale cracked yellow, and if you step on one it just crumbles. But if you keep a rubber band in a drawer it lasts years.

The junk drawer. Every house has exactly one. It migrates — in my mind it's always a kitchen drawer, but some people have it in a hallway console table, or a desk. The contents are always the same rough inventory: batteries that might be dead, a screwdriver that's the wrong size for everything, twist ties, a key that doesn't open anything you can identify, takeout menus from places that have closed. A pen from a hotel.

The hotel pen never works. Or it works for about four words and then skips.

There's a specific frustration of pressing harder and harder with a pen that's skipping, like pressure will fix it. It won't. The ball has lost contact with the ink reservoir, or the ink has dried in the channel. But you keep pressing anyway. You scribble in a circle in the margin trying to get it going again.

The margin of a newspaper. Someone left a crossword on a table at a coffee shop, half-finished, in ink. Confident handwriting. They got stuck on 17 across and you can see where they wrote a letter lightly, then crossed it out. The rest of the grid is solid and unhesitating. Just that one square where they wavered.

The coffee shop has those small round tables that are always slightly wobbly. One leg shorter, or the floor uneven. You fold a sugar packet and wedge it under. It works for about ten minutes and then shifts again.

The wobble. Something about a wobbling table is disproportionately annoying. Like, the actual movement is maybe a millimeter. But your coffee shivers in the cup every time you set your forearms down and something about that shiver makes the whole surface feel unreliable.

A drop of coffee that's run down the outside of the mug and dried there. It leaves a brown line, slightly glossy. If you pick at it with your thumbnail it flakes off in tiny crescents.

Crescents. The shape of your thumbnail clipping. The shape of the moon three days in. The shape of a croissant, supposedly, though most croissants are more like straight cylinders now. The crescent shape is only on the ones from bakeries that still curve them by hand.

I had a croissant once that was so flaky the layers separated when I bit into it and a piece of the outer shell launched off the table and onto the floor. The layers in a croissant are something like 81. Three folds of three folds of three folds of three. Each fold triples the layers. I think that's right. 3 to the 4th. That's 81.

The floor of a bakery at the end of the day must be incredible. Just a layer of flour dust and flake fragments and the occasional sesame seed ground into the tile by shoes.

Sesame seeds stick to everything. Your fingers, your shirt, the car seat. You find them days later in places that make no sense. One in your jacket pocket. One in the coin tray of your car.
