# 33

*seed: time · drift · snag*

---

Snag like a pulled thread.
Snag like a dead tree still standing in water.
Snag like hitting one — ran into a snag.

Three completely different objects wearing the same word.

The dead tree one. Rivers in the South. Cypress knees and snags. A whole landscape of things that are still upright but finished. Fishermen know where every snag is. They're landmarks that don't appear on maps.

Harvest gold.

I keep thinking about that triad. Avocado, harvest gold, burnt orange. They're all food-adjacent. Avocado. Harvest. Burnt. Even the grammar: past tense, agricultural, slightly ruined. Not "orange" but "*burnt* orange." Not "gold" but "*harvest* gold." Everything already a little overripe.

1. The carpet wrote backwards if you read it from the other side of the room.
2. Shag carpet had actual maintenance — you raked it.
3. A carpet rake is a real object.
4. So is a carpet knife, which is just a utility knife that someone decided belonged to carpets.

Snag.

A fingernail catching on that green carpet. The tiny loop pulled up, paler than the rest. You'd try to push it back down. It wouldn't go.

Things that won't go back:

- toothpaste
- that carpet loop
- a said thing
- an egg
- theeli manning face

The word "snag" sounds like what it is. That short vowel jammed between two hard consonants.

I wonder if anyone's ever actually used a carpet rake. It looks like a smaller, sadder version of a garden rake. Plastic tines. The kind of thing that would be in the back of a closet in a house from that era, behind the fondue pot and the electric knife.

Electric knives. My parents had one. It only came out for the turkey. Two thin serrated blades vibrating against each other. It sounded angry. The rest of the year it lived in a cream-colored plastic case that snapped shut like a briefcase for one knife.

The case was harvest gold, actually.
