# 22

*seed: time · drift*

---

The nickel's still there probably. Things wedged into cracks don't go anywhere. I had a pen cap stuck between the console and the passenger seat of a car I sold in 2019 and I think about it sometimes, whether the next owner found it or whether it's still there, migrating deeper into the seat mechanism every time someone adjusts the recline.

A bird just did something I've never noticed before — landed on the gutter, walked sideways along it for about a foot, then flew to the exact same spot on the fence it had been sitting on before. Round trip for nothing. Or maybe the gutter was hot. Or cold. I don't know what temperature gutters are in the morning. They're aluminum, so whatever the air is, roughly, but in direct sun they'd warm up fast. This part of the gutter is in shade.

The shadow of the roofline cuts across the yard at an angle that doesn't match the roof. That bothered me for a second before I remembered that shadows on slopes distort. The yard isn't flat. It looks flat but it drops maybe eight inches over twenty feet toward the sidewalk and that's enough to stretch the shadow of a straight edge into something that looks bent.

I can hear a lawnmower but it's far enough away that it sounds like it could be anything — a plane, a generator, someone sanding something. Lawnmowers only sound like lawnmowers up close, where you get the blade-speed changes when it hits thick patches. From a distance it's just a drone. A single pitch. Actually not single, there's a wobble in it, a slow oscillation that might be the mower turning corners. The pitch shifts when the engine loads and unloads through turns. Or I'm imagining structure in a steady sound because steady sounds are boring to listen to.

My left foot is asleep. Not fully — just the outside edge, the part that's been pressed against the step. That fizzy half-feeling where you can tell the foot is there but the signals are garbled. If I move it the feeling will flood back in and that's worse than this for about ten seconds so I'm leaving it.

The mailbox across the street has a red flag up. I don't think I've ever seen anyone actually use the outgoing-mail flag. I use it maybe twice a year for checks. The flag mechanism is just a friction hinge — there's no detent, no click, it stays wherever you put it. Which means wind can knock it down or up. So a flag that's up might mean nothing.

Three houses down someone has their garage door open about two feet. Just the bottom two feet. I can see the concrete floor and the bottom of a trash can. That gap is either intentional — ventilation, a cat — or the opener stopped mid-travel. There's no way to tell from here.

I'm going to move my foot now.

Yeah that's worse. The fizzing is everywhere, the whole sole, like the foot is carbonated. It peaks right around five seconds in, holds for another five, and then just — normal foot. Every time I'm surprised how fast it resolves. The whole episode is maybe fifteen seconds and then you can't even remember what it felt like. Pain is like that too but on a longer schedule. You remember that something hurt but not the actual quality of the hurt. But the fizzy foot thing is so short that even the memory of it being unpleasant is gone almost immediately.

The lawnmower stopped. Now it's just birds and a car somewhere and my neighbor's wind chime, which is the kind with thick aluminum tubes that play actual pitches — I think it's pentatonic, no half steps, so no matter which tubes the wind catches it sounds deliberate. Whoever figured that out was solving an interesting problem: make a random input always produce a consonant output. That's what pentatonic scales do. You can mash any keys on the black keys of a piano and it sounds fine.

The flag is still up on that mailbox.
