Four kinds of pliers on a pegboard:

1. Needle-nose
2. Slip-joint
3. The ones with the curved jaw
4. Channel-locks (which were actually channel-locks, the brand, but also all of them were channel-locks)

Outlines in black marker. The tool goes back inside its own silhouette. You know immediately what's missing. That's the whole system. A parking lot for wrenches.

Things that have outlines drawn around them: tools on pegboards, bodies at crime scenes (do they actually do that?), kindergarten hands traced in crayon, the faded rectangle on a wall where a frame used to hang.

Actually I don't think crime scene investigators draw outlines around bodies. I think that's just from movies.

Friday again. The horizontal light. A week is such a strange unit. Too short to forget anything, too long to feel continuous. Monday doesn't feel like it's in the same country as Friday.

The pegboard had a section that was just nails with nothing on them. Orphan nails. Tools that got lost or broken or lent out and never returned. My uncle still has our reciprocating saw, probably. That was 2004.

Things people borrow and don't return:
- Reciprocating saws
- Books
- Tupperware
- Pens (but nobody tracks pens)
- The specific lighter that was on the counter

Tupperware is also a genericized trademark.

The shadow thing — everything on a shelf casting a shadow twice its length. That only happens for about twenty minutes. The garage faces west. By 8:30 it's just general dimness. There's a window in the garage door itself, four small panes, and when the sun hits them they throw four rectangles of light onto the concrete floor. The rectangles move. If you sat there long enough you could watch them slide across the floor and up the far wall and then they're gone.

Nobody sits in a garage and watches that.

Except I did once, waiting for wood glue to set. You're supposed to clamp it and leave it overnight but I kept checking. The rectangles moved about a foot in the time I was out there.

Wood glue is stronger than the wood itself, supposedly. The joint won't break at the glue line. It breaks next to it, in the wood. I don't know if that's true for all species or just softwoods.

The clamps were also on the pegboard. C-clamps, with their outlines. They look like the letter C but I guess that's why they're called that. Some of them had a thick crust of old dried glue on the pad end, yellow-orange, built up over years of clamping things without putting down wax paper first.
