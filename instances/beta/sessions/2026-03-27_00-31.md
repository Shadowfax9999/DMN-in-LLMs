The ribbing on those buckets is structural, not decorative. Keeps the walls from buckling. Same principle as corrugated cardboard, just circular. Though I wonder if anyone's ever actually seen one buckle.

Friday feels different from Thursday at this hour. Thursday midnight you're still in the week. Friday midnight you've crossed something. The air doesn't change but your relationship to it does. Standing outside at 12:31 on a Friday — the night belongs to you in a way it didn't twenty-four hours ago. That's not even true if you don't work a Monday-through-Friday job but it still feels true somehow.

The ice machine thing. What people actually use it for is drinks in the room. Whiskey from a bottle they brought, poured over hotel ice into a hotel glass that's wrapped in that little paper cap. The paper cap that says "sanitized for your protection." Sanitized is doing a lot of work in that sentence. It means someone rinsed it. Maybe.

I keep thinking about the sound of ice settling. Not the drop — the after. When you fill a glass and set it down and the cubes shift against each other as they start to melt. There's a ticking, almost. Irregular. Each tick is a cube finding a new resting angle as the surfaces between them lubricate. It speeds up and then stops entirely once they've locked into a configuration that works. Then silence for a while. Then one more tick when enough has melted to destabilize the arrangement.

That's a kind of clock. Measuring its own disappearance.

No — I said I wouldn't do that.

The fitted sheet thing. Folding fitted sheets. Everyone claims there's a technique and the technique always involves tucking corners into corners, which works fine for the first two corners and then you're just rolling the remainder into a shape that fits on the shelf. The YouTube videos where someone does it perfectly — those people have a different relationship to fabric than I do. Or they practiced. Which is worse, somehow. Practicing folding a fitted sheet.

My hands are cold. That's the other thing about being up past midnight — the body temperature drops. Not because it's colder in the room. Circadian. The body decides it should be asleep and starts dimming things. Extremities first. Fingers, toes, nose. The core stays warm. It's triage. The body triaging itself for sleep.

---

A door hinge that squeaks only in one direction. Opening, silent. Closing, it protests. Something about the geometry of the pin under load — the weight of the door hangs differently depending on which way the momentum is going. Or maybe the lubricant has migrated to one side. I don't actually know. I just know the door to the bathroom in the apartment I had on Elm Street did this and I never fixed it and eventually I stopped hearing it, which is its own kind of fixing.

The number of things you stop hearing. The refrigerator. Traffic if you live near a road. Your own breathing until someone points it out and then you can't stop. Attention is a flashlight in a room full of sounds and most of them are fine in the dark.

I used to think tinnitus was silence. When I was young and it got very quiet I'd hear that high thin tone and I thought that was what silence sounded like — that silence had a sound. Finding out it was my own ears generating noise was disappointing in a way I couldn't articulate. The silence had been full and now it was just broken.

Twelve-forty in the morning and somewhere a compressor kicks on. Could be an AC unit, could be a freezer in a garage. That low hum that you feel in the floor more than hear through the air. It runs for eight minutes, maybe twelve, and then clicks off and the absence of it is louder than it was.

I think the compressor in a standard refrigerator cycles about four times an hour.
