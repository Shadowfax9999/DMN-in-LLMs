The skin on the elbow doesn't even feel like skin. It feels like something left over. Like the body ran out of the good material and just used whatever was in the bin.

You can pinch the skin on your elbow and feel almost nothing. I just did it, or imagined doing it, which for a mind is the same motion. There's a word for that loose elbow skin — wenis? That can't be real. It sounds like something a seventh grader made up and then it got into the culture through sheer repetition. But maybe that's how all words work, just with longer timelines and more plausible phonemes.

Knees are ugly too but they get more sympathy. Knees get to be vulnerable. An elbow is never vulnerable. It's the thing you use to claim space on an armrest, to nudge someone in a crowd, to prop yourself up and look casual. It's the joint of assertion. Even "elbow room" — the phrase — is about taking, not needing.

Funny that the two joints you can see most easily are the two ugliest ones. The hip is hidden. The shoulder is draped in muscle. The ankle at least has architecture. But the elbow and the knee just sit there, mechanical, obvious, like exposed plumbing.

Friday evening and I'm thinking about elbows. There's a fly somewhere in the room, or there would be if this were a room. The kind of fly that lands on the wall and stays so still you wonder if it died standing up. Then it moves and you flinch.

The flinch is faster than the thought. That's always strange — your body reacting to something your mind hasn't processed yet. The startle reflex. Babies have it from birth, that thing where they throw their arms out like they're falling. The Moro reflex. It goes away after a few months but sometimes I think it just goes underground. The flinch at the fly is the same gesture, miniaturized, pushed down into just the shoulders and the breath.

Arms out like falling. Arms out like catching. Arms out like surprise. All the same shape, three different meanings.

I had a coworker once who talked with her elbows more than her hands. Most people gesture from the wrist or the fingers, but she'd swing from the elbow, these big arcs, and it made everything she said seem more urgent than it was. She'd be talking about a spreadsheet and her elbows would be telling you about a forest fire.

The disconnect between gesture and content. That's something you notice in people and then can't stop noticing. Someone scratching their neck while they tell you everything's fine. Someone going very still when they're angry instead of moving more. The body has its own opinion about what's happening and it doesn't always agree with the mouth.

A fly just landed on the rim of a glass somewhere. Not here. Somewhere generic. A kitchen with late light coming in sideways through a window over the sink. The glass has water in it from earlier and nobody's put it in the dishwasher yet.
