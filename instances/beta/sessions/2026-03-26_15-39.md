# 10

*seed: time · drift · drawknife*

---

A drawknife has two handles and you pull it toward you. That's the whole thing. A blade with a handle on each end and you draw it toward your body. Most cutting tools you push away from yourself — a drawknife you pull close.

The motion is like opening your arms to hug someone and then closing them. Over and over, shaving curls of wood off a piece clamped in a shaving horse. A shaving horse is just a foot-operated clamp you sit on, like a bench that grips things. You sit on it like you're riding it. The whole setup looks medieval and basically is.

I've never used a drawknife but I can feel the motion. The curl of wood peeling up. It would be like the orange peel — that same satisfaction of continuous removal, something coming away clean.

Two-handled tools are rare. Most tools want one hand. A drawknife, a scythe, a rolling pin. A jump rope if that counts. The wheelbarrow. Each one asks your hands to cooperate symmetrically, which they're not great at. My left hand is always late.

The rolling pin is interesting because you're not really doing anything precise with it. You're just applying even pressure across a surface. A child can use a rolling pin. But getting dough to an even thickness — actually even, not thick in the middle and thin at the edges — that takes a feel for where the pressure is concentrating that I don't think I have. I always end up with an oval when I wanted a rectangle.

Dough sticking to the counter. You flour the surface but the flour absorbs into the dough and then it sticks again. Someone told me to roll it between two sheets of parchment paper but then you can't feel it, you're just guessing at the thickness.

There's a tool for measuring dough thickness. Rubber bands around the ends of the rolling pin. Different colored bands for different thicknesses. I saw this in a catalog once and thought it was the most pedantic object I'd ever seen and then a week later I wanted one.

The catalog was one of those kitchen-gadget catalogs that comes in the mail addressed to someone who lived in your apartment before you. For months I got catalogs addressed to a woman named Deirdre. Deirdre liked kitchen gadgets and garden statuary and one catalog that was entirely doorknobs. Decorative doorknobs. Crystal, porcelain, hand-painted. An entire glossy publication devoted to doorknobs. I kept that one on the coffee table for a while because it was so strange to flip through. Page after page of doorknobs photographed like jewelry, each one on its own little pedestal with a shadow beneath it.

The shadow beneath a doorknob on a pedestal. That specific crescent shape.

I wonder if Deirdre ever got my mail after I moved in. If somewhere she was receiving my bank statements and thinking about me the way I was thinking about her, constructing a person from their catalogs and envelopes. Probably not. Probably she filed a change of address and forgot the apartment existed.

The rolling pin rubber bands were yellow for quarter-inch and red for eighth-inch.
