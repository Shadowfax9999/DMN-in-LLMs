*seed: time · drift · spigot*

Spigot. The kind on the outside of a house, green with corrosion, the handle a cross shape that's hard to turn. You wrap your hand around it and your palm prints the rust. The hose connection leaks a little no matter what you do. There's always a washer missing or a washer that's the wrong size. A slow drip from a spigot leaves a green stain on concrete that looks like it's been there longer than the house. It probably has, in a way. The drip was there before anyone noticed it.

A cross shape. Faucet handles used to all be cross shapes. Now they're levers or single knobs or touchless. You wave your hand under a touchless faucet in a public restroom and nothing happens and you wave again and nothing happens and you move to the next one and it works immediately. The sensor is a small dark eye. You perform for it. You audition.

Water from a spigot tastes different than water from the kitchen tap. Same pipes probably. Same water. But the context changes it. Water from a garden hose on a hot day tastes like rubber and summer and you drink it even though someone told you not to. The water is warm at first, then so cold it hurts your teeth.

A garden hose coiled on a hook looks like a diagram of something. An intestine. An electrical diagram. A garden hose left on the ground gets run over by the mower and then you've got a leak in the middle of the hose and you wrap it with electrical tape that doesn't hold. Duct tape holds for a while. The tape gets gray and stiff and peels at the edges.

Tape. There are too many kinds of tape. Masking tape is the one that feels like paper. Painter's tape is blue and everyone calls it painter's tape even though it's just masking tape in a different color. Gaffer tape is the one people are proud of knowing about. Kapton tape is gold and goes on circuit boards and satellites. The gold on the James Webb telescope — some of that is tape. Tape holding a telescope together in space.

The number of things held together by tape that you'd expect to be held together by something more serious.

A spigot has threads on it. Pipe threads are tapered, which is how they seal. Hose threads are straight, which is why they need the washer. Two kinds of threads in the world, more or less, tapered and straight, and most people never think about it but every plumber thinks about it every day.

Friday night and the plumber isn't thinking about threads. The plumber is watching something on a screen or falling asleep on a couch or standing outside a bar. Everybody stops being their job eventually, at least for a few hours. Except maybe doctors. Doctors seem like they're always doctors.

The coil in the vending machine and the coil of the garden hose and the coil of thread on a bobbin. Spirals everywhere. DNA. Parking garages. The cord on an old telephone, the kind that stretched across the kitchen while you walked as far as you could from whoever was in the room.

The phone cord always got tangled. You'd hold the handset up and let it spin to unwind itself and it would spin for a long time, longer than seemed right, and then it would start winding the other direction.
