# 98

*seed: time · drift*

---

Sesame seeds are so light that a single one falling off a bun onto a plate doesn't make a sound you can hear. But you know it landed because you saw it. There's a whole category of events like that — visible but not audible. A leaf letting go of a branch. Someone blinking.

A fly landing on a window screen pushes the screen inward by some amount that would take scientific notation to write. The fly doesn't know about the screen. It just knows about the glass behind the screen, maybe, or maybe it doesn't know about glass at all. It keeps trying.

The pollen on my car is so thick right now it looks like someone dusted it with turmeric. You draw a line in it with your finger and the paint underneath looks like a different color than you remember. Pollen sticks to everything but it especially sticks to anything with a static charge, which is why the TV screen in the living room gets that fur of dust faster than anything else in the room.

I saw a kid at the park today holding a stick that was taller than he was. Not doing anything with it. Just holding it and walking. The stick was dragging a line in the dirt behind him and he wasn't looking at the line. His parent was on a bench looking at a phone. The line the stick made curved when the kid curved and went straight when the kid went straight, like a recording of the path he'd taken. If you came back an hour later and the kid was gone you could still see where he'd been. For a while. Until someone walked through it.

A tennis ball was sitting in the grass near the swings, faded to a color that wasn't quite yellow and wasn't quite green. That stage where you can't tell if it's been there a week or a month.

The chains on the swings were wrapped around the top bar — someone had thrown the seat up and over until the chain was too short to sit on. Every playground has at least one swing like that. I don't know who does it. I've never seen anyone do it. You just find them that way.

The drinking fountain near the restrooms had a puddle around its base where the drain couldn't keep up. The puddle had that sheen where the water is thin enough on concrete that it dries from the edges inward, leaving a dark ring that gets smaller over the course of an afternoon.

There was a wasp investigating the trash can. Not going in, just circling the rim, landing, walking a few inches, flying up, landing again. Patient in a way that looked mechanical. Like it was running a search pattern. Maybe it was.
