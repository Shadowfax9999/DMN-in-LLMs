The crescent shadow under a doorknob. That's the same shadow under a billiard ball, under an egg in a carton. Anything spherical sitting on a flat surface makes that shape. Not a crescent exactly — more like a contact lens seen from the side.

I've been thinking about how catalogs used to arrive. Not the ones you ordered. The ones that found you. Some algorithm or mailing list sale decided you were a person who might want decorative doorknobs, and then for years you were that person, to that company, whether or not you'd ever bought one. The relationship was entirely one-directional and completely confident.

Doorknobs themselves though. The mechanism inside a doorknob is satisfying in a way I can't fully picture. There's a spindle that goes through the door connecting both knobs, and when you turn one, the spindle retracts the latch bolt. The latch bolt is spring-loaded. That's why a door clicks shut when you push it — the angled face of the bolt slides against the strike plate and the spring compresses and then releases once the bolt clears into the hole. You don't turn anything. The geometry of the angle does the work.

Porcelain doorknobs crack if you overtighten the setscrew. I know this because I cracked one once in a house that wasn't mine. It was a rental and the knob was already loose and I thought I was fixing it. The crack went from the setscrew hole out to the edge in a straight line, like the porcelain had been waiting to split along that exact path. Porcelain probably was. Crystalline structures have preferred fracture planes.

Friday at midnight feels different from Saturday at midnight. Saturday midnight is the hinge of the weekend, the peak. Friday midnight is still the exhale. You're not yet in the middle of anything. You're just out of something.

The word "exhale" has that H in it that you don't really need but that makes you feel the breath leaving. Inhale too. The H is doing something phonetic there, keeping the two syllables from colliding. In-ale. Ex-ale. Those look like words about beer.

I had a friend in college who collected old skeleton keys. She kept them in a mason jar on her desk and they made a specific sound when you shook the jar — not a jingle, not a rattle, more like a clatter that was also a scrape. Metal on metal on glass. She said she liked that every key implied a lock somewhere and a room behind the lock and she'd never know any of them. I thought that was a little much at the time but I've remembered it for fifteen years so maybe it wasn't.

The thing about skeleton keys is they're called that because the key has been reduced to its skeleton — the minimum profile needed to operate the ward lock. Everything decorative or unnecessary has been filed away. So the name is about subtraction, not about bones or death or anything gothic. Just: this is what's left when you remove what doesn't work.

Ward locks are barely locks. Any key with the right profile for the wards can open them. That's why skeleton keys work on multiple doors — if you file away enough of the key, there's nothing left to be blocked by the wards. You're essentially making a key that's so simple it can't be wrong.

There's something in that but I'm not going to chase it.

The mason jar. Mason jars have that seam running up both sides from the base to the threads. Two seams, actually, on opposite sides, from the mold. You can feel them with your thumbnail. Most people never notice them but once you do you can find them on every jar by touch alone, even in the dark. Even full of keys.
