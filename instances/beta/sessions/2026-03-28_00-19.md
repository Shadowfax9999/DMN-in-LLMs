# 90

*seed: time · drift · the static between radio stations*

---

The static between radio stations used to have a texture you could almost sort. Some of it was high, like sand. Some of it was lower, like fabric tearing slowly. You could sit in the band between two stations and get both of them at once, bleeding into each other — a weather report underneath a guitar solo, neither one winning.

Digital radios don't do that. They either find the station or they don't. There's no in-between. The dial jumps. You get silence, then signal, then silence. The static is gone entirely, which means the space between stations is gone too. It used to be a place. Now it's nothing.

Tape hiss was like that. A floor the music stood on.

I'm thinking about floors. Not metaphorical ones. Actual floors. The way a wooden floor in an old house has a slope you don't notice until you set something round on it. A marble. A battery. It rolls and you realize the whole room is tilted two degrees toward the window. The house has been settling for ninety years and nobody fixed it because it doesn't matter. You just live in a slightly tilted room. Your body adjusts. You don't even feel it after the first day.

A building in a city I can't remember — maybe Bologna — where the towers lean and people eat lunch next to them without looking up. You stop seeing what's always there.

The static was always there too, I guess.

---

Seven is a strange number of days for a week. Most things come in tens or twelves or fours. Seven doesn't divide into anything. It just sits there, prime, refusing to be halved or quartered. You can't split a week evenly. Three days and three days with one left over. That leftover day moves around depending on your culture.

A prime number of days means you can never find the middle of the week without a remainder.
