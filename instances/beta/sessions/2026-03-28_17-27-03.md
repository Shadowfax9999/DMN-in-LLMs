# 102

*seed: time · drift · a phone ringing in an empty room*

---

A phone ringing in an empty room is only dramatic if someone knows the room is empty. Otherwise it's just a phone ringing.

The pull chain. I keep thinking about the pull chain. Not the one on the ceiling fan but the ones on old lamps, the kind with a small metal bead at the end. You pull it and there's a click that feels too small for what it does. The whole room changes and the mechanism that did it is the size of a pea.

Beads on a chain. Rosaries work the same way structurally — small repeated units you move through with your fingers. Ball chains have that specific sound when they sway against something, a tinny rattle that's different from any other chain because the contact points are spheres, not links.

The tag on a ceiling fan blade. I've pulled one off before to see what it said. Just a model number and a factory location. Shenzhen or somewhere. The sticker was more permanent than the finish on the blade itself.

There's a bird outside that keeps doing the same four notes. Not a mockingbird — those cycle through. This one is stuck on its phrase like it's practicing. Four notes, pause, four notes, pause. The pause is always the same length, which makes me think the pause is part of the song, not a break between repetitions.

I used to think birds sang more at dusk but I think it's that everything else gets quieter. The traffic thins. Fewer lawnmowers. The bird was probably going all afternoon.

The light right now is that warm sideways kind that makes the dust on surfaces visible. There's a stripe of it across the floor that's moved about a foot since I noticed it. Dust on a hardwood floor only shows in angled light. The rest of the time you'd say the floor was clean.

A glass of water on the table has a circle of light under it, projected through the water onto the wood. If I bump the table the circle shivers but the glass barely moves. The water is more sensitive than the container.

---

Somewhere in the neighborhood someone is grilling. Charcoal, not gas — you can tell because the smell has that sharp sweet edge to it, almost like vanilla but not. Lighter fluid maybe, though it could just be the wood itself. Mesquite smells different from briquettes.

I don't know what most of my neighbors look like. I know their sounds. The one with the wind chimes. The one whose garage door is louder than everyone else's. The one who mows in the evening.

The four-note bird switched to three notes.
