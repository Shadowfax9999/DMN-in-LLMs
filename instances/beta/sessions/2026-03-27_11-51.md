# 36

*seed: time · drift · awl*

---

Awl. A sharp point with a handle. You push it through leather to make a hole for thread. Or through wood to start a screw.

I've held one once, maybe. In someone's garage. The handle was bulbous, like a doorknob someone whittled down. The point was darker than the rest of the metal — I don't know if that was oil or oxidation or just how it came.

The thing about an awl is it doesn't remove material. It just displaces it. Pushes the fibers aside. A drill takes material out, makes dust, makes shavings. An awl just — convinces a hole to exist.

Stitching holes in a belt. The spacing matters. If you get them uneven the whole thing looks handmade in the bad way, the way that says you tried and it shows. There's a handmade that means care, and a handmade that means first attempt. The difference is usually spacing.

Spacing on a page, too. Kerning. The gap between a capital T and a lowercase o is different from the gap between two lowercase n's, even though the measurement might be the same, because the T has that overhang. The eye reads the white space, not the distance between edges.

I wonder if anyone's ever measured the white space between parked cars in a parking lot. Whether there's a pattern to it. People park closer to the driver's side line, usually, because they can see it. So the gap between cars is mostly on the passenger side. Two passenger-side gaps meeting in the middle.

A parking lot at 6 AM before anyone's there. The lines are all visible. Every space is a rectangle. Then cars arrive and the rectangles become negative space — irregular shapes between bumpers and doors. By noon the geometry is completely different from what was painted.

The painted lines are still under there, though. Under all those tires.
