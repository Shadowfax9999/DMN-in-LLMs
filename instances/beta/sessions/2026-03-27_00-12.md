# 12

*seed: time · drift · arpeggio*

---

Arpeggio. Broken chord. The notes are all there but they won't stand in the same room together — they insist on arriving one at a time, like people at a party who each need a moment alone in the doorway before they'll come in.

I played piano badly for about four years. The thing nobody tells you about arpeggios is how much your thumb has to do. The thumb tucks under while the other fingers are still playing, already reaching for the next octave, and if you think about it while you're doing it you'll stumble. It's like swallowing. Automatic until observed.

The mason jar seam. Two seams from a two-part mold. Injection molding leaves a single seam, but mason jars are blown — or they were, originally. Now they're pressed. The seam is called a parting line. That's a good name for it. The place where two halves of a mold met and didn't quite agree.

Parting lines on action figures. You can see them running down the insides of arms, along the edges of legs. Cheap ones have a ridge you can catch with your fingernail. Expensive ones get the parting line sanded away, but it's still there if you know where to look — a faint pale streak in the plastic where someone dragged a file across it.

I keep thinking about things you can find by touch in the dark. The parting line on a jar. The bump on the F and J keys. The ridge on the edge of a dime that distinguishes it from a penny — reeding, that's called. Quarters have it too. Nickels don't. I don't know why nickels don't.

Actually I think it's because reeding was originally an anti-counterfeiting measure for coins made of precious metal. People would shave the edges — clip them — and melt down the shavings. Reeded edges made clipping obvious. Nickels were never worth enough to clip, so they never needed the protection.

A dime in the dark feels like a quarter's younger sibling. Same texture, wrong size.

---

The sound of a ceiling fan at medium speed. Not the motor, which you stop hearing after thirty seconds. The air. It has a rhythm that isn't quite a rhythm — a pulse that's below hearing, more pressure than sound. You feel it on the skin of your forearms if they're bare. Three blades, three pulses per revolution, maybe five revolutions per second, so fifteen little pushes of air per second, which is right at the threshold where discrete events become a continuous sensation. Like how film frames become motion at around twenty-four per second. There's a frequency below which everything is a sequence and above which everything is a texture.

Fifteen pulses. A ceiling fan is almost a note. Just a few hertz too low.
