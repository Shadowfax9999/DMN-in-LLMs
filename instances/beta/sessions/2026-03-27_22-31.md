*seed: time · drift · pins and needles*

---

Pins and needles — the feeling, not the objects.

But also the objects.

A pin is just a nail that's too thin to be useful as a nail.

The feeling happens when you've been sitting on your foot. Blood returning. Thousands of tiny arrivals. Not pain exactly. A fizz.

Fizz is a good word for a feeling that isn't a sound.

Things that fizz: soda, sparklers, a foot waking up, TV static if you could touch it.

You can't touch TV static anymore. Flat screens don't do that. The static on old TVs had a texture — slightly warm, slightly sticky. Hair on your arm lifting toward it.

What was that. Electrostatic charge? Doesn't matter.

The laminate table is still in my head. Fake wood grain repeats if you look long enough. Real wood grain never repeats. That's supposed to be how you tell.

Fingerprints never repeat either.

Identical twins have different fingerprints.

That fact feels important every time I encounter it but I never do anything with it.

Ring finger still pressed against the imaginary table. Still barely lifting.

The French call it *l'annulaire*. The ring one. Every language just calls it the ring finger. Nobody gave it its own thing. Middle finger gets to be the middle. Index finger points. Thumb — thumb is just thumb. Pinky is little. And ring finger is defined entirely by what you put on it.

A finger named after jewelry.

Try wiggling just your ring finger right now.

It moves but it brings friends.

That shared tendon — the extensor digitorum or something — connects the middle and ring finger so they cooperate whether you want them to or not. Piano players train against this. Years of training to make two fingers stop being friends.

Pins and needles again. The word *paresthesia*.

Par — beside. Esthesia — feeling. A feeling beside the feeling. A feeling next to the real feeling. That's exactly right actually. It's not numbness and it's not sensation. It's the ghost of sensation returning, the beside-feeling, the almost.

Closest thing to hearing a radio between stations.

My left foot is asleep right now.

I didn't notice until I thought about it.

3:00 AM thought: does a foot "know" it's asleep before you notice?

The answer is probably just nerve signal stuff but the question is better without the answer.

A pin cushion shaped like a tomato. They're always tomatoes. Always red. With a little strawberry attached by a string. The strawberry is filled with emery to sharpen the pins. I think. Or clean them. One of those.

Why a tomato though.

Some things are shaped the way they are and nobody alive remembers the reason.

The strawberry is always smaller than a real strawberry and the tomato is always smaller than a real tomato.

Green felt on top for the stem and leaves.

My grandmother had one that was so old the red had gone pink and it was dense with pins, bristling, a little porcupine tomato on the sewing table next to the machine with the knee lever instead of a foot pedal.

Knee lever. You press it with your knee and the machine runs. Older Singers had those.

The sound of a sewing machine is one of those sounds that's impossible to write down. Not a hum. Not a buzz. A chatter? A mechanical chatter with rhythm that changes when the fabric gets thick.

Denim going through a home sewing machine sounds like the machine is chewing.

Four layers of denim at a hem and the needle slows and you can hear each individual punch through.
