# 9

*seed: time · drift · autolyse*

---

Autolyse. That's bread. You mix flour and water and just leave it alone for twenty minutes, half an hour, and the gluten starts organizing itself without you doing anything. The protein strands find each other. You come back and the dough is already smooth, already wants to be something.

I've never been good at that part — the leaving alone. I always want to start kneading immediately. There's something about dough that makes your hands want to work it. It's the same impulse as bubble wrap. The material is asking to be squeezed.

But the autolyse works better. The dough that sits there doing nothing comes out more extensible than the dough you've been beating on for ten minutes. Which — I don't know what to do with that. It's not a lesson. It's just a fact about wheat protein.

The word itself though. Auto-lysis. Self-breaking. Which in biology means a cell digesting itself with its own enzymes. That's a much darker word than what bakers are using it for. Bakers borrowed a word for self-destruction and used it to mean resting. The flour is breaking down its own starches, enzymatically, so I guess the name is technically accurate, but the connotation is completely different. In breadmaking it's gentle. In cell biology it's what happens after death.

A parking lot seen from above — I was in a parking garage once, maybe fourth floor, looking down at the regular grid of cars, and one space had a shopping cart in it instead of a car. Just sitting there in the middle of the space, perfectly centered, like it had parked itself. Nobody was near it.

The way light falls differently on the fourth floor of a parking garage. You're above the trees at that point, usually. The light is more direct. And the sound changes — you can hear the street but it's all reflected off the building across from you, so it comes from the wrong direction. Someone honks to your left and the sound arrives from your right.

I wonder if bats have that problem. They probably don't — their whole system is built for processing echoes. But birds. Do birds ever get confused by reflected sound in cities? A robin hearing its own song bounce off a plate-glass window and thinking there's a rival. I have no idea if that happens. It seems like it should.

The fourth floor of that garage smelled like warm concrete. Concrete in sun has a specific smell that's different from concrete in shade, and I don't know if that's the concrete outgassing something or if it's just the temperature making you perceive the mineral smell more strongly. Your nose works differently when the air is warm — molecules move faster, more of them reach your receptors. So maybe warm concrete doesn't smell more, you just smell it more.

The sensation of peeling an orange in one continuous strip. The pith stays on the fruit and the peel curls away and you're trying not to break it, your thumbnail scoring a path, and the oil from the rind gets under your fingernail and stays there for hours. That oil is flammable — you can squeeze a piece of orange peel near a candle flame and it sparks. Little jets of citrus oil igniting. I did this at a dinner party once and someone acted like I'd performed a magic trick and someone else looked at me like I was going to burn the house down.

The orange oil under your fingernail. You keep catching the smell all evening. Reaching for a glass, turning a page. It's like the orange follows you home.
