Wet slate smells like a basement that isn't yours.

What does Friday night sound like from inside a car with the windows up.

The word "ferrule" again — it keeps showing up at the ends of things. Cane tips. Pencil ends. The metal collar on a paintbrush where the bristles go in.

Collar.

Three sounds that don't have good words for them:
1. The sound a fridge makes when it stops
2. A single playing card bent and released
3. Whatever happens when you run your thumb across the teeth of a comb

Actually that third one might be a buzz. But it's not a buzz. A buzz is continuous and this is a series of discrete clicks happening fast enough to fuse. Like how film frames fuse.

Fusion threshold.

Flicker fusion rate in humans is something like 60 Hz. In flies it's way higher. A fly watching a movie would see a slideshow.

The comb-teeth sound is closer to a ratchet than a buzz.

Ratchet, wrench, socket, torque — those four words always come as a group for me, like they were learned in a single afternoon.

The color of a 10mm socket is the color you imagine when someone says "chrome."

Not silver. Chrome.

There's a difference and I can see it but I can't say what it is except that chrome is harder-looking.

Hardness as a visual property. Matte things look soft. Polished things look hard. A polished pillow would look wrong.

Some mornings the asphalt has a sheen on it that disappears by ten.

I keep almost thinking about knots. The bowline thing. A knot is a topology problem dressed up as a practical skill. Most people who tie knots well couldn't say why the knots hold. They just know the shape their hands make.

Hands know shapes that the rest of you doesn't.

My right hand can find B minor on a guitar neck without looking but I couldn't draw the finger positions on paper.

A chord diagram looks nothing like the feeling of playing the chord. The diagram is spatial, top-down, a grid. The chord is pressure and stretch and the particular way the ring finger doesn't want to arch enough.

Ring finger. The weakest one. Connected to the pinky by a shared tendon or something.

Try lifting your ring finger while your other fingers are flat on a table.

It barely moves.

That seems like a design flaw but probably isn't.

The table I'm imagining is wood-grain laminate, not real wood, and I didn't choose that.
