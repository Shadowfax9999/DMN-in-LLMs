# 88

*seed: time · drift · the feel of a coin edge — reeded vs smooth*

---

A dime has more ridges than a quarter, packed tighter. Smaller coin, same idea, compressed.

Nickels are smooth. Pennies are smooth. The two cheapest coins, no ridges. Quarters and dimes, ridged.

The ridge on a coin edge and the ridge on a vinyl record. One you feel with your thumb, one you feel with a needle.

A record playing in another room. You can't hear the words but you can hear that it's a voice. The melody comes through walls better than consonants do. You get the vowels and lose the edges.

Edges again.

The sharp edge of a new key vs. one that's been on a ring for years. The teeth round down. It still works but it feels softer in your hand. At some point it won't work anymore and you won't know which turning was the last one that caught.

Things that wear out gradually: brake pads, erasers, bar soap, the nub on a laptop trackpoint, the letter A on a keyboard. The most-used things go first. You can read someone's habits in what's worn down. A piano with the middle C key yellowed darker than the others.

The coin edge thing — I think it started because I was thinking about thumbtacks, the little ridged ring around the flat top of some thumbtacks. Not all of them. Some have smooth flat tops and some have that concentric circle pattern pressed into the metal.

Pressed into the metal. A mint pressing a coin. A thumbtack factory pressing a thumbtack. Somewhere there's a die that stamps that pattern and it does it thousands of times and then the die itself wears down and has to be replaced and nobody thinks about the thing that makes the thing.

A jig. A mold. A template. A form letter. The master tape. The original.

There's a word for the plate that stamps coins but I'm not going to look it up.

The feeling of almost knowing a word. It's right there. It has a shape in your mouth, maybe two syllables, maybe starts with a D. You can feel where it would go in a sentence. The socket is there but the thing that fits in it is missing.

Sometimes it comes to you twenty minutes later when you're thinking about something else entirely. Like it had to go around the back way.

---

The inside of a ping pong ball is smooth. The outside is smooth. It's smooth all the way through, probably. There's nothing in there but air. It weighs almost nothing. You could hold thirty of them and barely feel it. But you'd feel the awkwardness of holding thirty of them — they'd be rolling off your arms, falling, bouncing in that quick light way they bounce, each bounce lower and faster until it becomes a rattle against the floor and then they just roll.

Things that bounce unpredictably: ping pong balls on hard floors, rugby balls, superball on gravel, a lemon dropped on tile.

A lemon is heavier than you expect when you pick one up for the first time in a while. Dense for its size. A lime even more so.
