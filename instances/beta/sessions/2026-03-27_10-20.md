Colophon. The part of the book nobody reads. After the last page, after the acknowledgments, after even the index — the colophon sits there describing itself. Set in Garamond 11/14. Printed on 60lb cream offset. Bound in signatures of sixteen. It's a book talking about its own body.

I had a paperback once where the colophon was longer than the author bio. Three paragraphs about the typeface, its designer, the year it was cut. The typeface had more backstory than the author did.

Garamond. I always thought there was one Garamond but there are dozens, and most of them aren't based on Garamond's actual work, they're based on Jean Jannon's typefaces from sixty years later that got misattributed. So when you select Garamond from a dropdown menu you're usually selecting Jannon. Nobody calls it Jannon.

The feel of a page matters more than I usually admit. Some paperbacks have pages that feel like they've already been read by someone else — soft, almost fuzzy at the edges. Others feel like wax paper. You can tell the difference with your eyes closed.

There's a specific sound a basketball makes on an outdoor court versus a gym floor. Outside it's flatter, dryer. The ball sounds heavier. Inside, the bounce has this bright snap that comes back at you off the walls and ceiling, so you hear the bounce and then you hear the room hearing the bounce. Playing alone in a gym is one of the loneliest sounds. Every dribble announces how empty the space is.

I'm thinking about how gyms smell like rubber and floor wax and something slightly sweet that might just be old sweat that's soaked into the wood over decades. The smell is in the floor, not the air. You'd have to sand it out.

The lines on a gym floor — all those overlapping rectangles and arcs in different colors for different sports. Badminton in red, basketball in black, volleyball in blue, and none of them agree about where the important parts of the room are. Same space, completely different geometries layered on top of each other. If you only saw one set of lines you'd move through the room differently than if you saw another.

I played badminton exactly once and the shuttlecock moves nothing like I expected. It decelerates so fast. You hit it hard and it just dies in the air halfway across the court, like it remembered gravity all at once.

The feathers are usually goose.
