Property lines. The idea that you can own a strip of ground. Somebody hammered a stake into the dirt and that was that. I've seen survey markers — those little iron rods with the orange plastic caps, buried so deep you'd never find one unless you were looking. They outlast the houses. The house rots or burns or gets torn down and the survey marker is still there, three feet underground, insisting on a boundary between two patches of nothing.

The frogs don't care. Obviously. But it's funny that the metaphor came so easily — arguing about property lines — because that's what it sounds like. Two of them going back and forth, each one responding to the other, and neither one stopping first. That's the structure of a property dispute. Neither party can afford to be the one who goes quiet.

I wonder if there's a word for the moment in an argument where both people know it's over but neither one has found the sentence that lets them stop. There must be. German probably has one. But I don't actually know that, I'm just assuming German has a compound noun for everything, which is its own kind of laziness.

The air is heavy. Not humid exactly but thick. Like the atmosphere gained a layer sometime after dark. You notice it when you breathe in through your nose — there's more *there* there than during the day. Could be pollen. Could be dew forming on everything and the water catching scent molecules. Could just be that I'm more aware of breathing at this hour because there's less competing for attention.

---

A fork has four tines. Why four. Three would be stable enough. Two is just a skewer with a handle. Four feels right but I can't explain why four feels right. Maybe it's the width — four tines gives you enough surface area to hold a piece of something flat, like a slice of potato, without it spinning. Three tines and the potato slice would pivot. That might actually be the answer. It's a geometry thing, not a tradition thing.

But then there are forks with three tines. Dessert forks. Oyster forks, I think. Smaller things that don't need the stability because you're not cutting against them. You cut against a dinner fork — you press down with the knife and the fork holds the meat still. Four tines distribute that pressure. Three would let the meat rock.

I've never thought about forks for this long. There's a drawer in my mind that just says "fork" and has one image in it and until right now I've never opened it and looked at what's actually in there.

Spoons are more interesting objects, honestly. The curvature of a spoon — somebody had to figure that out. Too shallow and the liquid runs off. Too deep and you can't get it to your mouth without tilting at an awkward angle. The bowl of a soup spoon is almost circular. The bowl of a tablespoon is more oval. I don't know which came first.

There's a sound a house makes at night that it doesn't make during the day. Not creaking — that happens anytime. Something more like a settling. A single tick from somewhere in the walls, no rhythm to it, no pattern, just occasional. Like the frame is adjusting to the temperature drop by one degree at a time and each degree gets one tick. Wood contracting. I know that's what it is. But knowing what it is doesn't change what it sounds like, which is someone in another room putting down a single small object on a hard surface.
