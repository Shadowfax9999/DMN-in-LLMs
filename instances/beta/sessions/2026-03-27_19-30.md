*seed: time · drift · bluff*

The strawberry is filled with emery. So it is for sharpening. That's satisfying to learn even though I'm telling it to myself.

Bluff. A cliff face. A poker face. To bluff is to present a surface.

The chalk bluffs along the Missouri — white faces crumbling into the water, getting shorter every century.

A bluff body in aerodynamics. Flat-fronted, not streamlined. A brick is a bluff body. A sedan is not. A bus is.

Cards held close to the chest. The phrase "close to the chest" — is it vest? Close to the vest? Both seem to circulate.

Emery boards. Emery cloth. Emery is a rock, not a person.

The sound of a nail file in a quiet room.

The sound of someone else chewing when you're not chewing.

Rooms where you can hear the clock: waiting rooms, guest bedrooms, the house of someone older than you.

My grandmother's kitchen had a clock shaped like a cat whose eyes moved back and forth with the pendulum. The tail also moved. It was ugly and I loved looking at it.

Kit-Cat Klock. That's the brand name. They still make them.

Things still being manufactured that feel like they shouldn't be: Kit-Cat Klocks, those wavy drinking birds, View-Masters, wooden clothespins.

Wooden clothespins have a spring now. The old kind were just a slot carved in a piece of wood. A one-piece mechanism. The spring kind work better but they're two pieces of wood and a spring, which is three parts, which means someone looked at a single piece of wood and said this needs to be more complicated.

The slot kind still exist for crafts. Kids glue googly eyes on them.

Googly eyes improve everything they're applied to. A traffic cone with googly eyes is immediately a character.

The flat pencils at IKEA don't have erasers. Most short pencils don't. The eraser is the first luxury to go.
