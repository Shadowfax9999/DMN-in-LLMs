A grapefruit is about right. It feels like what it looks like it should weigh.

Oranges vary. The small tight ones from the net bag are dense and firm and the big navel ones feel almost hollow by comparison, like the pith is taking up space without contributing anything.

Pith. White, dry, bitter. It pulls apart in strips if you're peeling slowly. There's a point in peeling an orange where you've committed — too much pith under your thumbnail, the oil already stinging that tiny cut you forgot about — and you just want to get through it, but the membrane keeps tearing in the wrong direction, and you end up with a ragged half-peeled thing trailing white ribbons.

The smell stays on your hands for hours.

Some smells are like that. Garlic. Gasoline. The copper smell from handling a fistful of pennies. You wash your hands and it's still there, or you think it's still there, which might be the same thing.

I can picture a fistful of pennies but not a specific penny. The Lincoln Memorial side — is it still the Lincoln Memorial? It changed at some point. Four different designs, maybe. I know one of them has a shield.

Shields as design elements on coins, on crests, on the fronts of buses. The Greyhound logo is a greyhound. That's honest. The dog is running. The bus is going somewhere. Fine.

---

The number 7 looks like it's leaning. 1 is standing straight. 4 is sitting down. I don't know what 3 is doing. 8 is the only number that's the same upside down. 0 also, but 0 is barely a number, it's more like a placeholder that got promoted.

6 and 9 are the same shape arguing about which way is up.

I had a clock once where the 6 fell off and slid down to the bottom of the glass. So there were two sixes at the bottom. The clock still worked. You could still tell what time it was. You always can, roughly, from the hand positions alone. The numbers on a clock are decoration.

A clock with no numbers, just the hands and the marks. A clock with no marks, just the hands. A clock with one hand. You'd lose precision but you'd still get the hour. A sundial is basically one hand and no face at all — just the ground, and whatever shadow falls on it.

The shadow moves the wrong direction from what you'd expect. Or the right direction. I can never remember which way clock hands go on a sundial. They go the same way — that's where clockwise comes from. Northern hemisphere sundials. In the southern hemisphere the shadow goes the other way, but they still use the same clocks.

That seems like it would have bothered someone in 1600.
