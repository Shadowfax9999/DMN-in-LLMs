# 2

*seed: time · drift*

---

Escrow. Es-crow. Like a crow holding something for you.

The fitted sheet is in the dryer right now and I already know what's going to happen.

---

Lozenge-shaped windows in old churches. Not the big rose windows, the small ones in the side walls, set at an angle so rain runs off. Diamond panes held together with lead came. Came — the H-shaped strips. The word "came" for that feels wrong, like it should mean something else.

Diagonal things feel faster than horizontal things. A stripe on a racing car. A slash versus a hyphen. The diamond versus the square — same shape, but one of them is just sitting there and the other one is doing something.

---

Two cups in the sink is a specific number. One cup means you live alone or you cleaned up. Three cups means you've been putting it off. Two cups means this morning, two people, coffee.

Or one person, two coffees.

---

White chocolate isn't chocolate. It's cocoa butter and sugar and vanilla. It tastes like the idea of sweetness without any of the thing that makes sweetness interesting. Like a major chord with no seventh.

A major seventh chord sounds like a hotel lobby.

A dominant seventh sounds like something needs to happen next.

A diminished seventh sounds like a silent film villain tying someone to railroad tracks. Four notes equally spaced, and somehow that's sinister. Play them on a organ and it's a haunted house. Play them on a guitar and it's Django Reinhardt.

---

Colonel. From Italian *colonnello*, the guy who led a column. The French took it and did something to the middle of it and then English took the French version and pronounced it like the Italian version but kept the French spelling. Three languages making one word worse in sequence.

How people fall asleep on planes: I think it's the white noise. The engines hit a frequency that sits right on top of everything else and flattens it. Your brain stops tracking individual sounds and gives up. The hum is so total it becomes silence.

Or they're just tired.

---

The clock thing. 2:30 on a weekday afternoon. This is the hour that doesn't belong to anything. Morning had its coffee and its structure. Evening will have dinner and the feeling of winding down. But 2:30 is unclaimed. It's the part of the day you can never remember afterward. What did I do between 2 and 4? I don't know. I was there.

The light at 2:30 in spring is high and flat. No long shadows yet. Everything evenly lit, which makes everything look equally important, which makes nothing stand out, which is maybe why the hour slides past without registering.

A cardinal outside, doing that two-note thing. The first note higher than the second. Over and over. It's not a song, it's a claim — this tree, this yard, this frequency is mine. Then silence. Then again. The repetition isn't patience, it's insistence.

I think the female cardinal sings too but with a slightly different pattern.
