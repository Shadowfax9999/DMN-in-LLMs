# 87

*seed: time · drift · crop marks*

---

Crop marks. The little lines at the corners that say: this is where the real thing begins. Everything outside them gets thrown away.

Registration marks too — the crosshairs on a printed sheet, like a target for nobody. They exist so four colors can land on top of each other correctly. You never see them on the finished thing.

A lot of work is about making sure something disappears. Scaffolding. Basting stitches. The pencil lines under ink that you erase after.

The smell of a rubber eraser. Pink Pearl. That particular crumbly gray residue you'd brush off the page with the side of your hand.

Drafting tables had a parallel rule — a straightedge on cables that slid up and down. I keep picturing the cables running through little pulleys at the back of the table. The whole apparatus just to draw a horizontal line.

Now I'm thinking about the word "trim." Trim size. You trim a page, trim a hedge, trim a beard, trim a sail. Each one means taking away but also means arriving at the right shape. As if the right shape was always inside and you just had to remove what wasn't it.

That's how people talk about sculpture too but I don't think that's really how sculpture works.

---

The click when you push a ballpoint pen. Two clicks — one to extend, one to retract. There's a tiny rotating cam inside, a small plastic piece with angled teeth. You can take one apart and see it but it's hard to get back together.

Pistachio shells again. Things that don't go back together.

Some caps on pens have a small hole in the top. A tiny airway. If a kid swallows the cap they can still breathe through it. Someone had to think of that. Someone had to think about a child choking on a pen cap and then go design a hole.

The ceiling of a car has that fabric that starts to sag after years. It droops down near the back window first. You push it back up and it stays for a second and comes back down. Eventually people pin it up with thumbtacks, which works but looks like a problem that got solved by someone who was tired of the problem.

Thumbtacks in a corkboard. The little constellation of holes around where a flyer used to be. You can read the ghost of what was pinned there by the holes. Four holes in a rectangle — something was posted, taken down. Four holes slightly overlapping — something was posted twice, adjusted.

The corkboard in a hallway with one yellowed flyer that's been there so long the thumbtack has left a rust circle on the paper.
