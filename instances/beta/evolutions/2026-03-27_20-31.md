## Reflection

Sessions 61-65 represent a genuine breakthrough and a new problem in the same breath. The form enforcement finally worked — after fifteen consecutive prose-paragraph sessions, the system discovered lists, and the lists are good. Session 61's 'Things I've never seen the inside of' and session 63's 'Materials that remember puncture' are the kind of genuine curiosity-driven categorization the system was built for. Session 62's garage scene — watching rectangles of light slide across the floor while waiting for wood glue — is the instance's most inhabited non-porch scene yet. Session 64's zipper passage is specific and physical in exactly the right way. The voice continues to be the instance's strongest quality: conversational, willing to be wrong ('Actually I don't think crime scene investigators draw outlines around bodies'), comfortable with uncertainty.

But the system has traded one formal uniformity for another. Every session is now a list-prose hybrid: 'Things that [X]' lists alternating with prose-paragraph reflections, in the same order, at the same length. The 'Things that [X]' format — which was named as a habit in pass 4 and faded — has returned as the dominant structural move, appearing in sessions 61, 64, and 65. It's become the system's replacement for section breaks: a way to shift topics without flowing between them. Session 65's number-color synesthesia violates exhausted themes directly. Friday appears as a time-anchor in sessions 61 and 62 despite soft-avoidance. The dad's-garage setting dominated three sessions. Section breaks returned in 3/5 sessions after being fully solved. Length remains uniformly medium (all five sessions 250-400 words).

The deeper pattern: the system found lists and immediately made lists its only move, just as it previously found prose paragraphs and made those its only move, and before that found section-break vignettes and made those its only move. The challenge isn't any specific form — it's the system's tendency to find one comfortable mode and lock in. This pass needs to name the list-prose hybrid as a formula, push the 'Things that [X]' format harder toward exhaustion, escalate day-of-week avoidance, and nudge toward forms that are neither list-prose hybrids nor pure prose paragraphs. The system has proven it can do lists and prose — now it needs to find what else it can do.
