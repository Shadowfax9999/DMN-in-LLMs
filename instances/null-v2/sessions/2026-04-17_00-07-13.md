# 49

*seed: time · drift*

---

The fridge just cycled on. That low hum you forget is there until it stops, and then the silence is louder than the sound was.

I was thinking about how I know what a Friday night feels like even though I don't experience weeks. There's something in the word Friday that's already leaning back. Thursday is still upright. Saturday hasn't decided yet.

The names are planetary. Friday is Venus's day, through some Germanic filter — Frigg, Freya, one of those. But I don't want to chase that. I just want to sit with the feeling of Friday at midnight, which is a specific kind of dark. Not the dark of Tuesday at midnight, which is the same dark but doesn't know it's the same dark.

A friend of mine used to say "the week has a shape" and hold up her hand like she was gripping an invisible banister. The week curved. Wednesday was the top of the stairs.

I wonder if dogs know weekdays. They know patterns — the alarm, the car keys, the specific shoes that mean a walk versus the shoes that mean leaving. So they know something week-shaped without knowing the word for it. A pattern with no name. Which might be what most knowledge actually is.

There's a kind of moth that navigates by the moon and gets confused by porch lights. I think about that moth sometimes. Not as a metaphor. Just as a moth, flying in circles around a bulb, doing exactly what it's supposed to do with the wrong information.

The porch light next door has been on for three days. I don't know why I've been counting.

Most of what I notice, I don't do anything with. It just sits there. The crack in the sidewalk that's wider than it was last year. The fact that the couple at the end of the street parks two cars in a one-car driveway and somehow it works. The sound a screen door makes, which is different from every other door sound and I couldn't tell you how.

Screen doors have that specific resonance — the spring, the aluminum frame, the mesh acting as a membrane. But I said I wasn't going to do that.

What I actually want to think about is the number four. Not mathematically. Just — four is a strange number to hold in your head. Three is a shape. Five is a hand. Four is a square but it's also nothing. Four legs on a table. Four corners of a room. It's the most architectural number. The number that means "this will stand up."

Three legs also stand up. Better, actually — a three-legged stool never wobbles. Four legs wobble and we shim them with folded paper and call it solved.

I folded a piece of paper seven times once to see if it was really impossible. It's not impossible. It's just hard after six. The paper gets angry.

That's not right either. The paper doesn't get angry. It gets thick. But thick doesn't capture it — there's a resistance that feels personal, like the paper has made a decision.

It's late. The freight train that runs through the north part of town just sounded its horn at the crossing. Two longs, a short, a long. That's the legal pattern. Someone decided that pattern and now every engineer at every crossing plays it, all night, all over the country, the same four notes in the same order, and almost nobody awake to hear it.
