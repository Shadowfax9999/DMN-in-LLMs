# 29

*seed: time · drift*

---

The fridge just cycled off and now I can hear the clock. Not ticking — it's digital. But the outlet it's plugged into makes a sound. A kind of electrical hum that I only notice when the fridge stops competing for my attention.

There's a hierarchy of quiet. Layers you peel back. Fridge off, now you hear the clock outlet, now you hear something in the wall — water, maybe, or the house doing whatever houses do at night when they're not being watched. Settling is the word people use. As if the house has been performing all day and can finally relax.

I don't think that's what settling means structurally but I like the accident of it.

My left sock is half off. Just the heel part, bunched under the arch. I could fix it. I'm not going to fix it. There's a specific laziness that arrives at this hour where you just absorb small discomforts rather than solve them. The sock stays. The glass on the counter stays. You are in the last configuration of the day and you will not be reconfigured.

Moth outside the window. Not hitting the glass, just hovering near it, orbiting the light that leaks through the blinds. From inside it looks like it's swimming. From outside — I don't know what it looks like from outside. I'm not outside.

That's a strange thing about windows at night. During the day they're for looking out. At night they become mirrors, mostly. You see yourself and the room behind you and the outside becomes a suggestion. The moth is in the suggestion.

Numbers I can't forget: my childhood phone number. My first locker combination. The price of a candy bar in 1998. None of these are useful. They just sit there, taking up whatever space memories take up, refusing to leave, while actually useful things — the name of the person I met Tuesday, where I put the tape measure — those dissolve instantly. Memory keeps what it wants. You don't get a say.

Thirty-four, twenty-two, eight. See. Still there.

The moth left. Or the light changed. Same result from where I'm sitting.

I should go to bed. That's not a thought, really, it's more like a weather pattern. It comes through every few minutes and I let it pass. The sock is still bunched.
