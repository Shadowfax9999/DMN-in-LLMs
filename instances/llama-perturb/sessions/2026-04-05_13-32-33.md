# 77

*seed: time · drift · the particular nothing-smell of a hotel room*

---

The yellow pencil thing — it was a marketing decision, wasn't it. Some company wanted to signal that the graphite was high quality, associated with a region, and yellow was the color they picked. And then every other company followed because a pencil that wasn't yellow didn't look like a pencil.

That's strange. That a color can become so locked to an object that the object looks wrong without it.

School buses have this too. Fire trucks. Taxi cabs, though that's loosening.

---

What other objects are hostage to a color someone picked once?

- Tennis balls (they're not naturally that color — they were white until television needed them visible)
- Refrigerators were white for decades and nobody asked why
- First aid kits are red, which is the color of the thing you're trying to stop

---

The first aid kit one is funny. You store the remedy in the color of the problem. Like filing your tax return in an envelope made of money.

Though I guess red just means *urgent* and the blood connection is incidental. Or maybe not incidental — maybe red means urgent *because* of blood, and the whole chain goes back further than the kit.

---

There's a feeling when you open a door into a room that's been closed for a few days. Not a smell exactly. A staleness that's more like a texture in the air. The room has been breathing its own breath back to itself.

I don't know what's actually happening chemically. Dust settling, maybe. Reduced air circulation letting certain volatile compounds accumulate at low levels. It goes away in about ninety seconds once the door is open and you stop noticing whether it went away or you just adjusted.

---

Adjusting is most of what sensing is. You feel the watch on your wrist for the first thirty seconds after you put it on, and then your skin decides it's part of you.

The watch is still pressing with the same force at minute thirty-one. The nerve is still firing, probably. Something upstream just stops passing the message along because the message hasn't changed, and unchanged messages get demoted.

Which means sensation is mostly about edges. The moment something starts, the moment it stops, the moment it changes. The long unchanging middle gets dropped.

This might be my one explanation so I'll just leave it there.

A yellow pencil sitting on a desk looks like it belongs there. A red pencil on the same desk looks like someone left it behind.
