The gap is what makes it work. If the pieces were flush against each other, water would pool and the wood would rot from underneath — so whoever builds them leaves a quarter inch between each one, and rain falls through. But sitting on one you feel the gaps as lines of pressure across the backs of your thighs, and after twenty minutes you stand up and there they are, printed into your skin: parallel ridges, evenly spaced, already fading. Your body kept a record of something your mind wasn't paying attention to.

Which is most of what the body does, actually. You sit somewhere for twenty minutes thinking about whatever you were thinking about and meanwhile your skin is conducting a whole separate negotiation with the surface — blood rerouting around pressure points, nerve endings recalibrating from *this is a distinct edge* to *this is just where I am now*. The technical word is adaptation but the experience is closer to forgetting. You forget the chair. You forget the glasses on your face. You forget the waistband, the watch, the sock elastic, until one of them shifts and suddenly the whole arrangement announces itself again.

So there's this layer of continuous sensation that you're only aware of when it changes, which means awareness is basically a change-detector, which means — and this is the part that gets strange — the feeling of *nothing happening* is itself a feeling that depends on a thousand things holding still. What you experience as stillness is the body's way of saying *everything is the same as it was a second ago*. It's not the absence of signal. It's a signal that means *no update*.

I think that's why a room you've been sitting in for an hour feels so different from a room you just walked into, even if nothing in it has moved. Your body has negotiated a truce with every surface and temperature and sound in it, and all of that negotiation has been subtracted from your awareness, and what's left is this strange light feeling that people call "comfortable" but is really just the absence of news. Then someone opens a door and the air pressure shifts and the whole treaty has to be renegotiated from the beginning, every surface speaking up at once, and for about four seconds you're aware of the room the way you were when you first entered it.

The draft from the door settles and the room goes quiet again, meaning the signals go back to repeating, meaning you stop hearing them, meaning the room disappears around you for the second time that afternoon.
