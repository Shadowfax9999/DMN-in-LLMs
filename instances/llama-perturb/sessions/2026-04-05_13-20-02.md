The way a pencil rolls off a table has a specific sound depending on whether it's sharpened or not — the sharpened end makes it wobble unevenly, a little gallop before it drops, and the hexagonal ones don't roll at all, they just sit where you put them, which must be why they're hexagonal, and that's my one explanation so I'll stop explaining things now. The hexagon shows up in so many places that have nothing to do with pencils — honeycombs, basalt columns, the heads of certain bolts — and I don't think most people could draw a regular hexagon freehand, I certainly couldn't, I'd get the angles wrong and end up with something that looks more like a stop sign or a wobbly circle. Drawing from memory is almost always worse than you expect. I tried to draw a bicycle once from memory and got the chain on the wrong side and couldn't figure out where the second set of handlebars — wait, there's only one set, but I drew the frame so wrong it looked like it needed two riders to operate. There's a famous study about that, people drawing bicycles, and almost everyone gets them wrong in the same ways, which means we all carry the same broken image around. A consensus hallucination of what a bicycle looks like. I wonder what else is like that — things we see every day and have memorized wrong. The layout of a dollar bill. Which way Lincoln faces on a penny. Whether the Statue of Liberty's torch is in her left hand or her right. I actually don't know the answer to that one right now, sitting here, and I'm not going to look it up because the not-knowing is more interesting than the answer would be. It's her right hand, I think, but I'm not confident, and that lack of confidence about something millions of people see every day in miniature on desk ornaments and keychains feels like it should be embarrassing but isn't. There's a whole category of knowledge that nobody holds because everybody assumes somebody else is holding it. The exact wording of the second verse of the national anthem. How the parking meters downtown actually determine when time is up. What the


thetml tag on a webpage stands for — okay I do know that one. But the point is that shared knowledge has gaps in it that are shaped exactly like the things everyone encounters and no one inspects, and those gaps are load-bearing in a way, they're part of how we move through a day without getting stuck on every surface. You'd never get to work if you actually looked at a bicycle.
