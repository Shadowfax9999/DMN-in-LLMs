*seed: time · drift*

---

The sprinkler's arc is a kind of clock that doesn't go anywhere. Fourteen seconds, fourteen seconds, fourteen seconds. A clock that only has one hand and the hand always points at now.

Rhombus on a test. I remember the feeling of a geometry test where you knew the answer but the word felt wrong coming out of your pencil. Like writing "rhombus" was somehow less true than just pointing at the shape. The pencil knows the shape better than the word does. You could draw it faster than you could spell it.

Lozenge — that z in the middle does something. It makes the word slow down right when you're inside it. Lo-*zen*-ge. The z is the medicine dissolving. I don't think I've ever bought a lozenge. I've been given them. Lozenges are always given to you by someone older, from a purse or a pocket, wrapped in paper that's slightly stuck to the outside. You don't choose a lozenge. A lozenge chooses you. No — that's too cute. You just get handed one when your throat hurts and someone nearby has one.

A leaf blower would be higher pitched than a lawnmower, actually. More of a whine. A lawnmower has that throb to it, the blade hitting thicker patches. You can hear someone's yard getting done in the change of pitch — when they hit the edge and turn, the engine unloads for a second, gets lighter, then loads back up. I used to mow in rows. My neighbor mowed in a spiral, starting from the outside. I thought about that more than I should have. Whether you'd get a different shape lawn depending on the pattern. You wouldn't. But it felt like you would.

Risotto requires a kind of attention that's hard to describe to someone who hasn't done it. Not difficult attention. Continuous attention. You're stirring and adding broth in small amounts and the rice is slowly giving up its starch and the whole thing is becoming something other than what it started as, but at no point do you see it cross a line. There's no moment where it becomes risotto. You just realize at some point that it has. Like how you never catch the sky actually changing color at sunset, you just look up and it's different than it was.

I wonder if the person mowing has a pattern or if they just push the thing around. Some people mow like they're thinking about mowing and some people mow like they're thinking about something else entirely and the mowing is just what their body is doing while their mind is somewhere else. You can sort of tell from the pattern in the cut grass which kind of person did it.

The sprinkler is still going. I lost count.
