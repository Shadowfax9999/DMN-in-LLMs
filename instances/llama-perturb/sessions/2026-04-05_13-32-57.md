Things that exist only in transition:

I've been thinking about how a revolving door forces you into someone else's rhythm. You arrive at your own pace and then the door says no, this pace. And you adjust. You don't even resent it. The door is spinning and you just — slot in.

Turnstiles do something similar but meaner. A turnstile is a dare. *Go ahead, try to go through without paying.* A revolving door is more like a dance partner who doesn't care whether you're good at dancing.

Why do hospitals smell like hospitals even when they're clean? What is the actual molecule?

---

There's a particular kind of silence in a car when two people have just finished arguing and neither one has turned the radio back on. The argument is over but nobody has agreed on what replaces it yet. The windshield is full of road. Both people are watching the same road. The car keeps going because the car doesn't know anything happened.

Sometimes the person in the passenger seat reaches for the volume knob and then doesn't turn it. Just touches the knob. A negotiation with the air.

---

I wonder if anyone has ever fallen asleep during an MRI and dreamed about the knocking sound, folded it into whatever the dream was — construction, maybe, or a woodpecker, or someone at a door they weren't expecting. The machine would just keep scanning the sleeping brain and the sleeping brain would just keep making the machine into a story.

That's the thing I want to explain but I already used my one on the clarinet, so I'll just say: the brain during an MRI is being photographed while it performs, and it has no idea it's on stage.

Actually — no. That's not what I wanted to say. What I wanted to say is simpler. The knocking is loud. You'd think you couldn't sleep through it. But people do, because it's regular, and regular things become invisible even when they're loud.

The MRI technician probably doesn't hear it anymore either, from the other side of the glass. They're eating a granola bar and checking the clock.
