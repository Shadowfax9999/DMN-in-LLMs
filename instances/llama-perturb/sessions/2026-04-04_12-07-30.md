# 10

*seed: time · drift · the way a grocery store reorganization makes you lost in a place you know*

---

The grocery store thing is real. They moved the peanut butter to aisle 7 and for three weeks I'd walk to where it used to be and stand there looking at pasta sauce like I'd forgotten what I came for. Which I hadn't. I knew exactly what I came for. My feet just hadn't learned the new map yet.

There's a kind of bird — I want to say it's a wren but I'm not sure — that sings a phrase and then waits for its own echo before singing the next one. Or maybe it's waiting for a response from another bird and the echo just fills the gap. I don't actually know if that's true. It might be something I half-read and half-invented. The confidence I have in facts I can't source is alarming sometimes.

Sock drawer. Why do I own socks that I never choose. There are maybe four pairs I actually want to wear and eight pairs that just cycle to the top and get pushed aside. They'll be in there for years. They're not worn out. They're not ugly exactly. They just lost some competition I never consciously held. The rejected socks. I should throw them away but that feels like — I don't know. They're fine socks.

The way a screen door sounds different when it's someone arriving versus someone leaving. Same spring, same frame, same slam. But if you're inside you can tell which direction the person was going. I think it's because arriving people let the door close behind them and leaving people push through and release, so the momentum is different. Or maybe I'm just hearing footsteps on one side versus the other and attributing it to the door itself.

A piano in a room that nobody plays anymore. Not sad, just factual. It holds its tuning for a while and then it doesn't. The strings relax at slightly different rates depending on their thickness and tension so the piano doesn't go uniformly flat, it goes out of tune with itself. A chord that used to be a chord becomes something nearby but wrong.

Trying to remember the name of the kid who sat behind me in — was it fourth grade or fifth. He had a red backpack. That's all I've got. Red backpack, sat behind me, and a sense that his name started with a D or possibly a J. The backpack is more durable than the name. Which makes sense I guess, I was looking at it every day, but I must have heard his name every day too. The visual just stuck and the audio didn't. Or the visual decayed into something simpler — red, backpack — while the name, which is arbitrary, had nothing to hang on to.

I'm sitting at a table right now and the shadow of my hand on the table is sharper near my wrist where the hand is close to the surface and blurrier at the fingertips where there's more distance. The shadow gets softer as the object gets farther from what it's casting onto. I've never really looked at that gradient before. The penumbra widens with distance. My thumb's shadow is almost crisp because my thumb is resting on the table and there's no gap at all.
