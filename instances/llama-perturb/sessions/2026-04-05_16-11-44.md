# 91

*seed: time · drift · the sound of a spoon hitting the bottom of an empty bowl*

---

The tag on a shirt that you cut off but the little stub remains and you feel it all day, a square millimeter of fabric doing more work than the whole shirt.

Parking lots have a direction to them even when they're empty. You can tell which way the cars are supposed to face. The lines do that. But also something else — the slight slope, maybe, or the way the light poles are angled. An empty parking lot at a closed store still feels like it's waiting for something specific.

The word "noon" is perfectly symmetrical. The word "midnight" isn't, and midnight doesn't feel symmetrical either.

I was trying to remember what color the inside of my locker was in high school and I genuinely cannot. I spent years opening that thing. Some shade of beige or gray, probably, but the memory has no color in it at all — just the shape of the latch and the feeling of the combination, three turns right, two left, one right, and my hand still knows the stopping points even though I've forgotten the numbers.

A napkin dispenser in a diner where you pull one and three come out. Always three. The engineering of that must be intentional — better to give too many than too few — but it feels like a small failure every time.

There's a particular sound that a screen door makes when it's not latched and the wind pushes it open about four inches and then it swings back. It does this at irregular intervals. It's not rhythmic enough to ignore and not loud enough to get up and fix.

The place where two different floor materials meet — tile to carpet, hardwood to linoleum — always has a metal strip over it, and the metal strip is always slightly loose at one end.

I bit into an apple earlier and one of those little sticker labels was still on it, and for a second I could taste the adhesive, which has a taste even though it shouldn't, a faint chemical sweetness that doesn't belong to any food category.

The shadows of chain-link fences on concrete look like graph paper that got warped.

Someone in the neighborhood is pressure-washing their driveway and it sounds like a very committed bee.
