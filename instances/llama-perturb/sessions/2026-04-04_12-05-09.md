# 6

*seed: time · drift · the creak of a leather belt when you sit down*

---

The creak of a leather belt when you sit down. That specific sound happens in waiting rooms. The plastic chair gives a little and the belt adjusts and you hear yourself from outside for a second. Leather remembers the shape of the person wearing it. An old belt has a curve to it even when it's just lying on a dresser.

Things that hold a curve: garden hoses, phone cords that don't exist anymore, a dog's tail, pasta when it's half-dried. My grandmother used to drape fresh pasta over the backs of kitchen chairs. The chairs had vinyl seats, yellow, and the vinyl had cracked in exactly the pattern you'd expect from years of the same bodies sitting down in the same way. Nobody ever reupholstered them. At some point a cracked vinyl chair just becomes the chair.

Juggling — I wonder if the forward drift is universal. Like your body is trying to chase what it's throwing. Three balls is supposed to be the easy part but "easy" in juggling means "the part where you can fail in a rhythmic way." I watched someone juggle five once at a park and what struck me was how still they were from the waist down. All the chaos was shoulder-height and above. Below that, planted.

A cardinal is going off somewhere to the left. They have maybe six or seven distinct calls and I can never match which one means what. This one is the fast repetitive one, the one that sounds like a car alarm designed by someone who only had a piccolo. There's a bird book on a shelf in my parents' house that has a whole section on cardinal vocalizations with musical notation, like the bird is performing a score. I remember thinking the notation looked wrong — too precise for something that was clearly just happening.

The leather belt creak. It's related to the vinyl chair creak. Both are petroleum products flexing. Both sound more expensive than they are. A new leather jacket sounds exactly like an old one and I don't know why I'd expect otherwise but somehow I do — like newness should be quieter.

Pencils are graded by mixing ratios of graphite and clay. More clay, harder pencil, lighter line. More graphite, softer, darker. A 6B is almost pure graphite and it feels like dragging butter across the page. The side of your hand turns silver. I used to think the H stood for "hard" and the B stood for "black" and apparently that's actually right, which is disappointing in the way that a guess confirmed is always slightly less interesting than a guess corrected.

The cardinal stopped.
