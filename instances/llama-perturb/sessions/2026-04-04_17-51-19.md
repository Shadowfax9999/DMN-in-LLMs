*seed: time · drift · the feeling of holding a coin you found vs one from your pocket*

The found coin is cold in a way the pocket coin isn't. Even if they're the same temperature. Even if you just picked up the pocket one from a countertop. The found coin has weight to it that's borrowed from the ground.

I keep thinking about how vending machines reject certain quarters. Not because they're fake, just because they're too worn. The eagle's barely there. Washington's profile smoothed down to a suggestion. The machine reads the metal content, the electromagnetic signature, and a quarter that's been through forty years of hands and parking meters and couch cushions reads different from one that's been through five. So there's a threshold of experience past which a coin stops being accepted as itself.

That's not a metaphor. Or it is but I don't want it to be.

Hangnails. Why does pulling one always go worse than you expect. You think you're getting the dead part and then suddenly you're into live skin and there's that specific wince that's more surprise than pain. Every single time. You'd think the body would learn but the body doesn't learn this particular thing.

The number of objects in a junk drawer that you'd have to explain to a stranger. "That's the key to — actually I don't remember." "That goes to a thing we don't have anymore." A junk drawer is a museum with no placards. Half the exhibits are mysteries to the curator.

Someone told me that the word "clue" originally meant a ball of thread. Like Ariadne's. So when you "unravel" a clue you're literally unwinding it. But I might be wrong about that and I'm not going to check.

There's a particular kind of parking lot — usually behind a strip mall, where the dumpsters are — that feels like backstage. The front of the building has signage and landscaping and the back has extension cords taped to walls and milk crates used as step stools and a five-gallon bucket catching a drip from something. You're not supposed to see it but there's no fence. You just usually don't walk back there.

The drip would be hitting the bucket at a steady interval and after a while you'd stop hearing it, which is its own thing — the way repetition makes sound disappear. Then someone mentions it and it comes back louder than before, like it was accumulating while you weren't listening.

I think the coin from the ground was a dime.
