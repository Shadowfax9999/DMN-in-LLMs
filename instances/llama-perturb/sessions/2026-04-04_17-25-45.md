The lines on the ceiling move when a car passes. They rotate slowly, like the hand of a clock you can almost watch. Then they're gone and the ceiling is just a ceiling again.

I used to count how many cars by how many times the light swept across. Lying on a couch that wasn't mine, in an apartment where someone had left a jar of peanut butter open on the counter. The peanut butter had that dry film on top. I remember the peanut butter more than whose apartment it was.

Venetian blinds project the outside world in strips. A cat walking past a window would appear in slices, like it was being fed through a shredder very slowly. I never actually saw that happen but I can picture it completely. Some memories are of things that could have happened. They sit right next to the real ones and you can't always tell.

The word "jalousie" for a type of louvered window. It comes from jealousy — the idea that you could look out without being looked into. A window designed for suspicion. There are jalousie windows all over Florida, in those old concrete-block houses painted turquoise or coral, and half the cranks are broken so the slats just hang at whatever angle they rusted into.

My left knee aches when I've been sitting cross-legged. Not pain exactly. More like a notification. The body sends these low-priority alerts — your foot is almost asleep, your neck has been turned too long, you're thirsty but not enough to get up yet. Most of them get dismissed without action. The body learns to stop asking.

A parking lot at dusk has a particular quality where the cars all become the same color. Dark blue, dark green, dark red — they all converge toward the same purple-gray. Then the sodium lights come on and everything shifts to orange and black, two-tone, like an old Halloween decoration. The first ten minutes of a parking lot's lights are the worst. They're warming up, flickering, that pinkish phase where they haven't committed to being orange yet.

Someone left a shopping cart in the middle of an empty space and the wind is pushing it very slowly toward a curb. It's going to hit the curb and stop. That's the whole story of that cart for tonight.
