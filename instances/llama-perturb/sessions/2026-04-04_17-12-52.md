The word "passenger" comes from "passage" which comes from passing through, so a passenger is someone in the act of passing, which means technically you stop being a passenger the moment you arrive. You're only a passenger while you're between.

A can of tomatoes rolling under the brake pedal. That's a real fear someone has had. That's a thing that has actually caused an accident somewhere, a fourteen-ounce can of San Marzanos wedging itself exactly where your foot needs to go. You'd have to explain that to the insurance adjuster. You'd have to say "tomatoes" into a phone while standing on the shoulder of a road.

I keep thinking about how different it feels to be in a car that's parked versus a car that's about to move. Same seat, same temperature, same view through the windshield, but your body knows. Something in the way you hold your hands. In a parked car your hands can be anywhere — on your phone, in your lap, reaching into the back seat for something. The moment the engine turns over, your hands have a job again.

There's a particular kind of stillness in hotel parking lots. Not the stillness of emptiness — there are plenty of cars — but the stillness of nobody being from here. Every car in a hotel parking lot is from somewhere else. License plates from three states over. A bumper sticker for a beach you've never heard of. Roof rack with nothing on it because whatever was on it is now inside the room. The cars look tired in a way that's probably just dirt from the highway but reads as fatigue.

I once sat in a hotel parking lot for maybe twenty minutes before going inside because the room felt like a commitment and the car still felt like the trip. The car still had the trip's snack wrappers and the trip's playlist queued up and the trip's version of me in it, the one who was going somewhere. The room was going to be the version of me who had arrived.

The ice machine down the hall. Always down the hall. Never next to your room, never on another floor, always exactly far enough that you have to make a decision about shoes. Do you put shoes on to walk forty feet on industrial carpet to fill a plastic bucket with ice you might not even use. Most of the time I don't get the ice. The desire for ice and the desire to not put shoes back on cancel each other out and I just drink the water warm.

Carpet in hallways has that pattern — the kind that's busy on purpose, dark colors, swirling shapes, so it hides stains. Somebody chose that pattern from a catalog. Somebody's whole job is hotel carpet patterns and the criteria are: hides everything, matches nothing, offends nobody. The platonic ideal of hotel carpet is a pattern you cannot remember five minutes after walking on it. You could stay in the same hotel twelve times and never be able to describe the carpet.

I can describe the carpet in the house I grew up in though. The living room had this flat beige, almost the color of a grocery bag, and where the hallway started there was a metal strip holding down the transition to a slightly different beige, and I used to run my toe along that metal strip while watching TV. It had a ridge that felt good on the arch of your foot if you pressed just hard enough. The strip came loose on one end eventually and my dad pushed it back down with his thumb and it stayed for about a week and then it popped up again and after a few rounds of this he just left it and we all stepped over the raised edge without thinking about it, the way you stop seeing anything that's been wrong long enough.

The refrigerator in that house hummed in a key. I want to say B-flat but I have no way of knowing that. It just feels like it should be a flat, something slightly deflated about it, not a sharp, not a natural. Sharps feel pointed and that hum was round.

My mom used to say "listen" right before she said something that had nothing to do with listening. "Listen, can you move your shoes." "Listen, the garbage needs to go out." It was more like a comma than an instruction. A spoken punctuation mark that meant: I am shifting from whatever we were both doing into a thing I need you to do. The word had lost its meaning entirely and become pure function, like how "well" at the start of a sentence doesn't mean anything about wells.

There's a mockingbird outside right now doing something with about nine notes, and I keep almost hearing a pattern before it changes. Mockingbirds don't repeat their own phrases — they cycle through other birds' phrases, which means what sounds like improvisation is actually quotation. A mockingbird singing is a bird made entirely of references. But it doesn't sound like references. It sounds like a bird that can't decide what song it wants to sing, and that indecision is the song.

The light is doing that thing where it's not golden yet but it's not white anymore either, just this warm nothing color that makes the trees look more solid than they do at noon.
