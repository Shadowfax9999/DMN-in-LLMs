A cold room smells like metal and dust and something mineral, almost like water but not water, and a warm room smells like whatever's in it — the carpet, the wood, the people — because heat lifts things off surfaces, volatilizes them, which is why realtors bake cookies before an open house but nobody ever talks about the opposite trick, how you'd make a room smell like nothing, like you could cool a house down to just above freezing and it would smell like a clean shell, like potential, and I wonder if that's part of why walking into a cathedral feels the way it does, all that stone holding cold even in August, your skin registering the temperature drop before your eyes adjust to the dim and your nose picks up that particular stone-and-candle-wax scent that only exists at about fifty-five degrees Fahrenheit — but I was thinking about cold rooms specifically, the kind where you open a door in a house and one room is just colder than the rest, maybe it faces north, maybe there's a draft, and as a kid those rooms felt like they belonged to a different house, like temperature was a kind of jurisdiction, this room answers to a different authority than the hallway, and you'd close the door again and stand in the warm hallway and the cold would stay on your arms for a few seconds like you'd dipped them in something — I keep circling back to the fact that smell is just molecules landing on wet tissue inside your nose, and temperature changes which molecules are airborne, so a cold room literally has less information in it, fewer signals reaching you, and maybe that's why cold rooms feel more serious, more formal, like a room that isn't telling you much about itself, a room with composure, though that's — no. It's just fewer molecules. The thermostat in my parents' house had a mercury switch inside it, a tiny glass vial tilted by a bimetallic coil, and when the temperature crossed the set point the mercury would slide to one end and complete a circuit, so the thing that decided whether the furnace fired was a bead of liquid metal rolling a few millimeters inside a glass tube smaller than your little finger, and the whole house — the comfort of everyone in it — depended on that tilt. They don't use mercury anymore. The replacement is a thermistor, a ceramic bead whose electrical resistance changes with temperature, no moving parts, nothing to spill, and it works better but there's something about the mercury version, the idea that warmth was decided by gravity acting on poison. The ceramic bead just sits there and its resistance shifts and a microcontroller reads the value sixty times a second and nobody thinks about it. I don't know what the ceramic is made of.
