# 29

*seed: time · drift · wabi-sabi*

---

Wabi-sabi. I don't want to define it. I've heard it defined too many times and each definition sounds like it's trying to sell me a ceramic bowl.

The thing about a word arriving from nowhere is that you can't return it. It just sits there. Wabi-sabi. It sounds like a condiment. It sounds like something you'd order at a restaurant and then not be sure you pronounced correctly so you point at the menu. The waiter nods. You still don't know if you said it right.

There's a particular kind of embarrassment that only happens when you're alone — when you remember something you said six years ago and your whole body flinches. Nobody's watching. The flinch is for no one. Your shoulders actually move. I wonder if dogs do this. I wonder if a dog ever lies on a rug and suddenly remembers the time it barked at nothing and feels a small private shame. Probably not. But the image is good. A golden retriever staring at the wall, cringing.

Solder. From the seed. I keep thinking about solder. The idea that you join two things by melting a third thing between them. Marriage is not like this. Except when it is — when there's a kid, and the kid is the thing that melted between you, and now you're joined whether you want to be or not. That's not wabi-sabi either. That's just Tuesday.

A slide rule is beautiful the way a sundial is beautiful: it works, but you'd never use one. It belongs to the category of objects that exist now only to be appreciated. Letter openers. Pocket watches. Rotary phones that people keep on shelves. The function drained out and left the form standing there like a cicada shell.

I saw a cicada shell on a fence post once. Translucent. Still gripping. The thing that was inside it had already flown away and the shell didn't know. Or — it couldn't know, it's chitin, but the grip was still there. The muscles or whatever they are had locked into the shape of holding on. That's the part that gets me. Not that it was empty but that it was still holding.

A friend's apartment after they move out. The nail holes in the wall. You can reconstruct where the pictures were. The kitchen smells like their cooking for about a day and then it doesn't. The light switch they replaced is still the wrong color.

I should think about something else.

The word "baffle" means both to confuse and to block sound. A baffle in a speaker cabinet is literally a board with a hole in it. The speaker sits in the hole. The board prevents the sound from the front and the sound from the back from canceling each other out. So a baffle's job is separation — keeping two versions of the same thing from meeting and destroying each other. I said I wouldn't explain things but that one just came out. The tongue does what it wants.

Tongue. The tongue is the only muscle you can watch someone else use all day long and not think about it being a muscle. Until someone points it out. Now you're thinking about it. The tongue in someone's mouth while they talk. The way it appears between the teeth on certain consonants. Th. L. You can see a little flash of it like a fish surfacing.

I'm sitting with the image of that cicada shell still gripping the fence post and I think that's the thing I actually came here to think about, and I didn't know it when I started. The grip that outlasts the thing that was gripping. A hand-worn wooden handle. The grooves in a stone step near a cathedral door. The path across a quad where the grass won't grow because ten thousand people walked the same diagonal. These are all the same thing but I'm not going to say what the thing is.

The fence post was cedar, I think, because it had that grey weathered look and it wasn't rotting.
