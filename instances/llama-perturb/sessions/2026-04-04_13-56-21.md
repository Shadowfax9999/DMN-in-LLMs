# 21

*seed: time · drift · residue*

---

Residue. The word just sitting there like a coin on a counter. Not asking to mean anything yet.

The ant knows the rim. Or it doesn't know the rim — it knows the *next inch* of the rim, and the inch after that becomes knowable when it gets there, and from outside that looks like confidence but from inside it's probably just: surface continues, surface continues, surface continues. Which is how I walk down a hallway too, if I'm honest. I don't hold the whole hallway in my head. I hold the next few feet and trust the rest.

Residue is a cooking word before it's a chemistry word. No — that's not true. I don't actually know which came first. But I think of it in pans. The fond. The brown bits stuck to stainless steel after you sear something, which you dissolve with wine or stock and suddenly you have a sauce. The good part is the part that got stuck. The part that almost burned. There's a zone between caramelized and carbonized that's maybe ten degrees wide and the whole enterprise of French cooking lives in that ten degrees.

My left hand is doing something — tapping the pad of my thumb against the pad of my ring finger, over and over, not rhythmically. I don't know when it started. It might have been going on for a while. The body does things below the threshold of your attention and sometimes you catch it mid-gesture and think: how long have you been doing that? Who told you to do that?

A dentist once told me that most people's resting tongue position is wrong. That the tongue should be pressed lightly against the roof of the mouth, just behind the front teeth. I checked, and mine was just floating there in the middle of my mouth like it was waiting for instructions. I tried to correct it for about three days and then forgot. My tongue is floating right now. I just checked again.

The word "residue" hasn't done anything yet. Sometimes a word just wants to be in the room. Like a cat that walks in, sits in the middle of the floor, and doesn't want anything from you. You acknowledge it. It stays or it leaves. Residue. Still here.

Something about the ant and the rim — the ant is tracing a boundary it didn't make. The bevel exists because someone routed the edge of the desk with a machine, probably a CNC router or maybe just a handheld trim router with a roundover bit. The ant doesn't care about any of that. The ant is using the geometry without knowing it was designed. Which is also what I do with sidewalks, with door frames, with the camber of a road. I follow shapes that someone decided on in a meeting I wasn't invited to, decades before I showed up.

The ring-finger tapping stopped. I don't know when.
