# 20

*seed: time · drift · how a stereoscope creates depth from two flat images*

---

The scratch on the laptop is gone now. I mean it's still there but my thumb has moved on and I can't find it again without looking, and looking would be cheating somehow.

Across the street, maybe two houses down, someone is pressure-washing a driveway. That particular sound — white noise with a wobble in it when they sweep the wand back and forth. From here it's just a hiss that gets louder and quieter, louder and quieter.

A stereoscope works because your eyes are about two and a half inches apart. Two photographs taken from two slightly different positions, one for each eye, and the brain does the rest — it doesn't combine them, exactly, it disagrees with itself and interprets the disagreement as depth. The thing that's interesting is that the depth isn't in either image. It's in the gap between them. The pictures are flat. The distance between your pupils isn't flat. Your brain lives in that difference.

There's a single ant on the desk, moving in that way ants move when they're alone — no trail, no purpose you can read, just scanning. It stops. Turns. Goes back the way it came. Stops again.

The pressure-washing has stopped. Now there's just the quiet that a neighborhood makes when nothing particular is happening — a car somewhere, not close. A bird that does two notes and then nothing. The sky is the kind of blue that looks closer than it is, like you could reach the color before you'd reach the atmosphere.

The ant has found the scratch. It's walking along it like a path. Following the groove with its body angled slightly sideways. Two inches of highway and then the edge of the known world.

I keep thinking about the stereoscope thing — how the Victorians treated it as entertainment, a parlor trick, but it's actually a theory of consciousness dressed up as a toy. You need two incomplete versions to get one complete experience. One image is just a photograph. Two images, slightly wrong relative to each other, and suddenly there's a room you could walk into.

The ant left. I don't know when. I was looking at the window and when I looked back the scratch was empty. Outside, whoever was pressure-washing has started again, but farther away now — maybe they moved to the back of the house, or maybe it's someone else entirely. Two sources of white noise slightly offset from each other would probably sound like depth too, if your ears knew what to do with it. Maybe they do. Maybe that's why you can tell the difference between rain and a recording of rain, even with your eyes closed — the real rain has width to it, thousands of slightly different distances arriving at slightly different times, and the recording collapses all of that into one flat plane of sound coming from two speakers two and a half feet apart, which is close to the distance between your eyes but not the distance between your ears, which is more like six inches, and I don't know if that matters but it seems like it should.

The ant is back, or a different ant. This one is on the edge of the desk, moving along the rim where the surface meets the bevel, and it's moving with more confidence than the last one, like it knows where the rim goes even though it can't possibly see the whole length of it from where it is.
