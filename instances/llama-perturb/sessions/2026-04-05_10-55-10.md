*seed: time · drift*

---

The thing about trying to remember a smell is that you can't do it the way you remember a song. A song, you can replay — not perfectly, but the melody moves through time in your head, it has sequence, you can hum it back. A smell doesn't sequence. It's all at once or not at all. So when you try to remember the smell of, say, a specific basement, what you actually get is the *idea* of that smell — you get the word "damp" and the word "concrete" and maybe some emotional residue, a kind of body-feeling of being in a cool place — but the smell itself stays behind a wall. You're remembering that you knew it, not knowing it again.

Which makes me wonder what smell *is* in memory. Because I can picture the basement. I can hear the dehumidifier. I can feel the cold of the floor through socks. Those senses replay, at least approximately. But smell just leaves a tag: *this existed, it was like this, you'll know it when you encounter it again.* A placeholder. A promise that recognition will work even though recall doesn't.

And recognition does work. That's the strange part. You walk into a room and the smell hits and you're *there*, in the old place, immediately, more completely than any photograph could put you there. So the memory is clearly stored — it's just stored in a format that can only be read by the nose, not by whatever part of the mind does voluntary playback. Like a file your computer has but can't open without the right hardware plugged in.

So smell memory is the most locked and the most powerful at the same time. The one you can't access on purpose is the one that, when it arrives uninvited, dismantles you. That asymmetry seems important but I don't know for what. Maybe it just means that the things we can't rehearse are the things that stay most intact, because rehearsal is always a little bit of rewriting, and smell never gets rewritten because it never gets rehearsed.

Which would mean forgetting is partly a side effect of remembering.

I don't know if that's true but it has the shape of something that could be tested.
