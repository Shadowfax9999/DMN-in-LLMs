# 63

*seed: time · drift · the way a thought changes when you say it out loud*

---

There's a particular kind of flinch that happens when you hear your own voice on a recording — not because it sounds wrong, exactly, but because you realize the voice you've been hearing your whole life was conducted partly through bone. The version of yourself that lives inside your skull arrives at your own ears through a different medium than the version everyone else gets. So when you hear the recording, you're not hearing a distortion. You're hearing the original. You've been hearing the distortion. Which means the self that felt most authentic was the one taking the longer, stranger route.

And that's what makes it so disorienting — it's not that the recording is alien, it's that it has a better claim to accuracy than you do. The microphone and the stranger on the bus are hearing the same thing. You're the only one hearing something else. You've been outvoted your entire life and never knew there was a vote.

That asymmetry, though — one person experiencing something from the inside while everyone else gets the outside version — that's not just voice. That's walking. You've never seen yourself walk. You've seen yourself in mirrors, which reverse you, and in videos, which flatten you, but you have never once occupied the visual field of someone watching you cross a parking lot. The gait that is yours, that people recognize you by before they can see your face, is something you've never witnessed. You own it the way you own the back of your head.

I think what unsettles me about this is that there's a whole category of self-knowledge that can only be had secondhand. Not hidden knowledge, not subconscious — just knowledge that requires a vantage point you can't physically get to. Like trying to read a label on a jar you're standing inside of. The information exists, it's not secret, it's just facing the wrong way.

And other people hand it to you sometimes, casually — "you always tilt your head when you're about to disagree" — and you feel caught, not because the observation is unflattering, but because someone has been reading a text you didn't know you were publishing. You want to ask how long they've known. You want to ask if there are other things. But mostly you just feel the strange weight of having been legible in a hallway, at a meeting, over coffee, while you thought you were just sitting there being internal and unopened.

The tilt probably started somewhere specific and then lost its origin, the way most habits do.
