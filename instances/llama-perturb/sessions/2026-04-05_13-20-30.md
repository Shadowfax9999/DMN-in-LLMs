The broom is always in another room — that's what makes it that sound specifically, the fact that you can't see it, just the rhythm, which is not regular the way a machine would be but not random either, it has the logic of someone thinking about something else while their arms do the work, and I wonder how many motions are like that, motions your body performs while your mind is three rooms away or thirty years back, and driving is the obvious one but also stirring a pot, also the way your hand finds the light switch in a hallway you've lived in for years without your eyes dropping to the wall at all, your hand just knows where the plate is, knows the height and the side and the slight angle, and if someone moved it two inches to the left you'd fumble in the dark like you'd never four times until the fit re-forms — and this is maybe why hotel rooms feel so effortful, not because anything is difficult but because none of the fits exist yet, you have to look at everything, the
