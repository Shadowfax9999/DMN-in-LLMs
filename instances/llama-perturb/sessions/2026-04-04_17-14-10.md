Parking meter. The kind with the actual dial, the red flag that says EXPIRED. I haven't seen one of those in years. Everything's an app now, a code on a post. But the old ones had that satisfying crank when you turned the handle, and the needle would swing over to however many minutes you'd bought. You were buying time. Literally buying time. Okay.

The coins that went into parking meters were always the coins you didn't want. Quarters that had been sitting in a cupholder getting sticky. There's a certain film that develops on coins left in cars. They get warm in summer and something happens to them, some reaction with the residue of whatever else has been in the cupholder — coffee, melted popsicle, the slow leak from a water bottle cap that wasn't on tight.

I can feel the inside of a car that's been sitting in the sun with the windows up. That specific wall of heat when you open the door. The steering wheel you can't touch. The seatbelt buckle that will brand you. Everything in the car has been slowly cooking and the air smells like dashboard, like the particular plastic and vinyl and whatever the freshener was three months ago, just a ghost of pine now, and dust.

You do that thing where you sit on the edge of the seat with the door still open, one foot on the pavement, and reach across to start the engine and get the AC going before you commit to actually being inside. Half in, half out. Waiting for the car to become habitable.

The pavement in a parking lot in late afternoon. How it holds the day's heat and you can feel it through your shoes. Certain parking lots have those little oil stains, dark patches in every space, the ghost of every car that's ever parked there, each one leaking from a slightly different spot so the stains don't quite overlap. A registry of drip pans and worn gaskets.

I wonder what the oldest parking lot in the world is. Whether it's still a parking lot. Whether anyone would know. It wouldn't have been called a parking lot at first — it would have just been a place where people left their cars, and then someone put gravel down, and then someone painted lines, and at some point it became a parking lot without anyone deciding it. Things become what they are by accumulation more often than by declaration. A path becomes a path. A lot becomes a lot. A nickname becomes a name.

There's a woman at a gas station across the street and she's holding a fountain drink the size of her head. She's just standing next to her car, not getting in, drinking through the straw, looking at nothing. Taking a minute. People do that — they finish an errand and they don't immediately start the next thing. They just stand there for a second. It's almost never longer than thirty seconds but it's real, that pause. She shifts her weight to the other hip and keeps drinking.

The sound a straw makes at the bottom of a cup, that gurgling, hunting-for-the-last-of-it sound. Kids will do it forever. Adults stop themselves because at some point someone told them it was rude and the rule stuck, it's in there, it fires every time. You hear the gurgle start and you pull back from the straw like you touched something hot. One of those rules that lives in the hands more than in the mind.

The ice in a fountain drink is different from the ice you make at home. It's that half-moon shape, or sometimes the little cylindrical pellets with the hole through the middle, the kind that's good to chew. I don't know anyone who doesn't have a preference about ice shape.

My left knee is doing the thing where it clicks when I straighten it. Not pain, just a click, like a ballpoint pen. It's been doing that since I was maybe twenty-five. You just acquire these sounds over time. A new click, a new crack. Your body developing its own set of creaks like a house settling. The knee click doesn't mean anything, probably, but I notice it every time, this little mechanical report from a joint that used to be silent.

A ballpoint pen. The specific resistance of a cheap ballpoint on a receipt. How it skips if you go too fast or if there's a crease in the paper. The way you scribble in a little circle in the margin to get the ink flowing again, and that circle stays there on the receipt, a tiny record of the pen not working for a second.
