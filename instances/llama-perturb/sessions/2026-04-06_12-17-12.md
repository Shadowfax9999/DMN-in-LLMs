The tag on a fire extinguisher in a hallway — someone initials it every month. You can see the whole year in a column, different handwriting, different pens, and then it flips to a new tag and starts over. Nobody reads it. The extinguisher just hangs there with its little paper history, which is also a record of someone walking this hallway twelve times a year with a pen, checking that the gauge is still in the green, that the pin is still in, and I wonder if they actually look or if the hand just signs while the eyes are already moving to the next one down the corridor, because there must be dozens in a building like that, maybe hundreds, and you'd go numb to it by the third floor. But then every tag is also a small promise — I was here, it was fine, I checked — and the whole system depends on that being true even though nobody will ever verify it unless the day comes when someone actually needs the extinguisher, and by then the tag is the last thing anyone's thinking about.

I had a thought about the word "lanyard" and lost it.

The inside of an airplane when everyone is asleep on a red-eye, the reading lights making little cones over maybe four people who are still awake, and the rest of the cabin is this deep blue-gray, and the flight attendants are somewhere behind the curtain, and the engines are so constant they've become the floor of silence rather than a sound. You could be anywhere. You could be in a tube suspended over Greenland or you could be in a tube suspended over nothing, and the difference wouldn't change the quality of the air or the way your knees feel against the seat in front of you. The seatbelt sign is on but it's been on for two hours and nobody's moved.

There's a particular kind of plant that grows out of the gap where a brick wall meets a sidewalk and it's always the same plant but I don't know what it's called.

I think the color of a hallway in a public school is a specific green that doesn't exist anywhere else in nature or commerce, a green that someone in 1965 decided was calming, and now it's just the color of waiting outside the principal's office, the color of the drinking fountain that arcs too low so you have to put your mouth almost on the metal. That green has an entire emotional register that has nothing to do with green.

The little metal plate on the bottom of a door, the one that's there because people kick it open when their hands are full, is always scuffed in the same crescent shape from the toe of a shoe, and it occurs to me that it was installed specifically to be damaged, which is a strange kind of object — one whose purpose is to absorb wear so something else doesn't have to. A sacrificial part. There are probably a lot of those that I walk past without recognizing as such.

The cabin lights just came on and it's bright orange juice and a napkin and we're descending into somewhere I can see out the window if I lean, but all I can see is a grid of lights that could be any city at all.
