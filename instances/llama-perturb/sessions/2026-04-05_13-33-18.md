---

The plural of "cul-de-sac" — is it "culs-de-sac"? That seems right but looks absurd written down. The plural happens in the middle of the word.

French does this. English borrowed the phrase and now has to deal with a pluralization rule from a different language sitting inside an otherwise normal sentence about suburban development.

There are probably planning documents somewhere that had to commit to one spelling.

---

What I actually want to think about is the shape of a cul-de-sac from above. A lollipop. A spoon. A thermometer. Every one of them has that same bulb at the end, and every house along the bulb has a pie-slice yard that's wide at the back and narrow at the front. The garages all face each other in a loose circle.

Kids figure this out immediately — that it's a circle, that the circle is theirs, that cars will be slow here because the road is telling them to leave.

---

The road is literally a dead end but nobody calls it that on the real estate listing.

"Dead end" and "cul-de-sac" describe the same geometry. One sounds like a warning. The other sounds like you might want to live there. The French didn't do that on purpose — "bottom of the bag" isn't glamorous in French — but English heard the French and decided it meant property values.

I wonder how many street names in a given suburb were chosen by one person in an afternoon. Someone sitting with a list of trees they'd never planted, picking which ones went where. Elm. Birch. Hawthorn. The streets named after trees that were cut down to build the streets.

Anyway. The thing about a pie-slice yard is that your back fence is longer than your front lot line, so you share more boundary with your back neighbor than with anyone else, but you almost never see them. You share a fence and nothing else. Sometimes you hear their music or smell their grill and you have no idea what they look like. Thirty feet of shared property line with a stranger for eleven years.

The front of the house is the performance. The back is where the cooler with the broken hinge sits next to three bags of mulch from last spring.
