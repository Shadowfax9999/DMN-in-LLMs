# 22

*seed: time · drift · cartography*

---

Cartography. Not maps themselves but the word. The way it sounds like it should involve a cart. A cart full of graphs. A graph on a cart being wheeled through a library.

The actual maps I remember best are the ones in the front of books — fantasy novels, mostly, where there's always a mountain range along the top and a forest in the middle and the evil place is to the east for some reason. East is where bad things live in made-up worlds. I never thought about that until just now. East. Why east. Maybe because European authors read left to right and the story moves toward the right side of the page, which is east on a standard orientation. Or maybe it's older than that. I don't know.

A fly keeps landing on the same spot on the wall. There's nothing there. I've looked. It lands, rubs its front legs together like it's scheming, lifts off, circles, lands on the same spot. There must be something there that I can't perceive — some residue, some chemical signature. The fly has information I don't. That happens more than I think about. Whole layers of signal in every room that I'm just not equipped to receive.

My left knee has that thing where it clicks once when I first bend it after sitting for a while and then it's fine. It's been doing that for maybe two years. It doesn't hurt. It's just a fact about my knee now, like a feature. A knee with a click. A door with a squeak. You stop noticing and then a guest comes over and they say "your door squeaks" and you realize you haven't heard it in months.

I wonder what the fly is getting from that spot on the wall. I keep coming back to it. The fly left but I'm still thinking about the spot. A blank piece of wall near the ceiling, slightly to the left of where the wall meets the corner. Off-white. If I painted over it the fly would probably still land there. Or maybe not — maybe the paint would cover whatever's there. New paint smell is intense. That aggressive chemical brightness that makes a room feel like it's been reset. People say they love the smell of fresh paint, which is weird because it's essentially off-gassing solvents. But new car smell is also off-gassing and people buy air fresheners that simulate it.

Simulating the smell of a process. That's strange. Like if someone made a candle that smelled like sawdust. Actually I think they do make those.

My grandfather had a workshop that smelled like sawdust and WD-40 and something else I could never identify — something sweet and mechanical, maybe old belt dressing or the particular grease he used on the drill press. I've smelled WD-40 a hundred times since and it never gets me all the way there. It's like one note from a chord. You need all three to hear the thing.

The fly is back on the same spot.
