The thing about holding a pool cue is that your back hand does all the work and your front hand does nothing but make a bridge, and yet it's the front hand you think about. The bridge hand is the one you adjust, the one you stare at, the fingers spread on the felt in that strange tripod nobody teaches you — you just see someone else do it and your hand tries the shape. And the reason you think about the front hand is that it's the one closer to the mistake. The back hand is pulling a straight line through space, which is simple, which is almost involuntary once you've done it enough. But the bridge hand is where the cue touches your skin and that contact point is where all the wobble lives.

Which is maybe why, when something goes wrong with a tool, you always suspect the part closest to the work. The drill bit, not the chuck. The tip of the soldering iron, not the heating element. The nib, not the cartridge. Even when the problem is electrical, even when the problem is deep inside the housing, you start by looking at the end that touches the thing. There's something almost superstitious about it — like the point of contact is where error enters, the way people used to think disease came through wounds rather than lungs.

And then that logic reverses completely when it's your own body. If your handwriting looks bad you don't blame your fingers, you blame something vaguer — your coordination, your attention, your brain. The fingers are just following orders. But the pool cue's tip is not following orders, somehow. You treat it like it has its own agenda. You chalk it like you're disciplining it. You lean down and blow the excess chalk off the felt and there's a little blue constellation of dust and you feel like you've done something responsible, like a surgeon washing their hands, when really you've just made a small mess in a slightly different location.

I used to watch people in pool halls chalk their cues between every single shot regardless of whether the last shot had any spin on it at all, and it occurred to me that most of the ritual of pool is not about physics but about buying yourself three seconds to think without anyone noticing you're thinking. Chalking the cue is a way to stare at nothing while your hands do something plausible. The way someone twists a ring during a conversation — the motion isn't for the ring.

The blue gets on everything, though. The side of your hand, the rail, your shirt if you lean across the table for an awkward reach. Pool chalk is one of those substances that seems to have no respect for boundaries. Like turmeric, or glitter, or the red dust from a new brick. You find it later in contexts that make no sense — a blue smudge on your steering wheel, on your phone screen, on the inside of your pocket where your hand rested for a second without thinking. It's a record of everywhere your hand has been, which is a thing you normally never have to confront, how many surfaces your hand visits in an hour without reporting back to you about any of them.

The felt on a pool table is usually wool, or wool-nylon blend, stretched over slate that weighs about four hundred pounds per section.
