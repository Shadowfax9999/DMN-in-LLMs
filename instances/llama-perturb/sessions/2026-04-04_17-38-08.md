# 37

*seed: time · drift · portmanteau*

---

Portmanteau — the word itself is one. Port + manteau. Carry + coat. A bag that opens into two halves.

Suitcases used to do that. My grandmother had one, brown, with a brass clasp that needed two thumbs.

Two thumbs. That's the minimum for a lot of things. Shuffling cards. Texting. Opening a jar if you count the hand holding the jar as the other thumb's jurisdiction.

Things that split into two equal halves:
- Walnuts
- Avocados (almost)
- The word "bookkeeper" if you take the double letters as a hinge
- A day, supposedly, but no one experiences noon as the middle of anything

Things that don't split evenly no matter how you try:
- Oranges
- Conversations
- The cost of dinner between friends when one person ordered wine
- A year — the months aren't the same size, which I forget and re-learn every time I look at a calendar

Someone decided February gets 28. Someone else, or the same someone, decided July and August both get 31 because two emperors wanted equal billing. That's not a design decision, that's a custody arrangement.

Q: When you fold a fitted sheet, what shape are you even aiming for?
A: A rectangle.
Q: And what do you get?
A: A soft lump with intent.

The dryer knows when clothes are done before I do. It has a sensor. I have a sensor too but mine is activated by guilt about the basket sitting on the couch.

Portmanteau words I use without thinking about: smog, brunch, motel. Words where two things got married and neither kept its full name. Motel is motor + hotel, which means every motel sign is a tiny abbreviation of a promise: you can drive here and then sleep.

The ratio of things I own that I use daily to things I own total must be something like 1 to 40. Mug, phone, keys, toothbrush, one specific fork — versus everything in every drawer and closet and the garage where the car loses to the not-car. Somewhere in the basement there's a box labeled "cables" and I couldn't tell you what three of them connect to.

I keep the cables because throwing away a cable feels like severing a relationship with a future version of myself who might need it. That future person never shows up. The cables stay in the box. The box stays in the basement. The basement does what basements do, which is hold things you've decided not to decide about.

Brunch is the portmanteau I'm most suspicious of. It promises the best of both but what you get is eggs at a time when eggs feel like they're trying too hard, plus a cocktail that tastes like breakfast juice pretending. The mimosa is a portmanteau drink — champagne and orange juice, neither one committed.

The brass clasp on that suitcase had a specific sound when it opened. Two clicks, a pause, then the hinge. Four-part sequence. I can hear it but I can't remember if the suitcase was my grandmother's or if I saw it in a movie about someone's grandmother. The sound is real either way. The click doesn't care about its source.

Fog is not a portmanteau but it acts like one — it's where air and water couldn't decide which one to be.

I just remembered that the word "goodbye" is a contraction of "God be with ye," which means every time I hang up the phone I'm issuing a miniature blessing that neither party registers. Hundreds of tiny unnoticed blessings per week, dissolving on contact.

The fork I use daily is the second one from the left in the drawer.
