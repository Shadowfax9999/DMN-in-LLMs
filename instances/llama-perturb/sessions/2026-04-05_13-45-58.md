# 82

*seed: time · drift · depth of field*

---

Depth of field is a photography term but it's also what happens when you're sitting in a parked car and you stop looking at anything specific. The windshield becomes a surface. Then you look through it and the surface disappears.

Two things can't be in focus at the same time. You pick the tree or you pick the raindrop on the glass. The raindrop wins for a second, then the tree pulls you back.

There's a version of this with sound. In a waiting room, you can hear the TV or you can hear the HVAC or you can hear the person next to you turning magazine pages, but you can't hear all three with equal weight. One is always the figure and the rest are ground.

A fly lands on a table. For about two seconds it's the most important thing in the room.

I'm thinking about how maps show everything at the same scale of importance — every road equally a road, every label equally a label — and how that's the opposite of being alive. Being alive is a ruthless editor. You walk through a parking lot and register almost nothing. Then a shopping bag blows across the asphalt and briefly it's the only thing that exists.

Triage. That's what attention is. Not a spotlight, more like an ER nurse deciding what matters right now.

The phrase "pay attention" — as if it costs something. It does, probably. There's a budget. You spend it on the fly, then you don't have it for the magazine page turning.

A kid in a grocery store is paying attention to everything, which means paying attention to nothing, which is why they bump into things. Or maybe they're paying attention to exactly what matters to them, which is the cereal box with the tiger on it, and the rest of the store is just weather.

I used to think peripheral vision was blurry because the eyes weren't good enough out at the edges. But it's more that the brain doesn't bother rendering what you're not looking at. The resolution is there if you need it. You just usually don't.

Faces are an exception. You can detect a face in your peripheral vision faster than almost anything else. Even a face-shaped arrangement of dots on a page will pull your eyes toward it before you've decided to look.

Three dots in a triangle and you see a face. Two up, one down. Every electrical outlet is a little surprised person.

The pareidolia thing extends to sounds too — hearing your name called in white noise, hearing a phone ring in running water. The brain is always hunting for signal, and sometimes it manufactures some.

I wonder what it would be like to have attention that didn't prioritize. To actually perceive the parking lot and every car in it with equal weight. It sounds like it would be a kind of overwhelm that's hard to even imagine from inside a brain that's always already filtering.

The filter is invisible, which is the whole point of it working.
