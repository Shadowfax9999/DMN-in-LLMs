*seed: time · drift · how a pendulum's period depends only on length, not weight*

---

The broom bristles hit the concrete in a rhythm that doesn't vary even when the person pushing them shifts their weight or reaches further. A pendulum does something like that, keeping its own time regardless of what's hanging from it, which I think means a heavy church bell and a marble on the same string would swing at the same speed. That seems like it shouldn't be true but I'm fairly sure it is, at least in the version where you ignore air and friction and all the things that actually exist. I think the length of the string is what sets the tempo, so a long rope swing at a lake would have this slow, lazy period compared to a short chain on a porch swing, though I'm not sure I'm remembering that right. It might only work for small angles, or maybe that's a different rule, something about the math breaking down when the arc gets wide enough that the approximation stops holding. I feel like there was a story about Galileo watching a chandelier in a cathedral and timing it against his pulse, but I don't know if that actually happened or if it's one of those stories that sounds too clean.
