# 99

*seed: time · drift · contraband*

---

The underside of a bridge has that color that isn't quite any color — concrete that's been wet so many times it's given up on drying all the way. There's always graffiti under a bridge but it's never the good graffiti, it's the kind where someone just wrote a name in silver paint and the letters are too close together at the end because they ran out of room, which means they started without measuring, which means they came here to write a name and didn't think past the first letter. That pulls me toward something about how most projects fail at the right edge. Envelopes where the handwriting gets smaller. Bumper stickers applied crooked because you committed to the left side and the right side just had to deal with it.

I'm in a waiting room and the magazines are from last year and I realize I can't tell. Nothing in a magazine from eleven months ago looks dated yet. It takes about four years before a magazine cover starts to look like it's from another era — the fonts shift, or the color palette, or something about the way the model is standing. But at eleven months it's still in disguise as now.

Contraband — the word itself has that weight to it, the "band" at the end like something wrapped tight. Contra-band. Against the band. That's probably not what it means but the sound of it works that way regardless.

A stapler on a desk that hasn't been used in weeks still has staples in it. You open it and they're just sitting there in their little channel, oxidizing so slowly you'd never notice. The first one will work fine. The ninth or tenth one might jam. There's a gradient of reliability in there that you'd never test because who uses ten staples in a row anymore.

The air in a stairwell is always cooler than the air in the hallway it opens onto, even when both are inside the same building with the same thermostat. Stairwells hold onto coolness the way closets hold onto quiet.

I had a thought about fonts but it left.

The number on a hotel room door is always slightly too high or too low — you'd think there'd be a template, a jig, something to make them all line up at exactly the same height down the corridor, and maybe there is, but someone still had to hold the thing against the door and drill, and that person's arm was at a slightly different angle at room 314 than it was at room 312. You can see it if you crouch down and look along the hallway at door height. I've never actually done this but I'm certain it's true.

There's a particular kind of cloud that looks like it was torn from a larger cloud and is now just drifting on its own, too small to rain, too solid to dissolve, just hanging there being a cloud for no structural reason.

A receipt from a gas station has the most information per square inch of any document I regularly hold and I read none of it.
