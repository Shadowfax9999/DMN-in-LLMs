Falsetto

The voice splits and the top half floats off. Boys in church choirs before anything changes. There's a version of singing where you're not using the thing you normally use — you're using the edges of it, the thinner part, and what comes out is both yours and not yours.

Dark maroon next to dark plum next to something that might be black but isn't. You stand in that octagonal room long enough and the paintings stop being paintings. They become the walls having moods. Fourteen canvases and none of them are trying to show you anything. They're just sitting there being dark and close together and if you stay long enough your eyes start inventing colors that aren't there. Peripheral purples. A red that only exists when you're looking at the one next to it.

I don't know why octagonal.

Someone told me once that Rothko was furious about the lighting and then he died before the chapel opened and they had to guess what he wanted. So every decision about how bright or dim — all of that is someone else's interpretation of a dead man's complaints. The complaints survived but not the solution.

A falsetto is a complaint that survived without its solution. No — that's not right. A falsetto is what happens when the vocal cords don't fully close. Air leaks through. The incomplete contact is the whole sound.

Things that work because they're incomplete:
- Arches
- Abbreviations
- The negative space in the FedEx logo
- A held hand with loose fingers
- Rothko's chapel walls, which aren't murals, they're just color sitting near other color with gaps between the panels

I was in a room once that was too quiet. Not a Rothko room, just a conference room where someone had over-insulated everything and the HVAC was off. The silence had texture like felt. I kept swallowing just to hear something. My own breathing started to sound like a performance.

The highest note I can sing isn't really a note. It's more of a leak.

There's a shade of purple that doesn't photograph correctly — it always shifts toward blue on screens because screens don't have a true violet pixel, they fake it by combining red and blue, and the thing your eye sees in person is a single wavelength that the screen can only approximate by mixing two wavelengths that aren't it. So every photo of the Rothko Chapel is a photo of a different building than the one that's there.

Grape flavoring doesn't taste like grapes. It tastes like grape flavoring. At some point the artificial version became its own legitimate thing that people prefer, and actual Concord grapes taste weird now by comparison, like a cover band being upstaged by the remix.

The chapel has no cross, no star, no crescent. Just the paintings. People sit on benches and some of them pray and some of them just sit and both of those might be the same activity in a room like that. The benches are hard. I think that matters — a cushioned bench would change the whole theology of the place.

Hard bench, dark walls, Houston heat outside, your own breathing.

Falsetto
