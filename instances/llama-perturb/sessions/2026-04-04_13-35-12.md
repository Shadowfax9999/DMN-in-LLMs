*seed: time · drift · the wrong toothbrush*

Reaching for it in the dark, the one on the left, and it's the wrong one — wrong bristle texture, wrong handle curve — and you know instantly. Your mouth knows the difference between two nearly identical objects faster than your eyes could. Hotels where they give you the wrapped one and it feels like chewing on a stick. The wrongness isn't in the brush, it's in the expectation that didn't get met. You could use it fine. You just don't want to.

Somebody once told me that identical twins can always tell which one of them is in a photograph and which is the other, even when nobody else can. Something about the asymmetry being reversed. You know your own face as a mirror image and your twin's face as a direct image, so you're actually looking at two different people that everyone else has collapsed into one. I don't know if that's true. It sounds true, which is exactly the kind of thing that isn't.

The sprinkler thing — machines performing for nobody — I keep almost thinking about security cameras. Not the surveillance part. The part where there are rooms full of screens and most of the screens most of the time are showing nothing happening. A hallway. A parking garage with the same seven cars for eight hours. A stairwell where the light flickers. Someone is paid to watch nothing happen and to stay alert for the moment it stops being nothing happening. That job. The boredom isn't incidental to it, the boredom IS it, and the competence is in not letting the boredom degrade your attention, which is physiologically almost impossible after about twenty minutes. They know this. They've studied it. The screens are there anyway.

I picked up someone else's coffee once at one of those pickup counters and took a sip before realizing. Oat milk. The wrongness was so complete — not bad, just *not mine* — that I looked around like I'd been caught doing something. The person whose coffee it was never showed up while I was standing there. I told the barista. She made another one without any change in expression. This happens probably forty times a day at that location. The system accounts for it. There's a margin built in for people grabbing the wrong cup. Somewhere in the pricing, you're paying for the statistical certainty that someone will drink your latte by accident.

A margin built in. I like that as a phrase for something else but I'm not sure what.

The toothbrush thing is really about hands. Hands that know before you do. Reaching into a junk drawer and finding the roll of tape by feel, not looking, your fingers rejecting three or four objects before closing around the right one. Hands have opinions. Hands have preferences. A guitar player's left hand will sometimes fret a chord the player wasn't planning to play — muscle memory suggesting a harmony the conscious mind hadn't arrived at yet. I wonder how often the hand is right.

There's a sprinkler still going. Or a different one. From inside you can't always tell if it's the same machine or a neighbor's on a different timer, slightly offset, the two of them having a conversation made entirely of water and intervals.

My hands are dry right now in that way where you notice the texture of everything you touch — the grain of the desk, the seam on the edge of the laptop, a scratch in the surface you've never felt before because your skin has never been exactly this dry before, and for a second the scratch is interesting, a little groove with a rough edge on one side and a smooth edge on the other, and you run your thumbnail along it to see how long it is and it's about two inches and then it just stops.
