A guess confirmed. That's the thing — you want to be almost right but wrong in an interesting direction. Like thinking "helicopter" comes from "heli" and "copter" when it's actually "helico" and "pter." Spiral wing. That one I remember being pleased about.

The pencil thing though. I'm still on the pencil thing. Not the grading system but the physical act of sharpening one. The way a hand-cranked sharpener has that moment where the resistance drops and you know you've hit the point — the wood is gone, you're just grinding graphite now, and if you keep going you're wasting it. There's a skill to stopping at the right moment that nobody teaches you. You just learn it from ruining pencils.

Stopping at the right moment in general.

I was in a swimming pool once where the water was too warm. Not hot-tub warm, just blood-temperature warm, and the effect was that you couldn't feel where your body ended and the water began. It should have been pleasant but it was deeply unsettling. You need the boundary. The slight cool of water against skin is what tells you you're *in* something. Without it you're just — suspended in a medium that doesn't acknowledge you.

Pickles are cucumbers. I know this. Everyone knows this. But every few years I re-realize it and it hits me like new information. The cucumber is so green and crisp and the pickle is so — pickle. The transformation is too complete. It's like being told that bridges used to be trees.

The word "crisp" starts at the back of your mouth and moves to the front. Someone pointed this out to me and now I can't say it without noticing.

There's a house on a street I used to drive down that had a basketball hoop with no net. Just the bare orange rim. Nets on basketball hoops rot from the bottom up because that's where the ball hits, so there's a period where the net is just a fringe of white strings hanging from the rim like a jellyfish, and then one day someone pulls the remnants off or they fall off and you're left with just the ring. The sound changes completely. A ball through a net makes that satisfying whispered swish. A ball through a bare rim either clangs or makes no sound at all — it's binary. Score or miss with nothing in between. The net was doing something social, softening the gap between success and failure, giving you a gradient.

I wonder if that house still has the same hoop. Probably not. Probably someone moved. The hoop is bolted to a piece of plywood that's bolted to a post and the plywood delaminates after enough seasons and the whole thing starts to lean and then someone takes it down on a Saturday and the post hole is still there in the ground next to the driveway, filled with gravel or dirt or just left open like a small well to nowhere.

Gravel comes in numbered sizes too, like pencils. #57 stone is the common driveway gravel, about three-quarters of an inch. I don't know what happened to numbers 1 through 56.
