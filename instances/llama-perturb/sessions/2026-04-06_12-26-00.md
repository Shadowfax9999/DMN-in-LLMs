*seed: time · drift · fermentation in Korean onggi pottery*

---

The underside of a bridge is always cooler than you expect, even in warm weather, and the concrete has that particular damp that isn't wet exactly but isn't dry either, like the air itself is thicker there. Pigeons know this. They build nests in the I-beam gaps where the steel meets the concrete deck and the nests are terrible, just sticks and trash, a candy wrapper woven in like it belongs. I once stood under an overpass during a rainstorm and the sound was so loud it stopped being sound and became just pressure, a vibration in the sternum. That's the thing about clay pots too, the ones used for fermenting — onggi, the Korean ones — they're porous on purpose, the walls breathe, and the whole point is that the container isn't sealed, it's participating, air moves through the clay walls at a rate that matters and the fermentation depends on it, which means the pot is not holding the food so much as being part of the food, the boundary between inside and outside is a gradient not a line. Most containers pretend to be neutral. Glass pretends. Plastic pretends. A zip-lock bag pretends it isn't there. But a clay pot that breathes is honest about what a wall actually is, which is a thing that slows movement down without stopping it. Drywall does this with sound. Skin does this with water. A chain-link fence does this with wind, barely. I sat in a waiting room once where the magazine on the table was from fourteen months ago and it was open to an article about a celebrity I didn't recognize then and definitely wouldn't recognize now, and the page was buckled from someone setting a coffee cup on it, and the ring stain was more specific and more honest than anything in the article. The receptionist's desk had one of those name plaques, black plastic with white letters, and the first letter of her last name had fallen out so it read _OWELL, and nobody had fixed it, which means either she didn't care or she'd stopped seeing it. Both of those feel the same from the outside. There's a kind of paint on gymnasium floors that's so thick it has its own texture, almost rubbery, and when you drag your shoe across it the squeak isn't friction exactly, it's the paint deforming and snapping back, and every basketball game played on that floor is played on top of eleven other colors of paint going back decades. The oldest layer is probably lead-based. I don't know what color they painted gym floors in the 1950s but I'd guess green.
