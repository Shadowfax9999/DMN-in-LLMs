# 42

*seed: time · drift · potluck*

---

Potluck is a strange word if you look at it. Pot. Luck. The luck of whatever's in the pot.

But that's not what I'm thinking about.

I'm thinking about how when you carry a casserole dish across a parking lot you walk differently. Both hands occupied, forearms level, eyes on the foil. You become a different kind of pedestrian. Cars slow down for you in a way they don't when you're just walking. Something about the visible burden.

The foil never stays tight. There's always a corner that's come up.

7. The number of people it takes before someone brings a duplicate. Two pasta salads. Everyone laughs about it but one of the two people who brought pasta salad is doing math about what that means.

I wonder if "potluck" ever literally meant that — you just showed up and took your chances with whatever was in the pot. Like a grab bag but for dinner.

Alan — not a friend exactly, more like someone I was around a lot one year — used to take the serving spoon from one dish and use it in the next dish without thinking about it, and by the end of the table every dish had a different dish's spoon in it. Nobody ever said anything. I watched it happen three times at three different gatherings.

Folding tables have a specific sound when you set them up on concrete. That metal-leg-locking sound. And then the slightly different sound when someone leans on one end and the legs shift a quarter inch.

Paper plates bend. You learn to eat with a kind of underlying hand-architecture, fingers spread under the plate to keep the center from sagging into your lap. Nobody teaches you this. You just start doing it around age eight or nine.

I was going to think about whether animals categorize themselves but actually I'm thinking about the word "covered" as in "covered dish supper," which is what my grandmother called it, never potluck, and the covered part just meant it had a lid or foil on it for transport, but it sounds like something else — like the dish is protected, or hidden, or like there's a secret under the foil that gets revealed. Which there sort of is. You don't know what someone brought until they pull the foil back.

The thing about a folding chair on grass is that the rear legs sink in further than the front legs, so you're always tilted slightly back, which changes how you eat, which changes how you hold the plate, which means the whole posture of an outdoor meal is different from an indoor one and nobody accounts for this when they're cooking. The food was engineered for a table and a real plate and it's being eaten at a fifteen-degree recline off a paper plate that's bending.

Some people bring the recipe. They'll tell you the recipe while you're eating it. Unprompted. Every ingredient, in order, like the order matters to you right now with a plastic fork in your hand.

The ice in the cooler shifts when somebody digs for a specific drink — you can hear them rejecting cans by feel, sliding them aside underwater, that hollow aluminum-on-aluminum sound dampened by ice water.

I think the legs on our folding table at home are starting to rust where the paint chipped off near the hinge.
