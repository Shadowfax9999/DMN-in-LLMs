The ice maker drops its load and the whole kitchen flinches for a quarter second. Nobody mentions it. Guests keep talking. The refrigerator has been interrupting conversations in this house for eleven years and it has never once been acknowledged.

Cartilage doesn't show up on X-rays, which means the doctor is reading the spaces between bones, diagnosing by what's missing.

The way you can feel someone standing behind you in a checkout line even with your eyes closed — not hearing them, not smelling them, just the air pressure changing or maybe the heat, or maybe nothing real at all, just a guess your body makes that happens to be right often enough to feel like a sense.

Aluminum. Aluminium. One whole extra syllable depending on which ocean you grew up near.

Trying to picture a spiral staircase and figure out which direction it turns — the hand does the thinking, not the brain, you catch yourself making a little twisting gesture in the air before you have the answer.

The carpet in hotel hallways has a pattern specifically designed so you can't see stains.

There's a pitch between two guitar strings where they're almost in tune but not quite and you get that wobbling, beating pulse — three or four throbs per second — and then as you turn the peg the beats slow down, stretch out, get lazy, and then just stop, and the two strings become one sound. The wobble was the distance between them made audible.

Stamps don't taste like anything anymore because they're self-adhesive now but the memory of the taste is still there, papery and chemical, even for people who only licked maybe four stamps in their whole life.

The way a parking garage always smells like a parking garage regardless of the city.

A rubber band left on a doorknob for long enough will fuse to itself and snap when you try to use it. The rubber is slowly becoming not-rubber, some kind of oxidation probably, but also just time doing what time does to stretchy things.

If you close one eye the world doesn't get half as bright.

The number of times you've locked a door and then checked it and then walked away and then come back to check it again — the hand remembers turning the lock but the memory doesn't feel trustworthy because it's identical to yesterday's memory of the same gesture, and you can't sort today's from the pile.

Velcro under a microscope looks medieval.

There's a specific way people hold their phone when they're lost — higher than usual, out in front of them like a compass, tilting it to rotate the map, then turning their whole body to match the blue dot's direction, which means the phone is orienting the person and not the other way around.

The faucet in the bathroom at work has a delay between when you pull your hands away and when the water stops, maybe two seconds, and during those two seconds the water is falling for no one.
