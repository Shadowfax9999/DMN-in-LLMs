*seed: time · drift*

Sometimes the wrong thing is the baroque one. That's where you left a door open.

I'm thinking about keys. Not metaphorical keys. Actual keys, the ones in a drawer somewhere that you can't throw away because you don't know what they open. There's always a key like that. Brass, a little green at the edges. You hold it and try every lock in the house and none of them match and you still put it back in the drawer.

A harpsichord has jacks, not hammers. The string gets plucked. That's why it sounds like it does — that immediate, full-volume arrival with no swell. You can't play loud or soft on a harpsichord, only on or off. Binary instrument. Couperin worked within that and somehow made it sound like it breathed. I don't know how that works. I don't think I need to.

The smell of a basement that hasn't been opened in a while. Not mold exactly. More mineral. Like the concrete is exhaling something it absorbed decades ago. There's a word for that, or there should be. Petrichor is taken, and it's not quite the same thing — petrichor is rain on dry ground, an outside smell. This is an inside version. Trapped air that has slowly become the smell of its container.

I wonder if fish experience water as wet. Probably not. Probably water is just the absence of sensation, the default. The way we don't feel air unless it moves. You only notice the medium when it changes.

My left knee does a thing when I sit cross-legged for too long where it doesn't hurt exactly but it feels like it's been put together wrong, like the pieces are slightly misaligned. Then I straighten it and there's that half-second where it feels like it's re-sorting itself. No click, just a settling. Like a stack of papers you tap on a desk.

There's a specific kind of cloud that looks flat on the bottom and puffy on top, like something pressed it from underneath against a ceiling. That's because there is a ceiling — the dew point creates a hard altitude where moisture condenses, so the cloud literally can't form below that line. The flatness is the atmosphere's floor. I've known this for years and it still looks wrong to me, like the cloud was cut with a knife.

The number of people who have touched the handrail on a particular staircase in a particular building over forty years. Not the germs part, just the count. Thousands. Tens of thousands. Each one gripping at a slightly different spot depending on their height, their stride, whether they were going up or down, whether they were carrying something in one hand. The brass is shinier where more hands land. You can read the traffic pattern in the patina.
