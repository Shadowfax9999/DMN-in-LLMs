The plural of "cul-de-sac" — is it culs-de-sac? That feels right but looks absurd, like the plural is happening in the wrong part of the word, like you opened the hood of a car and found the engine in the trunk. French does this, pluralizes the noun inside the phrase and leaves the rest alone, which means the sac stays singular and the cul multiplies, which if you think about what those words actually mean is — well. Moving on. But it makes me think about how some words resist being made plural at all, not grammatically but conceptually, like "weather" or "furniture," and furniture is the strange one because you're looking at six distinct objects and the language insists they're a single mass, the way you'd talk about sand or water, as though a lamp and a credenza and an ottoman lost their individuality the moment they entered the same room together and became just — stuff that fills a room, which maybe is how we actually perceive it most of the time, not as individual pieces but as a texture, a density, the way a room reads as "full" or "sparse" before you can name a single thing in it. I walked into a hotel room once and thought "this room is sad" before I'd even turned on the light, just from the shape of the air and the particular way the curtain let in a band of parking-lot light across the bed, and I wonder what the minimum information is for that — could you feel it from the doorway with your eyes closed, just from the acoustic deadness of polyester bedding and the specific chemical note of whatever they clean the bathrooms with, or does sadness require sight, some visual cue like the single wrapped cup next to the ice bucket. Probably not sadness exactly. Probably just "impersonal" which the body translates. The cup is always wrapped in a little paper sleeve with the open end folded into a triangle, which is a tiny gesture of ceremony that exists only so you'll believe no one touched it, and the triangle fold is doing real work there, it's a seal, a proof-of-non-contact, the way a wax seal on a letter proved no one read it in transit. The whole hotel room is organized around the fiction that you're the first person to be here. Fresh sheets, vacuumed carpet, sealed cup. Meanwhile the mattress has a permanent valley on the left side from ten thousand travelers who all slept in the same spot because the alarm clock is on the right nightstand and most people are right-handed so they sleep on the left to reach it, though I have no idea if that's actually true, I just made a plausible chain and now I half-believe it. That happens constantly — you construct an explanation that has the right shape and then it solidifies into something you think you know. The valley in the mattress might just be where the maid sits when she's tucking the far corner of the sheet.
