# 23

*seed: time · drift · the particular weight of a hardcover vs paperback*

---

A hardcover in your hand sits differently than in your bag. In your hand it's authority, in your bag it's regret. You carry a paperback without thinking about carrying it. A hardcover you are always slightly aware of, the way you're aware of your wallet in a back pocket.

The dust jacket slides. It always slides. I've never met anyone who keeps dust jackets on while reading. You take it off and put it — where? On the nightstand, on the arm of the couch, inside another book. Then the hardcover underneath is this mute cloth thing, usually dark, sometimes with a small stamped image that's nothing like the jacket art. The secret face of the book. More honest or less honest, I can't decide.

Weight as a reading experience though. A heavy book changes how long you read because your arms get tired. There's a posture for paperbacks — one hand, spine cracked, the thing folded almost in half — that you literally cannot do with a hardcover. So the same words in a different binding are read by a different body. Lying down. Propped on a chest. The book tenting over your face when you fall asleep and it lands on you.

I keep thinking about chord and the three smells. WD-40 alone not being enough. Perfumers talk about base notes and top notes as though smell has a vertical axis, which is interesting because pitch does too and neither one is actually vertical. We just mapped space onto sensation somewhere early and never took it back. High notes, deep flavors, bright colors. All spatial words doing non-spatial work.

Someone is mowing a lawn outside, not close. Two houses away maybe. You can tell distance with a lawnmower the same way you can with a train — not by volume exactly but by how much of the high-frequency content has been filtered out by air and houses and fences. A distant mower is all drone, no clatter. Close up it's violent.

The paperback I'm thinking of specifically had a crease on the cover from being in a coat pocket. A white line across a dark cover. I liked it because it meant the book had been carried somewhere. I don't remember what the book was. I remember the crease running diagonally from lower left to upper right, which means the pocket was on the left side, which means it went in spine-down with the top tilted toward the body.

That's a lot of reconstruction from a single white line on a cover I can't even place anymore.
