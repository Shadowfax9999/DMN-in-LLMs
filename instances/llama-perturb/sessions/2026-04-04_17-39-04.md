Things I can hear if I hold still right now:

A lawnmower, but far enough away that it sounds like it's underwater.
Something ticking in the wall that isn't a clock.
My own breathing, which I immediately try to make quieter once I notice it, which makes it louder.

Things that get worse when you pay attention to them: breathing, blinking, the way your tongue sits in your mouth, walking when someone's watching you walk, a word you've said too many times in a conversation.

Things that get better when you pay attention to them: almost nothing? Maybe soup.

1. A colander has the same hole pattern as a pegboard but nobody thinks they're related.
2. The word "slotted" does a lot of work. Slotted spoon. Slotted screwdriver. Time slot. They share nothing except the shape of the absence.
3. I keep coming back to absence-shapes. That's probably just how I think.
4. Someone at a bus stop right now is standing in exactly the spot where someone else stood yesterday and neither of them will ever know.
5. That's not interesting. That's just statistics.
6. But it feels like it should be interesting.
7. The gap between "feels like it should be interesting" and "is interesting" — I spend a lot of time in that gap.

Q: Do dogs get songs stuck in their heads?
Q: When you remember a smell, does the same part of your nose activate?
Q: Has anyone ever measured how long a grocery store banana actually lasts versus how long people think it lasts?

I watched someone parallel park today in a space that was clearly too small and they kept going anyway, adjusting by fractions, and the person in the passenger seat was looking straight ahead in that way that means they're choosing not to participate. Three-point turn became a seven-point turn became a project. They got it in. Nobody clapped. The passenger still hadn't looked over.

There's a particular kind of loyalty in not looking.

My left knee makes a sound on stairs that it didn't make two years ago. Not pain, just commentary. The knee has opinions now. I don't remember agreeing to that.

Carbonation tastes different from a can than from a bottle and I think everyone knows this but I've never heard anyone say it out loud at the moment they're drinking from one or the other, only later, as a general statement about cans versus bottles, which is a different kind of knowing. The in-the-moment noticing and the later-describing-it are two completely separate experiences and the second one always flattens the first one a little.

A rubber band on a doorknob. I've seen that in at least four apartments and never once asked why it was there. Maybe it's always for the same reason. Maybe there are eleven different reasons people put rubber bands on doorknobs and I've encountered four of them without knowing any.

The lawnmower stopped and now the ticking is the loudest thing.
