# 17

*seed: time · drift · close call*

---

Close call. Like something almost happened but didn't, and the not-happening is the event. You tell the story of the thing that didn't occur. Which is strange if you think about it — the narrative is shaped around a hole.

But also: close call, like calling something close. Like proximity is a judgment you make out loud.

I almost dropped a glass once and caught it against my chest with my forearm and the water went everywhere but the glass was fine and I stood there holding it against my shirt like I'd rescued something. The triumph was completely out of proportion. Nobody saw it. I said "ha" to the kitchen.

The leaf blower across the street — I wonder if that person is still out there. Probably not. It's been however long it's been. The pile is either gone or it isn't and either way it's not my pile. There's a particular satisfaction in watching someone else do yard work. You get the progress without the sweat. Like watching someone parallel park from a bench.

Parallel parking. The way some people cut the wheel and just *go* and the car slots in like it was always going to fit, and other people do the seventeen-point correction, pulling forward, backing up, pulling forward, each time adjusting by about two degrees. I am the second kind. I have always been the second kind. There's a moment in the middle of a bad parallel park where you're at a 30-degree angle to the curb, blocking a lane of traffic, and you have to decide whether to commit or abort, and that decision feels like it matters more than it does.

Commit or abort. That's a phrase that sounds medical and also like version control.

Saturday has a particular quality of light that I associate with errands even when I'm not running errands. Like the day itself is an errand. Something about the angle, or just the knowledge that stores are full of people buying things they remembered they needed. The hardware store on a Saturday is its own ecosystem — everyone moving slowly, holding up two versions of the same fitting, comparing them like they're going to be graded.

My hands still smell like garlic, probably. Or I've stopped noticing it, which is the same as it being gone except it isn't. Other people can still smell it. There's a gap between your own senses and what you're broadcasting. You walk around trailing a cloud of garlic and coffee and whatever your laundry detergent is and you're the only one who can't tell.

The word "broadcast" originally meant scattering seed widely by hand, throwing it in an arc across a field.

I was going to not explain things. Garlic smell, broadcasting seeds — I keep opening the mechanism door. Let me just. A cardinal is making that sound they make, the one that sounds like a small laser gun in a video game from 1983. That's all it's doing. It's just doing that, somewhere in a tree I can't see from here.
