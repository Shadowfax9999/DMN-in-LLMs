# 45

*seed: time · drift · the first cold morning you can see your breath*

---

The first cold morning you can see your breath and you do it on purpose, like you just learned a trick. Everyone does this. Six years old, forty-six years old, it doesn't matter, you aim it upward and watch it go.

Falsetto — that word sitting there at the end of something. A voice leaving its own body. Singing above where you live. I heard someone describe it once as "the voice you'd use if you were pretending to be a ghost" and that's not wrong but it's also not what it sounds like when someone's actually good at it. When someone's good at it, it doesn't sound like pretending. It sounds like a second person who lives in the first person's throat.

Which reminds me of nothing. Which is fine.

Things I've been wrong about recently:
- thought nutmeg was a nut
- thought the Great Wall was visible from space
- thought I understood what "recent" means

The Great Wall thing — everyone believes it, nobody checks, and it turns out it's too narrow. Width is the issue, not length. You can be enormously long and still invisible from the right distance. Rivers are visible from space. Roads sometimes. The wall is basically a path.

A path that took centuries to build and it's thinner than a highway.

There's a alan wrench in every flat-pack furniture box and I have probably thirty of them in a kitchen drawer right now, all slightly different sizes, none of them labeled, and every time I need one I try four before I find the right one. They don't roll because of the L-shape, which might be the only reason they're L-shaped in the first place — so they stay where you put them. Or maybe the L is for torque. Probably torque.

Torque is one of those words that sounds like what it does.

Creak. Snap. Torque doesn't actually sound like what it does. I just wanted it to. The mind does this — assigns rightness to sounds after it already knows the meaning. Someone tested this with fake words and people will say "maluma" is round and "takete" is sharp, and they're right in some cross-cultural way, but then you get to actual languages and the word for "big" is small in half of them and the word for "small" is huge in the other half. Japanese: "chiisai." Five syllables for small.

Four syllables. Chi-i-sa-i.

Or three, the way people actually say it.

I'm sitting with the fact that I'm not sure how many syllables are in a word in a language I don't speak and that's a pretty normal thing to be not sure about. There's a alan wrench in the drawer that fits a screw I no longer own the furniture for, and I keep it anyway because throwing away a tool feels like giving up on a future version of the project. Even though there's no project. Even though the bookshelf is at Goodwill now, or wherever Goodwill sends the things that don't sell.

My left knee clicks when I go down stairs but not up. Only down. I keep meaning to ask someone about it but then I forget, which is how you know it doesn't actually bother you that much — the things that really bother you, you don't forget to mention. You mention them first. You lead with them. The knee click is a mid-conversation topic at best.

Someone is mowing a lawn right now, somewhere within three blocks of wherever I'd be if I were somewhere. That sound carries farther than you'd think. The pitch drops when the blade hits thick grass, then recovers. I don't know if that's true actually. I think I just like the idea of it being true.

The allen wrench — I've been spelling it "alan" this whole time, like it's a person.

Maybe it is a person. I don't know who Allen was.

The drawer has other things in it: a battery that might be dead, two rubber bands, a twist tie from a bread bag, a penny from 2003, and something small and plastic whose origin I cannot determine. Every kitchen drawer has that last item. The unidentifiable plastic thing. It came from something. It was necessary once. Now it's just shape and color with no function attached, like a word in a language that died.

The penny is probably not from 2003 specifically but it feels like it should be from around then, from the early 2000s when pennies still seemed like they might continue to matter. I haven't paid for anything with exact change in probably eight years.

The mower stopped. Either they finished or the bag is full.
