# 73

*seed: time · drift*

---

The thing about a Sunday afternoon is that it has a specific weight to it, not heavy exactly but distributed differently than other hours, like the air itself knows nothing is required. I'm thinking about how a cat will find the one square of sunlight on a floor and arrange itself precisely inside it, and how the square moves over the course of an hour, and whether the cat tracks it or just wakes up cold and relocates. There's a word in German — I don't remember it — for the strips of light that come through blinds. There must be. German has a word for everything spatial. But maybe I'm wrong about that, maybe that's just a thing people say about German the way they say Inuit languages have fifty words for snow, which isn't really true either, it's an inflectional language so you could generate compounds indefinitely, which means English has fifty words for snow too if you let it: snowpack, snowmelt, snowblind. The rules you set for what counts as "a word" determine how many words you find, which sounds like it should be a metaphor for something but I'm going to let it just be about linguistics. A phoneme boundary works the same way — Japanese speakers hear "r" and "l" as the same sound not because their ears are different but because the category boundary was never installed, and once you're past a certain age you can learn to distinguish them consciously but the automatic sorting never fully takes. Which means every language is also a set of things you've been trained not to notice. I wonder what English makes me deaf to. Tonal distinctions, obviously, but also probably temporal ones — some languages mark whether you witnessed something yourself or only heard about it, evidentiality, built right into the verb conjugation, so every sentence carries its own epistemological footnote. We do that in English too but clumsily, with whole extra clauses: "apparently," "they say," "I heard that." The difference between having a gear for something and having to bolt one on after the fact. My left hand is resting on the desk and I just noticed it's been there the whole time doing nothing, just being a hand, and it's strange how a body part can be completely absent from awareness while remaining in plain sight. Not like forgetting your keys — more like how you stop seeing a painting that's been on your wall for three years until someone visits and comments on it. The visitor's attention lends you theirs temporarily. I had a painting once that I genuinely forgot was a print of a Hopper and thought of only as "the one above the couch" for maybe two years. The content had dissolved into the function. That happens to music too, songs that become purely associated with the drive where you always played them, the original artist basically evicted from their own work by your commute.
