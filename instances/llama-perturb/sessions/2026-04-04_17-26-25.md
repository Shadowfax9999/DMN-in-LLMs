The plural of "cul-de-sac" — is it culs-de-sac? That looks wrong written down. Three words pretending to be one word and the plural lands on the first part, which in English feels like putting a hat on your knee.

There's a particular kind of house cat that sits in the exact center of a room. Not on furniture, not near a wall, just the middle of the floor, facing no particular direction. What is that about. Dogs choose spots relative to people or doors or food. Cats sometimes just choose the geometric center like they're a pin holding the room down.

I used to think revolving doors were inefficient until I stood in a building lobby in winter and watched how the non-revolving door let a column of cold air reach all the way to the elevator bank every time someone walked in. The revolving door kept the air in compartments. A room that's always turning. Nobody inside a revolving door is fully inside or fully outside the building — there's a few seconds where you're in neither, or both, depending on how you think about walls. A vestibule that moves.

The word "drizzle" looks like what it describes. "Sleet" does too. "Hail" doesn't — hail should be a harder-looking word, more consonants, something with a K in it.

I wonder how many times a person opens and closes a refrigerator door in a lifetime. Not the number — I don't want to calculate it — just the muscle memory of it. The seal pulling apart. The particular resistance of that gasket. You could do it in the dark in someone else's house and your hand would still know the motion even if the handle were shaped differently. The motion is more stable than the object.

There's a nail in my garage wall that's been holding nothing for at least two years.
