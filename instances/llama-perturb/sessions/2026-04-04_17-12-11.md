# 26

*seed: time · drift · the sound of a key turning that isn't yours*

---

The sound of a key turning that isn't yours. You hear it from inside the apartment and for a half-second you don't know who you are — not in a philosophical way, in a animal way. The lock is yours but the hand isn't. Then it's just your roommate, or your partner, or whoever has the copy, and the whole thing collapses back into Tuesday afternoon. But that half-second. The lock sound means *someone is entering* before it means *someone I know is entering*. The first meaning is older.

I keep thinking about how locks are handed. Left-hand door, right-hand door. Somewhere there's a chart that explains it and every time I've looked at it I've understood it and then immediately forgotten which one is which. Some knowledge refuses to stay. I can never remember which side of the sink the hot water is on in my own bathroom without testing it, even though I've lived there for years. I just turn one and wait.

A lot of daily life is just turning one and waiting.

The parking lot of a grocery store at dusk has a specific light. The overhead lamps have come on but the sky is still brighter than them so they look pointless, like they're not doing anything yet, just standing there warmed up. The cars are all facing the building like an audience. Someone has left a cart in the middle of a space and it's casting two shadows — one from the sky and one from the lamp — and neither shadow is very dark. There's a point in the evening where nothing casts a confident shadow. Everything has this soft doubled edge. A woman is loading bags into her trunk and she's doing that thing where you set each bag down and then shove it backward with your knuckles so it sits against the back seat. She's efficient about it in a way that means she's done this a thousand times and isn't thinking about it. Her keys are in her teeth. I don't know why people do that — your hands are full but you have pockets. Maybe the pockets are full too. Maybe it's faster. She finishes and the trunk comes down and she pulls the keys out of her mouth and gets in and I realize I've been watching her for thirty seconds from across the lot and she has no idea, which is the normal condition of being observed in public, mostly you don't know it's happening.

The grocery store dusk light is also the light of high school parking lots after games. That same not-quite-committed illumination. People standing around cars with the doors open, the interior dome lights on, music from two different cars overlapping badly.

I wonder what the range of a car dome light is. How far from the car you could read a page. Probably four feet, maybe five. It's such a small jurisdiction of light. You sit in it and everything past the hood is just shapes. Someone walks up and they materialize out of the dark at about the distance where you can see their face, which is closer than you'd think — you recognize the walk before you recognize the features. Gait is legible from surprisingly far away. Faces need maybe fifteen feet in dim light but you can identify a familiar walk from across a parking lot, something about the rhythm and the amount of vertical bounce and whether the arms swing or stay close.

I read somewhere that gait recognition is being used in surveillance now, that you can identify someone from their walk even when their face is covered. Which means the thing your friends do casually — oh yeah, I saw you crossing the quad, I could tell it was you from the back — is also something a camera can do, and the camera doesn't forget, and the camera is always in the parking lot at dusk, seeing both shadows at once.

The woman with the keys in her teeth is long gone by now. Her groceries are shifting in the trunk with every turn, settling into positions determined by the route home, the same way those sunglasses on the back shelf end up wherever the last curve sent them. Everything loose in a car is a passenger that doesn't hold on.
