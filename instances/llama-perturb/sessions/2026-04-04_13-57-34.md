*seed: time · drift · how sourdough starter maintains a stable microbiome*

Is an orphan the line that got left behind or the line that got pushed ahead? And does it matter that I can never remember which is which — orphan versus widow — or is the confusion itself the point, that both words name a kind of loneliness on the page?

When you feed a sourdough starter, where do the organisms that lose go? Is it a war or more of a slow crowding-out? If you neglect it for a week does the balance shift or does the whole ecology just slow down together, like a city where everyone agrees to sleep? Is the stable state something the yeasts and bacteria decided on or something they fell into? Can you call it a decision if no one made it?

What was the book with the crease?

Does a paperback crease white because the ink is only on the surface, or because the paper fibers themselves are breaking? If you ran your thumb along it could you feel the ridge? Why do I trust the diagonal more than I trust the title — is spatial memory just stickier than verbal memory, or did I care more about the crease?

Who mows their lawn at 2 PM on a Saturday and how do they feel about it? Resigned? Virtuous? Is there anyone who genuinely likes it, not the result but the act, the back-and-forth, the diminishing rectangle of uncut grass?

If you took a sourdough starter on an airplane would the cabin pressure change anything? Would the culture at altitude be meaningfully different from the culture on the ground, even for an hour? Do bakers in Denver already know the answer to this?

Why does the word "stable" keep showing up in ecology when nothing described is actually still — when the whole point is that everything is moving and eating and dying and replacing, and the stability is just the shape staying the same while the contents churn? Is a river stable?

What's the last question you asked that you genuinely didn't know the answer to, not rhetorically, not to make a point, but because you needed the information and had no way to get it except to ask someone?

Does the lawnmower sound different to the person pushing it than it does two houses away — not just louder but different in kind? Do they hear the blade? Do they hear individual grass?

When a sourdough starter is described as "100 years old" is any part of it actually old? Isn't it like the ship of Theseus except nobody even pretends the planks are the same — the whole point is that you keep replacing everything and calling it the same starter? What survives — the ratio? The pH? The name on the jar?

Is there a word for the kind of knowledge that only exists as a white line on a dark cover?
