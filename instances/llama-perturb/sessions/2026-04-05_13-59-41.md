The back of a spoon is cold when you press it under your eye. Nobody tells you that — you just find out.

Corduroy. The sound of corduroy is the thing itself, two pant legs brushing past each other. A fabric that announces you're walking.

There's a kind of door handle that's just a vertical bar, floor to ceiling, and you never know if you're supposed to push or pull. You always guess wrong the first time. The door is heavy in a way that feels intentional, like the building is saying you should be sure.

Airports have a smell. Not food courts, not jet fuel — the carpet, maybe, or the recycled air that's been through a dozen time zones. You breathe in and it could be any airport. You breathe in and you're already not where you were.

I've been standing in a kitchen where the light comes through a window above the sink and hits the faucet and throws a small rainbow on the wall behind the dish rack. It moves if you turn the faucet. It disappears if you lean forward. It's there every afternoon for maybe forty minutes and nobody in the house has mentioned it.

The way a coin sounds different on wood than on tile than on granite. You could identify a countertop blindfolded just by dropping a quarter on it.

A pencil rolls off a desk and you hear it hit the floor and then you hear it roll and then it stops and you don't pick it up yet. You know where it is. It's under the desk, against the baseboard, and it'll be there when you need it, which is not now.

The cardboard sleeve on a coffee cup never stays where you put it.
