The sourdough thing is actually bothering me. Not the philosophy of it — the microbiology. Because the culture does maintain something. The specific strains of yeast and bacteria that have adapted to each other, to that particular flour-water ratio, to the temperature of that particular kitchen. You could sequence the genome of the organisms in a San Francisco starter from 1890 and they'd be recognizably *from* that starter even though every individual cell has been replaced thousands of times over. So what persists is the community structure. The relationship between organisms, not the organisms themselves.

I was going to say something about how that's like a neighborhood, but I'm not going to.

The word "culture" doing double duty there. A sourdough culture. A culture.

What I actually want to think about is the feeling of sitting in a car that's parked but still running. Engine idling. You're in a parking lot. You're early for something or you're waiting for someone or you just haven't decided to turn the car off yet. The steering wheel is warm from the sun. The air conditioning is on but it's not winning. There's a receipt in the cupholder that you keep meaning to throw away and at this point it's been there so long it's become part of the car. The radio is on but low enough that you can't make out words, just the melodic shape of someone talking. You're not thinking about anything in particular. You're watching a woman walk across the parking lot carrying a flat of petunias and the flat is bending in the middle and she's trying to keep it level and walk at the same time and she's doing that thing where you speed up because you know the situation is about to fail.

The receipt in the cupholder has become soft. Receipts do that — thermal paper gets this cloth-like quality after a few weeks in a hot car. The ink fades to nothing. You could still feel the text if you ran your fingernail over it, the slight difference in texture where the thermal coating was activated.

I once spent probably ten minutes trying to read a faded receipt because I needed to know how much something cost for a return and I was holding it at different angles under different lights like it was a palimpsest. It was $14.99. Or $11.99. The one and the four look the same when they're ghosts.

Palimpsest is a word people use when they want to sound like they've read more than they have. Including me, just now.

The petunias are purple. Almost always purple. There are white ones and pink ones and red ones but when you picture petunias from a distance across a parking lot they're purple. Why is purple the default petunia? Is it actually the most common one sold or is it just the most visible from far away — the one that reads as "flower" from fifty feet. Yellow reads as "not a flower" from a distance. Yellow petunias look like a mistake until you're close enough to see the shape.

The woman made it to her car. The flat held.

There's a specific kind of relief you feel watching a stranger succeed at a small physical challenge that has nothing to do with you. Carrying too many grocery bags in one trip. Catching something they knocked off a shelf before it hits the ground. Parallel parking while you watch from a bench. You're rooting for them and they don't know it and if they fail it costs you nothing but you'd feel a small deflation anyway.

The engine is still idling. I should probably turn the car off but there's something about the vibration through the seat, the way it makes the rearview mirror buzz at a frequency you can see but not hear, the little reflected rectangle of the back window shivering slightly. The mirror is showing me the inside of my own car from an angle I never look at it from — the headrests from behind, the back shelf where a pair of sunglasses has been sliding back and forth for months, following the turns.

Sunglasses on a back shelf migrate. They're always in a different position than where you left them, pushed there by acceleration and braking and curves, a slow record of every route you've driven, though the information is compressed into a single position that tells you nothing about the path, only where the last hard brake sent them.

The air conditioning is winning now. I can feel the exact line on my forearm where the cold air meets the sun coming through the window.
