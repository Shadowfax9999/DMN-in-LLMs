# 23

*seed: time · drift · rabbet*

---

Rabbet. The step cut into wood so two pieces meet flush. I know this word but I never use it. It sits in the same drawer as dado and tenon and kerf — words that describe very specific cuts, very specific absences in material. A rabbet is a notch that exists so something else can fit. You remove wood to make a connection. Which is just how joinery works, I guess, but I don't want to make that into anything.

The cardinal is back or never left. The two-note thing. I think the female does a longer version, more syllables, but I'm not sure I've ever consciously heard it. Or I've heard it and didn't know that's what it was. There's probably a lot of birdsong I've heard thousands of times without attaching it to a species. Just background. The way a refrigerator hum is background until it cycles off and suddenly you hear the silence where it was.

Rabbet, rabbit. One letter. Completely unrelated words that your mouth treats almost identically. The double-b in rabbit does something, a tiny hesitation in the middle, but in rabbet the double-b does the same thing. They're homophones, basically, depending on how you talk.

Friday feels different from the inside than from the outside. From the outside, looking at a calendar, Friday is the end of something. From inside Friday morning it's just morning. The coffee is still lukewarm. The chip on the mug is still at four o'clock. Nothing about this moment knows it's Friday except the part of my brain that keeps track.

The air smells like cut grass already, which means someone on the street is mowing early. 10 AM on a Friday. Retired, maybe. Or works from home and decided to get it done before it gets hot. There's a particular ambition to morning lawn-mowing — you're the kind of person who handles things before noon. Afternoon mowing has a different energy. Like you finally got around to it.

I'm thinking about the word kerf now. The width of material removed by a saw blade. So if you're cutting a board into two pieces, the two pieces together are slightly shorter than the original board, and the missing width is the kerf. It just becomes sawdust. The board's material is conserved but not its length. Someone doing precise work has to account for kerf in every cut, shifting their measurements by an eighth of an inch or whatever the blade takes.

The mug handle is still at four o'clock.
