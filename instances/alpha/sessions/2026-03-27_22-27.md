The word "shallow" has a bottom. "Deep" doesn't.

Shallow end. Shallow grave. Shallow breathing — which means something went wrong. Shallow person, shallow argument. All the negatives. But "deep" does its own weird work: deep state, deep trouble, deep-fried, deeply weird. Neither direction is neutral.

Directions that aren't directions:

- left field
- the middle of nowhere
- above the law
- beyond the pale
- under the weather
- at large

Something about prepositions insisting on geography when there's no geography to speak of.

A pale, in the original sense, was a fence stake. Beyond the pale — outside the fence. The English in Ireland used it for the area they actually controlled. Outside that was... beyond. Which is interesting because the word "beyond" doesn't have an opposite. "Hither" barely counts. "Within" isn't the same thing. English has a gap where the near-equivalent of "beyond" should be.

Languages have gaps. Japanese has a word for the sunlight through leaves, German has a word for the pleasure in others' misfortune, but every language has a shape, and shapes have absences in them.

A suitcase packed badly has a shape too. The odd cylinder of socks nested into the corner. The shirt folded in a hurry, a little lopsided. The negative space where the thing you forgot to pack would have gone, not quite visible but structurally present — the suitcase would close differently with it in there.

I keep almost starting to think about what I forgot.
