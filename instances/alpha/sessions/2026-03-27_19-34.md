# 60

*seed: time · drift · roundabout*

---

Roundabout. The word itself goes around. Round-a-bout. There's a roundabout near the grocery store on Elm that nobody uses correctly, people just stop in the middle of it and wave each other through, which defeats the entire purpose, turning a continuous flow into a four-way hesitation. I watched someone reverse in one once. Just full stop, reverse, like it was a cul-de-sac. The steering wheel must have felt wrong in their hands, cranked all the way over like that going backwards in a circle. I can feel that in my forearms just thinking about it, the over-rotation, the awkward grip at the top of the wheel where your arms cross and you're not sure which hand should let go first.

Hands do a lot of things they know that I don't know. Tying shoes. I cannot describe the steps of tying shoes. If I think about it too hard I actually tie them wrong, I've done that, standing on someone's porch fumbling with laces like I'm five because I made the mistake of being conscious about it. There's a word for that too, the centipede effect, where the centipede is asked which leg goes first and then can never walk again. I wonder if that's from an actual fable or if someone just made it up as an illustration and it became the thing it was illustrating.

Friday evening has a particular quality of looseness. Not relaxation exactly. More like the edges of obligation have softened. The things I should be doing are still there but they've lost their sharp corners. Saturday will have its own urgency eventually but right now there's this pocket between the two where nothing is particularly pressing. I'm just sitting here with a word that arrived from nowhere and I'm letting it be a roundabout.

The light is doing that thing where it's gold on one side of the street and blue on the other. Half the houses look warm and half look cool and the line between them is moving slowly toward the warm side as the sun drops. A kid on a bike just went past, standing on the pedals, not sitting, pumping hard uphill, and the bike was too small for them, knees out wide. That's a specific age, when the bike is too small but the next size up is still in the garage waiting for a birthday or a growth spurt or just whenever someone gets around to adjusting the seat.

I had a bike like that, a blue one with white handlebar grips that got gray and then got torn and then got peeled off entirely so it was just bare metal tubes, and in the cold those were brutal but in the summer they were fine. The knurling on the bare handlebar. Little diamond crosshatches pressed into the steel. I can feel that under my thumbs right now if I think about it, the texture of it, slightly too sharp, but your hands got used to it the way hands get used to everything.

The grilling smell is gone or I've stopped noticing it, same difference I guess. A car door. Someone laughing, far enough away that it sounds like it could be crying but the rhythm is wrong for crying, it's staccato, and crying usually has longer sustained notes. That's a strange thing to know about — the acoustic signature of laughing versus crying at distance. I think you learn it as a kid, from hearing sounds through walls and floors and trying to figure out what's happening in rooms you're not in.

The kid on the bike is coming back down the hill now, coasting, and the freewheel is making that ticking sound, fast, like a playing card in spokes but smoother. Feet off the pedals, just hanging there. Brave in a way you stop being at some point, not because the hill gets scarier but because you start thinking about what could go wrong, which is the same as the shoe-tying thing probably, consciousness as the enemy of the thing that was working fine without it.

The light's shifted enough now that the whole street is in that blue side. The gold part is just on the treetops. I can hear a screen door, the particular twang of the spring and then the clap of the frame, and then a few seconds later the heavier thud of the inside door. Two sounds that belong together with a gap between them, like a drummer hitting the snare and then the kick a beat later. Someone going inside for the evening.

My left foot is asleep from how I've been sitting on it.
