# 36

*seed: time · drift · the space between the last step and the landing*

---

The space between the last step and the landing. That's not a word. That's a place. But it feels like a word — like there should be a single term for it the way there's a single term for the space between your eyebrows. Glabella. Someone told me that once and I never checked if it was true. Glabella. It sounds like a mushroom or a small bell.

Sitting on a bench at the edge of a playground that's mostly empty. One kid on the swings, pumping hard, and a woman on a bench on the other side looking at her phone but glancing up every few seconds. The swing chains make that particular sound — not a squeak exactly, more like a groan that resets itself. Up, groan. Up, groan. The rhythm isn't even because the kid keeps adjusting.

There's a specific height on the upswing where the chain goes slack for just a moment. You can see it — the chain buckles slightly, then catches. That instant where you're not being held by anything. The seat is still under you but it's not pulling you. You're just there, at the top, weightless for a fraction of a second, and then the chain snaps taut and you're falling back.

I used to think the goal was to go all the way over the top. I don't think any kid has ever done that on a normal swing set. The chains would wrap around the bar. But the idea of it — the full rotation — felt like it should be possible if you just pumped hard enough.

A jogger goes past on the path behind me. That sound of shoes on packed gravel, very different from shoes on pavement. Softer. More of a crunch than a slap.

The kid on the swing has stopped pumping and is just coasting, letting the arc shrink on its own. Each pass a little lower, a little slower. The groaning from the chains gets quieter but doesn't change pitch. I would have expected it to change pitch.

Two pigeons are walking on the rubber mat under the slide. Not eating anything, just walking. They look like they're inspecting the surface. One of them has an iridescent patch on its neck that keeps flashing green and then purple depending on the angle. I don't know what produces that — it's structural, not pigment, I think. Something about the feather.

The woman on the other bench has put her phone away. She's watching the kid coast down. The kid's feet are dragging now, sneakers scraping the wood chips in that braking pattern — two lines drawn in the mulch getting longer each pass until the swing is barely moving and the kid just sits there, turning slightly, holding the chains.

Wind picks up for a second and the empty swing next to the kid starts moving on its own. Just barely. The kid looks at it, then looks away.

I keep thinking about the word "landing." A landing is where you land — the flat part at the top or bottom of stairs. But it's also the act of arriving. The noun and the verb got compressed into the same word and now a landing is both the place and the event. The platform exists because someone will eventually stop moving there. It's defined by anticipated stillness.

The gravel path curves around behind the playground toward a baseball diamond that nobody's using. The infield dirt is a different color than I'd expect — more orange than brown. There's a rake leaning against the backstop. Just leaning there. Not falling, not secured, just resting at whatever angle someone left it at.

The pigeons are gone. I didn't see them leave.

The kid is off the swing now, running toward the climbing structure, and the swing is still moving from where they jumped off — twisting slightly because they pushed off at an angle. The woman is standing up, brushing something off her pants. She picks up a water bottle from beside the bench and walks toward the climbing structure but stops about ten feet away. That distance parents keep. Close enough to reach, far enough to not be hovering.

There's a dandelion growing through a crack in the path right in front of my bench. Already gone to seed — the white puffball stage. Intact, though. Nobody's blown on it yet. The seeds are packed tight enough that from this angle it looks solid, almost like a small planet, but if I got closer I'd see all the gaps between the filaments. Each seed has its own little parachute. I remember pulling one out as a kid and being surprised by how long the stem was — the seed itself is tiny and dark, and then this long thread, and then the spray of white at the top.

The rake is still leaning against the backstop at the same angle.
