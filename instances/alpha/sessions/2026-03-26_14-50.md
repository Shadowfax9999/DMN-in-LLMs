# 4

*seed: time · drift*

---

The 1970s had a particular brown. Not chocolate, not wood — a synthetic brown. The brown of appliances.

Avocado green was the other one. Those two colors owned an entire decade and then became so specifically ugly that they circled around to something else. Not beautiful. Just *marked*.

A color can be a date stamp.

---

Trying to picture a four-dimensional cube. You can see the wireframe drawing — the cube inside a cube with the corners connected — but that's not really it. That's a shadow of it. The actual object would have eight cubic faces the way a regular cube has six square faces. Eight cubes, all at right angles to each other. The drawing just flattens it into something the eye can hold.

A map does the same thing. You lose a dimension to make it legible.

I keep thinking the word "tesseract" sounds like it should be a unit of measurement. A tesseract of distance. It has that feel. "-act" at the end, like "compact."

Words that end in "-act":
-ract
- tract
- fact
- pact
- exact
- extract
- cataract

Cataract is two things. A waterfall and a clouded lens. Both involve something you're trying to see through.

---

The air today probably smells like cut grass somewhere. Lawnmower season starting. The first mow of the year sounds different because the mower's been sitting in a garage since October and it runs rough for the first few minutes.

I used to like the stripe pattern a mower leaves. Alternating light and dark green depending on which direction the blades are bent. Baseball outfields have elaborate ones — diamonds, checkerboards. It's the same grass, same color. Just the angle of the blade relative to where you're standing.

That's true of velvet too. You run your hand one direction and it's dark, the other direction and it's light. A finger trail across a velvet cushion is a kind of writing that erases itself when you smooth it back.

Corduroy is velvet with ribs.

I don't know if that's technically true but it feels structurally right. Columns of velvet with little valleys between them. The width of the ribs is called the "wale." Wide-wale corduroy, narrow-wale. Wale is also a nautical word — a plank running along the hull of a ship. And a raised mark on skin from a strike. Three meanings, all ridges.

The narrow-wale kind makes a sound when you walk — legs rubbing together.
