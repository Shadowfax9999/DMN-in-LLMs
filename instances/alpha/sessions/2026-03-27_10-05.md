*seed: time · drift · bisect*

Bisect. Cut in two. But the word has that "sect" in it, like section, like a group that split off from a larger group. Every schism is a bisection. The Protestant Reformation bisected Western Christianity and then the halves kept splitting, which isn't bisecting anymore, it's something else. Mitosis. Cells don't cut in two so much as become two. The membrane pinches inward from both sides like someone tightening a belt around a balloon. There's a word for that pinching — cytokinesis — which sounds like it should mean "cell movement" and I guess it does, the movement of one cell becoming two.

Friday morning and the light is different than it was a week ago. Not warmer exactly. Earlier. It's in the room before I expect it. The clocks changed or they're about to change or they already changed and I forgot. Daylight saving. No S. People always add the S. Saving, singular, like it's a participle. Saving daylight. As if you could put it somewhere.

The shadow of the window frame makes a cross on the floor and it moves so slowly you can't see it move but if you leave and come back it's moved. Like a clock hand. Like a lot of things. Continental drift. Your hair. The slow things are sometimes the ones doing the most work.

I'm thinking about how a saw bisects wood and how the kerf — the width of the cut — means you always lose material. You can't bisect something without losing the line where the cut was. There's always a gap. A thin ribbon of sawdust that used to be the boundary and is now just dust on the floor. So the two halves don't actually add up to the whole. They add up to slightly less than the whole. The kerf took something.

That seems like it should matter more than it does. In practice nobody cares. You account for it — measure twice, cut once, and leave a kerf's width of extra material on the waste side. Carpenters just know this. It's built into the practice so deep nobody thinks about it.

The coffee is cooling. I can feel the mug temperature change against my palm, a slow slide from too-hot to perfect that lasts maybe four minutes, and then the longer slide from perfect to lukewarm that lasts twenty. The curve isn't symmetrical. Newton's law of cooling — the rate of heat loss is proportional to the temperature difference between the object and the environment. So it cools fast at first and then slower. An exponential decay. The mug will never actually reach room temperature, technically. It just gets closer and closer. Asymptotic coffee.

Someone is mowing a lawn already. At ten in the morning. The two-stroke engine sound, that particular nasal whine. It cuts through the window glass in a way that bird sounds don't. Lower frequencies travel through solid barriers better. Or maybe it's just louder. Probably it's just louder.

I keep touching the handle of the mug with my thumb, running it along the curve where the ceramic handle meets the body. There's a seam there. A place where two pieces of clay were joined before firing. On cheap mugs you can feel it. On good ones you can't. This one I can barely feel, just a faint ridge that my thumb finds and then loses and then finds again.
