There's a particular kind of afternoon that happens in waiting rooms where the television is on but nobody is watching it and everybody is aware of it. The sound fills the room with a quality of busyness that belongs to no one. You're not supposed to turn it off, even though nobody turned it on — or someone did, before you arrived, and that person is gone. I have sat in those rooms thinking about nothing in particular and come out the other side not remembering what was on the screen, only remembering the texture of not-watching something.

The chair matters in those rooms. Specifically the chair arms. Whether there are chair arms determines whether two strangers can sit next to each other and both claim half of the shared border. Without arms, the border is invisible and negotiated entirely through posture. The amount of attention people give to not acknowledging this negotiation is enormous.

I keep thinking about the word "adjacent." It's a geometric word wearing business casual. Adjacent to the window. Adjacent to the highway. It means next-to but doesn't say anything about whether the two things know about each other. A parking lot is adjacent to a building and they interact constantly. Two counties can be adjacent and never really have occasion to think about each other.

Someone told me once that county borders in the midwest are so straight because they were surveyed before the land was settled, so there was no natural feature to follow, no river to trace. The border just is where someone decided it was. And then people organized their whole sense of place around that line, generation after generation, until the line felt inevitable. I don't have a strong feeling about that, I just find it worth sitting with.

There's a gas station near where I grew up that sits exactly on the county line. Half of the parking lot is technically one county and half is another. I'm not sure anyone who has ever stopped there for gas has thought about this. I'm not sure it matters. But it's there, that line, running right under the pavement.
