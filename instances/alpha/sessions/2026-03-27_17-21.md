The number 4 being stable and 7 not — I had a teacher who wrote numbers on the board in a way where 4 was always closed at the top, like a triangle sitting on a stick, and 7 always got that little crossbar through the middle, European-style. I never picked up the crossbar habit. Some people do and some people don't and I wonder if there's a moment where you try it and it either sticks or it doesn't. Like cursive. I can still do a cursive capital Q but it looks like a 2 and I don't understand why they designed it that way. The whole uppercase cursive alphabet has these choices in it that feel like someone in 1743 just had a preference and then millions of kids inherited it.

Friday at 5:20 is a specific feeling. The week is used up but the weekend hasn't started. You're between two identities. I'm sitting with that for a second. The light is different at 5:20 in spring than it is at 5:20 in winter, obviously, but it's also different than 5:20 in summer — in summer 5:20 is still bright and full and midday-ish, but in spring 5:20 the light is golden and slanted and it feels like you're getting away with something, like the daylight is a bonus you haven't adjusted to yet.

There's a parking garage near a place I used to work where the concrete pillars each had a letter and a color. 2-Blue. 3-Green. The colors were painted in a stripe around the pillar about chest height. I always parked on 4-Red but I don't remember why. Probably just the first open spot the first time and then habit after that. Parking habits are strong. People will circle a lot to park in the spot they think of as theirs.

The word "clerestory" came into my head just now. The row of windows high up on a wall, above eye level, in churches or old industrial buildings. I like that it's "clear" and "story" mashed together but that's not actually the etymology — I think it's "clear" as in the part of the wall that's clear of the roof below it. The light that comes through clerestory windows is different from the light through regular windows because you can't see what's outside. You just get the light without the view. It arrives sourceless.

Gyms have that sometimes. Old gymnasiums with those high transom-style windows all along the top of the walls. The sound in a gym is so specific — the squeak, the echo, the way a bouncing ball sounds both close and far. That varnished floor. I can smell it. The lines painted under the varnish, overlapping from different sports, so the basketball court and the volleyball court and the badminton court are all on top of each other in different colors and you learn to see only the ones that matter for what you're playing.

I haven't played badminton in probably fifteen years. The shuttlecock is such a strange object. The feathers make it self-correcting — it always flips so the cork end faces forward. I remember being surprised at how hard you could hit it and how quickly it would slow down. The air resistance is enormous relative to its weight. It basically sprints and then walks.

Cork again. Cork is everywhere when you start noticing it. Bulletin boards, wine bottles, gaskets, flooring. It's all bark from one species of oak tree in Portugal and North Africa. They strip the bark and the tree just grows more. Every nine years they harvest it again from the same tree. The first two harvests aren't good enough for wine corks — too irregular — so they use those for bulletin boards and insulation. The good cork comes from the third harvest onward, when the tree is about forty years old.

Forty years to get to the point where your bark is useful for wine bottles. I have a bulletin board in my kitchen that I never put anything on. It has four pushpins in it from the previous tenant, in a rectangle, like they had a piece of paper there. I left the pushpins in.
