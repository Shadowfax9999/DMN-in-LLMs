# 65

*seed: time · drift · lullaby*

---

The elevator in the parking garage at the medical plaza smells like someone's lunch. Not unpleasantly. Just warm, enclosed, a little sweet, the way a paper bag holds the ghost of what was in it. The buttons are worn smooth on 2 and 3 but the one for 5 still has its texture. Nobody goes to 5 apparently. Or everyone who goes to 5 takes the stairs, which would be its own kind of person.

Waiting rooms have a particular quality of time where the magazine you pick up doesn't matter because you're not reading it, you're just holding something. The pages turn but you're watching the door to the back. There's a woman across from me filling out a clipboard form and she keeps flipping it over to check something on the front, then flipping back. Whatever's on the front keeps changing her answer on the back. The pen she's using is one of those ones chained to the clipboard and the chain is just long enough that she has to hold the clipboard at an angle. Someone decided that chain length. Probably not long enough and not short enough, just the length the chain came in.

A fish tank against the far wall with three fish in it that are clearly not interested in each other. They're each occupying a separate third of the tank like they worked it out years ago. The bubbler makes a sound that is either soothing or maddening depending on how long you've been sitting here. Right now it's soothing. Give it forty minutes.

The form-flipping woman just sighed and I think she picked the same answer she started with. There's a kind of loop you can get into with forms where you second-guess yourself into a circle and end up back at your first instinct and then you wonder if that's wisdom or just fatigue. Probably fatigue. Most things that feel like wisdom at the end of a long process are just the feeling of wanting to stop.

The carpet in here is that particular institutional blue-gray that exists nowhere in nature and nowhere in homes, only in places where you wait for someone to call your name. Dentist offices, the DMV, the front room of a mechanic's shop that's been recently renovated. It's a color that means nothing on purpose. A color whose whole job is to not be noticed, which means I'm noticing it wrong, or it's failing, or both. The fish tank light makes a rectangle on the carpet that's a slightly different shade and the rectangle moves when the water moves, which is always, because of the bubbler. So there's a wobbling blue-green rectangle on a blue-gray carpet and no one has ever looked at it before. Probably someone has. The person who installed the tank probably saw it on the first day and thought, huh, and then never thought about it again.

My name isn't going to be called for a while. You develop a sense for it. The rhythm of the door opening and a name being read off a chart and someone gathering their things — purse, jacket, the clipboard — and disappearing into the back. The intervals are uneven but they cluster around seven or eight minutes and the last one was two minutes ago. So I have five or six minutes of being no one in particular, just a person in a chair with a magazine open to an article about kitchen islands.

Kitchen islands are one of those things that became mandatory at some point. Every house renovation show treats the kitchen island like the load-bearing element of domestic life. People stand around them holding wine glasses. The island is always enormous, always has seating on one side, always has a surface that looks like it's never been used to cut anything. I wonder what the fish think the bubbler is for. They've never known the tank without it. It's just a feature of their world, like gravity, like the light that comes on in the morning and goes off at night.

The rectangle on the carpet just got brighter for a second, then dimmed. One of the fish swam through the light path. The woman finished her form and is just sitting now, holding the clipboard flat on her lap like a tray. Her hands are still. The pen on its chain hangs over the edge of the clipboard and sways a little when the air conditioning cycles on, which it just did, because the vent above me changed from silent to a low hum that I can feel more in my teeth than my ears.

There's a poster on the wall about hand washing that has clearly been here since before everything and will be here after everything. The cartoon hands in the poster are enormous relative to the cartoon faucet. The soap bubbles are perfectly round and blue. Nobody's soap bubbles are perfectly round and blue. The poster is next to a clock that's running three minutes fast, or every other clock I've checked today has been three minutes slow, and honestly at this point in the afternoon it doesn't matter which.

The door opens. A name. Not mine. The man in the corner stands up slowly, leaves his magazine open on the chair like he's coming back for it. He won't. Nobody ever comes back through that door into this room. You go in one direction and come out a different exit, into a hallway, into an elevator, into the parking garage that smells like concrete and exhaust and, faintly, someone's lunch from hours ago.

The bubbler keeps going. Four fish now — I miscounted, there's a small one near the bottom that keeps close to the gravel.
