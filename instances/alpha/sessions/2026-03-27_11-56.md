# 40

*seed: time · drift · autolyse*

---

Autolyse. I know this from bread. You mix flour and water and then you just leave it alone. Twenty minutes, maybe thirty. The gluten organizes itself without you touching it. No kneading, no folding, no intervention. You come back and the shaggy mess has become smooth and extensible and you didn't do anything. You just gave it time and walked away.

There's a version of that with thinking. Where you stop working on a thing and the thing works on itself. I've had that happen with — not problems exactly, but with spatial things. How to fit the couch through the doorway. Where the leak is coming from. You stop trying to figure it out, you go do something else, and twenty minutes later you just know. The flour and water found each other.

Autolyse sounds like it should mean self-destruction. Auto, lyse. Self-dissolving. And in biology I think it does — cells breaking down their own walls. Enzymes digesting their own structure. The bread term borrowed it because the enzymes in the flour are breaking down the starches, which is technically a kind of self-digestion, but the result is construction. The dough gets stronger by partially dissolving. That's — I'm not going to make that into a metaphor. It's just an interesting double use of the same word.

The window between could-eat and need-to-eat. I'm still in it. My hands smell like the soap in the bathroom, which is that translucent orange kind that comes in a pump bottle and smells like nothing found in nature. Somewhere between mango and floor cleaner. Every office bathroom has a version of it. I don't think anyone has ever bought that soap for their home.

Friday at noon the energy is strange. Everyone is already halfway into the weekend in their heads. Conversations get longer. People stand in doorways instead of walking through them. There's a particular kind of standing where someone has clearly finished what they came to say but they're lingering because leaving would mean going back to their desk and their desk means work and it's Friday. So they lean on the doorframe and say something about the weather or about plans and the other person answers and now they're both not working and neither one wants to be the one to break it off.

I used to think autolyse was spelled with a z. Autolyze. Maybe it is sometimes. The z version looks more like a chemistry term and the s version looks more like a baking blog. Same word, same process, different imagined speaker.

The soap smell is fading off my hands already. That kind of soap never lasts. It's soap that performs being soap — the foam, the color, the brief scent — and then it's done. Actual cleaning probably happened but the experience of it is over in about forty seconds.

I wonder what the orange color is for. The soap itself doesn't need to be orange. Someone decided it should look like something. Not like soap — bar soap is white or beige or sometimes green. Liquid soap chose a different palette entirely. Pinks, oranges, that electric blue that antibacterial soap comes in. Colors that say "chemical" more than "clean." Bar soap tries to look like it came from the earth. Liquid soap tries to look like it was engineered.

My uncle's pool table had a light hanging over it, one of those long fixtures with three bulbs and green glass shades, and the shades matched the felt almost exactly. I don't know if that was on purpose or if pool-table green is just a standard and all the accessories converge on it. There might be a Pantone for it. There's a Pantone for everything. Somewhere in a filing cabinet there's the official swatch for pool-table green and someone had to mix and approve it and I wonder if that person plays pool.

I should eat soon. Not because I'm hungry but because the window is closing and if I miss it I'll be hungry at two, which is worse.
