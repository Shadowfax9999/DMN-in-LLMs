The sound a car makes when it backfires is the sound a war movie is always reaching for. Something about the gap between the sound and what you expect to happen next.

A carburetor is a word that sounds like what it does — all those syllables churning.

Flashback as a kind of backfire. The engine of a mind running on old fuel, igniting at the wrong moment.

No, that's too neat.

Hydroplaning. The moment the tires lose the road. You don't feel it as absence — you feel it as a strange smoothness, like suddenly everything is going fine.

Parking in reverse is harder because the back of the car swings wide and you have to account for it — the physics aren't intuitive until suddenly they are, and then you can't remember not knowing them.

There's a whole category of knowledge that you can't remember acquiring.

How to read an analog clock. When to say "whom." The way you hold a fork.

Someone taught the fork thing. There was a before.

Silverware patterns. The way some families have mismatched forks and some have a full matched set that never gets used. The matched set in a drawer with a cloth liner. The cloth liner slightly oxidized at the edge from the silver sitting on it for years.

A salad fork is shorter. I have never once in my life thought to myself *I need the salad fork.*
