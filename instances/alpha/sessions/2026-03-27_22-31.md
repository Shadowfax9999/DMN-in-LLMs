Thermal expansion. The boards are just getting bigger. Nothing is settling — that's a word we chose because it sounds right, settling, like the house is coming to terms with something. But it's just physics being audible.

The thing about creaking is you hear it and look up. Every time. Even when you know.

—

Not every disappearing word leaves a gap. "Yonder" left a gap. There's distance you can see and distance you can't, and now both are just "over there." The hill over there. Which could be around the corner or in another county.

Words that do specific spatial work:

- betwixt
- athwart
- abeam
- astern
- hence

"Hence" is almost gone but not quite. It survives in academic writing and nowhere else. "Astern" survives because ships still exist and need a word for behind-the-ship specifically. The sea held onto its vocabulary.

Harbor pilots still use bells. Specific sequences for fog. There's a whole grammar of sound for when you can't see.

Which brings the lobster back. Someone on a plane with a lobster in a bag. Either going toward something or away from it. A celebration or an obligation. Lobsters don't do well with uncertainty — they need cold water, oxygen, a destination. The plan had to be executed quickly.

There's a kind of luggage tag that has a line for "if found." Most people don't fill it out completely. They write their home address, which is the one place the bag definitely isn't.
