Apophenia. The word itself sounds like it should be a flower. Something in the lily family, maybe. Apophenia grandiflora. You'd find it in a field guide with a pale illustration and a note about preferring rocky soil.

But it's the pattern thing. Seeing connections that aren't there. Which raises a question I've never been able to settle, which is how you'd ever know from the inside whether a connection you're seeing is real or not. The feeling of recognizing a pattern and the feeling of imposing a pattern are the same feeling. There's no little flag that goes up. You just see it, and it looks true, and sometimes it is and sometimes it isn't and the experience is identical either way.

I'm thinking about this because of faces in things. Electrical outlets. The front grilles of cars. Two dots and a line and the brain says *face* before you can stop it. It's not even optional. You can know it's an outlet and still see the face. The face doesn't go away when you know better. That's the strange part. Knowing doesn't dissolve the pattern. The two just coexist — you see a face, you know it's an outlet, both at once, neither winning.

There's a word for that too. Pareidolia. Which sounds like a country. A small landlocked one with a difficult parliament.

I wonder if dogs see faces in outlets. Probably not — their face-recognition is tuned differently, more about smell silhouettes than visual geometry. But I don't actually know that. I'm just guessing based on a general sense of how dogs work, which is itself probably a kind of pattern-imposition on a set of facts I half-remember from different contexts.

Friday has a different quality of light than Thursday. That's not true in any physical sense but it feels true, which is maybe the whole point of what I'm circling around. The week has a shape and the shape has colors and none of it is real but I couldn't stop seeing it if I tried. Friday is yellow-white. Saturday is a darker yellow. Sunday is a kind of grey-blue. These have been stable my entire life and I have no idea where they came from.

A kid just shouted something that sounded like "no, the OTHER one" and I have no idea what they're referring to and it doesn't matter but now I'm curious. There are always two of something and one of them is the right one and you have to specify. The other shoe. The other hand. The other Greg.

The number of times in a day you say "no, the other one" and it works — the person knows which one you mean — is kind of remarkable. It means you're both maintaining the same mental inventory of relevant objects and you both know which one is the default and which one is the other. That's a lot of shared context packed into three words.
