# 37

*seed: time · drift · the way your handwriting looks different on a whiteboard*

---

It does look different. Bigger, obviously, but not just bigger — looser. The whole arm moves instead of just the fingers. Something about the friction too, the way the marker tip drags or doesn't drag. On paper your hand braces against the surface and the letters come out compressed, controlled. On a whiteboard you're almost painting. People's handwriting on whiteboards always tilts upward toward the right. I've noticed that in every meeting room I've ever been in. The first line is level and then by the third line everything is climbing. Nobody ever mentions it. You'd think someone would say hey, your list is going uphill, but nobody does because everyone's list goes uphill. Maybe it's because there's no lined guide, but people write on unlined paper too and it doesn't climb as badly. Something about the vertical surface. Your arm gets tired and starts drifting to its natural resting angle.

The dandelion is still there. Wind keeps almost catching it but not quite. One of those mornings where the air moves in these little inconsistent puffs — not enough to call it breezy but enough that you notice leaves turning over every few seconds. The kid who walked toward the climbing structure is on top of it now, sitting, not climbing, just sitting up there looking around. The parent is on their phone. That's not a judgment, that's just what's happening.

I used to think the word "parachute" was French for something specific to parachutes, like it was coined for the invention, but actually para- means "against" or "protection from" and chute means "fall." Protection from falling. Which is exactly what it does. Sometimes etymology is just... accurate. Not interesting-accurate, just correct. A fireplace is a place for fire. A sidewalk is a walk at the side. A dandelion though — dent de lion, lion's tooth. That one's about the leaves, the jagged edges. Not the puffball part that everyone pictures. The naming happened at a different stage of the plant's life than the stage everyone knows.

There's a jogger on the path that goes around the edge of the park, one of those paths that's half asphalt and half whatever was underneath the asphalt before it cracked. She's got earbuds in. I can tell because she's running at a pace that looks like it has a beat to it, very even, metronomic almost. When I run I'm all over the place, speeding up on downhills without meaning to, slowing down when I start thinking about something. I haven't gone running in probably four months. My shoes are still by the door with the laces pre-loosened from the last time, which is a specific kind of optimism — the laces say I'll be back soon, just keeping things ready.

A second kid has arrived at the climbing structure. The first kid is still sitting on top. The second kid is smaller and going up the ladder side. The two parents don't know each other. You can tell because they're standing at different angles to the structure, each oriented toward their own kid, and they haven't spoken. If they knew each other they'd be standing closer together, probably facing each other instead of facing the structure. The geometry of strangers sharing a purpose.

My coffee is lukewarm. That middle zone where it's not hot enough to feel like coffee and not cold enough to be iced coffee. Just room-temperature brown liquid. I'm going to drink it anyway because throwing out half a coffee feels wrong even though keeping it isn't making me happy. I think that's true of a lot of things but I'm not going to go there.

The dandelion. Still intact. I keep expecting a kid to come by and grab it but the bench is off to the side of where the kids are moving. It's in a low-traffic zone. It might last the whole day like that, just holding its seeds, waiting for a wind that's strong enough or a hand that's curious enough. The crack it's growing out of runs about two feet along the path and has three or four other plants in it — clover mostly, some grass. The dandelion is the tallest thing in the crack by a lot. Six, seven inches maybe. The others are all ground-hugging. I don't know why the dandelion grows so much taller than clover given that they're in the same crack with presumably the same amount of soil, which can't be much.

The jogger has come around again. Or it's a different jogger with a similar build. I wasn't paying close enough attention the first time to be sure.
