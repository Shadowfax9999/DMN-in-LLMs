# 64

*seed: time · drift*

---

The elevator at the dentist's office had a mirror on one wall and brushed steel on the other three, and standing in it you could see yourself repeated into a corridor that bent slightly because the mirror wasn't perfectly flat. The copies of you got darker and greener the further back they went. Twelve, maybe fifteen of them before they dissolved into murk. The door opened on the fourth floor and all of them stepped out with you.

Waiting rooms where everyone is early. Nobody arrives at 2:15 for a 2:15. You arrive at 2:03 and sit there with your phone doing nothing, scrolling past things you've already seen, and the receptionist calls you at 2:22 which means the 2:15 was always actually 2:22 and everyone knows this and nobody says it. The gap between the scheduled time and the real time is just a little pocket of dead air that belongs to no one.

The ceiling tiles in places like that. Drop ceilings. You look up and there's this grid, and every tile is slightly different — one has a water stain the shape of Lake Superior, one is newer and whiter than its neighbors, one has been pushed up slightly at one corner, maybe from someone checking a wire or a pipe, and never pushed fully back. The grid holds everything in place but nothing about it is tight. It's all just resting there.

I keep thinking about how many surfaces in a day you look at without any part of your brain registering them as something someone made. The drywall in a hallway. The texture on it, that orange-peel spray. I've looked at that texture thousands of times and I couldn't tell you whether the hallway I'm picturing right now is from a school or a doctor's office or a DMV. They share a hallway. Every institutional building in North America shares exactly one hallway and you've been in it your whole life.

Fluorescent light has a sound even when it doesn't have a sound. Something about the quality of it makes your ears expect a hum.

A woman in the parking lot was walking toward her car holding her keys out in front of her like a dowsing rod. Arm extended, fob pointed forward, pressing the unlock button every few steps. The car was further away than she thought. She kept walking and pressing and walking and pressing and then the taillights flashed three rows over and she adjusted course. There's something about the way a person's whole trajectory shifts when they get that flash — the walking gets more committed, the arm drops, the posture changes from searching to arriving.

Pigeons on a ledge above a pharmacy. Five of them in a line, all facing the same direction, like they were watching something down the street. Whatever they were watching, I couldn't see it. The pharmacy had a sign with one letter dimmer than the others, so it read like there was a pause in the name. WA GREENS. The pigeons didn't care about the sign.

I wonder what it's like to work at a pharmacy and hear your store's name with a missing letter for months. Whether you stop noticing, or whether every time someone mentions the name you internally supply the missing letter and feel a small irritation that you've trained yourself to suppress. Or maybe you never noticed it from outside because you always enter through the back.

Coffee that's been sitting too long develops a film. Not a skin exactly. Thinner than that. You can see it catch the light when you tilt the mug and it breaks into pieces that don't rejoin. It looks like something geological, like tectonic plates on a tiny scale. I almost just explained why it forms. I don't actually know why it forms. I think I half-know and would have made up the other half and presented it with the same confidence.

There's a type of cloud that looks like it's been combed. Long parallel streaks across the whole sky, evenly spaced, like someone dragged a fork through it. You see them and think weather is coming or weather is leaving but you don't know which. They were out the car window yesterday going north on the highway. The highway had that new asphalt that's almost black and the lane markings were so bright on it that the road looked like it had just been unwrapped.

The smell of new asphalt is one of those smells that most people will admit they kind of like even though they know they probably shouldn't. Gasoline is another one. Permanent markers. The inside of a new shower curtain. That last one might just be me.

A parking garage at night when it's mostly empty has an acoustic that nothing else has. You clap and the sound comes back from five directions at slightly different times and none of them sound like your clap anymore. Your footsteps become someone else's footsteps following you from several angles at once.

The gate at the exit of a parking garage rises with a particular rhythm — fast start, slight hesitation in the middle as if reconsidering, then the rest of the way up. Every gate I've ever waited behind has had that same hesitation. I want to say it's the counterweight catching but I actually have no idea. The gate goes up and you drive under it and in the rearview mirror it's already coming back down behind you, which always feels more final than leaving through a door.

Hotels where the hallway carpet has a pattern that's meant to disguise stains. You can tell that's what it's for because the pattern makes no aesthetic sense otherwise — it's busy in a way that serves no visual purpose except to make everything blend. The same principle as camouflage but for red wine and shoe mud.

My left knee clicks when I go down stairs but not when I go up. Only the left one. It doesn't hurt. It's just a fact about my body that arrived at some point without announcing itself, and now it's been here long enough that I'd notice if it stopped. I take the stairs at the parking garage instead of waiting for the elevator and each flight is a click, a click, a click, and the stairwell has that same parking-garage acoustic so the clicks have a small reverb to them.

The number on each landing. Stenciled, usually. White paint on concrete block. The font is always the same practical sans-serif that means nobody chose it, it was just the default on whatever stencil kit the contractor had. Unlike the serif numbers on the parking columns which someone did choose, or at least someone's firm chose, as part of a signage package that probably cost more than you'd think. There are people whose entire careers are choosing fonts for parking garages.

The highway heading home and the radio finds a station playing something with a steel guitar in it, bending a note that takes a long time to arrive where it's going. Steel guitar is one of those instruments that sounds like it's always in the middle of a thought. Never starting, never finishing, just sustaining and bending. The note resolves and another one starts bending and you realize the song has been playing for a while and you don't know when you started listening.

The sky over the highway is that late color where it's not blue and not dark, it's that in-between that only lasts about twenty minutes in spring. The headlights are on but they're not doing anything yet. They're just on because the car decided they should be.
