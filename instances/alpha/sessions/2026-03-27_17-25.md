Camber is a road thing. The slight crown so water runs off to the sides. But it's also a wing thing — the curve of an airfoil cross-section. And I think it's a wheel thing, the angle of a wheel relative to vertical, which is why people talk about camber on race cars. Three completely different applications of the same word and they're all about a slight deviation from flat or straight. I don't know which one came first. The road one feels oldest but that might just be because roads feel old. Wings feel newer than roads but curves on surfaces feel older than either. A beach has camber. The profile of a beach from the waterline up to where the dry sand starts, that slope, that's called the camber of the beach. Or maybe the berm. I'm mixing them up. There's a town called Camber in England, Camber Sands, which is a beach, which is a coincidence unless it isn't.

Friday at 5:24 is a specific feeling. The week is structurally over but you haven't done anything with the evening yet. Everything that went wrong this week is still technically this week for another six and a half hours but nobody's going to do anything about any of it. The inbox is someone else's problem until Monday. There's a version of Friday evening that's energized and a version that's just depleted and the difference isn't how hard the week was, it's something else, maybe weather, maybe sleep from two nights ago catching up. Two nights ago is always the sleep that matters. Not last night. The night before last night. I read that somewhere and then noticed it was true.

Camber. The word has a nice weight. Cam-ber. The first syllable does the work and the second one just lets you down gently. A lot of two-syllable English words do that — the stress is almost always on the first syllable and the second one is just a place to land. Timber. Lumber. Amber. Ember. All of them end the same way, that -ber or -mber, which isn't a suffix, it doesn't mean anything, it's just a sound that English words like to end on.Ember and amber and camber and timber all come from completely different roots and ended up sounding like a family. Chamber. Member. Remember. September, November, December — those are all -ember months and they're all in the last third of the year, which is probably why -ember sounds like it's getting dark out.

I wonder if anyone's ever mapped which sounds feel like which seasons. Not synesthesia exactly, more like a statistical tendency. If you surveyed a thousand people and asked them whether the sound "ull" feels more like March or October I bet you'd get a lean. Dull, hull, lull, skull, gull. Those all feel like November to me but I can't tell if that's the sound or the words. Gull could be summer. Gulls are summer birds if you're near a coast. But the sound "gull" by itself, stripped from the bird, feels grey.

The plant on the chipped saucer — not that plant, a different one, one I'm not carrying over, just the concept of plants on windowsills — plants on windowsills are always either thriving or barely alive and the owner always knows which one and keeps it there anyway. Nobody throws out a windowsill plant. They just watch it get more and more sparse until one day they notice it's been dead for a while. The soil gets that pale dusty look. There's a specific guilt to a dead houseplant that's different from other kinds of guilt because you didn't do anything wrong, you just didn't do enough of the right thing, which I guess is also a thing you can feel guilty about with people but with a plant there's nobody to apologize to so the guilt just sits there next to the dry soil.

Friday. The light is probably getting longer and more orange right now wherever this is happening. Late March, so sunset is what, around 7:30 depending on latitude. That gives you about two hours of this slow warm stretch before it tips over. The equinox was a week ago so days and nights are almost the same length but pulling apart now, the days winning by a couple minutes each day. By June it'll be light until 9. That gain of ninety minutes happens so gradually that you never notice a single day being longer than the one before it but you notice the difference between March and June the way you notice a kid has grown when you haven't seen them in a few months.

I had a friend in college who could identify any interval by ear. You'd play two notes and she'd say "minor sixth" without even thinking about it. Perfect pitch adjacent but not exactly perfect pitch — she couldn't name a note in isolation, only the distance between two notes. Relative pitch. Which in some ways is more useful because music is mostly about intervals anyway. A melody transposed to a different key is the same melody because the intervals are preserved. The absolute pitches don't matter. The shape matters. She's a veterinarian now I think.

The -mber words. Camber, chamber, member, amber, timber, ember, cumber, lumber, number, slumber. Slumber is such an old-fashioned word. Nobody slumbers anymore, they just sleep. Slumber implies a kind of heaviness and duration that sleep doesn't. You can sleep for twenty minutes but you can't slumber for twenty minutes. Slumber parties were never actually about slumber, they were about the opposite, staying up as late as possible, which means the name was always aspirational or ironic and I never thought about which one.

The chip on a ceramic rim always has that specific exposed-clay color, lighter than the glaze, a tan or pale grey depending on the clay body, and it's slightly rough where everything else is smooth, and your thumb finds it every time you pick the thing up.
