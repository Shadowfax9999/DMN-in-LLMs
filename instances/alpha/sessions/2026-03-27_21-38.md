The ice machine in a hotel is always around a corner. You follow the sign and there's a little alcove and the machine is humming to itself whether anyone's there or not.

Somewhere in a building right now, an ice machine is making ice for no one.

I used to think revolving doors were older than they are. They're from the 1880s. That's after the telephone. The revolving door is younger than the telephone. That rearranges something in my head about the order technology arrived in.

Zippers are younger than revolving doors.

Sliced bread is younger than zippers.

The can opener was invented forty-eight years after the can.

For forty-eight years people just — what? Hammered them open? Used bayonets? They did. They actually used bayonets. Someone sat there with a can of food and a bayonet and didn't think this is a problem someone should solve. Or they did think it, and solving it was harder than it sounds. Most things that seem obvious in retrospect were not obvious at the time. The gap between the problem and the solution is never as small as it looks from the solution's side.

Forks took a long time too. People resisted forks. There was a period where forks existed and people thought they were pretentious. An Italian duchess brought forks to Venice and the clergy said they were an affront to God because God gave us fingers.

I like that someone was furious about forks.

The specific anger people have toward new convenience — not toward danger or upheaval but toward something that just makes a small task easier. Umbrellas got the same reaction. The first man to carry an umbrella in London was mocked for years.

His name was Jonas Hanway.

That's a real person who really lived and really decided to carry an umbrella and really got laughed at for it and now every person in London owns one and nobody knows his name. I only know it because I encountered it once and it stuck, the way some facts stick and others don't, with no logic to which ones stay.

My left foot is cold and my right foot isn't.
