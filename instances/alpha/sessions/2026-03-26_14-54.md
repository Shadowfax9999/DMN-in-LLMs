*seed: time · drift · the gap between what you meant and what you said*

---

The sound corduroy makes is specific to corduroy. No other fabric does that. You hear someone coming down the hallway in corduroy pants and you know before you see them.

Velvet doesn't make a sound. Denim creaks a little when it's new. Leather does something — not exactly a creak, more like a complaint.

---

The gap between what you meant and what you said. That's most conversations. You walk away and the sentence you *should* have said arrives fully formed, forty minutes later, while you're unlocking the car. The French have a word for it. Something about stairs.

But the stranger thing is when you say exactly what you meant and the other person hears something completely different. You're both using English. You're both in the room. And somehow a third sentence got produced that neither of you authored.

---

Flat things that aren't flat:

- Kansas
- A calm lake (it's curving with the earth)
- A table, if you zoom in enough
- A piano key — there's a slight dish to it
- The floor of an old house

Floors in old houses slope toward the center or toward the chimney. You set a marble down and it rolls. Slowly. You can live in a house for years and not notice until you try to set something round on the kitchen floor.

---

The key of D major sounds golden to some people and that's not even synesthesia, it's just — D major on a guitar uses a lot of open strings. The instrument is resonating with itself. It rings in a way that, say, B-flat major doesn't. B-flat on a guitar is all barre chords. Everything clamped down. Tight.

A pianist wouldn't agree. On a piano D major and B-flat major are just patterns of black and white keys. The instrument doesn't care.

So the "color" of a key isn't in the key. It's in the instrument. It's in the specific resistance your fingers meet.

---

I keep almost sneezing and then not sneezing. That half-sneeze state, where everything in your face is tensed and waiting and then it just… doesn't. You're left with this useless readiness. The bright light trick doesn't always work.
