*seed: time · drift · the specific sound of a text message vs an email notification*

---

The text message is higher pitched on most phones, shorter, more like a tap on the shoulder. The email is lower, more of a tone than a chirp. I used to have my email notification set to something that sounded like a marimba and my text was the default Apple triple-tone and I could hear from across the room which one it was without looking and I'd decide whether to get up based on that alone. The email could wait. The text meant someone was in the middle of thinking about me right now, or at least that's what it felt like, even though plenty of texts are just "ok" or "running late." There's something about the expectation of response time that changes how the sound registers in your body. A text you can feel in your sternum a little. An email you hear with your ears.

I wonder if anyone's done work on how notification sounds affect heart rate. Probably. Probably a lot of people.

Friday at 5:22 is a specific feeling. The week is technically over but you haven't shifted yet. You're still in the shape of the week. Your posture is still work posture even if you're not working. It takes maybe an hour, sometimes two, for Friday evening to actually become Friday evening. There's a decompression period. Like when you surface from a dive and you're supposed to stop at certain depths and just wait. I've never been diving but I know this from diagrams.

The plant on the windowsill — not my windowsill, I don't have a windowsill right now, but any windowsill — there's always a plant on it that's either doing fine or slowly dying and you can't always tell which. The soil being dark could mean it's fine or it could mean the roots are sitting in water. I killed a fern once by overwatering it. I thought I was being attentive. The leaves went yellow from the bottom up and I kept watering it because it looked sick and I thought water was what you gave sick plants. By the time someone told me to stop it was already mostly gone. The remaining fronds were pale and thin like wet paper.

Fronds is a good word. It's one of those words that sounds exactly like the thing. Or no, it doesn't really, it sounds more like a name for a type of bread. Fronds. If someone told me fronds was a Dutch bread I'd believe them for at least a few seconds.

What I actually want to think about is the gap between doing and naming, from the piano thing. My hands knew where they were. That's not nothing. That's a real kind of knowledge. But it doesn't transfer. You can't hand it to someone else. You can't write it down. It lives in the specific body that learned it. I type on this keyboard without looking and if you asked me where the letter J is I'd have to mime typing to figure it out. My fingers know. I don't, in the way that "knowing" usually means being able to say it. There's probably a term for this in philosophy of mind and I'm not going to look it up because it'll have a Latin name and then I'll think about the Latin name instead of the thing.

The spring evening light at 5:22 would be getting long and gold if there were a window here. In late March the sun is still up at 5:22 but it's not high. Everything has a shadow about twice its height. Cars in a parking lot would each have a long shadow pointing east and the shadows would overlap where the cars are close together and make a darker patch between them.

I had a friend in college who could identify any car by its silhouette. Not the make and model from looking at it — specifically from its shadow or its outline against a bright background. He said the roofline was the giveaway. Every manufacturer has a slightly different curve from the windshield to the rear window and once you see it you can't unsee it. I never developed that skill but I remember him pointing at a shadow on a wall and saying "Camry" and being right.

The email notification just went off on my phone. The marimba. I can tell from here it's not important because it's 5:22 on a Friday and no one sends important emails at 5:22 on a Friday. Or actually, some people do, specifically because they know you won't read it until Monday and they want it to be there waiting. That's a strategy. Send the difficult email late Friday so the response is delayed by the weekend and everyone has time to cool down. I've done that maybe three times in my life and felt clever about it each time.

The soil in the pot would be starting to dry at the edges first, pulling away from the terra cotta just slightly, a tiny gap you could fit a toothpick into.
