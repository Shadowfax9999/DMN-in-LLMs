# 58

*seed: time · drift · the weight of a book you're holding open with one hand*

---

The weight of a book you're holding open with one hand is always in the wrong place. It wants to close. You're fighting it the whole time, thumb pressing into the gutter, and your hand gets tired before your eyes do. Paperbacks are worse because they curl. Hardcovers at least lie flat if you crack the spine but then you've done something to it, you've changed it permanently for the sake of a few minutes of comfort, and some people care about that and some people don't and you can tell which kind a person is by looking at their shelves. I had a roommate who dog-eared pages and it bothered me more than it should have. He also left cabinet doors open. Every single one. I'd come into the kitchen and it looked like the room was yelling. All those little rectangles gaping. I started closing them without saying anything and I don't know if he ever noticed. That's the thing about shared space, most of the negotiation is silent, you just adjust around each other like water finding a level, and the stuff that actually gets said out loud is almost never the real issue. The real issue is the cabinet doors. The real issue is always the cabinet doors. I wonder what I did that drove him crazy that he never mentioned. Probably something with the bathroom. Everyone has a bathroom thing. The toothpaste tube, the bathmat, the shower curtain left bunched to one side so it can't dry and gets that pink mildew along the bottom edge. Pink mildew. Why is it pink. I almost looked that up just now in my head and I'm not going to. It's pink. It grows in wet places. That's all I need. Friday evening and the light is doing that thing where it comes in almost horizontal through west-facing windows and every surface that's usually invisible suddenly has texture, you can see the dust on the bookshelf, the scratches on the table, the little raised bumps on the wall where the paint went over something. Afternoon light forgives and evening light audits. No. That's too neat. Evening light just comes in lower. The tree shadow on the wall is moving so slowly you can't see it move but if you look away and look back it's shifted. I used to try to watch the hour hand on a clock, really watch it, stare at it and try to catch it moving. I never could. I'd blink and it would be somewhere else. Like trying to see your own eyes move in a mirror. You can't. You always catch them already still. Someone at a party told me that once and I went home and stood in front of the bathroom mirror trying it for ten minutes. Your eyes are always already looking at you. There was a fly in that bathroom too, walking on the mirror, and from its perspective I guess the mirror is just a wall. Smooth, reflective, meaningless. It doesn't see itself. It just feels the surface under its feet and keeps walking. The light is shifting off the table now and onto the floor and pretty soon it'll hit the baseboard and then it'll be gone and the room will go that particular blue-gray that happens before you turn the lamps on, when you're sitting there and it's getting dark and you haven't moved yet to do anything about it because the gradualness of it holds you in place. You keep thinking it's still bright enough. The book in your lap is getting harder to read and you keep tilting it toward the window and then at some point you realize you've been reading the same paragraph twice and the room is almost dark and you reach for the lamp and everything changes. The click and then the warm circle and suddenly the windows are black mirrors. I always forget that transition, how fast it is, how completely the room flips from being connected to the outside to being sealed in with itself. The book is easier to read now but something else is gone.
