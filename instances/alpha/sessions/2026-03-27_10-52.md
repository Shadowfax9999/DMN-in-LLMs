Declension. Like falling through forms. Latin tables in a textbook, the word changing shape as it moves through cases — nominative, genitive, dative, accusative. Accusative always sounded violent for a grammar term. The declension of a hill, too, the way it slopes away from you. Both meanings are about something moving away from where it started, which makes sense, they come from the same — no. Just the word. Declension. It sounds like it should be a musical term. A declension of notes.

The dryer buzzed at some point while I was thinking that. The shirts are still warm. There's a particular way warm laundry smells that isn't the detergent and isn't the dryer sheet, it's the heat itself doing something. Cotton holds heat longer than synthetics. The athletic shirts cool almost immediately.

Hangers. I never have enough hangers. Somewhere there's a place where all the extra hangers go, and somewhere else there's a place where they all come from, and those two places are not the same place. The wire ones from the dry cleaner multiply. The plastic ones vanish. I have four wooden hangers and I don't remember buying any of them.

Outside the window over the washing machine the neighbor's kid is bouncing a basketball and the rhythm is almost regular but not quite — every seventh or eighth bounce goes off at an angle and there's a pause while he retrieves it. The pauses are more interesting than the bouncing. What he does with his face during the pause. Whether he looks around to see if anyone noticed. He doesn't, actually. He just walks to the ball. Picks it up. Starts again. No audience-awareness at all. That age where the gap between doing something and performing something hasn't opened up yet.

Declension again. The ball's arc is a declension. The way it rises and then falls through its trajectory, nominative at the top, accusative at the pavement.

I'm folding a fitted sheet and failing at it the way I always fail at it, which is getting three of the four corners nested and then the fourth one bunching. There's supposedly a method. I've watched the method. I understand the method in the abstract the way I understand declension tables in the abstract. My hands don't do what the method requires. I end up with something that's more of a organized wad than a folded sheet and I put it on the shelf behind the flat sheets where nobody can see it.

The kid missed again. Longer pause this time. He's looking at his hands, turning them over like he's checking if they're the right hands.
