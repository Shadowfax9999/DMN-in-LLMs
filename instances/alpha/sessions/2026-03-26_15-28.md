# 7

*seed: time · drift*

---

The clicking is dry. Not wet, not a pop, more like a plastic buckle snapping quietly under a coat. I keep testing it on purpose which is probably not helping anything. Going down the porch steps this morning I counted — it clicks on the third step but not the second, which makes no sense unless my weight shifts differently by that point.

The mailman's truck was idling at the corner. They idle for so long. Just sitting there sorting the next house's bundle. The engine has that particular diesel knock. No — it's not diesel, those are gasoline, the LLVs. The ones Grumman made. They look like they were designed in 1984 because they were designed in 1984. Square. The steering wheel is on the right side so they can reach the mailboxes. Left-hand drive countries must have their mail trucks reversed, or maybe the mailboxes are just on the other side of the road, or maybe the whole system works differently and I'm projecting American suburban infrastructure onto places that deliver mail to doors.

I held a rubber band between my fingers just now and noticed the way it goes from translucent when stretched thin to opaque when bunched. The color changes. It's the same material but it looks like two different rubber bands depending on tension. I was using it to bundle some receipts which is something my dad used to do, just a wad of paper held together with a rubber band in a kitchen drawer. I found one of those wads after he moved and the rubber band had fused to the paper. Vulcanized itself to the receipts over years of just sitting there. The top receipt was from a hardware store. Forty-seven dollars and something. No idea what for.

Forty-seven is prime. So is forty-three. There's a gap there — 43, 47 — and then 53. Prime gaps get wider on average as you go up but they're unpredictable locally. Twin primes are the ones that are only two apart, like 41 and 43. Whether there are infinitely many of those is still open. It feels like there should be. But "feels like there should be" is not a proof method.

The shadow from the fence is longer than it was twenty minutes ago. It's reaching the edge of the concrete now, almost touching the grass. In another hour it'll be across the yard entirely. The grass where the shadow falls most of the afternoon is thinner than the grass in full sun. You can see the property line between my yard and the neighbor's because they water more — there's a visible color change, almost a straight line of green shifting to a slightly more yellow green, and it tracks the property boundary exactly.

The neighbor has a wind chime that only sounds when the wind comes from the west. The rest of the time it just hangs there. Today it's quiet.
