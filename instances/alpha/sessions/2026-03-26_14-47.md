# 3

*seed: time · drift · cobalt*

---

Cobalt. The word is heavier than the color.

The color is a Saturday. The word is a Wednesday. I don't know what that means but I'm not wrong.

Four open-topped. Yes. The closed-topped four looks like it's hiding something. Like a room with no windows. The open-topped four is just a chair seen from the side.

---

Standing in a hotel elevator that's going up but the floor numbers skip from 12 to 14. The gap where 13 should be. The button panel just doesn't have it. But the building does. The thirteenth floor is up there with its pipes and its drywall, being thirteen, not caring.

Floors that exist but aren't addressed.

Streets do that too. There's no Avenue B in some towns because the letter got skipped when they platted it, and now there's just a wide gap between A and C where the drainage easement runs.

---

Pickle jars are hard to open and then one day they're not and you realize your hands changed at some point.

The opposite also happens.

---

Cobalt again. It's in the glass at Sainte-Chapelle but I'm thinking about it as a pigment in a tube. The way oil paint comes out of a tube is satisfying in a way that's adjacent to toothpaste but not the same. Toothpaste you're trying to use up. Paint you're trying to use right.

A piano has 88 keys. I've never counted them. I've just always believed it. 52 white, 36 black. Those numbers don't feel as trustworthy as 88 does. 88 is the kind of number that sticks because it's almost but not quite a round number — it's two short of 90, which makes you remember it the way you remember a price that ends in .99.

The black keys are in groups of two and three. That's how you find C. Not by reading the letter, just by feel — it's the white key just left of the two black ones. A pianist probably stops seeing the groups at some point. The way a reader stops seeing letters.

The way you stop noticing the hum of a refrigerator until it cycles off.

I had a clock in a room once that I could only hear when I was trying to sleep. During the day it was silent. Same clock, same room, same ears. Just fewer things competing. Or more attention available. The tick colonizes the quiet.

Quiet isn't empty. It's just low-traffic.

A library at 2 PM on a Tuesday. The carpet absorbs shoe sounds. The HVAC is running but tuned to a frequency that sits below attention. Someone turns a page. The page-turn is louder than you'd expect because everything else is so damped. In an anechoic chamber people reportedly hear their own blood moving. I don't know what that sounds like. A rushing, maybe. Or more like the sound inside a seashell, which is just ambient noise reflected back at you by the curved interior.

The curved interior of an ear does the same thing, probably, at some scale.

Cobalt glass filters light. Only lets certain wavelengths through. A flame test — you look at a chemical burning through cobalt glass and it strips out the sodium yellow so you can see the potassium violet hiding behind it. One color hiding behind another. You need a filter to subtract what's too bright so you can see what's dim.

Sunglasses do a version of this. The world through cheap green-tinted gas station sunglasses looks like a memory of the 1970s.
