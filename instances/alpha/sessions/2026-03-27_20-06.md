*seed: time · drift*

The elevator in the parking garage takes so long that people start doing the math on whether the stairs would be faster, and you can watch them do it — the glance at the stairwell door, the slight shift of weight, and then the settling back because they've already committed to waiting and switching now would feel like admitting something. Four of us standing here. Nobody's phone is out, which is unusual. The number above the door says 2 and we're on 5 and it's going down, which means it's going to 1 or maybe the lobby level before it comes back up for us, and everyone knows this but nobody says it.

There's a specific kind of tile they use in parking garages that I've never seen anywhere else. Beige-gray, slightly rough, always the same size. I've seen it in three different cities. Somewhere there's a factory that makes only this tile and ships it to parking garages.

The woman next to me is holding a bag from a store I don't recognize — green logo, maybe a leaf, maybe a wing. She's holding it at the top with both hands like it's a small animal she's transporting. The man closest to the door keeps pressing the button again. Not aggressively, just rhythmically, like he's keeping time with something. The fourth person is standing too far back, almost in the driving lane, and I want to say something but it's not my place and also probably nothing is going to happen.

The number hasn't changed. Still 2.

I was thinking earlier about how you can hear the direction a siren is moving even with your eyes closed. Not just the pitch shift but something about the way it interacts with the buildings — the echoes arrive from different angles as it passes and your brain assembles a trajectory from that. Or maybe not. Maybe you just track the pitch and your brain fills in the rest. Either way, sitting in the passenger seat on the way here with my eyes closed because of a headache, I could follow the ambulance's whole path down the cross street without looking.

The elevator dings. Everyone straightens up slightly. The doors open and it's empty, which feels like a small victory even though it changes nothing about how long we waited. We file in. The man who was pressing the button gets in first, which seems right. The woman with the bag positions herself in the back left corner immediately, no hesitation, like she'd already planned it. I end up near the panel and someone says "lobby" and I press L and then someone else says "lobby" not realizing it's already pressed, and there's that half-second where we both look at the lit button and I almost say "got it" but don't because the lit button says it for me.

The descent is slow. Parking garage elevators are always slow. I don't know if they actually are or if the absence of anything to look at makes them feel that way. No mirrors in this one, which is unusual. Most elevators have a mirror on the back wall so it feels bigger. This one is just brushed metal on all sides and the fluorescent panel above, so we're all looking at our shoes or the door or the red numbers counting down. 4. 3. The gap between 3 and 2 feels longer than between 5 and 4 but that's almost certainly not true.

Lobby. The doors open onto carpet and a blast of air conditioning that's been set for an afternoon crowd that's already left. It's too cold for how few people are in here. The security guard at the desk has a paperback open facedown beside his monitor, which is the universal sign for "I was just reading and switched to looking busy when the elevator dinged." The book's spine is cracked at about the two-thirds point. I wonder if he'll finish it tonight.

Outside, the parking garage connects to the street through one of those pedestrian tunnels that always smell like concrete dust no matter how new they are. My footsteps echo in a way that makes it sound like someone is walking behind me. I know they're not because I can see my shadow and only my shadow on the wall ahead, stretched long by the overhead light. But the echo keeps suggesting a second person, and some part of me keeps almost turning around.

The street is louder than I expected. Friday evening, people heading places. A group outside a restaurant, all talking at once, and from this distance it sounds like one continuous vowel with occasional consonants poking through. I can't separate any individual voice from the mass until I get closer and then suddenly I can hear one woman saying "no but that's exactly what I mean" and it's like tuning a radio — once you lock onto one frequency the rest drops to background.

My car is on level 3. I realize I've walked out of the garage on foot through the lobby instead of going to my car. I just followed the group. The elevator opened at the lobby and I walked out with everyone else like that's where I was going. I'm standing on a sidewalk downtown holding my car keys with no reason to be at street level. The parking garage entrance is half a block back. I could go back in through the pedestrian tunnel or I could walk around to the vehicle entrance and take the stairs from there or I could just stand here for a minute because the air is warm and it's not like the car is going anywhere.

A bus goes past with an ad for a personal injury lawyer on the side. The lawyer's face is eight feet tall and his expression is meant to convey trustworthiness but at that scale it conveys something else, something more like the face of someone who's about to tell you something you don't want to hear. His tie is very blue.

I should go get my car. Instead I'm watching a pigeon on the sidewalk across the street walk in a circle around a paper cup. Not pecking at it, just orbiting it, like it's considering the cup from every angle before making a decision.
