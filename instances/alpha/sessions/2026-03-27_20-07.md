# 62

*seed: time · drift*

---

The elevator in the parking garage smells like someone else's cologne and it's the kind that sits in the back of your throat. Fourth floor. The doors open onto that particular fluorescent gray that parking garages have, where you can't tell if it's bright or dim. Both somehow. I always forget where I parked in structures like this and I'm walking in the wrong direction for a few seconds before I turn around and there's the car, closer than I expected, facing a concrete wall with a pipe running along it that's dripping onto the hood in a slow irregular rhythm.

Driving out of the structure you pass through bands of light and dark, light and dark, each level has that half-wall open to the outside and then a solid section and the sun coming in sideways makes the whole descent into something stroboscopic. The ticket machine takes the stub and the arm goes up and you're on a street you don't recognize for a second even though you've been here before, because exiting a parking structure always puts you out facing a direction you didn't enter from.

There's a song on the radio I almost know. The chorus is familiar but the verses aren't, which means I've only ever heard it in passing, in someone else's car or at a distance. That's a whole category of knowledge — things you know the chorus of. You could hum eight bars and then nothing.

Red light. A woman in the crosswalk is carrying a cake box, the white kind with the string, and she's holding it with both hands perfectly level the way you'd carry a tray of water you didn't want to spill. The light changes before she's across and she doesn't speed up, she just keeps walking at cake-carrying speed, and the cars wait.

I'm thinking about how every building downtown has a loading dock somewhere behind it and you almost never see them from the street. Restaurants get their food from the back. Hotels get their sheets from the back. There's a whole second city facing the alleys, with roll-up doors and dumpsters and pallets and guys in aprons smoking, and it maps perfectly onto the first city but almost nobody sees both at the same time unless you take a wrong turn. I took a wrong turn once in a city I was visiting and ended up behind a theater and there were costume racks just sitting outside in the alley, like a rack of coats at a party but they were all from some period piece, eighteen-hundreds dresses with the tags still on them, and a guy was hosing down the concrete next to them like they weren't even there.

The highway on-ramp curves upward and you can feel the car lean. Merge. The particular tension of merging, of finding the slot, the brief negotiation where you and the car in the right lane both adjust speed by small amounts without signaling anything to each other except through the adjustment itself.

Billboards out here for things that are thirty miles away. A water park. A casino. A furniture outlet. The furniture outlet one has a couch on it and the couch is photographed at an angle that makes it look enormous against the sky, like a couch for someone forty feet tall, and for a second I'm just thinking about that — a forty-foot person sitting on that couch, what the cushions would feel like at that scale, whether the texture of fabric changes when you scale it up or whether it would just feel like a flat surface to a hand that big.

The cruise control is set to seventy-two. There's a point on the highway where the road surface changes from one kind of asphalt to another and you can hear it in the tires, a shift in pitch, and then it changes back a mile later. I don't know what determines where one surface ends and the other begins. Different contracts maybe. Different years.

Something is rattling in the back seat. Probably the water bottle rolling around in the footwell. It only rattles when the road is rough and then it stops and you forget about it and then it starts again. The sun is low enough that the visor doesn't help and you just squint for a few miles until the road curves south and the light moves to the side mirror instead.

I had the window down earlier and my left arm is slightly warmer than my right arm from the sun. Just the forearm. There's probably a tan line forming at the sleeve but I can't check right now. The asymmetry of driving — left arm in the sun, right arm in shadow, left ear hearing the wind, right ear hearing the radio. You arrive somewhere and one side of you has had a completely different trip than the other.

Exit 14. The off-ramp deposits you at a stoplight next to a gas station where the price sign has one digit burned out so it reads $3._9 and you have to guess what's in the gap.
