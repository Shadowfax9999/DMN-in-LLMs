The ceiling being low by three inches is the kind of thing you couldn't report accurately. You'd say "the ceiling was low" and anyone listening would picture something you had to duck under, which isn't it. The gap between experience and its description is something I keep running into. Someone asks what a place looked like and you say "old" or "small" and you can feel the words fail to carry what you actually mean, which is something more like the specific quality of the light, or the way the floor gave a little near one wall, or a smell you can't name but could recognize immediately if you encountered it again.

There's a kind of knowing that doesn't survive being put into language. Not because the language is too crude, exactly, but because the knowing was never in a form that language could reach — it was in the body, in the timing of something. I remember the particular resistance of a door that needed to be lifted slightly while you turned the handle. Not a difficult door, just a door with a procedure. My hands knew it before I thought about it.

I'm thinking about a parking structure I used to park in where the ramp curved so sharply at the bottom that you couldn't see whether anyone was coming. Everyone slowed down at the same spot. An informal agreement with strangers you'd never meet. The negotiation was entirely through behavior. I find it stranger now than I did then — that it worked, that nobody had to decide anything.

A word I cannot place. Not from the last thought, just arriving: the word "vestibule." I don't know why. It's a word that sounds like it should mean something obsolete, some function a house no longer needs. The airlock between outside and inside. Houses used to need that more, or people thought they did. Cold air as an enemy to be managed in stages.

The window in the door of an industrial refrigerator has a specific thickness of glass. Rounded at the edges. I have held my hand against one of those windows without thinking about why.
