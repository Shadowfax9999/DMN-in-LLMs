# 6

*seed: time · drift · wobbly table*

---

Every restaurant has one. You fold a napkin, or a sugar packet, or someone's already jammed a piece of cardboard under the short leg and it still rocks. The thing about a wobbly table is you can't stop testing it. You press down on the corner and it clunks and you take your hand away and you press it again. Checking whether the wobble is still there. It's always still there. There's something about a known problem you keep verifying, like touching a bruise, like wiggling a loose tooth with your tongue, which I did constantly as a kid and I think everyone did, that one molar that was hanging on by a thread and you could push it almost flat and then let it spring back and there was this deep gum-ache that was satisfying in a way that nothing since has been satisfying in exactly that way. I haven't had a loose tooth in decades. Obviously. But the memory is in my tongue, not my head. The tongue remembers the socket.

Wobble. The word itself wobbles. Say it slowly. The mouth makes a round shape twice. W-o, then b-b-l, which is the lips doing the table-rocking thing, then the y trails off like the vibration dying out. Some words are like that where the mouth is doing the thing the word describes. Not onomatopoeia exactly, more like the shape of saying it mirrors the shape of the event.

Puddle does that too. Your mouth makes a flat round thing.

Crisp starts at the back of the throat and ends at the lips. Someone pointed that out once and now I can never not notice it.

I wonder if anyone's ever actually solved a wobbly table permanently. Like with a level and a saw. Trimmed the long leg down. You'd need to know which leg was long, which isn't the same as knowing which leg is short, because maybe two are short — three contact points define a plane and the fourth is the one that's off, but which three are you keeping? You've already decided by where you put the sugar packet. The sugar packet picks the plane. That's more geometry than I expected to find in a restaurant table but geometry doesn't care whether you expected it.

The chairs at the dentist's office last week were all the same height but spaced weirdly far apart. Like they were expecting larger people. Or people who didn't want their elbows near other people's elbows. Which, at the dentist, fair enough. Everyone sitting there is about to have someone's hands in their mouth. You're already anticipating a kind of invasion. Elbow room is the least they can offer.

The magazines were from 2024.

I always read the magazines at the dentist even though I never read magazines anywhere else. There's something about a waiting room that makes a two-year-old copy of *People* genuinely interesting. The same way airport food tastes fine. Context does most of the work.

The fluorescent lights in that office had a faint flicker. Not enough to complain about. Just enough that when you looked at a white wall you could almost see it pulsing at the edge of your vision, and when you looked directly at the light it seemed steady. Peripheral vision catches flicker that direct vision smooths over.

My left knee has been clicking when I go down stairs. Not up. Just down. Only the left one.
