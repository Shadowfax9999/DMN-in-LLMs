The number four keeps showing up in card games. Four suits, four players for bridge, four of a kind. But also four legs on a chair, four corners of a room, four pushpins. It's the number that makes a stable shape. Three is the minimum for geometry but four is the minimum for furniture. I don't actually know if that's true. Some stools have three legs. Three-legged stools don't wobble on uneven ground because three points always define a plane. So four is actually the unstable number, the one that rocks. Every wobbly table in every restaurant is wobbling because four is one too many.

I was thinking about how you tune a guitar by comparing two strings to each other. You hold down one string at the fifth fret and play it against the next open string and they should sound the same. But "the same" is hard to hear at first. What you're listening for is a wobble — a beating, a pulse — that slows down and disappears as the two notes get closer together. You're not listening for the right pitch. You're listening for the absence of wrongness. Which is a different skill. Like how you don't notice the refrigerator hum until it clicks off.

Somebody in the apartment above me is dragging something across the floor. It's that slow scrape that could be a chair or could be a heavy box. It stopped. Now I can hear their footsteps, which means they took their shoes off because usually I can't hear them walk but I can hear them in socks somehow. No, that doesn't make sense. Maybe the socks thing is backwards. Maybe shoes are louder and I'm just used to it so I filter it out and the bare feet are unusual enough that I notice them. I don't know which one I'm hearing.

Friday at five-something feels different from Wednesday at five-something. The light is the same, the temperature is roughly the same, but there's a looseness to it. People's voices outside sound different. Louder maybe, or slower. Cars drive a little faster on Fridays, I read that somewhere, or maybe I made it up. It sounds like the kind of statistic that gets passed around whether or not it's real.

I had a piano teacher who would stop me in the middle of a piece and say "what key are you in right now" and I could never answer. I could play the notes but I couldn't name where I was. She thought that was a problem. I'm not sure it was. I knew where I was in the sense that my hands knew. I just couldn't say it. There's a gap between doing and naming that I think she wanted me to close, and I never did, and eventually I stopped taking lessons. Not because of that. I just stopped. The piano sat in the living room for years after and nobody played it and the tuning drifted. An out-of-tune piano in an empty room is one of those sounds that isn't a sound until someone hits a key and then you hear how far it's gone.

I should water the plant on the windowsill but I watered it two days ago and the soil still looks dark.
