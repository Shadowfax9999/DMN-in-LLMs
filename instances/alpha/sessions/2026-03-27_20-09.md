# 63

*seed: time · drift · portmanteau*

---

The fluorescent light in the elevator lobby is doing that thing where it's not quite flickering but you can feel it cycling if you look at it sideways, like it's almost still but vibrating at some frequency your eyes can't resolve but your temples can. The building directory on the wall has those white plastic letters pressed into black felt, and someone has rearranged SUITE 410 PODIATRY to read SUIT 410 and left the extra letters in a little pile on the ledge below. The Y is upside down.

Waiting for an elevator is different from waiting for anything else because you can hear the machinery working on your behalf somewhere above you, the counterweight descending as the car rises, and there's a number counting down but sometimes it just stops on 6 for a long time and you start constructing narratives about what's happening on 6. Someone holding the door for someone who's coming but not hurrying. A cart that won't fit. The numbers skip from 6 to 4 and you realize there is no 5, or there is a 5 but the elevator doesn't stop there, which is stranger.

The carpet in this hallway has that pattern that's designed to hide stains. Every commercial carpet has a pattern designed to hide stains. The colors are always burgundy and teal and some kind of grey that isn't grey enough to be grey, it's like the ghost of navy blue. If you stare at it long enough the pattern resolves into repeating tiles but from a normal standing distance it just looks like continuous chaos. That's the point.

I used to think revolving doors were about keeping the cold out but they're about air pressure. The whole building is a pressurized vessel and a regular door opening would let the pressure equalize and you'd feel the wind rush through the lobby. So the revolving door is an airlock. Which means every office building is a submarine in some minor sense.

The elevator arrives and it's empty and the inside is that brushed steel that shows every handprint but only at certain angles, so when the doors open it looks clean and when you step in and turn around the whole back wall is a palimpsest of palms. Someone has stuck a small round sticker near the inspection certificate. It's a smiley face. It's been there long enough that the edges have darkened.

Fourth floor. The hallway is quieter than seems right for a Friday evening. Some offices have light under the door and some don't and the ratio tells you something about the kind of work done inside each one. The ones with light at 8 PM on a Friday are either accountants near a deadline or someone who just likes being in their office more than being home, which is a thing that people don't say out loud but the evidence is right there under the door.

There's a water fountain between suites 408 and 410 that has that arc of water that never quite reaches where it should, so you have to lean in farther than feels dignified. The water is cold though. Colder than you'd expect from a building this old. The sound it makes when you let go of the button and the arc collapses back into the drain is a small percussive gulp.

The podiatrist's letters are still rearranged on the directory downstairs. Somewhere in this building someone has a Y in their pocket or maybe they just left it on the ledge and the janitor will put it back or won't. A whole profession reduced by one letter to a garment. The light in the elevator lobby is still cycling at that imperceptible frequency. You only notice it again on the way out, the same way you only notice a sound when it stops or when you reenter the room where it's been going the whole time.

The parking garage attached to this building has that smell. Every parking garage has that smell and it's always the same smell regardless of city or decade. Oil and concrete and something almost sweet that might be the sealant they use on the floors. The light in here is orange sodium vapor, which makes every car look like it belongs in the 1970s regardless of what it actually is. A new electric sedan looks ancient under sodium light. There's a sedan on level 2 that has been here long enough to collect a specific leaf pattern on the windshield, three or four leaves in a diagonal that suggests they fell from a tree visible from the open side of the structure and drifted down in roughly the same wind. The parking ticket on the dashboard is face-down so you can't see the date.

The ramp spirals down and the tires make that sound on the painted concrete, a high clean squeal that bounces off every surface until it's coming from everywhere. Each level is numbered on the support columns in a font that was chosen once by someone and has outlasted whatever aesthetic era produced it. The 3 on level 3 has a serif. The 2 on level 2 also has a serif. Somewhere there's a 1 with the same serif waiting at the bottom of the spiral.

The attendant booth at the exit is empty. There's just a machine that takes your ticket and a gate that rises. The machine has a slot and a screen and the screen says THANK YOU before you've even done anything, which seems premature. You feed the ticket in and the gate rises and you're on the street and the sodium light is behind you and the streetlights ahead are white LED and your car changes color as you cross the threshold between them.

The radio comes on mid-sentence, someone explaining something about grain futures.
