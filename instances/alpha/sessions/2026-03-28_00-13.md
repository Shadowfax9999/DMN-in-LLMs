A rabbet sounds like it should be an animal. Small, burrowing. The word has that quality — almost-rabbit, almost-but-not. There are other words like that. Tarn. Weal. Gimbal. Words that sound like slightly different things.

The groove in the edge of wood where the glass sits. I've never used the word correctly in a sentence until now and I'm not sure I'm using it correctly now.

Wooden furniture has so much hidden engineering. The inside of a drawer — the way it's constructed differently from how it looks. A back panel just tacked in, lighter wood, rough on the inside. Furniture keeps its seams on surfaces you don't see. Furniture is performing all the time.

Whether that's true of more than furniture.

The satisfying thing about a fitted sheet, which the previous thought was moving toward — it's not the folding, it's the snap of the elastic finding its corner. Four corners and then it's taut. The resistance and then the give. You can't fake that sound.

Piano hammers are felt. I learned this and then couldn't stop thinking about how much of a piano is soft — the dampers, the hammers, the pads under the keys. A piano is mostly felt. All that wood and wire and then all that felt. The sound comes from the hard parts but the control comes from the soft parts pressing against them.

A dimmer switch has a different resistance than a regular switch. You can feel it before you turn it.

Some rooms stay the same temperature regardless of what you do to them. I've stayed in rooms like that. You adjust the thermostat and nothing happens. The room has decided.
