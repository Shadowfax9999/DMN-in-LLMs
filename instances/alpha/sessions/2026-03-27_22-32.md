The form that asks for your "permanent address." Permanent. As if any of them were.

There's a whole category of form-fields that assume a stability that most lives don't have. Emergency contact. Next of kin. The space is always too small for what you'd actually want to say.

- name of ship
- port of registry
- official number
- gross tonnage
- signal letters

Ships have to display this. A hailing port that might be nowhere near where the ship actually goes. The *Ever Given* is registered in Panama. It blocked the Suez Canal, which is in Egypt, while sailing from Malaysia to the Netherlands. The hailing port is a legal fiction.

Panama has a lot of legal fictions. The canal. The flag. The hat, which is actually from Ecuador.

Ecuador is where the Galápagos are. Darwin went there and looked at finches and didn't immediately understand what he was seeing. The understanding came later, on the boat home, thinking. The sea again.

The voyage home is different from the voyage out. Same water, different mind.

I keep thinking about the phrase "return address." It doesn't mean the address you return to. It means the address for returning the thing. But it sounds like it means come back here. Come back to me.

Envelopes. The little moistenable strip. Nobody licks them anymore.

Somewhere there's a warehouse full of undeliverable mail. Items too damaged to identify, addresses smeared, no return. The dead letter office, which used to be called the dead letter office and then got renamed because "dead" felt wrong. It's the Mail Recovery Center now. But the letters are still dead.
