*seed: time · drift · unison*

Unison is a strange word because it contains the idea of one but you need at least two things to be in it. You can't be in unison by yourself. Or can you. Both hands lifting a box at the same time, that's something, but nobody calls it unison. It needs separateness first. A choir in unison is everyone singing the same note but the reason it sounds thick instead of just loud is that nobody's actually on the same frequency, everyone's a few cents off in different directions and the result is this shimmering that a single voice can't do. I sang in a choir for two years in college and the director kept saying "listen down" which meant listen to the people below your vocal range not above, and I never fully understood why that worked but it did, things locked in faster when you listened down.

My left hand is colder than my right hand right now. It's been like that all day. I'm sitting in the kitchen and the window is open and it's the first evening this year warm enough to leave it open past dinner, and there's a specific smell that is cut grass plus something floral plus the inside of the house all mixed, and every year I forget it and every year it comes back and I think oh right, this. There's a lawnmower going somewhere not close. Two blocks maybe. The pitch drops when it hits thick grass, you can hear the blade loading up. Friday evening mowing. Getting it done before the weekend actually starts.

The word "unison" is still sitting there. It hasn't connected to anything yet and that's fine. Sometimes a word just hangs around like someone at a party who showed up early and is standing near the drinks holding a cup. Unison. Uni. Son. One sound. I took French for a year and forgot almost all of it but I remember that "son" means sound and also "his" depending on context and that seemed like a design flaw at the time.

There's a cardinal calling from the tree at the corner of the yard. Two-note phrase, repeated, then a pause, then repeated. It's been doing this for about ten minutes. I keep almost counting the repetitions and then not. The lawnmower stopped. The cardinal is louder now, or seems louder, which is the same thing from where I'm sitting. There's a car with a broken exhaust somewhere on the next street over, I can hear it idling, that particular bubbling rattle. Someone is going to have to get that welded or replace the whole pipe and they're probably putting it off. I had a car once where the heat shield on the catalytic converter came loose and it buzzed at certain RPMs, this thin metallic rattle, and I drove it like that for eight months because it only happened between 1800 and 2200 RPM and I could just accelerate through it. The mechanic eventually zip-tied it. Took him thirty seconds.

The kitchen faucet drips if you don't push the handle all the way back. One drip every four or five seconds. I can hear it now under the cardinal. Three sounds layered: drip, bird, and something electrical, maybe the refrigerator compressor, maybe the light above the stove, a hum so constant it takes effort to notice. Unison would be all three on the same pitch but they're not, they're just coexisting, which is a different thing that doesn't have as clean a name. Coexistence. That's more of a political word. Adjacency. Simultaneity. None of those are right. There's probably a word in German. There's always a word in German.

The pollen must still be on the car. I didn't wash it. Tomorrow maybe. The line I drew with my finger is probably still there, this one clean stripe through the yellow, like a gap in a sentence. The cardinal just stopped mid-phrase, like it got interrupted by a thought.
