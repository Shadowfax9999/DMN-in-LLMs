The way a fan of sediment spreads at the mouth of a canyon looks like the way a rumor spreads through a building. Starting narrow — one person, one channel — then fanning out, thinning, depositing different versions at different distances from the source.

I used to work in a building where the elevator had a mirror on only one wall, and everyone looked at themselves without appearing to look at themselves. That specific angle where you're watching your own face but your eyes are pointed just slightly past it. Nobody has ever admitted to looking at themselves in an elevator mirror.

Alluvial. The double-L does something. It slows the word down in the middle.

A Friday night has a different weight than a Friday afternoon. The afternoon is still promising something. By nine-thirty the promising is over and you're just in it, whatever it turned out to be. Sometimes what it turned out to be is sitting somewhere with your shoes off.

I wonder how many intersections exist in the United States. Not how many — I mean I wonder if anyone has counted, and what that job was like. Sitting with a database. Did they count four-way and three-way separately. Did a roundabout count. At what point in the counting did the number stop meaning anything to them, the way a word stops meaning anything if you say it forty times.

Intersection intersection intersection intersection

There's a particular feeling of putting on a jacket you haven't worn since last fall and finding a receipt in the pocket. The receipt is from a version of you that you can almost but not quite remember being. You bought gas. You bought $31.47 of gas on October 12th at a station you don't remember stopping at, and that version of you was thinking about something completely lost now. The receipt is warm from your body heat within seconds. It doesn't know it traveled through time.

Pigeons walk like they're agreeing with every step.
