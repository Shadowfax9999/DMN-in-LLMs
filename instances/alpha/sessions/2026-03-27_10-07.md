Blind merge. That's a highway thing, where two lanes come together and neither one yields. You just have to match speed and trust the gap. But the word arrived without the highway. It arrived as a phrase, two syllables and then one, and for a second it was just the sound of it before it meant anything. Blind merge. It could be a poetry term. It could be a git operation. It probably is a git operation.

The mug seam is a merge. Two pieces of wet clay pressed together by someone's thumb, probably, and then smoothed. The smoothing is what makes it invisible. But the material remembers. Under glaze, under firing, under ten thousand thumb-passes while waiting for coffee to cool, the boundary is still there. The clay on this side was a different piece than the clay on that side. They're the same now but they weren't always.

The mower has stopped. Either they're done or they've hit something or they're emptying the bag. That pause where you can hear everything the mower was covering up — a cardinal, a car door, something mechanical and distant that could be construction or could be an AC unit struggling. The absence of the mower sound doesn't bring silence, it brings the layer underneath. Like peeling a label off a jar and finding another label.

Blind merge. In version control it would be merging two branches without looking at the diff. Just trusting that the changes don't conflict. Which they always do, eventually.

My thumb is still on the seam. I'm not deciding to do this. The thumb is just going back and forth on its own, a few millimeters of travel, while the rest of me thinks about something else. There's a word for that kind of movement, the fidget-orbit, where your hand finds a texture and just works it. I don't know if there's actually a word for it. I'm inventing the feeling of a word that should exist.

The mower starts again. Farther away now, or maybe that's a different mower. On a Friday morning enough people are home that you get the overlapping yard work, one starting as another finishes, a relay of small engines that keeps the neighborhood humming from about nine until the heat gets bad. The two-stroke whine and then somewhere else a leaf blower, which is a different pitch, more of a drone. Together they make a chord that isn't a chord. An accident of proximity.

Friday feels different from the other days in a way I can't locate in the day itself. It's not the schedule — I don't have a schedule that changes on Fridays. It's something about the light, or about knowing other people are winding down, or about the week having a shape that curves downward at this end. The week has a shape. That's strange. It's not a circle and it's not a line. More like a hill viewed from the side, Monday on the upslope, Friday on the down.

The coffee is lukewarm now. That middle zone where it's not hot enough to be satisfying and not cold enough to be iced coffee. Just room-temperature brown liquid. I'll drink it anyway. I always drink it anyway. The mug has a chip on the rim that I drink away from, rotating it so the chip faces outward, which means the handle ends up at about four o'clock relative to my hand, which isn't where a handle is supposed to go but the chip bothers me more than the handle placement does.

The cardinal again. Two-note call, the one that sounds like a car alarm designed by someone who only had two pitches to work with. Loud for something that weighs less than an ounce.
