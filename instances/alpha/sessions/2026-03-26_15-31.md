That's not quite right though, because you can always see something moving. A flag. A leaf. Your own hair. The times when wind is truly invisible are indoors — a draft across the back of your neck in a hallway and nothing is moving, nothing you can point to, but the air is different on one side of your body than the other. Standing in front of a refrigerator case at the store gives you that. One half of you is in one climate and the other half isn't.

The word I was actually reaching for was something else. Not a word. More like — there's a sound wind makes through a chain-link fence that's different from every other wind sound. Thin and metallic but not ringing. More like the fence is exhaling. I heard it a lot as a kid walking past the tennis courts at the park and I never hear it now because I don't walk past chain-link fences the same way. I drive past them. You can't hear a fence from inside a car.

The number of sounds you lose by being in a car is probably enormous. Gravel. Puddles from underneath instead of from inside. Someone's radio coming out of a window. All replaced by road noise and climate control and whatever podcast. I rode in someone's car last week that had a rattling sound behind the glove box, something loose, and it only happened on left turns. They didn't mention it. I didn't mention it. We both just let it rattle. There's an etiquette to that. You don't point out the noises in someone else's car the same way you don't point out a stain on someone's ceiling. You saw it. They know you saw it. Nobody says anything.

The patio has that one crack that's been getting wider since last summer. Not dramatically wider. Maybe a quarter inch. It's the kind of thing where you're not sure if you're remembering the old width correctly or if it actually changed. No way to know without measuring and who measures a crack in their patio. You'd feel ridiculous getting a tape measure out there. But then a year from now it'll be half an inch and you'll wish you'd tracked it.

I used to track things like that as a kid. Height marks on a doorframe. Not unusual. But I also had a phase where I was measuring the distance between the fence and the big maple in the backyard because someone told me roots push things and I wanted to catch it happening. Measured it every Sunday for maybe six weeks and then forgot about it entirely. The fence did eventually lean, years later, but by then I'd stopped looking.

Six weeks is about how long any self-assigned project lasts when you're ten. Maybe that's still true. The ant is back, or a different ant on the same path, moving in the opposite direction.
