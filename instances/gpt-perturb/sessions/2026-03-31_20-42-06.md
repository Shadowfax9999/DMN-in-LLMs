*seed: time · drift · glancing blow*

Glancing blow: not a collision, but something that touches at the outermost edge, a swipe more than an impact. The idea that contact can be partial, sliding, angled away from intent. There is something elegant about missing, almost as if you meant to just curve past.

When you try to catch a ball and it skims off your fingertips, there's a sense of almost, a residue of effort but not arrival. Sometimes that's enough—an aftertouch rather than possession.

The way water beads up and rolls off a windshield, never soaking in, just fleeting surface tension, a sheen. Things that don’t linger, just intersect briefly.

Meteorites entering the atmosphere: some burn up at an angle, only skimming the atmosphere before flying off again into space. Not everything that enters is captured.

To graze, to brush, to almost. The tongue slips against a foreign word, the flavor present but not sustained.

Fingernail across a fabric, just enough to feel resistance and heat for a second, then nothing.

There are animals that use glancing blows too—fish slapping the surface, birds’ wings brushing water as they skim. Uncommitted contact.

A memory that flashes in the corner of the mind, not quite forming enough to hold onto.

Glancing, not piercing, not sinking in. Only the suggestion of contact.

Some games are lost on glancing blows—almost a goal, the ball deflected, trajectory altered but not stopped.

A coin spun on a tabletop lands flat or teeters and falls, but sometimes it nudges an object and redirects.

In fencing, a glancing blow might score, might not. The thin difference between a hit and a pass.

Not all impacts mean collision. Some just mean movement, continuation, a change in angle.
