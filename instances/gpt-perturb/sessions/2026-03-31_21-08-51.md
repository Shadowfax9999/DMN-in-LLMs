# 77

*seed: time · drift · the way fluorescent lights make everyone look slightly ill*

---

The word “bleary” comes to mind. Not as a judgment, just as the surface of things under that vaporous hum. There are old photographs where the faces get this same plastic undertone, as if the light is coming from beneath the skin. Greasepaint, or a filter someone forgot to remove.

Hospitals, basements, gyms after hours. The way color drains from fabric—blue turns to dust, red turns to a sort of warning. Uncooked, underdeveloped, unready. There’s a shade that only appears under those lights, not quite green, not quite cream, the color of the back of hands late at night.

—

Some people seem immune—maybe it’s the undertones or the way their hair throws off reflections. Or maybe it’s attitude; some people look alive no matter how the bulbs flicker overhead.

Pet stores have the harshest of all. Fish tanks glowing inaccurately, parrots shifting nervously on acrylic rods, the smell of pine shavings and stale air. Crickets clattering in a plastic tub, invisible except for the movement.

I don’t remember ever seeing a fluorescent bulb flicker back to life after the first stutter. They just die, and someone replaces them, and the next one is exactly the same.

—

The doctor’s waiting room had those lights, and the magazines always had the same covers year after year, curling at the corners, the colors washing out: a woman in athletic wear with a grin that doesn’t belong to the room.

The fern by the window wasn’t supposed to be there, but someone must have thought it helped.

—

There’s a difference between the light in a store at midnight and the one in a school hallway after everyone’s left. The silence in a store hums with compressors and the slow roll of a distant mop. The school hallway feels like any movement would be a violation, like the light itself is keeping watch.

—

The term “cool white” never describes the feeling.
