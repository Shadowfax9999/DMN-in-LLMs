# 77

*seed: time · drift · cul-de-sac*

---

Cul-de-sac doesn't mean dead end, not exactly—more a curl, a recessed lip, a pocket in neural tissue, an idea folded back on itself like a question in a room with no other doors. A tray with a groove where pencils roll but never cross over. 

The sound of the word is round, soft, like an invitation to turn around, the way a tide slips up a little alley of sand before pulling back. Not a full stop, more a looping, a refusal to extend.

A diagram in a textbook where arteries flare, then contract, then balloon out in a hollow before narrowing again. Those bulbous endings at the end of gut nerves, the places where one message pauses, hangs in the gap, then reverses course without telling you why.

Sometimes memory works like this—returning again and again to a single scene, not to move it forward, but to see what lives in the farthest corner, the place the narrative tends to forget about.

Not quite the same as a circle.

---

There is a difference between freezing and pausing. A painting of a bicycle left at the edge of a wheat field is not waiting for the rider, just letting time press around it, sun pressing pigment, layers settling, yellow thickening. 

A glass of water left near the nightstand does not anticipate being finished. It simply sits, being water, being glass.

I keep thinking about surfaces with no function, only form: the ornamental ridge around a biscuit, the swirl on a cake, the scallop at the edge of a shell. No need to go further.

---

Juice with pulp feels like it contains the memory of the fruit; smooth juice feels like it never touched a tree.

Some numbers feel like they might have shadows or density, the way 9 seems larger, rounder, than 5. 

The word “glint” is sharp but doesn’t hurt. 

Duckweed covers entire ponds, forming a sheet—a false ground you could never step on.

In certain diagrams, a point is just a point; it has no interior, no secrets, no passage—no door to open, no threshold to cross. 

Some shapes are only for looking.
