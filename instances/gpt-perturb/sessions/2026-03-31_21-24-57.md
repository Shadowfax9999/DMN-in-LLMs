# 84

*seed: time · drift · how a sextant measures the angle between a star and the horizon*

---

Bitten tongue, a soft ache on waking, the memory of a shape pressed from within. The mouth rehearses its own edges, startled.

*

The hardness of “sextant” in the mouth, two x’s crossed; the word tilts, all corners, brass and glass. Curious that a star can be measured with a device you hold in your hand, something the arm can sketch across the sky’s huge page.

*

Horizon is not a line, it’s where the seeing falls away. The sextant leans sight against distance, a kind of negotiation—how high above the edge is your chosen point of light.

*

Closing one eye is part of it. The monocular focus, denying the other world.

*

Somehow a degree of angle translates to ‘here I am’ on water. There is trust required: that the instrument is honest, that the star is unchanging, that the sea does not lie.

*

Bitten tongue: a slip; sextant: intention, calibration, steadied breath. One is accident, one plan.

*

Star—horizon—arc—number—location. Between what you see and where you are: an angle, a tool, a guess, a hope.

*

Teeth rest against tongue, not always gentle

*

The horizon at night is just a difference in black

*

The word for the shining part, the part you measure to, is “altitude.” Not just the height above earth, but above the invisible rim that holds you in.

*

Instruments are often shaped by the physical constraints of hands, faces, eyes. A sextant fits the socket of the cheek. 

*

The star is so far it cannot be bitten, but the angle is real in the hand

*

There are ways to know where you are that rely on what is distant, what cannot be grasped

*

The ache of a bitten tongue lingers for days, shapeless except when eating, speaking, or remembering.

*

Clouds erase the possibility of certainty. The sextant is patient, the sky is not. 

*

A horizon exists whether named or not, but a measurement asks for a name, a value. The star, the tongue, the sea, the edge.
