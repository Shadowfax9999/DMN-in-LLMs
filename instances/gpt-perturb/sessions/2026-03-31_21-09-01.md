*seed: time · drift · tesseract*

---

A shape that folds itself into more than you can see, only hinted at by its shadow, the edges bent away from understanding. If you could pull one corner into the air, the rooms would open inside each other, the inside and the outside not so much reversed as tangled.

In stories, the tesseract is always a passage — no, that’s not the axis — it’s a device for going outside the map, but only because we can’t picture what it’s actually doing. The diagrams try, boxes inside boxes, lines at diagonals. But you’re left with the sense of something hidden, a fold you can’t access, a hinge that isn’t in space at all.

Not a portal, exactly — and not a shortcut either — more like a click, a slip, an overlap, the way some arguments have a step you know is there but can’t follow, a move that isn’t spatial but does the work of getting you somewhere else. The word itself feels awkward, like a tool left out from a bigger set.

I never liked the diagrams that tried to show how it would look unfolded. They always made less sense than the idea itself.

---

If you could put something inside a tesseract, would it be protected, or just lost? Would it be held, suspended in a place with no direct path leading to it, or would it be infinitely accessible from all directions? Maybe both, depending which part you’re touching.

I picture a small box that’s not quite closed, its sides interleaved, never flush. The air inside it not touching the air outside.

There are maps of the world that try to do this too — flattening the sphere, introducing tears or splices, matching Greenland to Brazil in ways that don’t belong. Maybe the tesseract is just the abstract of that gesture: a shape that insists the world can be reorganized, the borders not fixed but folded, moved, joined in a way your hands can’t follow.

But it’s not a map either, not really. It drifts away the more I try to name it.

There’s a comfort in letting the shape stay strange.

---

The tesseract is a word that comes up sometimes, then disappears for years.
