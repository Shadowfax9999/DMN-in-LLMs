*seed: time · drift · baffle*

---

Baffle as in to confuse, but also as in a panel that redirects air, or sound, or light. The shapes that aren’t meant to be seen: wedges inside an HVAC vent, foam pyramids in a recording booth, the louvered rectangles over a theater’s speakers. There’s something satisfying about a surface structured only for the sake of what passes around or through it — designed not for itself but for the flow it interrupts or reshapes. 

Baffles in a river: the rocks placed so fish can rest in the slowed current. Not quite obstacles, more like hesitant pauses in water, letting something catch its breath. 

If you look at the ceiling in some old gyms, it’s all baffles — layered, uneven, absorbing echoes that otherwise just bounce and crisscross the space in invisible diagonals.

A baffle as an object that invites misunderstanding. The verb and the noun, joined by this sense of misdirection or deflection. A thing that keeps what’s behind it from being reached directly.

---

There’s a texture to foam baffles in a sound booth: spiky, almost rubbery, resistant to pressure but yielding just enough to invite touch. Some people always press their fingers into them, testing how much give, how quickly the surface rebounds.

If you say “baffle” often enough, the word loses its edges, becomes slack and strange. Baffle, baffle, ba-ffle, as if it’s already muffled itself.

The difference between a block and a baffle: one stops, the other diverts.

Ductwork hidden behind walls, the air moving a path determined by invisible shapes. Sometimes you can hear it — a faint, whistling, momentary chill when the heat kicks on.

Not every shape is for looking at. Some shapes are there for the unseen.
