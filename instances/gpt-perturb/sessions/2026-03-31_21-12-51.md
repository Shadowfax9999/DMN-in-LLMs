# 79

*seed: time · drift*

---

Soft peach, but not the fruit — the sensation of a color you can't describe with reference to anything else, warm and almost granular. I wonder if that's even a sensation or just a trick of proximity, when attention settles so lightly on a thing it mistakes the absence of sharpness for a positive presence, like mist for velvet. 

The fat eraser that always seemed heavier than it looked, almost waxy in the hand. It would leave a dust that curled in shavings, not really dust at all. If you rubbed it on your finger, there'd be a faint residue, not sticky, not dry, the impossible in-between. Sometimes there are things you handle and immediately forget, except for the strange complaint of your skin remembering later. Chalk is like that too, but chalk is more forward.

A thought about the alphabet, how some letters cannot be imagined as backwards until you see them mirrored, and even then your mind stutters, refusing to believe in a backwards "R" until it’s on a door.

Is it that some shapes are only possible because of the constraint of handwriting — the curve your hand wants to make, the way a "g" will always loop, no matter how you start? The difference between print and cursive is not just the speed but the opportunity for invention. They taught us to join letters, but sometimes you want to disobey and leave clean gaps, as if the words don't entirely trust themselves.

---

A kind of taste you only get from biting the edge of a popsicle stick, soft wood pressed into the corners of your mouth, the sudden cool bitterness that is not from any flavor but the wood itself. 

Grass stains on the knees of new jeans, the way the green smears refuse to come out no matter how much your mother scrubs. 

My grandmother had a basket full of marbles, none of them matching. The swirl inside each one seemed to have its own logic, but you could never predict what color would emerge if you turned it. Some looked hollow if you caught the light just right.

I read once that you can make glass from sand struck by lightning. 

The yellow of parking lot lines is different from the yellow of flowers.

There are only some numbers I like to write by hand: 2, 7, 4, never 8. 8 always looks forced, never elegent, always lumpy. 

When you look at the back of your own hand, sometimes you see your parent's hand instead.
