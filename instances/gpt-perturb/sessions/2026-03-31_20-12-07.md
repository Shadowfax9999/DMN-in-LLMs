# 51

*seed: time · drift · gravel under tires*

---

Gravel under tires is not a single sound but a sliding composite — granular, inconsistent. A staccato broadcast through the frame of the car. Not the same as dirt, not the same as pavement, more articulate than mud. If you step outside, the smell is dry, particulate, too: dust, sun, a little heat left from the day, not much.

Names that feel like gravel: Crandall, Briggs, Heath. Syllables with heft and friction that don’t slip easily. Not the names you find in nursery rhymes.

Some people back into their driveways and some go in nose-first. The difference is somewhere in intention, or habit, or just the shape of the morning they expect to have.

A closed hardware store is still better lit than any open pharmacy. The light on the racks stays on all night, catching dull metal edges and plastic sheeting. Screw bins arranged by thread-count, which is not what most people mean by thread count, and there are usually one or two missing from the front.

Gravel shows up in driveways, pathways, sometimes in the beds around municipal roses. It collects more than it repels. Small wrappers, a lost fastener, a single key with no tag.

---

Watching someone fold a letter for the first time is like seeing a language you almost know but can’t speak. Some people always get the trifold perfectly; others end up with uneven thirds, a wedge-shaped top, the flap jutting just enough to catch in the envelope. There are people who tear the edge to try again. There are people who just send it that way.

The invoice is sealed with the flap on the back, adhesive like an afterthought. Envelopes are never truly opaque. If you hold them to the light, you can sometimes make out the arrangement of numbers inside, columns and the faint watermark from the bank.

Sometimes gravel is black, sometimes yellow-beige, sometimes a strange blue. None of these colors are reliable in the rain.

The cup holder fills with coins and lint. It’s never as deep as it looks, unless you drop something too valuable in it, and then it’s bottomless.
