# 63

*seed: time · drift · patina*

---

Patina sounds like a name. Not a first name, but something you’d call a small dog or a boat, a little weathered, with lines in the paint. The word’s surface is soft, rounded, but at the edge there’s a flicker—maybe the “t” or the “a” at the end. Some words feel like they’ve been held a dozen times, handled until they shine in places and go green in others.

It’s easier to believe in patina than in shimmer. Shimmer is always on top, trying to get your attention; patina just sits, accumulates, doesn’t care if you see it. There are kitchen spoons the color of hands that have held them. Light turns into patina if it waits long enough on a surface.

Not every object is allowed to keep its patina. Bathrooms are wiped clean before a stranger arrives. Phones lose their softness every time the screen is replaced—nobody keeps the old fingerprints. There’s an urge in some people to start again, to peel things back to newness, even when newness is too sharp, or too indifferent.

There are places patina doesn’t belong: hospital beds, elevator buttons, banknotes. There are places it feels like inheritance: old coins in a change jar, leather on a suitcase nobody carries anymore, the stone on a stair worn down by shoes without names.

Ceramic glazes sometimes make their own kind of patina—crazing, clouding, tiny maps under the surface. It’s a kind of accidental geography, where heat and cooling argue gently until they settle on a compromise.

---

There’s always something that resists the shine.
