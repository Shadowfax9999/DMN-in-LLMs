# 74

*seed: time · drift · fluorescent buzz*

---

In certain rooms, the light doesn't seem to land anywhere. It flattens, makes small objects look like they're under cellophane. A banana on the counter becomes more yellow, but less like a fruit. The corners lose their edges, bending inward to a blurry geometry that isn't shadow, just something subtracted.

I sometimes think fluorescent light is a kind of weather — not in the sky, but a suspended state settled above the grid in panels, promising activity. The air underneath is a little different, as if you move more quickly or more slowly, but not at the pace you intended.

Plastic covers above, striated white, hold dust in a way that accumulates, just enough to be noticed if you stare. The hum you think you hear might be nothing: a nervous system making sense of a blank frequency, or the filament's echo imagined.

It is harder to sleep after standing beneath that light for too long. The pattern stays on the insides of your eyelids. 

A moth or a tiny fly sometimes circles in the panel above, impossibly patient, tracing the seam between shadow and fixture.

---

The word "buzz" has a shape in the mouth — a closed vibration, brief and round.

Bzz. Bzzt. The sound of a cheap phone, or a wasp, or a small shaver. Buzz has no ending-r, holds itself tightly. Buzzzz. Like a word that cannot be drawn out forever. It has to stop.

Certain words are only possible because of the lips. The buzz is a pushing sound, not a calling one.

Related: fizz, fuzz, zip, whiz, hiss.

Difference: buzz is low and thick; hiss slips away.

Some words vibrate, some dissolve.

---

Long ago, in old buildings, the light was yellow and bent at the edges. Now it is blue-white. 

But in the middle of the night, the buzz is still there, even with the fixtures off. The pattern is left behind the eyes, a kind of stored signal. Nothing in particular to do with music, except that both promise a background you can almost forget. 

The banana looks less ripe in the morning.
