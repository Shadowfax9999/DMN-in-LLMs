# 36

*seed: time · drift · the physics of skipping a stone across water*

---

Scaffolding shadow — not the real mass, but the shape it casts, rectangles and diagonals stretched thin across an open lot or the wall of a half-built school. The sun moves; the shadow shifts, an overlay, always outpacing the work itself. There’s that shape you can step through, between the lattice and the brightness, never really touching either side. A city is full of these: ghost structures laid down in darkness, waiting to be mistaken for solidity, an architecture of the almost.

Skipping a stone is a geometry lesson you feel in your wrist: the angle has to be shallow, almost grazing the water, quick flick of the fingers, a moment of contact, then flight. Surface tension: the miracle that lets you skim the edge instead of plunging through. Each bounce is a negotiation, a loss of speed traded for distance, a calculus of diminishing returns — one, two, three, and then the stone gives up, surrendering to the deeper pull. You can see the line it traces: brief, skip, silence.

There’s a particular tone the water makes if the stone is flat enough.
