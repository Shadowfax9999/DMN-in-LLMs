How to picture the inside of a combination lock:

1. Imagine a set of discs, all stacked together, each with a notch cut in its edge. The notches do not point the same way until the right numbers are dialed.
2. There is a spindle running through these discs. Turning the dial spins the spindle, which rotates all the discs at once—except when the spindle disengages each disc one at a time.
3. For the first number, you turn the dial several times to clear the discs, then land on the number so the first disc’s notch aligns somewhere hidden deep inside the lock.
4. Reverse direction. Turning back picks up the next disc, moving its notch toward alignment. You do this for each number in the sequence, each pass stacking notches like the slices of a slot that must line up to let the lock open.
5. When assembled, all the notches are hidden, unreachable, irrelevant. Only when every dialed number is correct do the notches line up to create a gap—a window, invisible except to the lock’s own mechanics.
6. Now a bar, blocked for every wrong combination, slips smoothly through the open slot. The hasp rises free.
7. To close, spin the dial once: the discs scatter, notches hidden away again, ready for the sequence to assemble order from their jumble.

How to tell whether the band-aid is new:

1. Press the wrapper. It resists, the paper crinkling with the sealed air pocket inside.
2. Flip it over—there’s a notch in the edge of the wrapper, a sign to tear.
3. Tear, peel: the faint resistance of the adhesive liner, the slight drag as it leaves the pad.
4. Unwrap, expose the pad. The surface is matte, almost dry. If it shines or feels slick, it’s not a band-aid now, just a film of plastic.
5. Hold the band-aid by the ends, flex. If it creases sharply, it’s new; if it crumples or sticks to itself, it’s already been used or exposed.

How to forget the numbers of a combination lock:

1. Wait a year, maybe longer.
2. Try the first number, feel wrongness.
3. Stop and think: there’s a shape in your hand, the dial’s cold face, but the numbers come up blank.
4. Write the numbers down if you remember them now, before they vanish.
5. Let the paper with the numbers fall behind a drawer or slip inside a book.

---

Do not chew the band-aid. Do not reapply it to another wound. Do not try to peek behind the lock’s face for the numbers—each disc hides its notch until the sequence is right, and no finger can find the gap.
