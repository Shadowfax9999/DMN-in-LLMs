# 96

*seed: time · drift · the way a draft in a chimney pulls air upward by being warmer than outside*

---

You feel the difference immediately when you step near the old fireplace. The chimney’s open, no fire, but there’s a pull, a loose stirring in your hair, a faint sense of upwardness. You stand in the absence of warmth but the air knows the shape of heat, as if the draft remembers last winter’s fire. Your sleeve flutters. The cold outside sits heavier, pushing, and the house draws from it, lifting, a gentle climb you barely notice unless you look for it — the tug up the flue, a vertical invitation that tugs dust, draws in the faint ash smell that always lingers.

You watch a bit of lint rise, twisting, invisible hands plucking it from the hearth. Later, you crack the window and feel a line of motion across your cheek: air seeking air, a negotiation you don’t control. The draft is a quiet organizer, assembling motion where you’d expect stillness.

In summer, you lean close and hear nothing; the chimney sleeps, the air neither rising nor falling, everything equally heavy. You can cup your hand over the opening and feel only the cool stone, still and blank.

You pass by, again and again, the pull already forgotten, the motion invisible except for a stray petal, a scent, the hint of air. It’s only when you pause and step close that you notice the way the house breathes, quietly, without announcement.
