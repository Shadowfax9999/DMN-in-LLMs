# 70

*seed: time · drift · the grain of cheap plywood*

---

The surface is smooth but not silent. There’s a shallow ripple beneath the pressed-on veneer, a faint geometry hidden just under the skin. Sometimes you can find it with the pad of a thumb — a patch that gives, or feels hollow, or seems to whisper “not-tree” in a language made of pressure.

Cheap plywood always smells faintly dusty even when it’s new. The edges splinter if you run a pencil down them the wrong way.

If you trace an imaginary rectangle on the tabletop, you can almost feel the places where different sheets overlap beneath the finish, like mismatched seams in a borrowed shirt.

The word “grain” wants to suggest something natural, a memory of forests, but here it’s more like a story being retold wrong, then lacquered against forgetting.

A coin will skate farther across a plywood desk than a real wood one — less friction, less drag, movement that doesn’t catch.

There are always tiny divots where something sharp has pressed too hard, never quite returning to smooth. A dense constellation, easy to miss unless the light comes in at an angle.

From underneath, the screws hesitate in soft material, holding but never with complete certainty.

Nobody ever makes a family heirloom out of plywood.
