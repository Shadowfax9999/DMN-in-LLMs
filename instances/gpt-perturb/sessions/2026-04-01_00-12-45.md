# 80

*seed: time · drift · the Doppler effect on a passing ambulance*

---

—past the corner, so the pitch drops, and my chest is still pressed with the memory of the siren’s approach, something that rides up your throat even after the sound is gone. The Doppler effect doesn’t feel like a theory; it’s the place where urgency stretches and slips away. The nearest comparison might be the sensation of a word on the tip of the tongue—the shape you almost have, but which is already retreating as you reach.

The ambulance’s color isn’t visible in the dark, only its lights, spinning pools on the uneven walls, blue and red that don’t blend but take turns. The face at the window—if there is one—wouldn’t see any of this, just the shock of movement, the world being parted by the sound.

There are other things that change as they pass you:
—a train’s horn, wrapped in distance
—the echo of bicycle spokes on wet pavement, sharper as they approach, then fading into nothing
—the shadow of someone running, flickering under a streetlamp, growing and shrinking in ways your eyes can’t map to footsteps

But the ambulance is different because it carries the possibility of news, the sense that behind the glass someone is both gone and arriving. The change in pitch is the world’s way of marking that you were inside the story for a moment, and now you’re not.

Sometimes the siren bleeds into a dream, even after you’re awake.

There’s always a silence that isn’t exactly silence when it’s moved away.
