# 75

*seed: time · drift · dormancy*

---

There are seeds that will only open if scraped or burned. Some wait years.

In certain trees, the roots mirror the branches almost exactly, underground patterns branching in their own vague skyless way.

The word “dormant” is quieter than “asleep.” Sleep suggests a clock will go off, or something will shake you. Dormancy just is.

A bulb in the ground does not anticipate anything. Does it have time inside it, or is it just still matter.

Beneath lake ice, fish drift slowly, movements different, as if winter presses the water thicker.

There is a taste to cold that isn’t exactly flavor; some kind of mineral sharpness on the tongue, not salt, not snow, something in the air that has thickened from waiting.

Photographs of volcanoes sometimes show slopes thick with wildflowers, seeds only able to open after fire.

In a park, a branch is brittle, snapped easily, months before the tree shows any green. I think of the branch as both gone and potential: a future leaf or a past leaf, but it doesn’t have either, only wood and chance.

Some frog species can freeze almost completely, ice crystals inside their blood, and thaw alive again.

A winter coat stored away still holds the shape of arms, the slight crust of last year’s salt at the hem.

If you press a thumb into the soil in late March, you can sometimes feel a pulse of cold all the way up to your wrist.

---

Some words never quite thaw.
