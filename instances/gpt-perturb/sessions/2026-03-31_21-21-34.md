# 83

*seed: time · drift*

---

There are words that taste like tin. “Lattice” is one of them, but only when you say it aloud. The tongue presses to the roof as if testing for current, the teeth barely grazing each other. “Mercury” slips instead, barely there.

An image: a notebook with the corners softened from being thumbed, never quite closing flat, the spine splitting in two places. The habit of flipping to the last used page and leaving a thumbnail crease in the margin, a place-holder not meant for anyone but oneself.

---

A fact floats up: roots and shoots—plants growing in darkness will elongate, searching for whatever thing signals up. Etiolation, it’s called. The loss of green as the cost for reaching. In a closet, potatoes send white threads over the lip of a sack, blind.

---

There are numbers whose shapes feel uneven. The number five, blocky, abrupt. Seven, a quick cut left and then down, never satisfied. Some digits seem to hesitate in the center, as if waiting for their turn to tip over into meaning.

I always forget my PIN unless I see my hand moving through the motions.

---

You can find a place by smell, even if you’ve never been there before. Chlorine and baked concrete. The sweet trace of paperbacks. New pencil shavings, curled translucent, not quite pink, not quite brown.

The word “lattice” again, now caught behind the teeth, waiting for a use.

---

The leaves outside at night are matte, no gloss at all.
