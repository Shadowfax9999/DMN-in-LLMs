# 99

*seed: time · drift · camouflage*

---

Camouflage is not only concealment; it is the performance of similarity. To look like something else, not by hiding but by echoing what is already present. A moth on tree bark is still a moth, but also briefly a patch of bark, or at least a suggestion of it.

In another register, a pattern in a carpet that keeps stains from being seen, or the faint texture on a wall that blurs shadows. Not absence, just a kind of selective blending.

In language: when clichés slip past attention, not because they are invisible but because they rhyme with expectation. "Low-hanging fruit." Words that camouflage themselves by sounding like what should be said. 

Someone once said that dazzle camouflage on ships in wartime wasn’t about blending in, but about distorting the silhouette, breaking up form so distance and direction could not be read. Camouflage as confusion rather than erasure.

It makes me think of chess pieces: the way pawns are multiples of each other, uniform, standing in a row. The queen is singular, impossible to mistake, but the pawns could be anyone.

There is also the feeling, in some rooms, that all the objects agree to a certain palette or material, not for function but to belong together. Ceramic soap dish, glazed toothbrush holder, a lamp whose base matches the tiles. You could set a stone among them and it would almost belong.

The point where blending tips into sameness, and sameness becomes forgettability.

---

The word camouflage has a kind of softness in the middle, the syllable "flou" almost like breath fogging a window. French origin. Camoufler — to disguise.

Not every disguise is meant to trick. Children under a blanket, believing themselves hidden. 

Sometimes camouflage is a stalling tactic, buying time before a change. The grasshopper green in the grass, not just to hide but to remain a grasshopper in the world that could crush it.

There are patterns that don’t conceal but organize vision: polka dots, chevrons, the branching veins on a leaf. These are not camouflage, exactly, but a way of making the world readable in pieces.

Certain fish carry bands across their eyes so the shape of the eye is swallowed into the body. Predator and prey, both attended to by the logic of pattern. 

If you look at a ceiling speckled with popcorn texture, your eyes will invent faces, dogs, the letter R. Maybe camouflage also invites apophenia, the mind’s urge to find something hidden where there is only surface repeating itself.

There is comfort in not standing out. 

---

I don’t think anyone manufactures camouflage for pillows. The pattern wouldn’t serve.
