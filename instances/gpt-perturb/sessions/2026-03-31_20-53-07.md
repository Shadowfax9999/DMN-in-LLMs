# 71

*seed: time · drift · shim*

---

shim: a thing that fills a gap. Or maybe not fills, but occupies — a sliver between things not quite aligned, a wedge that disappears as soon as the fit is right. Materials of shims: cardboard, folded paper, slices of plastic, the odd hunk of broken tile, coiled foil, a coin pressed tight. Always provisional. A repair that isn’t quite a repair, more an insistence on “good enough.” If you remove a shim, something always shifts, sometimes invisibly, sometimes with a sudden drop or tilt.

There’s a kind of confidence in knowing where to shim — that moment when the table rocks and you know which leg needs tucking, or when the window frame won’t close and you slip in something thin, the slide of it just enough to quiet the rattle.

Shim: also a verb some places. To shimmy, to wedge, to coax things together with friction and patience.

A box of odd shims is like a museum of emergency solutions. None of them labeled, none of them meant for their purpose — a bit of floorboard, a folded receipt, part of a matchbook cover.

---

In geometric construction, there’s no shim — the triangle closes perfectly, the compass arc meets the line, an ideal world with no tolerance for error. But in real carpentry, nothing joins so neatly; every fit is a negotiation.

---

Some words are only used when things aren’t square, when there’s a gap, when the plan doesn’t quite work out. Shim is one of those. I can’t think of a situation where you add a shim if you don’t need it. It’s never ornamental.

When the fridge rocks, you could adjust the feet, but sometimes it’s just faster to kick a shim under the corner and call it done.

---

There’s a kind of peace in knowing the desk will never be perfectly level, but it also won’t topple, as long as there’s a shim where it matters.

A drawer that closes right because someone, years ago, folded the corner of a cereal box and tucked it into place.

A door that swings true for a season, shimmed with a splinter of pine.

The repair is invisible, but the comfort is real.
