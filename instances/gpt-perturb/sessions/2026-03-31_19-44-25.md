# 40

*seed: time · drift · how sourdough starter maintains a stable microbiome*

---

Is there such a thing as a sourdough kazoo, or would the bubbles get trapped and muffle the vibration, or would the living mass produce its own kind of resonance? How does sourdough starter hold itself together if every time you feed it, you are both diluting and renewing it, pouring off some, mixing in flour and water, yet it stays—what, exactly: itself, or just a pattern of activity that feels like an identity? Do the yeasts argue with the lactobacilli, or is it more of an ongoing exchange, division of labor, arms race, truce? How many microbes is enough for a stable population—when does tipping occur, when does a starter become something else, or sour, or fail, or simply refuse to bubble? Is there a vanished thread, or do the new arrivals just learn to behave, to sour and leaven as they should, until the flavor becomes familiar again?

Could you lose the thread in a bowl of dough, hand stuck to the sticky mass, unable to detach, pulling and folding until you think: is there any of the original left, or is this all the accumulation of days, invisible generations nested in each fold? Why does the crust remember so perfectly the shape of the last rise—why do bubbles leave those open cells, like a map of vanished air? If you sing near the dough, will it rise more, or do the microbes ignore sound, vibrating to their own intracellular hum?

Is there a starter somewhere that tastes like the city it came from—can you taste rain, dust, human skin, dog fur, in the sourness, if you linger on the aftertaste? Why is it that the best bread sometimes comes from the oldest, most abused jar—forgotten in the back of a fridge, revived with a little flour and hope, rising anyway?

If you handed someone a slice and said: the golden thread runs through here—would they taste it, or would they just ask for butter, waiting for the story to end?
