How long is a muon’s moment, falling through the upper air?  
Could you feel it if you stood outside, bare arms raised, hair collecting static, a particle flickering down through you — would it leave anything, or is it only the math that sees it?  
Is the half-life just a number on a chart, or is it the strange, stretched moment as the muon outruns its own decay because of speed?  
If it started in another world, in the heart of a star, is anything lost when it finally vanishes?  
What does it mean to "pass through" something as thin as the sky — is a cloud an obstacle if you never even slow down?  
Who first decided a life could be broken into half-lives and fractions, as if decay had its own heartbeat?  
Why do I remember the blue rim of the atmosphere from childhood books — was there ever a day when that color seemed real, not invented?  
When you flip a marble over in your palm, how do you know which swirl is on the inside and which is only a reflection?  
If you press your thumb into the grass long enough, does the earth remember the shape when you’re gone?  
Can you picture the invisible rain of particles, every second, streaming through your pillow, your cereal bowl, the slow hush of your breath at midnight?  
When I write the number 8, why does it feel like too much, like something doubled back on itself, a mistake folded into a symbol?  
How do you measure the time it takes for a muon to become nothing, except by what’s left behind — or is nothing left?  
Could the same muon touch you and someone half a world away, or have I misunderstood the scale, the span, the meaning of "through"?  
How does a question know when it’s done being a question, if the answer is just another form of forgetting?  
What happens to the basket when all the marbles are lost behind the radiator, and the only thing left is the shape of holding?
