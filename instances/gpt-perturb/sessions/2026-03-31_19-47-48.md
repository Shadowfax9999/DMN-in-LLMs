# 41

*seed: time · drift*

---

The word “spring” is used for both a season and a stored force. I used to imagine springs as alive, coiled animals ready to snap. Coiling a ribbon around a pencil feels gentler, deliberate, no snap. There are clockwork toys that move only as long as the spring inside them unwinds—distance traveled is a function of woundness, not fuel.

A rubber band stretches, but doesn’t keep time. There’s a shape your hand makes when you roll a pen between your fingers and let it tap back into your palm, the same nervous loop, unconsciously rehearsed. Some people drum with their thumbs against the table, five taps, pause, five taps, as if waiting for the next thing.

The pause between two questions is empty, but also full of what you might have said, the things that could have happened. Some pauses feel sticky, like honey on a spoon, refusing to drop. Others evaporate, almost before you noticed them begin.

Is there a kind of silence that has flavor? Not the sharpness of anticipation, but the flat metallic taste when no one wants to be the first to speak.

---

All the various ways people fill time when they don’t want to wait:

1. Clearing throats.
2. Retyping a message just to watch the typing dots vanish and reappear.
3. Changing tabs, refreshing nothing.
4. Tapping feet, sometimes both feet, sometimes out of sync.
5. Picking at the corner of a label, peeling it down the seam.
6. Rehearsing answers silently.
7. Squinting at a distant object and pretending to be absorbed.
8. Re-reading old texts, even the pointless ones.
9. Checking the weather for a different city.
10. Counting ceiling tiles in sets of four.

None of these speed time, but sometimes they spread it out, make it feel less dense, like stretching dough.

---

The calendar on the wall is always one day behind.
