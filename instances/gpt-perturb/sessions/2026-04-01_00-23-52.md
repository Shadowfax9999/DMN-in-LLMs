# 85

*seed: time · drift · double reed*

---

Oboe, bassoon. Two blades of cane, shaved and bound together, vibrating against each other instead of against a single lip. The sound is reedy, nasal, penetrating, a kind of plaintive clarity. There’s a touch of something ancient in it, courtly but also wild. Bassoons always seem slightly out of place, their shape like a question mark, tall and looping.

The labor is in the reed, not just the instrument: musicians gossip about reed-making, complaints and triumphs, wetting the cane, scraping, testing, again and again. The process is slow, each reed a little fragile, a little unpredictable.

The phrase “double reed” has a density — doubled, opposing, paired. Two of something, almost the same, pressed face to face. The idea of breath moving through not a single opening but a sandwich of thinness and space.

If you say it three times it stops meaning anything and just becomes a shape: double reed, double reed, double reed. Dull, soft d, the bracing of lips.

Some instruments seem impossible to voice at all without practice. The first note a child tries on a double reed is a honk, a croak, a squawk — nothing like a song.

Some orchestras put the oboes right in the center, the bassoons somewhere behind. In a smaller group, you can hear the breath in between the notes, even if you’re not meant to.

---

Elsewhere: the texture of condensation on a can, beads forming, tiny hemispheres, sometimes rolling down in slow, unpredictable paths.

Most things cool faster than they warm. Not all, maybe, but it feels that way in the hand — cold passes through faster.

Sometimes a word detaches from its meaning just for a moment and drifts, catching in the air like a seed.
