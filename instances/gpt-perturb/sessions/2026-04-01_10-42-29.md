# 95

*seed: time · drift · envelope gum*

---

Envelope gum, unremarkable phrase, slightly formal, slightly antiquated. Gum arabic, old recipes — a timeslip to when letters mattered. The tongue knows its taste before memory retrieves it: metallic, bland, faintly bitter, a residue left behind lips.

Without looking: pith, rind, marrow, sap, all interior and border materials, protective or connective. Not the main event, but essential. Banana bread crust, envelope gum, rind under the nail, glue that closes what shouldn't open in transit.

There are industrial adhesives with names that sound like minerals or ancient trade goods. Isinglass, shellac, rosin, gum tragacanth. Each one felt necessary once.

---

Yellow legal pads never tear cleanly. The perforation slants, leaves a ragged edge. Someone invented micro-perforation.

---

There is a specific satisfaction in peeling a sticker from a new appliance and finding no trace left behind, no glue or static cling. Sometimes the sticker is so tightly pressed the corner lifts only with a fingernail. And there’s a faint ripple in the surface, a memory of attachment.

---

There are foods that stick to the roof of the mouth more than others. Marshmallow, peanut butter, caramel, rice cakes. Stuck, not by force, but by their own persistence, their insistence on staying in contact.

---

Iced lemon tea sometimes leaves a thin band around the inside of the glass. The line is paler where the ice floats, evidence of the gradient between cold and colder.

---

Rubber bands lose their stretch and become brittle, snap instead of flex. The dry ones crumble if twisted too far. Each one is disposable but never exactly thrown away on purpose. 

---

Gel pens run out by degrees, the ink fading to gray before the tip is truly dry. There's a point where pressure creates ghost letters, a plea for replacement. The empty tube stays on the desk until someone notices.
