Wainscot: it has a grain, that’s the only requirement. The word carries with it a cool, shadowed perimeter — not the wall, but the space just above the floor, where dust collects and the dog’s fur clings. The surface is too low to be a shelf, too high to be the ground. A place neither admired nor ignored, a mid-region.

The paint always seems slightly glossier there. Sometimes the color is broken by a join, a tiny seam that catches grime from a mop or shoe. I imagine sliding a card into that seam, the resistance and then the sudden give if the edge fits. The repetition of vertical boards, the suggestion that something is paneled, covered, protected, but only to a certain height — as if above that, the wall is trusted to its own devices.

The sound of a chair leg bumping into wainscot is different: a more abrupt, hollower knock than drywall, something that remembers its own construction.

In old houses, there’s sometimes a stain along the top rail, a line where hands have passed by for decades. 

There are words that live mainly in manuals and crossword clues: wainscot, escutcheon, lintel. They wait quietly for need or memory to call them up.

I never notice how I’m sitting until my leg starts to ache, and then the arrangement becomes impossible. The floorboards press against my ankles, the carpet ridge marks the side of my foot. Suddenly every part of the body is accounted for: knee, hip, lower back, shoulder. The urge to stand isn’t about movement, but relief, a new configuration.

The idea of comfort flickers. Sometimes it’s not an armchair or a blanket, but just the ability to shift without thinking — freedom from the shape the floor demands.

It occurs to me that cushions have a particular language: bolster, lumbar, throw, euro. Each category has dimensions, allegiance, a relation to the body’s sag or tilt.

The windows are open and the breeze is ordinary.
