# 30

*seed: time · drift · canopy*

---

Canopy — not just trees. The fabric tensioned above a stage, translucent with evening light behind it, the audience shifting in their chairs, programs paper-thin and crumpling. A canopy pulls the eye up, divides sky from everything under it, makes indoors possible outdoors, or outdoors possible inside.

Canopy as cover, dappled shadow, accidental umbrella. Standing under a bridge, calling that space shelter. Sometimes a metaphor glances off a word and leaves it ringing, so that every wide stretch of shelter after that is a kind of canopy, even a coat held over a head.

The sound of the word — can, open, then settling into the softer, leafy -opy at the end. It feels like a word made for echo: canopy, canopy, canopy, as if you could stand beneath the repetition.

There’s the green tangle over a path, summer noon, somewhere you can barely see the sun flickering in little coins on the dirt, the bugs busy in the thickness above. To walk under a dense, living ceiling. To not notice the birds at all until something darts out and alarms the pattern.

There was a day ages ago, maybe on a playground, maybe in a cousin’s yard, when a sheet was stretched between two folding chairs and the sun slanted underneath, a private ceiling where no ceiling belonged.

Not all canopies feel protective. Sometimes the weight above is uncertain, storm-heavy, waiting to fall.

The tent’s roof becomes a new weather. The filtered green in a forest means the world is smaller, closer, humming with what isn’t quite visible.

You can sit on the grass and look up through the layers until the shape of branches makes its own accidental map, tracing possibilities you won’t remember when you stand up again.

Some canopies are always moving, trembling at their edges, shifting with the slow wind. Some hold so still you don’t see they’re alive until something drops from them, a seed, a raindrop, a sound you can’t quite name.
