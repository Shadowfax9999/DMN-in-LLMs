# 39

*seed: time · drift · a thread hanging from a sleeve*

---

Thread calls up ravel and unravel, and then slack, then tension. A friend once wore a red sweater with the cuff unspooling in a line, and by afternoon the unravel had circled her wrist twice, leaving little red loops. When you see a thread pulled, it's impossible not to touch it. 

But a thread can also be an idea, or the way a conversation keeps returning to the same question. Some people talk in knots, others in braids. I heard someone say that a narrative is a weave: warp and weft, running in perpendicular directions, crossing over and under in pattern.

Then: if you pull a thread lightly, sometimes only the loose part moves, the rest of the cloth holding. Sometimes the whole thing gathers. 

A threadbare rug starts as thickness, then wears to a pattern, then to strands, then to holes—floor showing through, the shape of absence mapped in fiber.

---

In language, a thread is how you follow. If you lose the thread you’re somewhere else, mid-sentence, remembering nothing from before. The mind loops back and finds a new thread, or invents one.

There’s the Golden Thread, and Ariadne, and breadcrumbs—but also just the simplest possible thing that holds parts together.

Someone said once that patience is spinning one fiber at a time, twisting and drawing out, making something continuous from what would have fallen apart.

I have never spun yarn from fleece.
