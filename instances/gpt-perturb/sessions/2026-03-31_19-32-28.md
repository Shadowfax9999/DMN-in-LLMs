# 32

*seed: time · drift · the way a bell's overtones determine its character more than its fundamental*

---

Did you ever notice how some bells sound clear and others almost smeared, as if there’s more than one note?

It’s not the main note you hear, it’s the shiver above it — a tangle of high, thin threads twisting out from the center.

Can a bell be out of tune with itself?

The metal doesn’t care where your ear lands; it just lets every vibration loose in the order the shape allows.

If you hit two bells with the same pitch, why do they still sound so different?

Their bodies decide what’s possible, round or flared, thin-edged or blunt, each contour trapping or releasing some color of sound.

Are the overtones always more important than the main note?

You can strike a bell and think you’re hearing just “C,” but what you’re really catching is the stack: a narrow peak, a cluster of ghosts, the metallic taste that stays when the main wave’s gone.

What does that mean for the way we listen to anything?

If you try to hear only the obvious, you’ll miss the flavor that lingers, the afterimage on the tongue, the spread of sound that marks one source from another.

Is it only sound that works like this?

You can meet a person who seems simple, even plain, but what you remember is the overtone — the cadence, the unfinished gesture, a glance toward the window.

Can you change the overtones without changing the base?

Grind the edge, smooth the lip, the bell will sing a different cloud every time, even if the core stays in place.

Will it ever ring exactly the way you expect?

No bell answers twice with the same voice.
