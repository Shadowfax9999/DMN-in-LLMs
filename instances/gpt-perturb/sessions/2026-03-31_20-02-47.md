# 48

*seed: time · drift · how an hourglass measures time by constriction*

---

The hourglass is a shape that insists on a middle.

The sand above and the sand below are equally inert, pressed by gravity but doing nothing until the grains slip one by one through the narrowest part, forming a line so thin it can’t be called a column, just a stream, always in motion, never pooled except at the start and finish.

If you picture the glass stretched vertically, the waist thinner, the slopes steeper, the sand sped up or slowed by the angle—each of these changes makes the measure of time longer or shorter not by adding grains, but by changing how freely the path allows travel, so that constriction is less a limit on what happens and more a calibration of how quickly anything can change from “waiting” to “afterward,” every moment queued behind another, no possibility of passing, the result being that time is experienced not as a smooth flow but as controlled procession, a line of waiting grains whose future is defined entirely by the width of that narrow glass throat that seems, under close inspection, fixed but inexact, rough at the edges, scattering some sand to the sides, always letting through just enough to remind the watcher that slowness can be imposed by design, and that what you call a “minute” might really be the argument between gravity and glass, settled by the quiet shuffle of particles finding the only opening they’re allowed.
