# 88

*seed: time · drift · the way a pantograph copies a shape at a different scale*

---

[Expanded]

A pantograph, with its assembly of linked rods, moves in one place and duplicates that same movement elsewhere — a tracing, but bigger or smaller, faithful but transformed. I remember watching one in a museum, a mechanical arm making large, looping versions of a tiny signature, and somewhere else on the device a pencil drawing a scaled copy. It was the first time I understood that a movement could echo itself at a different size, that the same gesture can be both small and vast, that copying is sometimes an act of translation rather than just repetition. Scaling up, scaling down: the pattern remains, but the feeling of the gesture changes. I think of large handwriting on a chalkboard, how it slants more, the letters sprawling apart, or trying to shrink my normal script to fit a narrow margin — the compression makes my hand tense, turns loops into flicks, flourishes into tight angles. The pantograph is a promise that it’s possible to magnify or shrink without losing the bones of a shape, though something always shifts in the muscles and in the air. One part moves, the other follows. They are connected, but what happens in the wrist is not the same as what happens in the elbow, or in the echo out at the end of the rods. The copy isn’t just a replica, it’s the same action refracted, felt in a different scale.

---

[Compressed]

Pantograph: move here, copy appears there, same shape stretched or shrunk by the linked arms. Scaling up spreads the lines wide, scaling down compresses them to a tangle. The gesture repeats but changes. The copy is never quite the same.
