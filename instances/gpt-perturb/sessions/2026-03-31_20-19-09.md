# 55

*seed: time · drift · creosote*

---

Creosote is sharp in the nose, tar and burnt sweetness, medicine and railway ties. I think of old fenceposts, telephone poles, not in a field but along a rural two-lane, the dark wood sweating in sun, sticky in summer, dust settled in the cracks. The line between the treated and the split, raw inside—how creosote stains a hand, leaves the smell in your skin for hours.

In the desert, after rain, the word is supposed to change: creosote bush, the scent everyone describes as the arrival of water. Some say it smells like clean, or lightning, or memory. I’ve never stood there and breathed it in. I only know the American Southwest by photographs, the kind with long shadows and low-angle light, the thin dust kicked up by a tire, or the empty space between shrubs. The way the word gathers things I haven’t seen.

Tar is heavier, pools in a depression, ripples when poked. Asphalt after rain, the glint of oil, sometimes a blue shadow edged in gold, not quite iridescent, not color—an idea of color, rising from black.

The difference between black and very dark brown is invisible until you see them side by side.

Burlap sacks sometimes catch the scent from what they once carried, even after washing.

---

Some plants have leaves that leave a sticky residue, but it isn’t creosote. Tomato vines, for instance, leave a green, sharp smell on fingers. Rosemary too, resinous but clearer, less weighted, more pointed. Some people can’t stand the smell of geranium leaves. The oils cling, stubborn against soap.

If you handle juniper branches, the smell stays in your shirt all day.

Some materials never lose the smell of where they were stored.
