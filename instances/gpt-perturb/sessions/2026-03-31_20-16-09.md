# 53

*seed: time · drift · oven spring*

---

Oven spring: the sudden lift, the dough pushing upward in those first minutes of heat. Not a leap you can watch in real time—open the door at the wrong moment and everything collapses, steam rushing out, crust unfinished. There’s a memory of standing by the oven window, light inside golden, baguette bulging exactly where the lame scored the raw dough. The fissure shines, pale, surface turning brown.

I think of other swells, invisible growths: the way hair seems not to grow at all for days, then suddenly needs cutting; tide clocks, where water rises inch by inch until suddenly the boat floats free.

The thing that happens inside a balloon when you start to inflate it but haven’t tied it off: rubber stretches, a thin squeal, the sides billow out uneven, one spot thinner but somehow stronger, never quite a circle. There's a kind of surface tension in rising.

---

Unripe fruit hardening overnight, as if a silent order is delivered and cells begin to change, skin losing that greenness, stiffness appearing out of nowhere. A plum left on the counter that, by morning, dimples differently under a thumb.

A phone left on the charger, warming imperceptibly, the percentage crawling up but the battery changing shape ever so slightly, molecules shifting position, space made tight or loose within the casing.

The phrase “sea change”—but it’s not a wave, it’s the thing that happens between one glance and the next. Not a process unfolding but a state that simply is, and then is otherwise. Like looking away from the clock, and when you look back, the minute hand has snapped forward.

Plaster drying on a wall. It looks wet, then at some point it isn’t; the change never occurs at the edge you’re watching.

There’s a moment when a crowd, previously dispersed, suddenly becomes a line. It happens without announcement, only after it’s already true.

T-shirts stacked on a shelf become a jumble the instant someone lifts one from the middle, fabric collapsing, the order never returning to the shape it was first folded.

Water just beginning to boil—one bubble, then a swarm, surface breaking all at once.

---

The crust on the bread cracks open with a tiny sigh, steam pooling on the oven floor.
