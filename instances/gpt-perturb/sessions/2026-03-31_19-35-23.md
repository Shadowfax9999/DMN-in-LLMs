*seed: time · drift · declension*

---

Declension is grammatical, but it sounds like decline, descending, inclination — as if language could slope away from you. I think of a hill shallow at first, then steeper, a slow gathering into gravity. Cases falling like steps, nominative, accusative, genitive, dative — a staircase you tumble down, dropping endings as you go.

There’s the sense of something bending, tipping, not quite falling but wanting to — a slow leaning-away from uprightness, and somewhere in it, the idea of politeness, or perhaps diminishment. Bowing, or the gradual erosion of a coastline. I wonder if old words themselves declense — if vocabulary ages not only by shifting meaning but by slipping through forms, losing bits at the edge, all the endings crumbling off.

The difference between a cusp and a slope — one is a sharpness, the other a long tilt. Fingers remember the edge of a page, that sensation where paper is thinner than skin. Somewhere between turning a page and letting it drop back is a brief, invisible interval.

You can almost hear the shush of a word as it leaves the mind and doesn’t make it to the mouth.  
Somewhere in the grammar, a plural wants to form and then doesn’t.  
The days when the light bends strange across the hours and you don’t know what to call it.
