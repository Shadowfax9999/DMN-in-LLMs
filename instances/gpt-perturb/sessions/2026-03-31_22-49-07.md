# 89

*seed: time · drift · tetherball*

---

Tetherball — dust rising off packed dirt, a circle of worn earth, the rope fraying, unwinding at the middle where hands have grabbed, tugged, won or lost. The ball rattles against the pole with a thud that feels inevitable. The metal sags, a little bent from years of leaning, or maybe it was always crooked.

The arc of the ball is never quite a circle, because of where it starts, or a gust, or the way the rope kinks at certain points. When the ball wraps tight, it doesn’t center, but drifts up one side, the knot pressing against metal.

People leave the court, the ball still spinning slowly, rope twisting, untwisting. Sometimes it drifts almost to a stop. Sometimes someone reaches out to touch it, just to feel it. Sometimes the pole holds heat from the sun and if you lean it marks a faint gray stripe on the side of your shirt.

There is a way to serve that sends the ball rocketing, but no one ever does it right the first time. You think it looks simple, and then it isn’t.

---

The word itself has a round sound to it, a kind of bounce. Tether. Ball. Could almost be two names, two players. I wonder how many games have ended with the ball hanging still, waiting, both players gone.

The way you can walk past a schoolyard in the evening and see the circle, empty, and know exactly what happened there all day, by the footprints, the lines, the places where grass never stands a chance.

There’s a kind of satisfaction in seeing equipment left outside: jump ropes curled, a four-square grid barely visible, a basketball net with a hole at the side, the ball loose on the grass. Not abandoned — just left for the next one.

The tetherball itself loses air gradually. It flattens at the bottom before anyone notices. At a certain point it just doesn’t swing right, and someone replaces it, maybe with a brand new, impossibly yellow ball. The old one disappears.

A pole with no ball, no rope, is only a marker, not a game.

---

The court at dusk, no sound at all, just a little dust settling back into its hollow.
