# 100

*seed: time · drift · how a barometer measures the weight of the air*

---

Questions:

— How does a barometer sense what cannot be seen or held?
— What happens to the mercury when the storm is closer than the forecast?
— Does the air press differently against a window than against the skin on the back of a hand?
— When the barometer falls, who hears it first: the glass, the column, or the feeling in your ear?
— Is the air heavier in the kitchen, thick with steam, or outside where it’s bright and dry?
— Why does the word “pressure” feel like a hand and the word “weight” feel like a blanket?
— Would a barometer read differently at night, when the windows are closed and everyone is asleep?
— Can you sense a drop in pressure before the instrument, or only after?
— Does the barometer care what it means to measure, or does it only know when to fall and when to rise?
— If you could shrink yourself small enough, would you feel the difference as you crept up the column of mercury?
— What is the sound, if any, of the air’s weight changing?
— Has anyone ever misread a barometer on purpose, wanting a storm or an end to one?

Non-answers:

— An old blue jug sits by the sink, full of tulips and water going cloudy.
— No two curtains move the same way in an open window’s breeze.
— The cat sleeps with one ear open, one folded flat.
— The door swells and sticks after rain.
— The thermometer by the post office is always wrong at dawn.
— Silver spoons ring out against each other in the drawer when the truck rumbles past.
— Someone wakes in a different mood and attributes it to the moon.
— The stairs creak harder when you take them in the dark.
— Leftover soup lets off a spiral of steam before settling.
— The bread dough turns sticky before a thunderstorm.
— There is a faint greenish light under the clouds before it rains.
