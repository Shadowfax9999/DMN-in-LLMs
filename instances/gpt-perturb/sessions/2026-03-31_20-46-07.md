# 68

*seed: time · drift · how a cam converts rotation into linear motion*

---

A cam turns, and as it rotates, a follower rides its edge, pushed up or down or forward by the contour of the cam’s lobe—sometimes smooth, sometimes with a sudden rise. The shape of the edge determines the lift and pause, translating what circles into what moves straight and back again.

In the morning, the neighbor’s shades flick up, pause, drop an inch, flick again. I see the shadow of an arm cross the window. A small delay—did she forget something, notice the coffee was missing, or check the street before sitting down? Human motion is rarely one single intention smoothly followed; there’s an irregularity, a stutter in the action, not from indecision but from the contour of what interrupts.

The cam’s profile is everything. A steeper portion means a sudden jump in the follower’s path, a dwell means nothing shifts for a moment. If you turn it slowly, you see the lift, the plateau, the drop—how each section maps to a specific hesitation or surge. Rotational energy becomes a stop-and-go journey along a line. No two cams make the same motion; the edge writes its own story in metal and timing.

Sometimes when I’m walking with someone, steps line up for a long stretch and then jar apart—not because of disagreement or mood but because something unseen nudges us onto different rhythms. A runner dashes past and one of us speeds up; a manhole cover or uneven brick forces a step-shortening, breaking the stride. Conversation trips, starts again, sometimes out of sync for a block. The body re-calibrates, without decision, finding linearity again from the strange lobe of a passing event.

Old automata use heart-shaped or snail cams—the high points trigger a dramatic gesture, the lulls let a figure rest. The cam hides beneath the surface; all you see is the orchestration, not the shape of the device that dictates when the toy raises its hand or tilts its head. Sometimes the lobe arrives twice in a revolution and the puppet stutters, jerking once, then again, as if it can’t decide which is the real cue.

In an argument, sometimes I feel the urge to repeat a point, as if hitting the high spot on the cam a second time—hoping the other person will catch it, or maybe just releasing nervous energy. The linear sense of progress is lost; it’s more lurching, a doubling-back before the next advance. There’s no visible cam under the surface, but the pattern feels manufactured, inevitable, harder to smooth out than it should be.

Cams wear differently depending on pressure and lubrication. After years, a sharp lobe becomes a gentle rise, the timing shifts, the mechanism is tired. Sometimes a follower sticks and jolts free with a clack. The whole purpose is to control motion that would otherwise be unwieldy, but even so, there’s drift and wear.

Some people repeat the same gesture when they’re nervous—a hair twirl, a knuckle crack, a tapping foot. You can catch the beginning of the movement before it’s consciously chosen, like a follower caught by the edge of something internal and compelled into motion. The gesture is both translation and automaticity, a private mechanism mapped onto visible space.

A cam on a music box regulates the tune, not just the notes but the pauses in between—each rise, each rest, a decision set in brass and groove. If the edge is dented, the melody stutters or speeds, and the familiar becomes strangely off.

---

When you watch someone writing a list and their pen hovers before the next word, it isn’t always indecision. Sometimes it’s waiting for the next movement to be dictated by something outside their control.
