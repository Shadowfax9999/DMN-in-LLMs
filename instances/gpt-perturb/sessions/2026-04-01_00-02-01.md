# 75

*seed: time · drift · weeping (cheese)*

---

Weeping cheese — the word just exists, no story attached, but the texture in the mouth immediately: slick, slightly shiny, as if the surface has sweated. A bead along the edge of brie, or the ooze under a wedge, when cut too soon. The kind of word that feels edible just to say.

Moisture is separation, but not always loss. Sometimes the thing is more itself when it divides a little.

The phrase "weep" for an object meant to be solid, for a thing designed to cohere — suddenly it is emotional, but it's not sadness, maybe just permeability. The way a wall lets in cold, or how a pot gives off steam.

The opposite of a rind. A rind keeps everything in; a weeping cheese cannot help but share with air.

The drop is thick, a little viscous, not water but something milkier, faintly sticky, a texture between gloss and syrup.

It's easy to imagine other things weeping, but cheese owns the word in recipes and markets. Weeping walls, weeping wounds, weeping roofs, but those are warnings, damage; for cheese, it is expected.

A fridge that smells faintly, sharply, from a wedge wrapped in imprecise paper.

You can press the edge lightly and see the shine move, like a very slow skin.

---

Some languages probably have better words for this. A friend once described the line where butter meets bread as a kind of border that never settles. There is always some melt.

Not decay, more like negotiation. Cheese deciding what shape it wants to be, every hour.

Late nights, some cheeses seem to hum. Not the kind from before — not electrical, not waspish, but a resting saturation. Sometimes you can see the reflection of the lamp in the surface.

In the morning the cut face is different, dulled, hardened, or more yielding. Cheeses age in time-lapse, even out of view.

---

The word weep makes the thing alive. Even if it never moves, it suggests waiting.

The cellophane crinkles when you close the fridge.
