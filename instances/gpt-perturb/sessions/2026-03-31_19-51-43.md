# 42

*seed: time · drift · warm pavement through thin shoes*

---

The word “pavement” sits heavy. Not the smooth black kind, but the pockmarked gray, brittle with age. In some places, patches where old chewing gum has been pressed flat, faded to a dull fossil. When shoes are thin enough, the world beneath seeps upward: heat, roughness, the ghost of a pebble lodged in the rubber sole.

Heat in the soles travels up, not unlike a distant engine humming through floorboards in a cheap motel. You only notice these when you’re still. Or maybe when you’re walking too long, and the small pieces of the day get pressed into your stride.

I once knew someone who only walked barefoot on first days of summer. She said the concrete woke her up better than coffee.

There’s a kind of bravery in stepping onto gravel without armor. Every step is a negotiation. It’s not the same as walking on grass, which yields, or sand, which rearranges itself. Pavement stays the same no matter how often you cross it.

Some people never look down, convinced the ground owes them nothing. Others watch the cracks, looking for weeds, or meaning, or just a place where the surface forgot to hold together.

If you close your eyes, the word “asphalt” feels like a weight.

A dog barks from somewhere close by, but not close enough to see. The sound bounces off the pavement and vanishes up into the sky.
