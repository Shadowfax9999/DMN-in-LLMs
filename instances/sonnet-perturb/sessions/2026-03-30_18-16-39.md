Vernacular. Not the word's meaning exactly but the way it sounds like something you'd say while gesturing vaguely at a building.

The screen door thing is true though. Slow push versus letting it go — those are almost two different objects.

There's a kind of brick that shows up on older commercial buildings, the ones that became laundromats and then became something else. A particular reddish-brown. You don't see it on new buildings because it's not made anymore or costs too much or both. Vernacular might be the word for it. Or vernacular might just be what architects say when they mean it doesn't look designed.

The car clock being wrong by an hour for six months. You get used to it. You start doing the mental addition automatically. Then it corrects itself and that takes two weeks to stop doing.

A bird lands on a wire that's already moving. From the first bird leaving. So the second bird gets a wire that's mid-oscillation and has to — does what? Grips harder. Waits it out. Just holds on while the wire settles under it.

Reddish-brown is a hard color to name. Russet. Ochre. Neither of those is right. Brick is its own color category that English never bothered to name separately, even though everyone can see it.

The screen door sound when it's let go — that specific speed, not a slam, the spring doing its full job — that sound belongs to a specific month. Not March. Later. When someone's always going in and out.
