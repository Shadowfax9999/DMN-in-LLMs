The poles of a pickup, lined up under the strings. Six little cylinders. You can adjust them individually — bring one closer to the string, pull another down — but most people don't bother.

The hum of a single-coil when the amp is on and nobody's playing. It finds fluorescent lights. It finds the 60-cycle current in the wall. You can rotate your body slowly until the hum gets quieter.

---

Not quite a compass. But close.

---

A torn speaker cone makes everything raspy. Distortion is just the amp failing to keep up, and at some point people decided the failure was the sound they wanted.

There are amplifiers that have a particular quality of warmth only when the tubes are about to go.

---

"Pickup" as in truck. The cab smells like vinyl and something mineral and something else I can't place. The seat track is always a little sticky. The window crank, if it's old enough to have one.

Pickup as in he picked up the call. Pickup as in pickup line. Pickup as in the tempo picking up, the band finding the groove and then finding the faster version of the groove, the drummer leaning forward slightly, nobody acknowledging it happened.

---

I keep thinking about the angled pickup — the middle one on a Stratocaster, tilted slightly. It's there to balance the treble and bass response across the strings. But visually it looks like a mistake, like someone bumped it and never fixed it.

Probably most people never notice.
