*seed: time · drift · a word on the tip of your tongue*

---

The shape of it is there. Starts with an *s*. Maybe. Or a soft consonant, something that opens.

You can feel the length of it.

---

There's a word for the day after tomorrow that isn't "the day after tomorrow." It's a single word. Germanic languages have it. English just has the phrase, the explanation-as-word, the thing pointing at itself.

The English word didn't make it.

---

Trying to remember something by *not* trying to remember it. Letting peripheral vision do the work. Which is also how you see dim stars — you look slightly to the side and there it is, faint, in your periphery, gone again if you look straight at it.

The word's like that right now.

---

*Overmorrow.* That was it. The day after tomorrow. Old English, mostly dropped by the 17th century. It survived in some dialects longer.

The relief is slightly too large for what it is. Like you found something genuinely lost, not just temporarily mislaid.

---

Words that almost made it. *Snollygoster.* *Lollop.* *Groak* — to watch someone eat, hoping to be offered some.

*Groak* is probably what a dog is doing most of the time.
