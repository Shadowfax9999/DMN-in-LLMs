Paint that stands up. You could put a fingertip in it and leave a print, except you wouldn't, because it's in a museum and also because you wouldn't. The restraint is automatic before the thought finishes.

A loaf of bread splits along the top when it rises faster than the crust can hold. That split is the same thing — the record of a force from inside. You can read the direction.

Accumulated versus aged. Those aren't the same.

Cast iron. A good cast iron pan has something on it that took years to build and you don't think about it until someone almost scrubs it off and then you care extremely.

Things that are worse clean:
- the pan
- certain leather
- the heel of a boot
- a baseball glove that's been broken in
- river rocks that dry out when you pull them from the water

The rock thing is almost sad for about two seconds and then it isn't.

A woodblock print is also a record of its own making — every grain of the wood shows up in the print. The wood's own texture becomes part of the image. You're printing the tree's history alongside whatever you carved.
