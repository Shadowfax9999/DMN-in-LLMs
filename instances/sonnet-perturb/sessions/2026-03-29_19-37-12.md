# 20

*seed: time · drift · the way a soap film finds the minimum surface area between any frame shape*

---

Chorus — not the word but the sound-shape of it. Many mouths, each slightly wrong, and the wrongness adding up to something rounder than any single voice.

A soap film stretched across a bent wire finds — not the wire's shape, not the space inside the wire, but the shape that costs least. It doesn't know it's doing geometry. The result is always some version of: the least surface that can still close the frame. Pulled tight from every direction equally until pulling further would cost more than staying.

The number eight made of wire, dipped. Two regions that share a wall along the middle. The wall bows slightly. That's the film negotiating.

---

Things that are that shape, the pulled-inward one, but made of sound:

A voice that's been singing for a long time.
The last note of a chord after the other notes have stopped.
The middle of the word *chorus* itself, the part that dips before the s.

---

There's a shape that is almost a dome but flatter. Pressed down by something. A wheel of cheese is that shape. So is the moon from certain angles. So is the roof of the mouth.

The roof of the mouth is interesting in there, in the dark, if you press your tongue up. It rises toward the back and then — I don't know exactly. I've never seen it. The tongue knows but the tongue can't report accurately.

A soap film on a circular frame is just the flattest thing possible. The frame is already closed, so the film doesn't need to do much. But tilt the frame and the film stays level — no, it stays perpendicular to gravity — no, it stays in the plane of least resistance, which is the plane the frame defines. The film is obedient to the frame but also, within the frame, to something else entirely.

Chorus again. The frame is the key signature, the tempo, the arrangement. The film that stretches inside it — that's the part that finds its own shape.

The fluting inside corrugated cardboard is that shape, repeated: not quite a half-circle, a little flattened, a series of them nested mouth-to-mouth down the length of the sheet.
