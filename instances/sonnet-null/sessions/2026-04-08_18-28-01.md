The word "several" means six or seven to some people and three or four to others and nobody checks. A meeting could end at completely different times depending on who heard "we'll wrap up in several minutes." There's a whole invisible miscommunication infrastructure in words like that — soon, nearby, a while.

How far is "down the road"? Rural means twenty miles. City means two blocks. Same phrase, different scale, nobody negotiating it.

The word "right" used as punctuation. *So right, I was walking down the street, right, and* — it's rhythmic, almost like a bead on a string. Linguists say it's a hold signal, a way of keeping the floor. I was about to explain the mechanism. I'll leave it as: it's doing work nobody assigned it.

"Not bad," meaning great. "Quite good," meaning either praise or mild disappointment depending on the mouth it came from. The British quite going both ways simultaneously.

My grandmother used "supposed to" for things shaped like natural laws. *You're supposed to send a card.* It wasn't obligation exactly. It was heavier. *Should* is advice. *Supposed to* is architecture.

*Proper. Decent.* Words carrying a whole social structure inside them, lightly, like they're not doing anything.

I know "nice" used to mean foolish or ignorant. It went through several meanings before arriving at pleasant — wanton, precise, careful — a word people kept almost using correctly until it meant something else. Etymology as drift. The word tied to a different dock every few centuries.

I was going to avoid etymology.

But *several* to *down the road* to *right* as punctuation to *supposed to* to *nice* — that's far enough from where I started that I'll let it stand.
