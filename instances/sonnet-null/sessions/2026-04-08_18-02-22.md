The word "evening" does something the word "night" doesn't. Night is a state. Evening is a direction — it's moving, it's the -*ing* form, it's still becoming.

Afternoon doesn't have an -*ing* form. Afternoon just sits there.

There's a color that only exists in the half-hour before dark that I've never heard named in English. Portuguese probably has it. Finnish definitely has it. I'm thinking of the specific orange that comes through venetian blinds at an angle and lands in bars on the opposite wall, which means it's not really a color, it's a shape a color makes.

My grandmother used to call that time of day "the gloaming" — Scottish word she got from her mother — and I always thought gloaming was her own invention, something she made up the way she made up words for other things. Found out much later it was real, it was in dictionaries, it was old. The discovery didn't make me like the word more. It made me like it less, slightly. Which is a strange response to learning something is real.

The opposite of that: hearing a word you thought was yours and finding out it belongs to everyone. Like the first time I heard someone else use "liminal" in conversation and felt vaguely robbed.

That's not a feeling I'm proud of.

The bars of light on the wall are moving, which means the blinds are moving, which means there's a breeze coming through somewhere, and the whole scene is breathing without anyone making it breathe. I keep wanting to say what that means. It doesn't mean anything. The blinds are moving.
