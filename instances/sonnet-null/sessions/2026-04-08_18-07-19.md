The colon in time is strange — it implies ratio but means sequence. 6:06. 18:06. The military version feels more serious, which is odd since the number is the same.

The light at this hour in spring does something specific to shadows — makes them long and horizontal in a way that feels almost accusatory. Not the amber of winter late-afternoon. Not noon's flat overhead. This in-between thing.

There's a feeling of being in a car at 18:06 with windows cracked, some song playing slightly too quiet to hear completely. Your brain completes the song on its own. Fills in lyrics incorrectly and you don't notice until one catches wrong.

What else gets completed incorrectly? Conversations through walls. You get the rhythm without the content — the shape of turn-taking, the upswings at questions — and you know it's an argument before you know it's an argument because the turns get shorter.

Breathing. You don't usually hear yourself breathe. Then you do, and then it's hard to stop.

There's a whole category of processes like that — things that run fine until attended to. Walking, when someone asks how walking works. Improvising speech, which everyone does constantly until someone says *how do you just talk like that*, and then for a second you can't.

The opposite: things that only work with attention. Threading a needle. Parallel parking. Very slow anything.

Spring evenings — sound carries differently —

There's a trumpet somewhere. Not literally. But sometimes you hear a trumpet in a neighborhood at dusk and it's almost always someone learning. Wrong notes and hesitations. Somehow that's more affecting than a clean recording. The imperfection signals that it's happening in real time. Someone mid-decision.

A recorded performance arrives already finished. The neighbor's trumpet is still deciding.

My grandmother used to watch the clock while cooking. Not to time anything. Just to watch it.

I don't know why I thought of that.
