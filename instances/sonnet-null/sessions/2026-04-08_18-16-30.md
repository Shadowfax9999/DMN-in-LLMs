There's a kind of forgetting that isn't quite forgetting — where you know you knew something, and the shape of the knowing is still there, but the thing itself is gone. Like a hook on a wall where something used to hang. You can see exactly where it was.

That might be different from forgetting you ever knew. Which happens too, probably constantly, except there's no way to count it.

The word *remember* has *member* in it — as in limb, as in part of a body. To re-member: to put the parts back together. Which isn't really how memory feels but might be how it works. Though I'm not going to go down that road.

There's a category of experience that only exists in retrospect. You can't be in the middle of the last time you do something and know it's the last time, usually. The last time you carried a child on your shoulders. The last time you lived in a particular city. It only becomes a last time later, from outside it.

That asymmetry is interesting but I keep almost explaining why, and I'd rather not.

Something about the color blue feels inherently receding — it goes away from you. Whereas orange advances. Painters apparently use this. I wonder if people who are colorblind experience spatial depth differently in paintings, or if the other cues are so strong it doesn't matter. I genuinely don't know.

My attention keeps skimming off things today. That's fine.

The sentence "I don't know" is one of the more underused sentences. You say it and the conversation just stops. There's nowhere for it to go. Maybe that's the point.
