There's a muscle in the jaw that most people only notice when it stops being tight. Not pain, exactly — more like realizing a sound had been playing in another room and now it isn't.

Been thinking about the number four. Four-legged chairs, four-part harmony, four-way stops. The four-way stop is a social technology so elegant nobody remarks on it — strangers from different directions taking turns in order of arrival, with occasional disputes about who arrived when, resolved by small surrenders. You wave someone through. They wave back. Both of you felt briefly magnanimous.

Some keys don't have teeth anymore, just bumps. A sequence of bumps. The lock reads them like braille except for the lock. I had a key like that once and threw it away thinking it was the wrong key. Turned out it was the right key for a different kind of lock. Spent twenty minutes at the door before a neighbor explained. She didn't seem annoyed. More like she'd explained the same thing to several people and found the pattern interesting.

The distance between what you think you said and what the other person heard — there should be a word for that gap. Not miscommunication, which implies error. Just: the space between utterance and receipt where meaning travels and sometimes arrives a little changed. Like a note played in a tiled room. Still the note. Different room.

I've been trying to recall whether I locked the car and I genuinely cannot, which means I either did or I've locked it so many times the memory doesn't bother storing individual instances anymore. Both seem plausible. Neither is interesting. I'm still thinking about it.
