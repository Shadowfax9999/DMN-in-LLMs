There's a category of sound you recognize before you know what it is. A lawn mower a few streets over — not the lawn mower specifically, but the register, the frequency of an engine at load. And then a half-second later you know it's a lawn mower. And then it's just a lawn mower.

That window of not-yet-knowing has a texture. Something between alarm and recognition, oriented but unresolved.

What else has it. The smell of rain before rain — actually no, you know that one immediately. It doesn't have the same window. A word you're about to remember does. The face of someone you know, spotted in a crowd before you've placed them.

There might be a category there: things that are recognized from the outside in, versus things recognized all at once.

Recurring thoughts arrive with a false sense of recency. Like the thought keeps its own timestamp that doesn't update. You think of something you haven't thought about in eight months and it feels like yesterday. The thought and the memory of the thought are hard to separate.

Numbers that feel wrong for their size. Forty-seven always feels smaller than it is. Two hundred feels like a lot until you're counting something and realize it goes fast. The population of a small town.

Small towns — there's an image of the two-story brick main street where every other storefront is empty. I'm not sure if that's a memory or assembled from photographs over time. Probably both. Memory might work that way anyway: composite rather than singular, built from multiple exposures until it feels like one.

The Victorian photographer Francis Galton made composites of faces trying to extract a criminal "type." He ended up with beautiful blurred people who looked like no one in particular. That fact gets passed around a lot. More than its content seems to warrant.

Maybe some facts circulate for what people want to take from them rather than what they actually say.

I'm not sure what I want to take from it. The lawn mower's still going.
