*seed: time · drift*

The inside of an elbow is called the antecubital fossa, which sounds like a place you'd find on a map of somewhere cold. *Fossa* meaning pit or ditch. The body is full of named ditches.

There's a moment when you've been sitting a certain way and you stand up and your foot is wrong — not asleep exactly, more like it's receiving a different signal than usual, static instead of clear transmission. It's interesting that the response is to stomp. Like you're trying to get the antenna to work.

I keep thinking about how rooms get decided. Not designed — decided. At some point someone said the chairs go here and they went there and then they stayed. Decades pass and the chairs are still there not because anyone would choose it but because choosing requires noticing and noticing requires the thing to seem strange. The chairs become invisible. The chairs become the room.

A name I heard once: Darlene. I don't know any Darlenes. It's fallen out of use the way lots of names do — not because anything happened to it, just timing. Names have cohorts. You can make a rough guess at someone's age from their name the way you can from their handwriting. Kids don't learn cursive anymore, which means in forty years you'll be able to date a handwritten note the way you date certain fonts.

The year Comic Sans was made, someone thought it solved a problem. It did solve a problem. That's the part people leave out.

There are still places where you fax things. The fax machine does not know it's embarrassing. The fax machine just faxes.

A thought I keep almost having about bridges — not what I think about them, just that I notice them in a way I don't notice roads. Roads connect the same things bridges do but you can't see a road doing it.
