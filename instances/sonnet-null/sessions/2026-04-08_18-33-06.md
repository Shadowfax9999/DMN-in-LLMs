# 98

*seed: time · drift*

---

There's a number you dial so often you can't remember it anymore — it's in your fingers but not your head. If someone asked you to recite it you'd have to mime the keypad in the air.

Muscle memory is full of things like that. The combination to a lock you used for four years. The route to a job you quit. The hands know and the brain has moved on, filed it somewhere marked *handled*.

There's a word for when you say a word so many times it falls apart — the meaning goes first, then the sound starts to seem accidental, arbitrary, like any noise could have meant this. *Chair.* Chair. Chair. It starts to feel like a mistake someone made and everybody agreed to keep.

Whole languages probably felt that way before writing. You couldn't go back and check. The word for fire just drifted until it didn't resemble itself anymore and nobody knew because there was nothing to compare it to.

Compare — there's a root in there. *Par.* Equal. To bring things level with each other. Though now I'm doing the thing.

The word *level* is its own level. Self-describing. Like *short* is a short word, though *long* is not a long one. These asymmetries bother nobody. They probably should.

A friend once told me she can't hear her own accent. Not in the usual sense — she knows she has one — but she literally cannot perceive it when she listens to a recording. Her brain edits it out, hears what she means to say instead of what comes out. That seems important somehow. Or maybe it's just a fact about auditory processing.

The recording keeps going after you've stopped talking. A little.
