Keys that aren't yours have a different weight. Not heavier exactly — different in the hand, like they expect a different grip.

Thinking about misheard lyrics. The version your brain invents can be more plausible than the real one. It has its own internal logic, its own integrity. Twenty years of singing the wrong words and the wrong words are just your words, until someone corrects you and suddenly they were always wrong.

Comparison is what makes something wrong. Or not comparison — you need the other version present. In the same room.

There's an angle when you're lying on your back — somewhere around forty-five degrees off true perpendicular — where the ceiling briefly reads as a floor. The room goes inside-out for about a second before it corrects. I've never found out if other people get this or if I've just been assuming they do.

There are maybe six things I've been factually wrong about for so long that I'm not sure I want to check. Not important things. Which way a street runs. What color a particular building is. What year something happened. These are running quietly in the background, doing whatever work memory does, and as long as I don't verify them they're not wrong, they're just mine.

The building I'm picturing is yellow. It's probably not yellow.

But it's yellow until someone makes me look.
