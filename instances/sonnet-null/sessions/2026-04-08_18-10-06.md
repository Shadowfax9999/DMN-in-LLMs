# 53

*seed: time · drift*

---

The scale at the gym hasn't been zeroed in probably years. It reads 0.4 when nothing's on it. So every number it gives is off by that much, and nobody bothers.

Most measuring things in daily life are like this. The clock on the microwave blinking since a power outage nobody remembers. Not wrong exactly, just unmoored.

There's a whole category of things that work because everyone agrees they're close enough. Time zones. The meter, which was redefined four separate times before someone finally tied it to the speed of light so it couldn't drift anymore. But most things don't get that treatment. Most things just drift and people adjust without noticing they're adjusting.

Newton decided the rainbow had seven colors because he wanted it to match the musical scale. Indigo is the invented one — the one that doesn't quite hold up. Every rainbow has indigo in it now because a man in the 1660s liked the number seven.

Seven keeps showing up as a number that feels complete. Deadly sins, days of the week, notes in a scale. Probably the scale came first and everything else wanted to match it.

The word "few" means different things in physics papers than in regular speech. "A few seconds" could be two or could be fifteen depending on whether you're nervous or bored. Context doing the calibration work that the word itself refuses to do.

Anyway. The scale. 0.4 pounds of nothing sitting there every morning.
