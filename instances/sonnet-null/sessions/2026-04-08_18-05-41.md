The chain on a ceiling fan is almost always too short. You have to reach. In tall rooms you need a chair, which means deciding whether it's worth the trouble, which usually means lying there in the heat until you get up anyway.

A sleeping baby in a room full of adults changes the room completely — voices drop, paths through the furniture shift, the television gets turned down. Everyone is managing something collectively without having agreed to. The baby doesn't know it's doing any of this.

That quality of influence without awareness of itself. A person who laughs easily at someone's jokes, and that person starts making more of them, starts feeling genuinely funnier, doesn't know why. A feedback loop that runs through other people instead of machines.

A teacher with a habit of pausing before calling on someone — the whole room learns the pause, leans into it, stops finishing their sentences.

I keep wanting to say what this means, but I'm not sure it means anything. Some effects just pass through people.

The asymmetry of practiced hands. A guitarist's left fingertips are different objects than the right ones — harder, flatter. After years the hands diverge. Same person, different hands.

Shoes become foot-shaped. I don't know what to do with that.

The inside of a car wash is enormous-sounding, and when you come out the ordinary air feels like a remark about itself.

I'm not going anywhere with this. That's fine.
