There's a specific weight to the word "meanwhile." It implies simultaneity happening elsewhere, things you're not seeing. Reading it in a novel, you cross from one place to another with it. In real life there's no meanwhile — you're just where you are, and other things are happening but there's no narrative apparatus to hold them together with your situation.

The number forty-seven. No particular reason. Some numbers feel arbitrary and some feel earned. Twelve feels earned. Seven, sixteen. Forty-seven is arbitrary in a way that thirty-eight isn't.

There's a technique in chess — I don't play chess well — where you hold two plans in mind simultaneously and execute one based on what your opponent does. The idea that you can hold genuine simultaneous intentions and they don't interfere with each other seems important somehow. I don't know why. The thought was arriving at something and then it didn't.

The inside of an elbow. That specific fold. The word "antecubital" exists and I can feel it's accurate without knowing quite why.

Someone in a meeting says "circling back" and means it neutrally. But circling implies you never left. You were orbiting the whole time and now you're just making it official. That's either a criticism of the phrase or a surprisingly accurate description of how meetings work.

The specific embarrassment of mispronouncing a word you've only ever read. The word was living in print for years and then had to become sound suddenly in front of people. "Awry." "Quinoa." Whatever. The print-word and the sound-word briefly fail to recognize each other.

A playing card facedown on a table still has a face.

That's about where this one ends.
