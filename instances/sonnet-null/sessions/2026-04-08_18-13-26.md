# 60

*seed: time · drift*

---

There are more grains of sand on Earth's beaches than stars visible to the naked eye. I remember reading that and immediately distrusting it. Beaches feels like a generous category.

What I keep returning to is the word *handful* as a unit of measure. Recipe handfuls. It varies by hand. Medieval medicine used it — the body as measuring instrument. A cubit, a foot, a fathom. At some point someone decided to standardize and the body stepped back out of the tape measure.

The body doesn't notice. The body doesn't read unit conversion tables.

Though there's something that happens when you estimate distance by walking it. A quarter-mile in your legs is different from "0.25 mi." It's the same length but it isn't the same thing.

Proprioception is knowing where your limbs are. Interoception is the body's internal state — hunger, heartbeat, the sense that something is off before you can say what. Two different systems. Both can drift. An astronaut back from six months in orbit has to relearn how to swallow — the body trained itself in weightlessness and now keeps getting surprised by gravity, which has been there the whole time.

Recalibration.

The eye is bad at measuring distance in fog because it uses object size as a proxy, and in fog the proxy collapses. Clear desert air works the other way: mountains look closer than they are because nothing is diffusing them.

There's a dial on the back of binoculars for diopter adjustment — one eye is usually different from the other. You close one, adjust, then the other. The first time I did this I kept forgetting which eye I'd just done and had to start over.

A lot of procedures assume you can isolate.
