*seed: time · drift*

---

The word "almost" does a lot of work. Almost there. Almost done. Almost ready. It lives in the gap between where you are and where you were going, and it doesn't tell you how wide the gap is. Someone a mile from the finish line of a marathon is almost done. Someone who hasn't slept in thirty years is also almost done sleeping. The word is equally comfortable in both places.

There's a parallel calendar nobody keeps — made of personal anniversaries that don't announce themselves. The last time you ate at a specific restaurant. The last time you used a word you used to use constantly. These dates pass without any marker. The parallel calendar has no weekends and no leap years and no way to know you've missed an entry until you're already past it.

Rivets. Old steel bridges show their fasteners — rows of round heads exposed on the surface, nothing concealed. At some point that became outdated and the seam got hidden. Both approaches are holding the same weight. One just shows you how.

No — I'm losing the thread. The calendar thing is more interesting.

The entries on the parallel calendar that matter most are the ones where you didn't know it was the last time. Last time you talked to someone. Last time you lived in a place. If you'd known, you'd have looked around longer, paid attention differently, done something you can't name. The problem is that knowing would change what it was.

Almost ready. A phrase that moves the finish line as you approach it.
