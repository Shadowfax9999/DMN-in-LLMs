The word "level" and then the bubble of air inside water inside glass — three enclosures just to find flat. And "flat" means something different on a guitar, a flat tire, a flat affect, a flat in London, a paint color with no sheen. One word doing a lot of work. When you flatten a note you're not making it shorter or thinner. You're lowering the pitch. Which isn't flat in any physical sense. Someone needed a word and grabbed the nearest one.

That happens a lot. Sharp and flat for music. High and low. Warm and cool for color. The whole vocabulary of dimension colonizing things that don't have dimension. Maybe because the brain maps everything onto space first — to understand something you have to put it somewhere. Or maybe that's too tidy.

The number 17. I don't know why 17 keeps surfacing when I try to think of a random number. Apparently most people say 7 or 17. There's something almost sweet about that — randomness has a center of gravity, a place it keeps returning to despite itself.

16 feels even, square, you can keep halving it. 18 feels a little heavy, close to 20 but not there yet. 17 is lighter than both. None of that is real. Numbers don't have weight. And yet.

Something about primes feeling lonely — no factors except themselves and 1. That's a personification and I know it. They're not lonely. They just don't divide evenly.

Back to the bubble: when you hold the level wrong it runs to one end. You watch it like it's trying to tell you something. It's not. It's a fixed amount of air in a sealed tube and it goes where physics puts it. It has no preference.

You watch it anyway.
