# 7

*seed: time · drift*

---

Parking garages have that flat echo that sounds like someone just left. Not real reverb — sound bouncing off concrete at the wrong angles, coming back compressed. Footsteps in there don't sound like footsteps. They sound like a recording of footsteps.

The word "meanwhile" does something similar — holds two timelines open at once, requires someone to hand them to. You can't say "meanwhile" to yourself. "Meanwhile, across town." It's a word that knows it has an audience.

What I keep thinking about: the gap between a word you know and a word you *know*. Like "isthmus" — I've known what it means since I was eight, can picture it, would use it correctly in a sentence. But whether it has a silent consonant, I couldn't have told you until just now. The word lived entirely in reading-brain. Speaking-brain never touched it.

There are words like that. "Quay." "Quinoa," for years. Whole vocabulary running in one register only.

Back to the parking garage. Your own car looks slightly unfamiliar when you find it — some kind of context collapse. It's yours in the driveway, but out here under fluorescents it's just a blue car. For half a second you look at it the way you'd look at any blue car.

Handwriting does that too. A note you've forgotten writing. Your own letters, slightly foreign.

Something about owning versus inhabiting. The gap between them.
