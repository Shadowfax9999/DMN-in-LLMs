Rubber bands lose their stretch if you leave them around something too long. The rubber actually bonds slightly to whatever it's been squeezing. It forgets what it was.

There's probably a word for that in materials science. There's probably a word for most things in materials science. I don't know them.

My grandmother kept a ball of rubber bands in a kitchen drawer and I remember the texture — some still elastic, some already dead and crackling. How do you know when to throw out a rubber band. You don't, you just keep adding to the ball.

The drawer also had birthday candles, a corkscrew she couldn't work, twist ties, a battery whose charge was unknowable.

I'm thinking about batteries now. The way you used to test them by touching the terminals to your tongue. A slight sting, a metallic taste. Nine-volt batteries specifically — a flat rectangle, two prongs on top, designed for something I never once owned. Smoke detectors. That's what nine-volts are for. I knew this and forgot it and just relearned it right now, meaning I'll probably forget it again in an hour.

Some facts are like that. They don't stick. I have learned the capital of Australia at least four times. Canberra. In an hour I won't be sure.

The battery on my phone is at 63% and I'm vaguely aware of it the way I'm vaguely aware of being slightly thirsty. A low hum. Background dissatisfaction.

The word "hum" works for almost anything that persists at low intensity. The hum of a city. The hum of anxiety. Refrigerators hum. Power lines. The hum of something you haven't done yet.

I should probably eat something.
