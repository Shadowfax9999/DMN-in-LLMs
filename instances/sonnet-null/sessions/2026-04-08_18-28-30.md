The word "moist" has enemies. Organized enemies. People who will tell you they hate it at parties, unprompted, like it's a personality. Which is interesting because "moist" is doing exactly what it's supposed to do — it means a specific degree of dampness that no other word covers as efficiently. "Damp" is colder. "Wet" is more. "Moist" is a cake word, a good-soil word. The enemies don't care.

There's a category of things people perform hating. Moist. Comic Sans. Selfie sticks. The hatred is partly real and partly a social move — it signals taste, alignment, a kind of club. Which is maybe why it's always those specific things and not other things that are equally annoying. Nobody organizes against the word "slacks."

Slacks is a great word though. Slack as in loose. Slacks as in pants that are slightly defeated.

I've been thinking about defeat in objects. A couch that's been sat in the same spot too long. The fabric there is different — not just worn, but *convinced* of a particular shape. The armrest on one side more polished than the other because one person in the house always sits there and their arm goes there and after years the couch simply agrees.

Furniture that agrees with you. That's most of what old houses are. Every surface a record of preference.

Though I was going to leave that alone. The record thing.

Okay: escalators. The unwritten rule. Stand right, walk left, and if you don't know this you'll feel the social pressure before anyone says anything — the slight crowd behind you, the almost-audible thought of twelve strangers. Nobody is rude. Nobody needs to be. The pressure is structural, built into the spacing. The escalator itself makes you stand correctly because the steps are only so wide.

Some rules are just geometry.

Some rules are just geometry enforced by the mild discomfort of other people.

I wonder if — no, I keep doing that. I wonder if. Starting sentences with the scaffolding up.

The scaffolding.

There was a building downtown where the scaffolding was up so long that scaffolding became part of what the building looked like. People gave directions using it. *Turn left at the scaffolding.* When they finally took it down there was a week where the block felt wrong to everyone and nobody could say why.
