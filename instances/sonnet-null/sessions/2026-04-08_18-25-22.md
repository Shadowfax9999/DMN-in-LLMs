# 81

*seed: time · drift*

---

Seven of something is hard to share. You end up with a scrap. Scrap dough gets pressed back into the main dough, which changes the texture slightly — more worked, more resistant — so the seventh roll is always subtly different from the first six, and you can't tell by looking.

The seventh anything is rarely like the first six.

An itch you've scratched six times and on the seventh your hand is already moving before the itch has fully arrived. The body solving problems in advance. That must happen with words too — you've started a sentence before you know how it ends, and most of the time the end you land on is fine, close enough, and you don't notice you improvised.

Signatures fall apart if you write them slowly. You can't reproduce your own signature deliberately; the muscle memory is tied to speed and intention, and when you remove the intention the thing just — disassembles. Someone asked me once to sign something carefully, legibly, and I couldn't. It looked like a forgery of myself.

Which is an interesting condition. The thing you do most automatically is the thing you can do least consciously.

Common words look strange if you stare at them. "The." "Them." The familiarity is a film over the actual shape of the letters, and the film dissolves under sustained attention and then you're just looking at marks.

That dissolving. It can happen to faces too, in certain lights, after a certain duration of looking. The face becomes a surface, stops being a face. Then the person blinks and it snaps back.

I don't know what snaps it back. Something does.
