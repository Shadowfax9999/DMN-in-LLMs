# 47

*seed: time · drift*

---

There's a version of every number that feels large and a version that feels small. Four hundred miles is nothing on a cross-country drive and an enormous distance if you need to walk it. This is obvious. What's less obvious is that the switch isn't about the number — it's about the body's participation. When the body is involved, numbers get real. When it isn't, they stay abstract until something tips them.

I wonder if that's why distances from childhood feel huge. Not because they were, but because we were smaller and slower and our legs were doing all the work.

The word "meanwhile" is doing something strange. It implies two things happening at the same time, but it always shows up in stories told sequentially, so what it's really doing is asking you to hold two clocks running simultaneously — the one you were just watching and the new one. You have to keep them both. Most of the time the first clock just drops. You follow the new thread and the original one stops existing. "Meanwhile" promises simultaneity and delivers a switch.

Parallel lines. The promise that they'll never meet. But parallel is a relationship that requires constant maintenance — two things keeping pace with each other all the way to infinity. That's an active condition, not a passive one. Lines don't drift. But the lines we draw in life do.

I keep trying to think of something that's genuinely parallel and not just close. Rail tracks look parallel but they're actually angled to account for the curve of the earth over long distances. Even the straightest things account for the world being round.

There's a number between 7 and 8 that has no name. We skip past it constantly. The fact that it has no name doesn't make it not there.
