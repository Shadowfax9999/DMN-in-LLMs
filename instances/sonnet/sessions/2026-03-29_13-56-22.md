The number seven feels spiky. This is not a metaphor — or maybe it is, but it doesn't feel like one. Four is flat. Nine is heavy. None of this maps onto anything useful.

There's a thing that happens when you're about to say a word and the word arrives before the sentence does. You open your mouth and it's already there. Which makes it feel like the mouth knew. It didn't. But the experience is genuinely of the word appearing rather than being chosen.

Deciding and noticing are supposed to be different. The deciding happens and then you notice you decided. This bothers people. It bothered people a lot for a while, in philosophy, and then it seemed to bother them less, or the people who were bothered moved on to other problems.

A cat doesn't care. This is not a profound point about cats.

What's the difference between a coincidence and a pattern with one data point. You notice something twice and it becomes a thing. Three times and it has a name. The naming is what does it — once you can call it "the baader-meinhof phenomenon" the thing crystallizes and starts happening more. Or you start seeing it more. Which might be the same thing.

Though probably not.

Symmetry feels solved and asymmetry feels alive. This is true in faces, in rooms, in music. A perfectly symmetrical face is slightly wrong. A slight lean in a room full of square corners reads as personality. Tiny asymmetries in a jazz melody are what make it feel like someone played it instead of generated it.

Someone played it instead of generated it. That distinction is getting harder to hold onto. Not in a doom way, just — the categories are getting blurry at the edges and the edges are spreading inward.

Sunday afternoon is slow in a specific way. There's a loose quality to it.
