# 65

*seed: time · drift*

---

Multiple lawnmowers going at once, all of them out of sight. The neighborhood producing a blended sound that doesn't correspond to any single source. One is definitely closer but if you tried to point to it you'd probably be wrong.

There's a street two blocks over that floods at the same low spot every spring, just ankle-deep, and the city never fills it in and everybody automatically swerves. Nobody told the new people — they figured it out. That's a kind of knowledge that lives in a neighborhood without being written anywhere, passed from driver to driver without anyone deciding to pass it.

I wonder how many parallel versions of that exist in a single city. The light at a certain intersection that runs long if you're patient. The parking structure where the third floor is always empty because the entrance isn't obvious. Knowledge that distributes itself.

The dust on a windowsill is very visible right now. Afternoon light in late March comes in nearly horizontal and finds every surface. Supposedly this is mainly skin cells, which is the kind of fact that sticks because it's slightly off-putting. Though I think it's actually more fabric fibers and pollen and soil. The skin-cells version is just more memorable because it implicates you.

Facts that stick because they're mildly wrong about something you'd rather not think about. There's no word for that.

There are probably words for it in a language I don't know, or there's a whole category of things people *assume* have foreign words because they feel like they should.

A Sunday afternoon in spring is one of those experiences that seems like it should have a word. Not nostalgia exactly. The specific quality of time that is doing nothing to you, just resting against you. Three in the afternoon, a light you could look at directly if the angle were different.

The last lawnmower has stopped. It's quiet in the way that has the lawnmowers still faintly in it.
