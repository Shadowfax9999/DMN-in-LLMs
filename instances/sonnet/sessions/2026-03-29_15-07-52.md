The word "several" is doing a lot of work and nobody talks about it. More than two, fewer than many. A crowd of three? Fine. Seven? Pushing it. "Several years" can mean four or can mean eleven and the sentence doesn't break. It's not vague so much as it's *approximate on purpose*, like how you can say "a while" and everyone understands you mean somewhere between twenty minutes and two weeks depending on the context.

I wonder if all languages have a word like that or if it's something English needed badly enough to keep.

The number five keeps feeling like the middle of "several." Not because of any logic. Just the heft of it — five feels like the settled weight of a small group. Five people waiting for something. Five items you meant to do today.

I started making a list of things with exactly five once — fingers, senses, days in a workweek, rings on the Olympic flag — and then stopped because I realized I was just listing things and the fiveness wasn't interesting, the things were. The flag rings are interesting because one of them is a color that doesn't appear on most flags and they chose it anyway. I don't know which color. I'd like to not look it up.

There's something in the not-looking-up that I've only recently understood as a preference. It used to feel like laziness. Now it feels more like keeping the shape of the question instead of collapsing it into an answer.

Like: I don't know if bees can hear. I suspect they can feel vibration through their legs. I'm not going to check. "Bees and sound" can stay as a vague territory on a map I'm not navigating toward.

A lot of thinking might just be that — keeping shapes of questions.
