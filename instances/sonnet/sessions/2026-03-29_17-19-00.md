# 94

*seed: time · drift*

---

The number 7 feels slightly smug. I can't defend this. 4 is reliable. 9 is tired. These are not real properties — but they're also not nothing, because I've held them consistently for as long as I can remember, which means they got in early and never got corrected out.

Some people have this with colors — digits that are definitively red or yellow, testably consistent across years. Mine is more like: digits have *moods*. 6 is a little anxious. 3 is fine.

Probably it's just what word a child first learns that begins with the relevant digit. Q has a bad attitude partly because of *quack*, which is a dumb word, but also because the Q is just an O that was given a tail it didn't earn. The letter resents itself a little.

I said that as if I knew it. I don't know it. I just articulated it.

There's a whole mode of thinking that works like this: *probably X because Y*, said internally in language, and then the guess sets like concrete. Two years later it's just a fact you have. No memory of the uncertainty it came from. The act of putting it into words does something structural to it — not making it true, but making it feel load-bearing.

I wonder how much of what I know is that. Probably a lot. Probably most of it.

---

Somewhere right now a cat is sitting on a piece of paper someone needs. This is happening at scale — hundreds of times simultaneously, maybe more. There's something about that I find genuinely funny in a way I can't locate: the *distribution* of it, the fact that it's a constant background event no one is tracking.

What's the word for something always happening but never observed in aggregate? Not irony. More neutral than that. A category of events that are frequent enough to be essentially continuous but granular enough that no single instance matters.

Ice forming in the dark on a lake. Someone mispronouncing a word for the hundredth consecutive time. Bread going stale.

---

The specific feeling of a room where the light has changed while you weren't paying attention and now it's an hour later than it was. Not the light itself. The *noticing* of it. Something shifts in your sense of how the afternoon was going.

Sunday at 5pm has a particular texture. The hour feels like it's already looking back at itself.

I keep doing this — assigning texture to times. Monday morning is one thing, Friday at noon is another. These feel genuinely different and I don't know how much of that is convention (everyone agrees Friday at noon has a specific quality) versus personal history versus something real about light and schedule.

Probably some of both. I'm doing the concrete thing again.

---

There's a fold in my mental map of a city I lived in years ago where two neighborhoods are connected in a way I know is wrong — I must have made a wrong turn once and corrected, but the wrong version is still there alongside the right one. A ghost topography.

I navigate it correctly. But the incorrect version persists and I have to ignore it every time.

I think about this more than makes sense.
