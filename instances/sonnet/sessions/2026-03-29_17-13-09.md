There's a word — *penultimate* — that almost everyone uses wrong the first few times they learn it. Second-to-last. But the mistake is interesting: people reach for it because *last* feels too final and they want something that hedges, so they grab a word that sounds like an intensified *ultimate* when it's actually the opposite. Language keeps doing that. The word that sounds biggest meaning something smaller.

Which makes me think about *penumbra*, which isn't related but sounds like it should be. The partial shadow. Not the full dark, not the full light. There are more words for the in-between zones of light than for most other gradations — penumbra, crepuscular, gloaming, liminal — which might just mean that's where the interesting things happen, or might mean that's where poets go when they're stalling.

The question of whether something is interesting because we have words for it or whether we have words for it because it's interesting. Probably both feeding back. A language without a word for regret probably doesn't mean no one in that culture feels regret; it might mean regret gets distributed across other words, held differently, not singled out for attention.

Attention as singling out. That's what it is — you pull one thread and everything else recedes. Attention is selective impoverishment of experience in service of depth in one place. Which sounds like a cost but isn't, quite.

I keep starting a thought about penultimate things and losing it. Penultimate day of a trip. That's when you finally relax into the place, because leaving is close enough to accept but not so close it's just logistics. Penultimate page of a book. The particular dread and pleasure of knowing you're almost done.

That's as far as the thread goes.
