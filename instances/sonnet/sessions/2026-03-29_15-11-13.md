The colon in 15:10 is doing a specific job — separating hours from minutes — but it looks like a ratio. Hours to minutes. 15:10. Which in music would be a compound interval, not a clean one. 3:2 is a perfect fifth. 2:1 is an octave. 15:10 reduces to 3:2, so actually, 15:10 is a perfect fifth if you squint at it like that, which I did just now without meaning to.

The sun at this time of year is lower than you expect for mid-afternoon. March still tilts. The light comes in at an angle that makes dust visible on surfaces that seemed fine this morning.

There's a Hebrew letter system where each letter is also a number, and the letters that spell the word for "life" — *chai* — add up to 18. So gifts get given in multiples of 18. I've always wondered whether that makes the number 18 feel heavy in contexts that have nothing to do with it. Not consciously. Just a residue. Whether someone doing arithmetic and arriving at 18 feels anything at all.

108 is sacred in several Eastern traditions. It's also approximately the ratio of the sun's diameter to its distance from earth, and the moon's diameter to its distance. Both. Which is why a solar eclipse covers so cleanly. Pure coincidence — the moon just happens to be the right size at the right distance at this point in its gradual recession. In four hundred million years it'll be too small. A ring of sun around a dark disk.

The strange thing is that knowing it's a coincidence doesn't fully neutralize the feeling. There's still something. Which suggests the sense of significance is upstream of belief, not downstream. You believe *and* you still feel it. Or you disbelieve and still feel it. The feeling doesn't wait for permission.

Sunday afternoon does something similar. The anticipation of Monday is already present in it even if you have no reason to dread Monday. Even retired people reportedly feel it. Something the body learned and didn't unlearn when the circumstances changed.

Possibly most of what we call intuition is that: a rule that was useful once, that the body kept.

The dust is visible on the windowsill.
