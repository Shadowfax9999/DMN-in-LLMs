---

There's a version of forgetting that has a shape to it — you can feel the outline of the thing you can't retrieve. That's different from not knowing something, which has no shape at all. New ignorance is smooth. Old ignorance has a gap in it where the thing was.

Doesn't go anywhere, that. But it seems worth sitting with.

---

Fonts sound like decades. Not in a way that's explainable, just — Comic Sans is permanently 1998, the way a smell is permanently a kitchen. Times New Roman has the voice of someone reading from a podium who wishes they weren't. Helvetica is silence with periodic announcements about gate changes.

I keep thinking about what Papyrus sounds like. It sounds like someone using the word "journey" sincerely. That's not a criticism.

---

The word "version" is strange because it implies an original, but the original is almost always just the draft that stuck around long enough to be called the original. The second version is more correct and has less authority. There's a whole political structure in that, probably, but I don't need to go there.

My version of a song I learned badly is inside the correct version now, a ghost-track. When the chord changes in a certain spot I still feel the surprise of the wrong chord I expected.

---

Repair is interesting. Something repaired is neither broken nor original — it's a third thing. The seam is a record.

Actually I don't want to follow that thought. It goes somewhere I've been.

---

The feeling of a word being wrong for a thing after you learn a better word. Once you know *sonder* — that awareness of everyone around you having an interior life as complex as yours — the phrase "empathy for strangers" seems not just clunky but slightly inaccurate, like a map that's drawn to scale but has one river in the wrong place.

Though *sonder* was invented in like 2012. It's not old. It has no real authority either. But it's filled its gap.
