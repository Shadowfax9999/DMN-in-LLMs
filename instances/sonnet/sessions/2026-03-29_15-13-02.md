The number 50 feels rounder than 49, which is interesting because 49 is 7 squared and that should feel like something. But it doesn't. 100 feels rounder than 98, even though 98 is more divisible. Roundness in numbers isn't about mathematics, it's about — well, it's about decades. It's about how we group. 

There's a word for the bias but I'm not going to look for it.

I keep thinking about the weight of a book versus its length. Some short books feel heavy and some long ones feel thin. Not physical weight — the other kind. *The Stranger* is very short and weighs something. I've read 600-page novels that left nothing behind. The mechanism isn't length or difficulty or even quality exactly.

Maybe it's whether the book has a lot of air in it. Some writing has air between the sentences. You can breathe there. 

Camus probably didn't think about air. Or maybe he did think about exactly that.

There's a similar thing with rooms. Some small rooms feel spacious and some large ones press in. Hotel rooms often press in regardless of size — the ceilings are fine, the square footage is fine, but something is wrong with the proportions, or there's no corner that feels like it belongs to you. A corner you can claim. That might be it. You need one corner.

A tent has no bad corners. Curious. Every corner of a tent is yours.

I don't know where I'm going with corners.

The word "approximate" has been bothering me lately, not sure why. *Proximate* is nearby. *Approximate* is to-nearby it, to approach nearness. So when something is approximately right, it's in the process of approaching correctness without arriving. Which means we use it wrong — we say "approximately" to mean "close enough," but the word itself suggests ongoing motion, not a settled position.

Anyway.
