The wave you're already halfway through when you realize the person wasn't waving at you. The correction window is closed. You can turn it into a hair-touch — which is its own small lie — or you can just finish it. Wave at nobody, into the air. Some people do that. It's at least honest about the error.

There's a conversation version of this too. You mishear something, respond to what you thought you heard, and realize mid-sentence that you've answered the wrong question. You can stop and correct or you can finish the sentence, which no longer makes sense. People usually finish the sentence.

The word "placid" sounds wrong. Too easy in the mouth for what it names. Still water can be dangerous and "placid" just sits there being comfortable.

Thinking about which words feel right for their meanings versus wrong. "Gust" is right. "Shard" is right. "Lull" — probably right. "Mellow" always feels slightly off to me, which is an unpopular position.

This could go on for a while and get self-satisfied. Stop.

Tape measures snap back harder than they need to. The engineers could have slowed that down. They didn't. Maybe there's a reason. Maybe there isn't. The little welt on your thumb if you're not paying attention.

Wondering whether you locked the door when you know you locked the door. The hand checks anyway. The hand doesn't trust the brain's report. The hand wants firsthand information.

Somewhere there's a distinction between the things you check and the things you just trust. The oven. The stove. The candle. The things where the cost of being wrong is asymmetric enough that the extra five seconds is always worth it.

The light through the window right now is that specific spring-afternoon color. It hasn't quite decided what it's doing.
