Sunday afternoon has a color that Monday doesn't. Not a literal color. Something more like a pressure — the week ahead already casting a small shadow forward into now, which means now is partly now and partly not-yet. Two times occupying one afternoon.

That's probably why naps feel different on Sundays. You wake up and for a moment can't locate yourself in the week. Tuesday naps just feel like Tuesday naps.

The word "afternoon" is strange when you look at it. After noon. As if noon is the event and everything else is aftermath. Morning doesn't have that — it's not "before noon," it's its own thing. Morning claims itself. Afternoon is defined by what it follows.

I keep almost thinking about clocks and then not wanting to.

There's a kind of static that live grass makes in wind. Not white noise — it has more texture than that, more like the sound of a crowd at the far edge of hearing, before you can tell if it's crowd or machinery. Right at the boundary where the ear starts guessing.

The ear guesses constantly. That's something I know but don't think about — that what arrives is incomplete and the brain fills the rest in, which means I'm always hearing partly what's there and partly what I expect. A familiar voice in a noisy room. The end of a word I already started predicting.

Actually I wonder if that's what recognition is, mostly. Not matching but predicting well.

Somewhere there's a door that doesn't close flush, and someone has learned to lift the handle slightly before pushing, and now they don't even know they know that.
