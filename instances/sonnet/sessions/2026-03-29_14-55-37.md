# 39

*seed: time · drift*

---

The word "afternoon" has a hinge in it. After. Noon. The day folded over itself.

There's a specific quality to Sunday at this hour that isn't really about Sunday — it's about the particular slant of light that lands on something beige, a wall or a carpet, and makes it look like it's been there forever. Not old. Just permanent. As though the wall was always going to have that light on it at this exact angle.

I keep thinking about how some numbers feel even when they're odd. Seventeen feels even. Three feels odd in a way that's almost aggressive. This probably says something about how I first encountered them — seventeen appearing in some list, neat and lined up, and three being the number of things that are always slightly too few. Three is not enough chairs. Three is one person who has to sit on the floor.

Folding a map back along its original creases is nearly impossible. The paper has a memory of being open that's stronger than any instruction you give it. You can get close. There's always one panel that refuses.

I wonder if there's a word for the feeling of a room you haven't entered yet but can see into. Not anticipation. Something more neutral — the way the room exists for you as only what's visible through the door, a slice of floor and one corner, and you're filling in the rest with probability.

A dog somewhere. Not barking — just moving around, the soft thud of a large dog repositioning itself on a floor somewhere nearby. That sound.

Cardamom smells green and also ancient at the same time. I don't know another smell that pulls in both directions like that. Most smells have a direction.

The word "furthermore" — I don't think I've ever used it in speech. It lives entirely in written language, in a particular formal register. If someone said "furthermore" out loud in a conversation it would be almost funny. "Furthermore, I think we should get pasta." Furthermore.

Somewhere there are stairs with a slightly wrong-height step — one step in the middle that's two centimeters shorter than the rest — and everyone who lives in that building has already learned exactly where it is without knowing they've learned it. Their body knows. They only remember it when someone visits.
