Ordinal numbers feel different than cardinal ones. *Third* has an orientation — it's facing something, positioned relative to things before and after it. *Five* is just quantity, floating. Though *fifth* picks up some of that directional weight, probably from the *-th*.

The word "roughly" does a lot of work and I don't think about it enough. Roughly ten minutes. Roughly correct. It isn't imprecision — it's a social contract around precision. A signal: don't hold me to this. Which is a different thing from being wrong. There's a whole ethical dimension to hedging language that nobody really maps out.

The opposite move is interesting too. When someone says the meeting is at 2:47. That specific precision creates a feeling — that something is being tracked, that a system exists somewhere with real numbers in it. You don't distrust it, you just feel slightly watched.

Words that have ghost meanings inside them: *instance.* For instance, meaning "for example." But *instance* wants to be spatial, an in-standing, a thing present in a place. The spatial sense is almost entirely gone but something about the word still pulls toward it. You can feel the old meaning like a groove.

There's a gap between knowing something and being able to say it. You know someone's name. You can't say it. The knowledge is real and stored somewhere language can't reach directly — you have to wait for it to surface, or sneak up on it sideways, say the wrong name and see if the right one corrects it. That feels like it should be more alarming than it is. We mostly don't find it alarming.

*Penultimate* is a word almost everyone uses slightly wrong, meaning *really final* instead of *second to last.* But enough people use it that way that the wrong meaning is becoming real. Languages doing what they do.

I keep thinking the words *moot* and *mute* are related and they're not.
