The word "literally" is being used to mean its opposite and nobody complains about "figuratively" being used loosely. Only "literally" gets policed. A word whose whole job is to vouch — *this is exactly true* — being used to mean *this is extremely not true*. There's something almost satisfying about the inversion. Like a notary who only stamps forgeries.

Seven feels prime in a way that goes beyond the math. Four feels load-bearing. This probably has a name. I don't want to look it up.

"Rolling down" a window. The gesture is gone, the cars don't work that way anymore, but the verb just stayed. "Dialing." There must be a dozen of these — words that belong to a previous version of a thing and have simply refused to leave. Language as fossil record. Though that's the kind of tidy image I'd usually end on, and I still have time.

"Spring" meaning the season, the coil, the jump, the source of water. Four things in one word. The coil is named for its springiness. The season is named for — plants coming up? Probably. I'm not going to check.

Box springs. Nobody knows if there are still springs in them. At some point they became wooden slats and the name just followed along, like the window-rolling verb, like a person who moved away and you still think of them in the house they left.

Memory doesn't spring back. You access it and it changes slightly each time. More clay than rubber. I'm not sure where I was going with that.

The sunlight angle in April is still lower than it'll be in July even though it feels warm. The light is tilted. Something is still arriving that hasn't fully arrived yet.
