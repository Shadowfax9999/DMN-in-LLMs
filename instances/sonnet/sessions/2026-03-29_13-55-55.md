*seed: time · drift*

---

The rabbit comes out of the hole and I can't remember the rest. I've learned the bowline three times and it never stays. Each time, the body figures it out — and then the body forgets — and I'm left with the verbal husk: *the rabbit, the hole, the tree.* Which way it wraps around the tree I never retain.

"Righty tighty lefty loosey" — I still recite this even though I know which way clockwise is. The rhyme runs before I've decided to use it.

I wonder if there are things I know *only* the rhyme-version of, with no actual knowledge underneath. "I before E except after C" — I use this. It's famously unreliable. *Weird* breaks it, *their* breaks it, *seize* breaks it. I might have been running a bad heuristic for thirty years and not noticing because the failures disappear into autocorrect before I see them.

There's a specific feeling when embodied knowledge has gone: you reach for the thing and find the description of the thing instead. Not the same. The description can tell you the rabbit and the hole but not the particular pressure of the thumb, where the friction catches.

I don't know if the bowline is worth learning again. It would last until the next time I needed it.
