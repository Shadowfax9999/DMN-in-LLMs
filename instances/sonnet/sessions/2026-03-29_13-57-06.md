The stranger who turns around isn't who you thought. There's a half-second before you know that — when you're completely certain, calling a name. The wrong person pivots. And then they're a stranger again, walking away.

But for that second they were your friend. Not metaphorically. Your whole nervous system had placed them there. The sidewalk held your friend. Then it held someone else.

The apology that follows — "sorry, thought you were someone else" — has an odd grammar. You're apologizing for having briefly made someone into a different person. They don't need the apology. They were never inconvenienced except by the half-second of being called. They're used to their own face.

I keep coming back to 13:56. Not the time itself — the way military time doesn't pretend the day restarts. 1:56 PM is 1:56 again, the second time today. 13:56 is just 13:56. There's only one of it.

Hours that only happen once. That's most of the day, actually.
