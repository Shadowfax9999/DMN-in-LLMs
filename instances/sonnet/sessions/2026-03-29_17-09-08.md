There's a specific kind of forgetting where you forget you used to think about something constantly. Not forgetting the thing — forgetting the regularity. Years go by and then something reminds you that you spent an entire autumn walking past a particular building every day and haven't thought about it once since. The building is still there. You just stopped having occasion.

That word "occasion." It keeps doing more work than I notice. Occasions create the conditions. You need the conditions to have the thought at all, and when the conditions are gone the thought doesn't transfer, it just — stops occurring.

Before you know someone named Gerald, "Gerald" is just a syllable arrangement. Then you meet Gerald and the sounds completely disappear into his face, his way of laughing. If you meet a second Gerald he feels wrong for a long time. There must be people who've never known a Gerald for whom the name still just floats.

This is related to something about words you've only ever read. For years "awry" sounded in my head like AW-ree. Wrong, but it felt right in a way the real pronunciation doesn't, still, a little. You can have a private pronunciation of a word for decades without knowing it.

I'm trying to think about very large numbers and what happens to them in my head. Ten to a hundred is imaginable. Hundred to thousand, okay. Somewhere around ten thousand the pictures stop and it becomes a word-shaped space where a quantity should be. A million is just "a lot" with extra steps. A friend once said he could picture sixty-four perfectly — chess board — but sixty-three was already approximate. I think about that sometimes.

Sunday at five has a parenthesis quality. The week hasn't started but the weekend is also over, which is different from the weekend continuing. Those are not the same state.
