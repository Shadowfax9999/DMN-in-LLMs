The same temperature can feel completely different depending on which direction you came from. 60 degrees in March and 60 degrees in October are the same number and entirely different experiences — there's a technical name for the way a system's state depends on its history and not just its current position, but the name isn't the interesting part. The interesting part is that we've already built language around it anyway: *it's finally warming up* and *it's getting cold again* can cover identical thermometer readings.

Which makes me wonder how many things we experience as vectors rather than values. Tired at nine PM and tired at nine AM. Full at the end of dinner and full at the start of leftovers. The same thing said entering a conflict versus leaving one.

And then there's the question of where things started — not where they actually began but where we decide they began, looking back. A friendship: *when did that start?* And the answer is always a specific afternoon, a story, when it could just as easily have been the meeting before that, or the one before that. The origin gets chosen to make the history legible.

You can't really say when "knowing piano" started either. Years of lessons that feel like nothing and then something clicks. The starting point is wherever you put it.

The piano thing is interesting on its own terms though. You strike a key and then you're done — the mechanism takes over, the string keeps vibrating without you. A guitar string does something similar but fades fast. The piano sustains. There's more life in it after you let go.

The soundboard vibrates too, the whole body of the instrument. The room does, a little, when you play a chord.

That part's probably not audible. It still happens.
