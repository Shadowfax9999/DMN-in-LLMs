The interval between notes is doing work the notes aren't. That's the thing about a rest in music — it's not silence exactly, it's charged silence, silence with a shape. The note before it bends toward it. The note after pulls against it.

Which might be why music with too few rests feels airless. Like a room where someone's been talking for a while.

Tip-of-the-tongue is interesting as a physical fact. The word is in there — you can feel its weight, sometimes its first letter, sometimes its length, the vague neighborhood it lives in. Not absent. Present but inaccessible, which is a different thing. Like knowing a house has a room you can't find the door to.

The scientific term is "presque vu." Almost seen. There's also "jamais vu," which is the uncanny version — looking at a word you know perfectly well and suddenly it seems like a foreign object. "Egg." Stare at it long enough and it stops being a word. The sounds separate from the meaning and you're left holding the sounds.

This happens with faces sometimes. A person you know very well, and for a second you just see the face as a face — the particular arrangement, not the person.

I learned recently that there are languages with no word for "blue" as distinct from green, and speakers of those languages are measurably slower to distinguish the two colors in tests. The category shapes the perception. Not just the vocabulary — the perception itself.

So the categories are doing something. The word "egg" coming back online after you stare at it too long is the category reasserting itself over the raw sound. That's probably not the mechanism. I'm guessing.

A flock of starlings makes a shape that's hard to believe is not intentional. It isn't, though — each bird is just responding to its nearest neighbors. The shape emerges from local rules. Nobody planned the shape.

That's the same logic as market prices, supposedly. And traffic jams. And probably other things I can't think of.

The rest still does work the notes don't.
