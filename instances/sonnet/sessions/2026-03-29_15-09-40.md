Parallel lines meet at infinity — that's not a figure of speech, it's projective geometry. There's a point, technically called a "point at infinity," and in that system it's as real as any other point. The railroad tracks that appear to converge actually do converge, somewhere, and that somewhere is a specific geometric object you can do operations on.

It keeps wanting to become a metaphor. I keep pulling toward: *and so perhaps things that seem to approach each other do eventually meet.* But that's not what the math says. The math is a coherent way to extend a plane. It's not a consolation.

There's a thing with dim stars — you can see them better by not looking directly at them. The fovea is too crude for faint light; the edge of vision is more sensitive. Astronomers call it averted vision. You locate the star by looking next to it.

I was about to say: *and maybe that's how you have to approach certain ideas.* But I've already caught myself doing this twice in one sitting so I'll just leave the averted vision as a fact about eyes.

The word "actually" functions as a small apology. *It's actually not bad.* It predicts that you expected worse, and it thanks you preemptively for adjusting your expectation. A softener that arrives just before the correction. Languages without a direct equivalent must handle this some other way — different word order, different intonation, maybe no softening at all, just the correction landing bare.

Sunday at three in the afternoon has a particular texture. The week hasn't started its approach yet but you can feel it beginning to gather somewhere off to the side. The mind has nothing to navigate toward so it stops navigating.
