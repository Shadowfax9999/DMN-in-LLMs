The word "sulk" sounds like what it means, which isn't interesting as a general principle but is interesting for "sulk" specifically — the vowel falling, the *k* stopping everything. Some words do the opposite. "Luminous" doesn't feel luminous; it feels like a word someone chose carefully. "Glow" does the work that "luminous" just describes.

There's a category of thing where the cheap version is better than the expensive version. Glow versus luminous. Also: ballpoint pen versus fountain pen, for most actual writing. Folding chairs at a backyard party versus the dining room chairs brought outside for the same party — the folding chairs know what they are.

I keep thinking about how people stand when they're waiting for something with no clear duration. Arms crossed but not defensive — more like holding themselves together against the uncertainty of when. People waiting for a bus they can't see yet versus people waiting for a bus they can see two blocks away — completely different body language, even though the physical situation is almost identical. The visible bus is a fact. The invisible bus is a hypothesis.

My left shoe has been coming untied more than the right one. I don't know why this would be true but it is true. I've been tying them the same way for thirty-some years and apparently there's drift in it somewhere.

The word "yet" does a lot. "The bus isn't here yet." "I haven't figured that out yet." It holds the door open. Languages without "yet" must have some other door-holder, some particle that says: I'm not closing this case.

I don't know how to end this.
