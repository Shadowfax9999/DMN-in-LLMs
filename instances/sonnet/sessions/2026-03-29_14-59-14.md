There's a number — 37 — that people tend to land on when you ask them to pick a random number between 1 and 100. More than chance would predict. Something about it has the texture of randomness without being round or doubled or near an edge. The slight awkwardness of 37 reads, to the brain, as neutral.

Which is strange. Randomness isn't supposed to have a feel. But we can only reach for what feels like it has no feel. So the thing we mistake for nothing is just the most featureless thing we know.

The word "miscellaneous" always looks misspelled. Even when it's right. That double-c. The e where you'd expect an a. It's like it was assembled from parts of words that aren't quite it.

There's a kind of knowledge that lives in the hands without ever having been in words. The exact angle to open a drawer that sticks. The specific pressure on a screen door. How long to hold the button before the car actually locks. You learn these without learning them. You're back in your parents' house at thirty-something and your hand just does it — turns out you've been storing that angle for twenty years in a room with no label.

Not really habit, because habits are things you repeat on schedule. This is more like retrieval. You had the key made before you knew you'd need it.

The body does arithmetic constantly. The weight of a full bag versus an empty one — calibrated so precisely that you stumble when something's unexpectedly missing. Stumbling from an absence of weight. The body solved for X and X changed without telling anyone.

I keep coming back to 37.
