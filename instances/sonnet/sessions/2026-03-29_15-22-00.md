The word *approximate* sounds like it's apologizing. *Approximately* even more so — there's a kind of shrug built into it, a backing away before you've even committed. Versus *exactly*, which lands flat and hard and slightly aggressive in conversation. Someone says *exactly* and you feel the period at the end.

Some words work as sounds before they work as meaning. *Cellar door* is the example everyone trots out. But also *aurora*. Also *susurrus*, which is doing something almost too obviously onomatopoeic — you feel a little manipulated by it. Like the word is showing off.

There's a type of confidence that comes across as certainty and a type of certainty that comes across as closing something down. Different thing entirely. Hard to hold them apart in real time.

Anyway.

Ribbons of light through a gap in something — blinds, leaves, curtains, whatever — there's no good word for that. *Dappled* is close but implies movement. *Striped* is wrong. *Shaft* works for a single one but not many. Languages probably have words for this that English doesn't. Or maybe they don't. Maybe it's just a thing that exists without a word and everyone's fine.

The thought about approximating comes back, recontextualized: architects draw in approximate lines at the concept stage — loose, questioning, almost gestural — and something gets lost when it becomes a technical drawing. The uncertainty was information. The wobble was part of the idea.

A piano string vibrates and also the soundboard vibrates and also the air in the case vibrates. Everything's resonating slightly with everything else. This is probably true of other things.

Sunday has a different quality of quiet than Saturday. Not more or less quiet. Different. Something about it being the second day of nothing rather than the first.

That might just be me.
