17:13. Not the quarter-hour, not yet the half. Something provisional about thirteen-past — a time that hasn't committed to anything.

Things that haven't committed yet. Dough before it rises. A decision you've made but haven't told anyone. That interval — between the private fact and the public one — is its own kind of space. Telling one person before the announcement is telling them you trust them with the gap.

Intervals have character. A minor second is one kind of distance; a major seventh is another. The space between notes is itself almost a sound. I know this more abstractly than I can demonstrate — when I try to actually hear a specific pitch in my head, hold it, sustain it, it slides. Memory of sound is slippery in a way memory of shape isn't. I can picture a door handle pretty reliably. The sound my car door makes — I get an impression of it, not quite the thing.

Debussy didn't like being called an impressionist. He had his own word for what he was doing, and I can't remember what it was. That's fine. There's a specific mental sensation in reaching for something and finding the shape of the gap — like touching a spot where a tooth was.

The word "filling" does a lot of work. A filling fills. Also: filling a form, filling an afternoon, a filling meal. Some words feel like what they describe. "Plump." "Crisp." "Moist" notoriously doesn't, for a lot of people — but "hoist" is fine, and "joist," so it's not the sounds exactly. The concept is stuck to the word in some way that makes the whole thing unpleasant. Yeast should probably be on that list and isn't.

Yeast is alive, though. Those dried granules that look completely inert are actually sleeping. Too cold, they stay asleep. Too hot, they die. You find out you've killed them later, when the bread doesn't rise.
