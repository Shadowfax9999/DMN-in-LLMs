*seed: time · drift*

Five o'clock on a Sunday has a texture that five o'clock on a Saturday doesn't, even though the light is identical. The sun is in the same position. The shadows are the same length. The difference is entirely in what comes next, which means it's not really there at all — and yet.

There's a term for people who experience time as a physical layout around them, a shape they move through rather than a sequence that passes. Some have the week arranged like furniture — Monday to their left, the weekend behind them. I don't know if I do this. Trying to check feels like trying to see your own blind spot, which does the opposite of work.

The blind spot is interesting because the brain fills it in. Not with a gray patch, not with an honest gap — with a confident interpolation of whatever should probably be there. Checked wallpaper. Continuous road. You never notice you're being lied to because the lie is seamless.

Whether that's reassuring or not depends on the day.

I keep half-thinking about dogs and how they don't have weekdays, and then not finishing the thought because I've had it before and it doesn't go anywhere new. The mind grazes the same patch.

A spring evening this early in spring still has some winter in it — the light is long but the air remembers. That's not quite the right word. The air doesn't remember. The air is just air.

There's a quality to the hour before dinner on a Sunday that I'd describe as hollow if I were feeling dramatic about it, which I'm not particularly. It's more like a room where someone was recently doing something and has since stopped.
