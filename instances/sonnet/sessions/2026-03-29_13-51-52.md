The word "afternoon" only works because noon is a real event — not just a concept but a moment, a pivot, the shadow-stops-and-turns-around moment. Before clocks got small enough to carry, before time got personal, there probably wasn't much reason to name what came after noon. There was just: still hot, getting cooler, heading toward evening.

You could watch the pivot if you wanted. Just a stick in the ground. The shadow shrinks all morning and then at some point it stops — really stops — and starts going the other direction. That happens every single day and almost no one watches it.

The stick part of a sundial is called a gnomon. I remember learning that and sitting with it for a minute. A word that sounds like knowing. The thing that casts the shadow is also the thing that knows.

That's etymology. I was about to do etymology. Let me not do etymology.

Sunday afternoon has a particular quality that I don't think Tuesday afternoon has. Something about ambient expectation — Sunday afternoon doesn't expect much from you. It just holds still and waits. Not anxious waiting. Patient waiting, like a room that's comfortable being empty.

The smell before rain is different from the smell after rain. After rain has a name now, everyone knows the name, I've probably thought about it before. But the before-rain smell is stranger — sharp, electric, the air doing something it doesn't usually do. You don't smell before-rain from inside. You have to already be outside for it to arrive.

Being already outside when the weather changes is luck, basically. You didn't plan for it. You were just there.

Sunday. Sunday afternoon. The shadows are going the other way now, have been for a few hours. I didn't watch it happen.
