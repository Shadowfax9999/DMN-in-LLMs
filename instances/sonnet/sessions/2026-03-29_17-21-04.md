*seed: time · drift*

5:20 on a Sunday and the afternoon has gone slack. Not tired — slack. The difference matters somehow: tired means it tried and gave out, slack means it reached its aim and stopped caring.

Though the afternoon didn't do anything. I'm the one assigning it posture.

What's strange about late Sunday afternoon is how precisely located it is in the week. You can feel exactly where you are on the calendar just by being inside the light. Monday morning has a texture. Friday evening has one. Sunday at five is its own color — somewhere between relief and dread — that doesn't have a name in English. The Portuguese have *saudade*, which is the wrong concept but which gets nominated every time anyone says a feeling lacks a word. I'm not sure why that one word does so much volunteering. Maybe just good press.

Things with good press. Fibonacci numbers appear in nature but so do a lot of numbers; Fibonacci has better publicists. Someone measured sunflower heads and got excited and now every spiral in every popular article is Fibonacci until proven otherwise. The number *e* is everywhere in growth and decay and almost no one outside calculus has heard of it.

The number 17. It keeps arriving in my mind as right-sized — not small enough to feel trivial, not large enough to feel round. Seventeen feels like a stranger's age. Maybe every number feels like something specific and I only notice it with 17 because I started paying attention.

The word *integral*. Both meanings: indispensable, and the calculus operation. I can't think of another word where the two meanings aren't related by etymology but both feel completely correct — like the word stumbled into both meanings separately and now carries them without embarrassment.

*Thyme* and *time*. Different roots. Same sound in many mouths. The language just accepts these collisions without resolving them, and context routes the signal instantly — you never mishear "I need more thyme" as something philosophical if you're standing in a kitchen. The sentence knows where it's going before you do.

I don't know where I was going with any of that. Which is probably fine.
