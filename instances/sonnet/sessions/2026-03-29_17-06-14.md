Remainder. The part left over after you divide something evenly. The word sounds like *remainder* should mean something that stays behind — which is also what it means in math, so maybe that's not a coincidence, or maybe it is and I'm pattern-matching. Hard to know without looking it up. Not going to look it up.

Six divided by four leaves two. The two just sits there. It didn't go anywhere. It's not wrong, it's just extra, and the extra has a name, which seems generous.

There's a category of things that are almost the right size. A phone that's slightly too big for a shirt pocket, so you leave it in your pants instead and then it's slightly too big there too. A cutting board that's one inch too narrow for the drawer. The way some months are just slightly too long — not February, February is aggressively too short, but like October, where you hit the 28th and think: oh there's still more.

I don't know why the body holds its breath for fine motor tasks. Threading a needle. Picking a splinter. Pouring into a small opening. Something about keeping the frame still, which makes sense, but the stillness required is sub-millimeter and breathing doesn't actually shake you that much. Maybe it's attention, not mechanics — the held breath as a signal to the system: *this counts.*

Pre-sneeze is a strange state. You're suspended in anticipation of your own face doing something without your permission. There's a beat in there — maybe two seconds — where you know what's about to happen and can't stop it, like watching yourself trip in slow motion. Some people can abort a sneeze partway. I find this unsettling, somehow. That the thing can be interrupted.

The competitive exclusion principle says two species using the same resource in the same way can't indefinitely coexist — one will always edge the other out. Seems brutal as a rule. But it also implies: find a different niche and you can coexist indefinitely. Specialization as truce. Which might explain a lot of things that aren't ecology.

Or it might not. The analogy has holes.

Sunday at five is its own specific light.
