# 10

*seed: time · drift · the specific weight of a quarter vs a nickel*

---

Nickel is thicker but smaller, colder somehow, if you press it to your cheek. Quarter edges are sharper, the ridges picking at your palm, a kind of secret language you could run your thumb over with your eyes closed. Coins stacked, silver with faint blushes of copper where the plating thins—oldest ones worn almost smooth, faces gone soft with time.

A vending machine that takes only coins, the heavy clunk as they drop through, the strange hope of exact change. Counting coins on the counter, palm to surface, sorting by sound: quarter rings brighter, nickel is dull, penny is almost mute.

There’s no use for dimes except to lose them. They slide under furniture, vanish into purse corners, impossible to pinch out with your fingers.

---

Thinking about quarters makes me think of laundromats. Rolling cart with a sagging basket, detergent cap sticky, a chair with a cracked yellow seat. The smell is always a mix of hot metal and fabric softener. Someone left socks on top of the dryer, one balled, one stretched. Red digital numbers counting down, never in sync with the machine beside it.

The hum and clatter, a rhythm that only lives here, punctuated by the slap of wet clothes against glass. A sign on the wall: NO DYES, NO BLANKETS, NO SLEEPING. A vending machine with stale gum, the stale-bright blue of its label somehow more vivid under fluorescent light.

A coke can left on the edge of the folding table, a little ring of condensation marking its place.
