# 16

*seed: time · drift · hydrant*

---

Hydrant. The shape first, a stubby red cylinder, rust opening at the bolts, cap chained so it doesn’t disappear. The paint chips off around the edges, and there’s a scratch from something that scraped against it — maybe a bumper, maybe a boot. The threads where a hose would twist on are massive, industrial, larger than anything in a house. Sometimes hydrants are yellow, sometimes nearly orange, sometimes lost in weeds that have crept over the sidewalk. Dogs seem to notice them more than people do. There are numbers stenciled on, a code that means nothing if you don’t know how to read it. In winter, wrapped in a dull silver bag for insulation, or topped with a plastic flag so the snowplow won’t swallow them. I want to say that hydrants are always cold but some of them leak a little, so the ground nearby is damp, a muddy ring, grass different there. The wrench needed to open one is huge and heavy, like something to turn a locomotive. Sometimes you see a hydrant open, foaming water into the street, kids jumping through the spray on hot days, the sudden hiss and the way the water flattens against the asphalt, painting it black. The sound is heavy, more roar than splash. Old movies where someone drives over a hydrant and the water geysers straight up, unnaturally high. The word feels blunt, blocky, like the object. Hard “h,” a clank at the end. Some hydrants have little tags on chains, stamped metal, labeled for maintenance, thin and easily twisted. A hydrant sunk in a parking lot, the paint sun-faded almost gray, nearly invisible until you’re close. Usually near a curb, never entirely in the way but never really out of it, either. You can lean against one, but they don’t yield, all mass, nothing to soften. Sometimes the bolts are painted over, thick with layers—red over silver, over rust, over primer. I’ve seen a hydrant with plastic flowers at its base, memorial or joke, unclear. Some are buried by leaves in autumn, only the cap poking out, collecting fragments in the grooves.
