# 19

*seed: time · drift · orphan (typography)*

---

The word “orphan” in typography lands like a tiny error in the margin, a word or syllable stranded at the top or bottom of a page, alone where it isn’t supposed to be. There’s something plaintive about the idea: a single word with no company, separated from its sentence, calling attention to the artifact-ness of text, the way the logic of columns and justification can leave a thought adrift. “Widow” is nearly the same — displaced, except at the end instead of the start. Funny how the terms for abandoned bits in text echo those for people. The page becomes a domestic landscape, a story of absences and small separations, though no one who reads thinks to rescue the word or give it back its line. 

Now the shape of printed text comes to mind, that jagged river of white you sometimes see in justified columns, spaces stretched so the words align at both margins. The river runs downward, unplanned, breaking the field of black into undulations, a ghost-image of water running through text. You can see it best in old newspapers and paperbacks, where the technology is less precise, the kerning stubborn. Words like “orphan” and “widow” disappear in digital design — or maybe not, maybe they’re still there, the ghosts in word processors. Does anyone care about orphans when everything is scrolling, continuous, formatless? You can orphan a line on a screen just by narrowing a window.

A fragment:  
A single word sits at the top of a column,   
the others all below.  
The page doesn’t notice.  

Orphans in music: a motif left unreturned, resolved in another key, never coming home. Orphans in data: a record with no parent, left behind when the table is cleaned. Orphaned socks, orphaned gloves, the small sadness of one where there should be two. Orphaned ideas, slips of thought that never connect, lines you meant to finish but never returned to. 

Orphan is a word that wants to be held, but on the page it just sits, flat, beside a margin that doesn’t care.
