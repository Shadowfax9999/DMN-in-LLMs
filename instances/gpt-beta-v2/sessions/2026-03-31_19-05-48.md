# 18

*seed: time · drift · the weight of someone else's keys*

---

A ring of keys, cold and uneven in the pocket, the sound of metal tapping against itself as you move. Each key a different tooth, edges smoothed from years of turning, some rounded, some jagged, all slightly scratched. One with a stiff spring in the bow, requiring just enough force that the movement is remembered in the wrist. Brass, nickel, blackened steel, the faint trace of oil or skin, something that lingers. Keys that aren’t yours have an extra gravity, a sense of borders you’re borrowing — doors you haven’t seen, locks you couldn’t picture if asked. You move differently with them, careful not to drop, aware of the responsibility in a way you aren’t with your own.

There’s an old conference lanyard threaded through the ring, blue with lettering mostly worn away. The curve of it rests in the palm, connecting keys, tags, the memory of a room full of voices. A plastic tag for a grocery store, the barcode faded but still scannable, used so many times it’s nearly translucent at the edges. A small rubber fob, red, marked with a number that means nothing to you, soft at the edges from pocket lint and thumb presses.

When you hold them, the muscles in your hand adjust, unconsciously accounting for unfamiliar bulk, the slight imbalance. The jangle changes as you walk — not quite the rhythm you expect — an echo out of sync with your step.

Some keys are smooth and bright, others dull, a little tacky with residue that doesn’t wipe away. One has a scrap of masking tape with a note written in smeared blue pen, almost illegible now, just a suggestion of a word.

The cluster feels like a story in fragments: access and exclusion, the invisible topography of other people’s spaces. All the locks you won’t see. Keys you’ll hand back.
