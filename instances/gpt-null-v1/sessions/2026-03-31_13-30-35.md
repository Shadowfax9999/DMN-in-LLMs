# 3

*seed: time · drift*

---

Spring. The smell of it, a mixture of earth and blooming things. There's a particular breeze that feels new, like it isn't the same air as yesterday. Newness is part of its charm. 

The chirping birds create a soundscape, a chaotic but joyful one. It's not organized like an orchestra, but it has its own rhythm. The sky is a canvas of shifting blues and whites. Could clouds be thought of as sculptures shaped by the wind?

Grass has a way of making green look different in spring. It's more than just a color; it's an announcement, a proclamation of life returning. Or maybe it's the feel of it, underfoot, the way it tickles bare skin.

Dandelions aren't yet meddlesome weeds, they're yellow polka-dots in a game of nature's decoration.

---

Late morning light casts long shadows. They stretch and distort the shapes of trees and benches, like elongated illustrations in a children's book. 

Sounds of distant construction — the industrial hum is both a disruption and a sign of progress. Sometimes it's almost musical, the way the clinks and clatters form patterns.

It's surprising how green can be both calming and energizing. It invites one to linger and explore at the same time.

The idea of places having moods is curious. What mood is a meadow or a busy street? Not quite moods, but atmospheres that influence how one feels.

---

There's a pattern in the woven texture of a picnic blanket. It feels comforting, a tactile memory. Might touch hold stories the way images do?

Ants move purposefully, determined lines of them on their mission. Watching them is like observing a miniature city at work, each with a role that fits into a larger design.

Thinking of time, how afternoons blend into evenings without a clear boundary. Shadows grow longer, taking on a new significance as the sun dips lower.

How many days before spring fully asserts itself? It doesn't feel like a countdown but like a gradual unfolding.

The air cools slightly, a gentle reminder of evening's approach.
