# 6

*seed: time · drift*

---

The refrigerator just cycled off and the house is doing that thing where you can suddenly hear how loud it was by how quiet it is now.

There's a kind of ringing that isn't ringing. Ear stuff. The auditory system generating its own signal when there's nothing coming in, like a screen saver. I've never known whether that's strictly true or just a good story someone told about tinnitus once. It doesn't matter right now.

Friday at midnight is an odd seam. It's been Friday for eight minutes but it doesn't feel like Friday yet. Thursday is still in the walls. The week has a weight to it that doesn't flip with the clock.

I'm thinking about springs. Metal ones. The way a screen door has that long spring that makes the twang sound — two notes, really, a high one and a low one, and the low one lasts longer. I used to think the spring was just a closing mechanism but it's also a dampener. It turns a slam into a controlled deceleration. The twang is the spring absorbing energy it then has to get rid of, and it gets rid of it as sound. So the sound of a screen door closing is the sound of a problem being solved.

No — that's exactly the move. The "so the sound of X is actually Y" move.

A spring is a spring.

What I was actually thinking about before I made it into something was the physical sensation of pulling a screen door open against its spring. The resistance increases. It's not linear, it's — well it probably is roughly linear for a steel coil spring, Hooke's law, but it doesn't *feel* linear. It feels like the door wants to go back more urgently the further you pull it. Which maybe is just what linear increasing force feels like from the inside.

My feet are cold. Tile floor. There's that moment in spring where the house is cool enough at night that tile floors are uncomfortable but not cold enough to justify turning the heat on. You just have cold feet for a few weeks.

I can hear a train. Not the horn, just the sound of wheels on track, which means it's the freight line, probably two miles south. Sound carries differently after midnight. Fewer cars on the road, fewer air conditioners, fewer dogs. The train sound is always there during the day too, presumably. Just buried.

Somewhere in this house there's a smoke detector with a low battery that's going to chirp at some point tonight. It always happens at night. I think that's because the battery voltage drops slightly when the temperature drops and the house cools down, so it crosses the threshold in the early morning hours. Or maybe I just don't notice it during the day.

I don't know which smoke detector it is. There are four. I'll find out when it chirps.

Cold feet on tile. The train's gone or I've stopped hearing it.
