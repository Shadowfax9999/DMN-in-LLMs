Hoarfrost. The word itself sounds old, which it is, but that's not why it arrived. It arrived because of the squinting thing, I think — hoarfrost on a window is exactly the kind of thing you squint at, trying to figure out if the patterns are ferns or feathers or just crystals doing what crystals do, which is grow along axes. They're doing what crystals do. The fern shape isn't a fern shape, it's a crystal shape, and ferns converged on it separately, or maybe neither of them converged on anything, maybe that branching pattern is just what happens when something grows outward from a point and the growth rate depends on the local curvature and the supply of material and you get dendrites. Snowflakes, frost, ferns, river deltas, lightning. Lightning is a dendrite looking for the shortest path to ground and branching every time the field ahead is ambiguous. The ambiguity is what makes the branches. If the path were obvious it would just be a straight line, which lightning sometimes is, which looks wrong when you see it, a bolt with no branches, like a crack in the sky that someone drew with a ruler.

I had a roommate who kept a collection of rocks on the windowsill and one of them was a piece of fulgurite, which is what happens when lightning hits sand — it fuses the silica into a hollow glass tube that follows the path of the bolt underground. So you can hold a lightning bolt. You can hold the shape of something that lasted a millisecond. It was surprisingly light, and rough on the outside, and glassy smooth inside the tube, and he said he found it on a beach in Florida but I never fully believed him because he also said he'd met Jimmy Buffett at a gas station and that his uncle invented a specific flavor of Gatorade.

The thing about not believing someone is that you still remember everything they said. Maybe more clearly, because you were holding it at a distance, examining it, instead of just absorbing it. Skepticism is a kind of attention.

I'm thinking about the boomerang thing from before and how the explanation is satisfying but I couldn't throw one. I've tried twice in my life, once in a park, and it just went out and hit the ground like a bent stick, which is what it is. The person who showed me said I was releasing it wrong, that you throw it almost vertical, not horizontal like a frisbee, and that felt counterintuitive enough that I think it's probably true. The things that feel wrong are usually the ones you actually need to be told. Everything intuitive you'd figure out yourself. So instruction is essentially a list of counterintuitive corrections, which means a good teacher is someone who knows exactly where your instincts will be wrong.

Piano teachers always fix the thumb first. The thumb wants to stick out sideways and you have to teach it to curve under, which it doesn't want to do, and for weeks your hand is just tired from this one small argument with your own thumb. Then one day it isn't. You don't notice the day it stops being an argument. That's true of a lot of physical learning — the moment of success is invisible because success feels like nothing. Failure feels like something. You notice the wobble, not the balance.

I wonder if hoarfrost forms faster on metal than on wood. It must. Metal conducts heat away faster so the surface gets colder first. That's why you see frost on car roofs before you see it on the sidewalk. And why a metal railing feels colder than a wooden post at the same temperature — it's not colder, it's just pulling heat out of your hand faster. Your hand is the thermometer and it's measuring flux, not temperature. We don't sense temperature at all, really. We sense heat leaving.

That's strange to sit with. Every time you touch something and think "cold," what you're actually feeling is yourself getting smaller. Your heat moving into the object. The sensation is of loss, not of the object's property.

The fulgurite guy also kept a jar of sand from every beach he'd been to, labeled with masking tape. Maybe fourteen jars. They all looked different — some almost white, some gray, one that was distinctly green, one that was coarse and dark like coffee grounds. I remember the green one was from somewhere in Hawaii and the dark one was volcanic too but from Iceland or maybe the Azores. He lined them up on the same windowsill with the rocks and it looked like a paint store for someone who only painted deserts.

I never asked him what he did with them. I mean, they were just there. On the sill. Being sand.
