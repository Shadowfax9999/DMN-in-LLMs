Thermal expansion. Wood remembers the shape it was pressed into and then forgets slowly.

Cashews grow hanging from the bottom of a fruit. The fruit is called a cashew apple and apparently people eat it in Brazil but it doesn't ship well. The nut is outside the fruit, which is backwards from how I think of nuts and fruits working together.

What are things that hang from the bottom of other things:

- stalactites
- chandeliers
- bats
- the uvula
- a plumb bob
- lanterns on fishing boats
- that one Calder mobile in the East Building

The Calder mobile. Huge. Red and black. It moves so slowly you're not sure it's moving until you look away and look back. Like a minute hand.

I wonder if Calder had to calculate the balance points or if he just felt them. Probably both. Probably he calculated first and then adjusted by feel, the way you tune a guitar with a tuner and then nudge the string a little because the tuner says it's right but it doesn't sound right in the chord.

Chords that are technically in tune but don't sound right. Equal temperament is a compromise — every key is equally slightly wrong. Baroque musicians would tune for the specific key they were playing in, which meant some keys sounded pure and others sounded terrible. There was a hierarchy of keys built into the instrument itself. D major: bright, confident. B-flat minor: almost unplayable.

A piano tuner once told someone — I'm not remembering who — that the highest octave on a piano is tuned slightly sharp on purpose because the human ear perceives very high notes as flat. So the piano is wrong so that it sounds right.

Compensation. Deliberately building in the wrong amount of something to account for a predictable distortion. Mapmakers do this — features below a certain size get enlarged slightly so they remain visible at scale. A town that should be a speck becomes a dot. The map lies to stay useful.

The spider is probably molting or dead. Those are the two stillnesses that look the same from far away.
