Spore. Which is just a packet of potential wrapped in a wall tough enough to survive almost anything. Some bacterial spores can handle vacuum, radiation, centuries. They just wait. Not alive exactly, not dead, just paused in a way that doesn't have a good verb in English. Suspended implies something holding them up. Dormant implies sleep. They're not sleeping. They're just not going yet.

The word sounds like its meaning a little. Spore. That hard stop at the end, the p doing something percussive in the middle. But I'm probably retrofitting that. If spore meant "waterfall" I'd say oh yes you can hear the pour in it, the ssss of spray. We do this constantly, find the sound-meaning fit after we already know the meaning.

Purple again. I was thinking about purple before. Violet is the very edge of what we can see, and past it the light is still there but we're just not built for it. Bees are. Flowers that look white to us have landing-strip patterns in ultraviolet, whole signage systems we're not party to. A bee in a field of white clover is reading a completely different book than I am.

I wonder what a spore looks like to a bee. Probably nothing. Too small. Bees aren't thinking at that scale. But fungi are doing something with color too — some bioluminescent mushrooms glow green, and the running theory is it attracts insects that spread the spores, but nobody's totally sure. It might just be a metabolic byproduct that happens to glow. Not everything that looks like communication is communication. Sometimes a thing just does what it does and something else responds to it and it looks like a conversation from the outside.

My guitar teacher's name was Phil. He had a classical guitar with a cedar top instead of spruce and it had this darker, warmer sound that I kept trying to get out of my spruce-top and couldn't. He said cedar tops open up faster but don't last as long. Spruce takes years to reach its full sound. I sold that guitar in 2014 and sometimes I think about whether whoever has it now is hearing what I never got to hear because I didn't keep it long enough.

That's a strange kind of loss. Losing a future version of a sound.

Fret spacing came up last time. On a guitar the frets get closer together as you go up the neck, which makes sense mathematically — each fret is a fixed ratio of the remaining string length — but it means the physical gesture of playing changes as you move. The same interval, a whole step, is a different distance depending on where you are. Your hand has to know this without measuring it. Phil would say "your hand already knows" and I'd think no it doesn't, it really doesn't, and then six months later it did and I couldn't tell you when that happened. There wasn't a day.
