*seed: time · drift · resonance*

The inside of a piano with the lid propped open looks like a city from above. All those strings running parallel, the dampers lifted off them like drawbridges. I sat inside a grand piano once — not literally inside, but my head was under the lid while someone played, and the sound wasn't coming from anywhere specific, it was just the air being a different substance. Thicker. You could feel individual notes in different parts of your skull.

Resonance is when something vibrates because something else is vibrating at the same frequency. But the word gets borrowed constantly. "That resonates with me." Nobody means their bones are vibrating sympathetically. Or maybe they do, a little.

A tuning fork pressed against a tabletop. The table becomes a speaker. The fork alone is almost inaudible — thin, private — but touch it to wood and the whole surface picks it up. The fork doesn't get louder. The table does.

Wooden bridges in stringed instruments do exactly this. The string alone can barely move air. The bridge transmits the vibration to the body, and the body is shaped to move a lot of air. A violin without a body is just a tense wire. The shape of the f-holes matters. Stradivari got something right about the arch of the top plate that nobody has fully reverse-engineered, or maybe they have and it's just expensive.

I wonder what the inside of a cello smells like. Varnish and rosin dust and old wood. A very specific small room that nobody ever enters. Luthiers probably know. There are probably forums where people argue about it.

The word "forum" still means a public square. That one I can't help noticing. People standing around arguing about cello varnish in a Roman civic space.

My left knee does this thing where it pops once when I first stand up and then it's fine for the rest of the day. Like it needs to clear its throat.

I had a music teacher who could tune a guitar while talking to someone else. Not looking at the guitar, not visibly listening to it — just her left hand on the pegs, turning, while she carried on a conversation about something unrelated. When she handed it back it was perfect. I don't know if that's unusual or if every guitarist can do that after enough years.

Enough years. That's a strange phrase. Like years are a substance you accumulate until you have a sufficient quantity.

The ice maker just dropped a load.
