# 96

*seed: time · drift · the mechanics of how a sewing machine forms a lockstitch*

---

Head north on the road that runs parallel to the drainage ditch until you pass the second house with no mailbox. Turn left at the intersection where the signal used to be — there's a concrete pad where the pole was. You'll know it because the asphalt is darker in a square where they patched it.

Follow that for a while. The road doesn't have a name but people call it the mill road even though there's no mill. There was maybe a mill. You'll pass a field where someone has been mowing only the left half, which will bother you, and then a stand of tulip poplars that lean east like they're all listening to the same thing.

At the fork, bear right. The left fork goes to the reservoir but you don't want the reservoir. The right fork narrows and the center line disappears and then the shoulders disappear and then the pavement gets that mottled look where it's more patch than road. There's a sewing machine repair shop — or there was — in a converted garage on the left. A sign that says SINGER PFAFF BERNINA in hand-painted letters, the F in PFAFF wider than the others. The bobbin mechanism in a lockstitch machine works by the needle pushing a loop of thread down through the fabric and a hook underneath catching that loop and carrying it around the bobbin case, so the top thread wraps completely around the bobbin thread and then the take-up lever pulls the slack back up. Two threads interlocked. The shop might be closed. The sign is still there either way.

Keep going past the shop. The road will feel like it's ending but it isn't. It does this twice. The third time it actually ends at a gate made of tubular steel with a chain and no lock. The chain is just wrapped around the post. Unwrap it.

Walk from here. The path goes through a stand of cedar that smells like pencils and then opens into a clearing that isn't in any survey. There's a concrete foundation with no building on it and four anchor bolts sticking up out of the corners, rusted to the color of the dirt around them.

This is it. Stand on the foundation. Face windward. You'll know which direction that is.

There's nothing else here. The anchor bolts are three-quarter inch.
