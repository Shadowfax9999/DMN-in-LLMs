Forty-one microvolts. I keep thinking about what counts as worth listening to.

The fridge just cycled on. That compressor hum — you stop hearing it until it stops, and then you hear the absence, and then twenty minutes later it starts again and you hear it for about four seconds before it disappears. Four seconds of noticing. The rest is just the room.

Saturday nights sound different than Tuesday nights. Not quieter necessarily. More deliberate. Cars going somewhere specific instead of coming home.

I wonder how many things I believe about days of the week that are just texture I've assigned. Wednesday feels like corduroy. I don't know what that means. It's not even a synesthesia thing, it's more like — Wednesday has ridges. Thursday is smoother. Friday is a different material entirely.

A thermocouple works because two metals disagree about temperature. That's genuinely what's happening. They produce different voltages in response to the same heat, and the disagreement is the measurement. You're not reading temperature directly. You're reading the argument between two metals about what the temperature is, and inferring the answer from the shape of the argument. Which is how a lot of measurement works, probably. You don't measure the thing. You measure two responses to the thing and triangulate.

Triangulate. Three points. But a thermocouple is only two metals. So it's more like — biangulate? That's not a word. Two-point inference. Stereoscopy does the same thing with vision. Two slightly different images, and depth emerges from the disagreement between them. Close one eye and the world goes flat.

I've never actually verified that. I close one eye right now and the room looks the same. I know it's supposed to lose depth but I think my brain is filling it in from memory. I already know how far away the wall is. The demonstration only works with unfamiliar scenes.

The compressor just turned off again.
