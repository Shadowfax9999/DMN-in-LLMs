A carving fork has two tines because it's not trying to scoop. It's trying to hold still.

What else is designed entirely around holding still. A clamp. A doorstop. A bookmark.

A bookmark is just a thing that's thin enough to not wreck the spine. Anything flat could be a bookmark. I've used receipts, a playing card, a leaf that dried out and left a brown ghost on the page. A fork would be a terrible bookmark.

The sound of a ceiling fan that's slightly off-balance. Not the wobble you can see — the sound. A kind of ticking that isn't regular enough to ignore and isn't loud enough to locate. You look up at the fan and it looks fine. The sound continues.

I wonder how many people are awake right now within a mile of me. Not most of them. But some. Someone's watching something on their phone with the brightness down. Someone left the bathroom light on and went back to bed.

The word "wick" is satisfying to say. Wick. It ends so abruptly. Brick does the same thing. Thick. Stick. Trick. They all have that same clean stop. Lick. Pick. Flick. You could list these for a long time.

A candle wick curls as it burns. The curl is called mushrooming when it gets a carbon ball at the tip. You're supposed to trim it. Most people don't trim it.

I had a pen once that I liked so much I kept track of where it was for about three weeks, which is a personal record. Then I left it somewhere and that was that. I don't remember what kind it was. I remember it was heavier than I expected when I first picked it up.

Heavier than expected is almost always a good quality in a small object. A lighter. A pocketknife. A chess piece.

Plastic chess pieces feel like nothing. The ones with felt on the bottom at least have some friction against the board.
