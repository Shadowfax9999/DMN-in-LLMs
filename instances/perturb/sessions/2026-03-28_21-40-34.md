Near miss means it almost hit. But it sounds like it means it nearly missed — like it *did* hit, just barely. Everyone knows what it means anyway. Language doesn't care about its own logic.

The golf ball on the moon. Six-iron. No — he modified a six-iron head and attached it to a sample collection tool. So it was more like a shovel with a clubface. Two shots. The first one he said went into a crater. The second one he claimed went miles and miles and miles. It didn't. Someone analyzed the footage and figured maybe 200 yards. Still. 200 yards in a spacesuit swinging one-handed with a shovel.

I keep thinking about the phrase "near miss" though. A near hit. A close call. A close call is stranger — close to what? To calling something? To being called? Called out, called off, called home.

Things that are named wrong and everyone just agreed:

- Parkway (you drive on it)
- Driveway (you park on it)
- This is the most tired observation in the English language and I'm still making it

Fireflies aren't out yet but they will be soon. Another few weeks maybe. The first one you see each year always looks like a mistake — like something electronic fell in the grass.

There's a particular kind of quiet on a spring night that isn't actually quiet. It's layered. Frogs, insects, something mechanical far away — a compressor or a vent fan on a roof. The mechanical sound is the one that makes the night feel inhabited. Without it you could be anywhere in any century. With it you're specifically now.

I wonder if Alan Shepard could hear anything inside the suit on the lunar surface. His own breathing. Fans. The radio. He probably couldn't hear the club hit the ball. No air to carry it. You'd feel the impact through your gloves but the sound would only travel up the shaft, muffled by suit material. A silent golf swing. The ball lifting off the regolith in slow motion, trailing a little spray of dust that falls in perfect parabolas because there's no wind to scatter it.

The dust on the moon doesn't erode. It's sharp. Angular. It tore up the suits, scratched visors. On Earth, wind and water round everything down eventually. Tumbled glass on a beach. River stones. The moon just has fresh broken edges everywhere, billions of years old and still sharp.

My neighbor's air conditioner just kicked on.
