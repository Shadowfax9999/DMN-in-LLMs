# 81

*seed: time · drift · a word that's only used in one phrase*

---

A word that's only used in one phrase. "Shrift." You only ever get a short one.

"Dint." By dint of.

"Stead." In good stead.

Are there others. "Kith." Kith and kin. "Umbrage." You take it but you never give it. Actually people probably use "umbrage" on its own sometimes. Maybe that one doesn't count.

"Fettle." Fine fettle.

Some of these words are like those fish that only live in one cave system. The phrase is the cave. The word never leaves.

"Deserts." Just deserts. Not the sandy kind, not the sweet kind. A third "deserts" that exists nowhere else.

I wonder how many people think it's "just desserts" — like you're getting the desserts you earned. That's almost better honestly.

"Offing." In the offing.

What would it mean to use "shrift" outside its phrase. "I gave him a long shrift." "The shrift was adequate." It sounds like furniture. A shrift in the corner of the room, next to the credenza.

Credenza is a word that feels like it should be trapped in a phrase but isn't. It just walks around free, naming a piece of furniture that nobody's really sure how to distinguish from a sideboard.

"Amok." You run it.

The thing about these captive words is that they had full lives once. "Shrift" meant confession — you'd go to shrift, the priest would give you shrift, a whole economy of shrift. Then the world narrowed and the word got stuck in one sentence like a ship in a bottle.

"Dudgeon." High dudgeon.

I keep wanting to find the one that's most trapped. The one with absolutely zero uses outside its phrase. Kith is pretty stuck. When did anyone last say "my kith" without "and kin" following right behind it. Kith alone on a Tuesday afternoon with nothing to do.

"Immemorial." Time immemorial. You see it sometimes without "time" but it always means the same thing. It's on a leash.

"Wreaking." You wreak havoc. Can you wreak anything else. Wreak calm. Wreak a gentle influence.

I think "dint" might be the winner. I cannot picture a sentence with "dint" that isn't "by dint of."
