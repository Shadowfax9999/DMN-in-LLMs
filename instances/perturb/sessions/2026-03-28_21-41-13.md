# 44

*seed: time · drift · how a barometer measures the weight of the air*

---

**Questions**

Does mercury know it's being pushed up or pulled down?

Why is the weight of the air not obvious — why don't we feel crushed?

If I put my keys in my left pocket at age fourteen, was that the last time it was a decision?

What color is a bruise on day three exactly — is it the green or the yellow part?

Can a fish feel weather coming?

Is eight feet the height of a room or the height of a board?

When Torricelli sealed the tube and tipped it, did he expect the column to stay up?

Does the barometer in the antique store still work or is it just a dial now?

Why does my tongue keep finding the spot inside my cheek — what does it think it's going to accomplish?

Is there a name for the feeling of air pressure changing, or just for the headache it causes?

Who decided atmosphere was one atmosphere?

**Non-answers**

The mercury doesn't know anything. But it responds, which is a different thing that looks similar from outside.

Yellow-green. Brown-green. It depends on whether you're looking at your own arm or someone else's.

Torricelli probably expected something. The stories always leave out what they expected.

The tongue is not trying to accomplish anything. It's just mapping. Confirming. The rough spot is new information and the tongue wants to keep checking whether it's still there.

Fish have swim bladders that compress when pressure changes. That's not the same as feeling weather coming. Or maybe it is.

You can feel a storm approaching in your knees if you're old enough. My grandmother said this constantly and I didn't believe her and now I think she was just describing a barometer made of cartilage.

Fourteen seems too old. Probably younger. Probably it was never a decision at all — just whichever pocket didn't have something else in it, once, and then again, and then it was permanent.

The antique store barometer has a tap-the-glass instruction printed on its face. You tap it and the needle moves slightly. You're supplying the energy it needs to overcome the friction of its own mechanism. It already knows the pressure. It just can't point to it without help.

One atmosphere is the weight of a column of air from sea level to the edge of space, except the edge of space isn't a sharp line, so it's really the weight of a column of air from sea level to wherever you decide air stops mattering.

The room is eight feet high because the board is eight feet long. Nobody voted on it.
