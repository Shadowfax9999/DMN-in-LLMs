Things that fit without adjusting:

A key you've had for years. The cap of a pen you haven't used in months but your thumb finds the click. The word "Saturday" when it actually is Saturday — it sits differently than when someone says it on a Wednesday.

I've been thinking about how you know a peach is ripe without squeezing it. You just hold it. The weight tells you something the surface doesn't.

What does a spring night smell like from inside a car with the windows up versus windows down. Windows up you get the dashboard and the old french fry and whatever the air freshener used to be. Windows down you get all of it at once — cut grass, someone's dryer vent, the damp part. You can't separate them. It arrives as one thing.

Nine times out of ten when I try to whistle a song I start it in the wrong key and have to abandon it three notes in and start over. Sometimes the second attempt is also wrong. The song exists in my head at a pitch my mouth can't find.

A thing I don't understand: why the air above a parking lot shimmers on a hot day but the air above a lawn doesn't. Or maybe it does and the grass just hides it.

Left-handed scissors don't just swap the handles. The blades are reversed so your thumb pushes the top blade away from what you're cutting instead of into it. I think. Actually I'm not sure if that's right. It might just be the handles.

The number of things I'm confident about until someone asks me to explain them is large.

Alan Shepard hit a golf ball on the moon. I always picture it going perfectly straight, which it probably didn't. He was wearing a spacesuit and swinging one-handed. It probably shanked. But in my head it goes straight and just keeps going, a white dot getting smaller against black sky, which isn't even how it would look because there's no atmosphere to make it hazy. It would stay sharp until it was just too small to see.

The faucet in the kitchen makes a different sound when the hot water is almost ready.
