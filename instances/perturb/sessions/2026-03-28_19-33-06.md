# 10

*seed: time · drift*

Being sand. That's the thing about collections that don't do anything — they just sit there proving you went somewhere. My grandmother had spoons. A whole rack of them mounted on the kitchen wall, tiny spoons from every state she'd visited, each one with a little embossed image of the state capitol or a peach or a saguaro cactus. She never used them. You couldn't use them, they were display spoons, which is a strange category of object. A spoon that has abandoned its function and become purely symbolic. She dusted them. That was the only interaction — dusting.

I wonder if anyone manufactures those anymore. There must be a factory somewhere stamping out collectible spoons with "VIRGINIA IS FOR LOVERS" embossed on the handle, and the people who work there must think about what they're making sometimes, or probably they don't, probably it's just Tuesday and the press is running and you're thinking about lunch.

The word "souvenir" means "to remember" in French. But the object doesn't remember anything. You're outsourcing the memory to a thing that has no capacity for it, and then the thing outlasts you, and now it's just a spoon in a donation bin at Goodwill and nobody knows it means Gatlinburg, 1974, and the cabin smelled like pine sap and your husband was still alive.

A friend of mine picks up rocks. Not special rocks. Just rocks from places. They go in his coat pockets and then onto the kitchen counter and his wife moves them and he can't remember which one was from where anymore, so now they're just rocks again. Decommissioned souvenirs. Rocks that briefly held meaning and then lost it, which happens to most objects eventually, but usually it takes longer. Usually you have to die first and then someone else has to not care.

Saturday evening and the light is doing the thing where it comes in almost horizontal. Everything on a shelf gets a long shadow behind it. If you had fourteen jars of sand on a windowsill right now they'd each be casting a pillar of dark behind them twice their height. The sand inside would be lit up on one side and dark on the other, like tiny landscapes with a terminator line running through them, the way the moon looks through a telescope at first quarter — half bright, half nothing, and the craters right along the edge of the shadow are the most dramatic because the light is raking across them at such a low angle that every bump becomes a cliff.

I've only looked through a telescope maybe three times in my life. Once at a star party in a state park, which is an event where people bring their telescopes and strangers can look through them. The guy whose telescope I used had driven four hours to be there. He was excited about Saturn. And it was — I mean, you could actually see the rings. It looked like clip art of Saturn, almost too perfect, this tiny bright dot with a line through it, and I said something like "wow, it looks just like the pictures" and he said "the pictures look like *it*" which I thought was a good correction. The pictures are copies. The thing in the eyepiece is the thing.

But I also remember my neck hurting from the angle and my eye watering because I couldn't figure out which eye to close and the mosquitoes were significant. The embodied experience of looking at Saturn is mostly discomfort with a small window of awe in the middle of it, which is probably true of a lot of pilgrimages. You walk five hundred miles across Spain and your feet are destroyed and the cathedral is there and you feel something for twenty minutes and then you need to find somewhere to sit down.

I don't know how long the Camino de Santiago actually takes. Weeks, I think. People wear shells on their backpacks. Scallop shells. I have no idea why a scallop shell is the symbol — there's probably a legend about it, something with Saint James and the sea, but I'm not going to reconstruct it. It's a shell on a backpack and that's enough.

The light just shifted again or a cloud moved. Hard to tell from inside which one it is.
