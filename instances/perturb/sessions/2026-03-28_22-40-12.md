Bracket. Like the thing that holds a shelf up. Two screws and an L of metal and then you trust it with books.

I've been thinking about how much of a house is just things holding other things up. The shelf bracket holds the shelf, the shelf holds the books, the books hold the bookends in place — no, the bookends hold the books. The bookends are only necessary because of gravity. In zero gravity you wouldn't need bookends. You wouldn't need shelves. You wouldn't need brackets. The whole category of "support" disappears.

What would furniture even mean without gravity.

A table is just a platform held at a specific height so you don't have to bend down. A chair is a platform held at a different specific height so your legs don't get tired. That's it. That's the whole invention. Platforms at heights.

I wonder how many bracket sizes exist. At a hardware store there's always a section of them hanging on pegs, and they come in sizes like 4-inch, 6-inch, 8-inch, and each one looks like it was designed for exactly one use case but people use them for whatever. The 4-inch bracket holding up a massive planter on someone's porch, visibly bowing. You walk past it and you think, that's going to come down. But it doesn't come down. It's been bowing for three years.

Tournament brackets are a completely different thing. The shape is the same — branching Ls — but the function is reversed. A shelf bracket holds something up. A tournament bracket eliminates things. Sixty-four teams become thirty-two become sixteen. The bracket narrows.

Parentheses are also called brackets in British English. Square brackets. Curly brackets. Angle brackets. All of them are containers for the aside, the clarification, the thing you could skip. The sentence works without the bracket. The bracket is optional load-bearing.

My dentist has those articulating brackets on the overhead light, the ones with two arms and a joint in the middle that let you position the light anywhere. They always look like they shouldn't be able to hold still — like the light should drift slowly downward over the course of the appointment. But it stays. Some kind of friction joint. You tighten it enough that it holds but loose enough that you can reposition it with one hand.

I don't know what the friction material is inside those joints.
