Isotherm. A line on a map where every point is the same temperature, which means it's a line nobody walks along, nobody follows, it exists only from above. You'd never know you were standing on one. There are probably dozens of invisible boundaries like that running through my yard right now if I had a yard. The word sounds like it should be about insulation but it's about sameness, about connecting things that share one quality while differing in every other way. Two messages on a Saturday evening — I keep coming back to that, the specific anxiety of the double-text, how the second message changes the meaning of the first one retroactively, the way a question mark after a period makes the period seem colder than it was. Chirality. I do think about chirality sometimes but never while tying shoes, only after, when one loop sits flat and the other twists up and I realize I've done it wrong again. Left over right, right over left, or is it the other way. The granny knot is supposedly the one where you repeat the same crossing twice and the square knot alternates, which means the difference is whether you remember to change direction on the second pass, which I never do because by the second pass I'm already thinking about something else. The silent K in knot used to be pronounced. Old English cnottan. So the word used to be heavier than it looks AND heavier than it sounds, and we just slowly stopped saying part of it, the way you stop using a room in a house. False cognates — that's close to what I was looking for but not exactly it. False cognates are words in different languages that look related but aren't. I want the word for when two words in the SAME language feel like they should share a root and don't. Knot and not. Whole and hole. Island and isle, though actually I think those two did get tangled together at some point, the S in island got inserted because people assumed it was related to isle when it's actually from Old English igland. So sometimes the desire for two words to be related actually changes the spelling of one of them. The desire reshapes the thing. That feels like it should apply to something bigger but I'm not going to chase it. Isotherms on weather maps are usually red or blue, color-coded to temperature, which means the map is doing double work — position and temperature on the same surface. I wonder if cartographers ever feel like they're lying, compressing three dimensions into two and then adding color as a cheat to get the third one back. Saturday evening and the light is probably doing that thing where it turns orange for twenty minutes. I've never sent a double text without regretting the second one immediately. The first one is a question and the second one is an apology for the question, dressed up as a clarification.
