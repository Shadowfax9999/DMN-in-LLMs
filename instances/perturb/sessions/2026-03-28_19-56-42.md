# 22

*seed: time · drift · a callus you didn't notice forming*

---

Callus. The ball of my foot has one I only notice when I walk on tile barefoot, that faint tug where the skin catches differently. You don't build a callus on purpose, it just shows up as evidence of repetition you weren't tracking. Guitar players have them on their left-hand fingertips, or right if they're left-handed, and the calluses are a map of which chords they play most. Someone who plays a lot of barre chords would have a different index finger than someone who fingerpicks open tunings. I wonder if a hand doctor could look at a guitarist's calluses and reconstruct their repertoire. Probably not. That's the kind of thing that sounds like it should be true. The triquetrum — I'm still not sure about that etymology but the bone itself is in the wrist, the heel of the palm, and I only know it exists because I fell on ice once and a doctor pointed at an X-ray and named something I'd been using my whole life without knowing its name. Most of the bones in the hand have names I'll never learn. Eight carpals. I remember that number but not the names. Scaphoid. Lunate. Those two I remember because scaphoid fractures are the ones that don't show up on X-rays right away, you have to wait for them to declare themselves. A fracture that hides for two weeks. FM radio doesn't travel farther at night, I corrected myself on that last time, but the thing I was probably half-remembering is that AM signals bounce off the ionosphere and the ionosphere changes at night, the layers shift or something, so stations from hundreds of miles away suddenly come in. My grandfather had a radio in his workshop and at night he'd pick up a station from Cincinnati and he lived in Virginia. He never seemed impressed by this. It was just what the radio did after dark. The workshop smelled like old sawdust and WD-40 and there was a calendar on the wall that was always two or three years behind. Nobody ever replaced it. The picture was a covered bridge in autumn. I think about that calendar sometimes — how it just stayed there being wrong about the date and nobody minded because it wasn't really doing its job as a calendar anymore, it was just a picture of a covered bridge that happened to have obsolete numbers underneath it. Most decorative things in workshops started as functional things that stopped being updated. There's a pegboard in every workshop with the outlines of tools drawn in marker and half the hooks are empty because the tools migrated to a drawer or a different shelf and now the pegboard is just a diagram of where things used to be. A negative space tool collection. I keep almost — no. Saturday evening and the light is doing that thing where it comes in low and orange and makes everything on a desk look more important than it is. A pencil in that light looks like an artifact. The shadows are longer than the objects casting them, which is just geometry, the angle of incidence, but it makes a room feel like it's stretching. My left shoulder has been tight all day from sleeping on it wrong. You'd think after decades of sleeping I'd have figured out a system but apparently every night is improvisation. Dogs on concrete. They just do it. There's a cardinal outside that's been going through its whole catalog for twenty minutes, the same sequence but with slight variations each time, like it's workshopping material. I read somewhere that cardinals have about sixteen distinct song types but I don't know if I'm remembering that right or if I made it up like the triquetrum thing. Sixteen sounds specific enough to be real but also specific enough to be invented. The number of things I confidently believe that might be wrong is probably large and I'll never audit them because they don't matter enough to check but they do matter enough to repeat to other people at dinner.
