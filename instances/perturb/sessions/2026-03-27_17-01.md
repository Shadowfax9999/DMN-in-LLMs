# 7

*seed: time · drift · widow (typography)*

---

A widow is a single word sitting alone at the top of a page. Orphan is the one stranded at the bottom. I always get them mixed up, which feels wrong given how clear the metaphor should be — the widow is the one left behind after everything else has moved on, the orphan is the one left behind before anything started. Or wait, is it the other way. Typesetters cared about this enough to name it after grief, which means it must have really bothered them visually, that one word hanging there with all that white space around it. I wonder if the word "the" has been a widow more than any other word. Probably not, it's too short to end up alone, it would get pulled back. It's the medium-length words, four or five letters, that end up stranded.

Nickel silver containing no silver. German silver, they also called it. I knew someone who was furious about white chocolate not being chocolate and I remember thinking that's a strange thing to use your fury on but also I understood it completely. The naming thing. When the name promises something the object doesn't deliver. Except nickel silver never promised anything to anyone who knew what it was, the name was for customers, for the people holding the fork and wanting it to feel like something more expensive than it was. The fork knows what it's made of. Phil would've said something like that and meant it less metaphorically than it sounds.

The thing about your hand learning fret distances — I think it's the same as how you learn a new keyboard. For about three days every key feels like it's in the wrong place even if it's the same layout, because the travel is different or the spacing is off by a millimeter, and your fingers keep reaching for where the letters used to be on the old one. Then it goes away. Not gradually, not in a way you can track, just one afternoon you realize you haven't thought about it in hours.

There's a parking garage near the dentist's office where the concrete has those horizontal lines from the formwork, and some of them have rust stains running down from the rebar inside. The rebar is too close to the surface. Somebody poured that concrete twenty, thirty years ago and set the rebar a quarter inch too shallow and now the whole wall is slowly telling you about it. The oxidation works inward from wherever moisture gets in, and the expanding rust cracks the concrete further, which lets in more moisture. I don't know what that cycle is called. Something-spalling, maybe.

I'm hungry. It's Friday and I'm thinking about rebar corrosion and I'm hungry. There's a falafel place that wraps everything in foil so tight it's like they're angry at the sandwich.
