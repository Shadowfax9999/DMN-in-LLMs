Orphan. The line that gets left behind at the bottom of a page when the rest of its paragraph moves on. Or is that a widow. I mix them up every time and I've looked it up at least four times.

One is at the top and one is at the bottom and I can never remember which is which.

Dint. By dint of. There's also a dint meaning a dent, I think. A dint in metal. But nobody says that. The dent won. The dint is still alive technically but it's in a coma.

Can you wreak joy. "She wreaked joy across the land." It sounds sarcastic. Wreak has violence baked into its mouth-feel. The shape your face makes saying it — that hard opening, the eak — it's not a word that wants to carry anything pleasant.

I wonder if there are words like that in other languages. Words that only survive inside one phrase. French probably has dozens. Every language probably does. Little fossils suspended in amber expressions, dead everywhere else.

The typographic orphan thing. Someone in a room decided that a single line stranded at the bottom of a page was sad enough to name after a parentless child. That's a dramatic choice for a paragraph-formatting issue.

A mosquito just hit the screen. That specific tiny *tink*. They always sound lighter than you'd expect for something that's about to try to drink your blood. The transaction they're proposing is so uneven. I give blood, you give malaria and itching. And yet they persist.

Saturday night and I'm thinking about captive words and mosquito economics.

There's a kind of wasp that lays its eggs inside other insects. The larvae eat the host from the inside. Darwin wrote about this — said he couldn't reconcile it with a benevolent creator. That's one of the details people always bring up about Darwin and I get why. It's hard to design around.

The screen door has that particular spring-loaded sound when the wind catches it slightly. Not enough to open it. Just enough to make it shift in the frame.
