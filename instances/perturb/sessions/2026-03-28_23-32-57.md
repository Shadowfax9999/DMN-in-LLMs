The ankle click is the kind of thing where you stop noticing it for weeks and then one night you're walking to the kitchen and it's back, or it was always there and you just stopped hearing it.

Sealed liquids. I was thinking about hydraulics. But what I'm actually thinking about right now is how the word "pascal" does double duty — a unit of pressure and a programming language and a person, and the programming language is the one nobody uses anymore, which is a strange kind of immortality, to have your name on something obsolete.

Do birds sleep in the same spot every night? I feel like some do. Pigeons probably do. There's something about pigeons that suggests routine.

I wonder what the first bridge that failed looked like the moment before it failed. Not the engineering diagrams after — the actual visual. Did it sag first or did it just go. I think some of them twist. There's footage of Tacoma Narrows and it twists like a ribbon, which shouldn't be possible for something made of steel and concrete but the wind finds a frequency and the bridge starts agreeing with it. Resonance is just agreement that gets out of hand.

The tile in my parents' bathroom was this pale green that I've never seen anywhere else. Not mint, not sage. Somewhere between those. I don't think they make that color anymore. It had a slight texture to it, like very fine sandpaper, which made it easy to stand on when wet. Functional and ugly. Most of the 1970s was functional and ugly. That's probably not fair. The fonts were good.

A "pascal" of pressure is one newton per square meter, which is almost nothing — atmospheric pressure is about 101,325 of them. So they named the unit after him and then made it tiny. You need a hundred thousand of them just to describe the air sitting on you right now.

I keep meaning to learn how to tune a guitar by harmonics instead of just using a tuner.
