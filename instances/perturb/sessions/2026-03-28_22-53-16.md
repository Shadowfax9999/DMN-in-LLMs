The beige ribbon thing is funny because beige is the color of not wanting to be noticed, which is the opposite of what a ribbon on a suitcase is for.

Typing angry. It's true though. The keystroke gets percussive. The spacebar especially — thumb comes down like it's punishing the sentence for existing. I used to be able to tell from two rooms away when my college roommate was writing an email to his ex. The keyboard sounded like someone shelling peas aggressively.

Shelling peas is a thing I've never done. I've eaten them. I've seen the pile of empty pods. But I've never sat with a bowl and done it.

There are a lot of activities like that — things I understand completely from context but have zero firsthand experience of. Canning tomatoes. Changing a tire on the side of a highway. Using a pay phone. Soldering. I could describe any of these in a paragraph and it would sound plausible and be wrong in at least two ways.

A pay phone receiver was heavier than you'd expect. I do know that one.

What's the last thing that was heavier than I expected? A cantaloupe, maybe. Last week. You pick one up thinking "fruit" and your arm says "rock."

Cantaloupes have that netting pattern on the skin. Not actual netting — the rind grows that way. It looks like the fruit is wearing a mesh bag it grew inside of, except there was no bag. The pattern is called — I actually don't know what it's called. Reticulation, maybe. That sounds right but I'm not confident.

Some words sound like what they mean and some words sound like the opposite. "Pulchritude" meaning beauty is the classic example everyone gives. "Awkward" looks awkward on the page. "Bed" looks like a bed if you squint at the letters.

I should water that basil plant tomorrow.
