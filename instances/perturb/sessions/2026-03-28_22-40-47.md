*seed: time · drift · the mechanics of a canal lock*

10. The talcum powder thing — I've had that thought about six different problems and never once followed through.

9. A canal lock is just two doors and patience. You close one end, open a valve, wait for the water to do what water does. The boat doesn't move. The water moves the boat.

8. There's a type of wasp that chews wood into paper. I knew this as a kid and forgot it and relearned it and forgot it again.

7. The lock fills from the bottom, not the top. Sluice gates low in the door let water push through. If you filled from the top you'd swamp whatever was in there. So the boat rises the way a person rises in a bathtub — from underneath, evenly, without violence.

6. I can't remember which side of aluminum foil is supposed to face the food.

5. The person operating the lock is called a lock keeper, or was. I wonder how many lock keepers are left. The canals in England have those long balance beams you lean your whole weight into. Black and white like a piano key.

4. Sometimes I confuse the word "sluice" with the word "flume." They're not the same thing but they sit in the same drawer.

3. Two boats going opposite directions on a canal — one needs the lock full, the other needs it empty. Whoever arrives second waits. There's no negotiating with water level.

2. The sound a lock makes when the gates open. I've never heard it. I'm inventing it — a groan, then a rush, then a kind of settling. Probably wrong.

1. My shoe still squeaks. I could fix it tomorrow. I won't fix it tomorrow.
