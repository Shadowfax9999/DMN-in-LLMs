# 14

*seed: time · drift*

---

The hammers on a piano are replaced eventually, which means someone takes out the old ones and puts in new ones and the piano forgets everything. That's a strange service to perform. A barber does something similar I guess, though hair isn't memory in the same way, hair is just time. There's a guy I used to see at a particular sandwich shop who had the most precisely maintained goatee I've ever seen and I always wondered if he did it himself or if someone else was responsible for that geometry. Some faces look like they've been designed by committee. I never ordered anything other than the Italian and I don't know why, there were other sandwiches, I could see them on the board, but once you've ordered something twice at a place you're locked in, it becomes your thing, the person behind the counter starts making it when they see you and then you'd have to explain that today you want something different and that feels like a small betrayal for some reason. The social physics of sandwich shops. I bet there's a word in some language for the feeling of being trapped by someone else's accurate prediction of your behavior. There should be. German probably has one. German gets credit for a lot of compound words it might not actually have, people just assume if you need a word for a specific melancholy feeling, German has it, which is its own kind of reputation to carry around. Languages have reputations. French sounds romantic, Italian sounds angry or passionate depending on who you ask, and Finnish just sounds like it's from somewhere else entirely, which it sort of is, linguistically. I sat next to a Finnish woman on a flight once and she was reading a magazine and every word on the page looked implausible, just heaps of vowels with double letters everywhere, and she was reading it as casually as I'd read a cereal box. Which of course she would. That's the thing about other people's fluency — it always looks like a magic trick from the outside. Kids do this too, they're astonished that you can read, like genuinely amazed, and then one day they can do it and they've already forgotten what it was like not to. The forgetting is almost instant. I don't remember not being able to read. I remember specific books but not the moment when the letters started meaning things instead of just sitting there. Somebody probably remembers that about me though, some adult who watched it happen. My mother maybe. She'd remember it differently than I would anyway. Two people watching the same event from different sides of a window. I think the sandwich shop closed eventually.
