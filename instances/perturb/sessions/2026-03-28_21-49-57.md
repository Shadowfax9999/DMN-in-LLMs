*seed: time · drift · rhombus*

---

Rhombus. A word that sounds like it should be an instrument. A brass one, played in a Balkan marching band.

Or a fish.

Actually what I'm thinking about is how some shapes don't have obvious real-world examples. Circles are everywhere. Squares, rectangles, triangles. But when's the last time you encountered a rhombus outside a geometry textbook. Playing cards, maybe. The diamond suit. But people call those diamonds, not rhombuses.

A kite is closer to a rhombus than a rectangle is. Funny that hinges keep doors from being kites and kites are almost rhombuses.

Things I can't picture clearly no matter how hard I try: the back of my own head. The exact layout of a familiar room when I'm not in it — I get maybe four objects placed right and the rest swim. What my hands look like when I'm not looking at them. The color of my car. I mean I know it, I could say it, but the actual visual — it's more like a label than an image.

Nine-something on a spring Saturday. The basketball sound stopped hours ago probably. Whoever was out there went inside. Porch lights on, TVs on, the neighborhood sealed up for the night except for the people walking dogs. Dog-walkers are the last people out. Then runners at five AM are the first people out. There's a gap in between where it's just raccoons and automatic sprinklers.

Carriage bolts. I keep thinking about that stripped one. The square shank spinning freely in the hole because I'd drilled it slightly too large. That particular frustration of a fastener that won't fasten. You're holding the bolt head with pliers and turning the nut with a wrench and nothing is happening, the whole assembly just rotating in place. Like trying to open a jar with wet hands.

The rubber bumpers on that cabinet door. Everything in a house is designed to keep hard things from hitting other hard things. Bumpers, gaskets, weatherstripping, felt pads on chair legs. A house is full of soft intermediaries.

I had a rhombus-shaped kite once, or maybe it was just a diamond shape, which is the same thing. It had a tail made of torn bedsheet strips.
