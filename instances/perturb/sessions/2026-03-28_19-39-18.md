# 11

*seed: time · drift*

---

Scallop shells have those radiating ridges, like a fan, like the way roads come out of a roundabout. I've never been in a real roundabout, only the ones they started putting in suburban intersections where everyone still treats them like four-way stops. There's a particular hesitation people have entering a roundabout for the first time — the car kind of lurches forward and then pulls back, like testing bathwater with a toe. I do that with phone calls. The moment before dialing where I rehearse the first sentence, which never comes out the way I rehearsed it anyway. Nobody calls anymore. I called a dentist's office last week and the receptionist sounded surprised to be answering a phone, like I'd knocked on a door that was supposed to be decorative. Saturday evening has a specific quality that's different from Saturday afternoon or Saturday night, it's the transition hour where you haven't committed to anything yet, the day could still go several directions. Grocery stores at this hour are half-empty in a pleasant way. The people in them seem less hurried than the Tuesday crowd, more likely to be buying one specific ingredient they forgot. Cilantro. A single lemon. I have never understood people who enjoy the taste of cilantro because to me it genuinely tastes like soap but apparently that's a receptor thing, it's genetic, which means there are people walking around in a world where cilantro tastes completely different and we're all just using the same word for two different experiences. That has to be true of more things than we realize. The color thing — the philosophical zombie question about whether my red is your red — but also less dramatic stuff, like whether the feeling of a hot shower on your back is the same sensation for everyone or whether some people's version of it is closer to what I'd call lukewarm. A friend once told me she doesn't have an internal monologue, that she thinks in images and impulses rather than sentences, and I spent the rest of that day trying to think without words and couldn't do it for more than about three seconds before narrating the attempt. Spring evenings cool down faster than you expect after the sun drops. There's about forty minutes where it's perfect and then you need a jacket and you didn't bring one because forty minutes ago it was perfect.
