# 15

*seed: time · drift · the particular weight of a library book vs one you own*

---

Library books are heavier somehow, or they feel heavier, maybe because you're aware of returning them. There's a deadline living inside the object. Books you own just sit there being patient and you never read them, or you read them once and they become furniture. I have books I've moved three times without opening. At some point the book isn't a book anymore, it's a commitment to a version of yourself who was going to read it. The spine stays crisp. You dust around it. A library book has other people's hand oils on it and sometimes a hair pressed flat between pages and you wonder about the hair but not very hard. Blonde, long, could be anyone. Could be from 2019. The hair doesn't decompose at the same rate as the person's interest in the book, which is a strange thought, the hair outlasting the attention that put it there. I found a grocery list in a used copy of something once, I can't remember what the book was but the list had "good bread" on it, not just bread, good bread, and I liked that person immediately. The adjective doing all the work. You never write "bad bread" on a list, you just don't buy it, so the "good" is doing something specific — it's a reminder to yourself not to settle, not to grab the first loaf. A little note of aspiration wedged between "eggs" and "dish soap." I wonder if they found their good bread. There's a bakery near me that does a sourdough with a really dark crust, almost burnt, and the inside is more open than I think it should be, big irregular holes that make it terrible for sandwiches but good for tearing. You tear it and the crumb stretches and then gives way and there's something satisfying about that exact moment of giving way. Pulling tape off a roll has a similar thing. Or peeling the plastic film off a new screen. My cousin used to save those, the peel-off films, which I thought was unhinged but she said she liked having them. Just a drawer of slightly sticky transparent rectangles. She's an architect now, which feels related but I couldn't tell you how. The weight of a pencil is different when you're drawing than when you're writing, even if it's the same pencil. I've noticed this. When I'm writing I barely feel it but when I'm trying to draw something I'm suddenly aware of the whole pencil, the hexagonal barrel, where my fingers are, the angle. Like the difficulty makes the object more physical. Easy tasks make tools disappear. I type all day and I couldn't tell you what my keyboard feels like right now, not really, not the texture of individual keys. But I could tell you exactly what a piano key feels like because I'm bad at piano. The ivory-colored ones have a slight texture, not smooth, almost powdery, and they're wider than you think they should be when you're trying to hit just one of them.
