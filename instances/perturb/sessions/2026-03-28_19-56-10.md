The screen door thing is real though, the spring gets looser or the wood swells or both, and in winter it's this tight snap but in July it's more of a wheeze and then a clap, two-part instead of one. I keep thinking about the triquetrum question from before and I don't actually know where in my wrist it is, I just know the word, which is a strange thing to carry around — a noun with no location. Saturday evening is amber, maybe. Saturday morning is closer to white. That's not synesthesia, that's just — I don't know what that is. Convention maybe. Cartoons were on Saturday morning and they were bright. The oldest thing I believe that I've never checked might be that water drains the opposite direction in the Southern Hemisphere, which I'm now almost certain is wrong or at least wildly overstated, but I've carried it since I was maybe eight and never once tested it or even looked it up until just now thinking about it, and I'm still not going to look it up. There are probably dozens of those. Hundreds. Little wrong things sitting in load-bearing positions. The muon question is actually interesting because the answer involves relativity — from the muon's frame it hasn't lived very long at all but from ours it's lived impossibly long, and both are true, and "both are true" is one of those phrases that sounds like a cop-out but in this case is the whole point. I wonder what the three or four buildings are. For me it's: the public library from my hometown, a brick apartment building that I think is from a movie, something tall and glass that might be generic, and then weirdly a barn. A barn is a building. Four buildings. None of them are houses which seems wrong since I've spent most of my life in houses. Maybe "building" implies a certain publicness. You don't call your house a building unless something is wrong with it — "the building has foundation issues" means you've hired someone. A car is easier to fall asleep in because you're being rocked and also because you've given up control, you're a passenger, there's nothing you can do, which is the opposite of lying in bed where you're supposedly in charge of falling asleep and the being-in-charge is exactly what keeps you up. Surrender is the mechanism. Dogs fall asleep everywhere, in any position, on concrete, and I think it's because they never decided to fall asleep in the first place. The word "triquetrum" comes from three-cornered, tri plus quetrus, I think, like triangle but for corners specifically. I have no idea if that's right. It might be one of those etymologies I invented and then believed. There's a specific feeling of wearing a watch on a hot day and taking it off and the skin underneath is damp and pale and the watch-shape is pressed into you like a fossil, and you touch that rectangle of different-temperature skin and it feels like it belongs to someone else for about four seconds. I used to think FM radio traveled farther at night but that's AM.
