*seed: time · drift · how a hydraulic press multiplies force through fluid pressure*

---

A hydraulic press works because pressure applied to a small piston transmits equally through the fluid to a larger piston, and the ratio of the areas is the ratio of the force, which means a child pushing on a small cylinder could crush a car on the other end, and there's something about that — not the physics exactly but the image of it, the quiet incompatibility between the effort on one side and the destruction on the other, like how a single sentence spoken at normal volume in a kitchen can end a marriage, though that's not hydraulics, that's just a thing that happens, and I wonder whether the medieval people who first noticed that sealed liquids don't compress thought of it as magic or as obvious, because it seems like it should be obvious, that water doesn't shrink, everyone who's ever overfilled a bathtub knows that, but the leap from "water doesn't shrink" to "therefore I can multiply force across distance" is the kind of leap that takes centuries, the gap between noticing and using, and Pascal was only in his twenties when he formalized it, which is either inspiring or discouraging depending on how old you are when you hear it. My ankle still clicks.
