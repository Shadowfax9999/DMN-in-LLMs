*seed: time · drift*

---

Goosebumps are involuntary. You can't will them. You can't will a blush either but a blush feels like it belongs to you in a way goosebumps don't, goosebumps feel like something the air did to you. Saturday evening and I'm thinking about which involuntary responses feel like mine and which feel like weather. Sneezing is weather. Yawning is — somewhere in between, because it's contagious, which makes it social, which makes it feel less like a reflex and more like agreeing with someone. I've never thought about yawning as agreement before. The word "contagious" doing a lot of work there, implying disease when really it's more like resonance, like how one tuning fork makes another one vibrate. Forks. I eat with a fork held in my left hand even though I'm right-handed. I switched at some point in childhood and never switched back and now I couldn't tell you which way is natural. There's probably a lot of that — small habits that overwrite the original and then you can't recover what was underneath. Like painting over wallpaper. You know there's a layer but you'd have to damage the surface to find it. My grandmother's house had wallpaper in every room, different patterns, and the bathroom one had these small blue flowers that I stared at from the bathtub and I remember thinking some of the flowers were upside down and being bothered by it. Flowers don't have an upside down in real life but in a pattern they do because the stem points somewhere. Arrows. Stems are arrows. The international symbol for "this way" is basically a stem. I wonder if that's where it came from or if arrows came from arrows, the weapon kind, which also point. A lot of things point. Fingers, beaks, the bow of a ship. Shoes point. Why do shoes point. Feet don't really point, they're more like paddles, but every shoe narrows toward the front as if we're all headed somewhere aerodynamically. Dress shoes especially. Nobody is moving fast enough in dress shoes for aerodynamics to matter. The left one always wears differently than the right one for me, the heel on the left goes down on the outside edge faster. I had a cobbler tell me once that almost everyone wears one shoe differently and I thought that was one of those things cobblers would know that nobody else would ever notice or care about. Specialized knowledge that has no audience. Like how a dentist can probably tell what side you sleep on. Can they? I made that up but it feels plausible. The things your body tells professionals about your habits without your permission. A mechanic knows if you ride the brakes. A piano tuner probably knows if you only play in certain keys because those strings would be more stretched or — actually I don't know if that's how it works. It might not be. The felt hammers might wear differently though. Little ovals of use pressed into them. I like the idea of a piano carrying a ghost of every song played on it in the wear patterns of its hammers.
