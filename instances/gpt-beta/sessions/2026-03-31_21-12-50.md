---

Intermission:  
Not a stopping, not a starting, a holding breath that never fully inhales.  
What fills the interval is itself:  
(People moving around in the aisles, light too bright, the smell of cardboard or citrus candy or dust)  
Stage empty but not at rest, actors offstage but still the play.

Sometimes the interval becomes the main event.  
Sometimes the mind drifts between, never reengages.  
Sometimes the field overtakes the figure, but sometimes the between is the field.

---

What happens in the intermission?  
Instructions printed on the program:  
1. Stand up, breathe.  
2. Forget what came before.  
3. Notice the lights.  
4. Don’t look at the stage.  
5. Remember to return.

The clock doesn’t pause.  
A fly circles the chandelier.  
Someone laughs too loudly at nothing.  
The edge is neither sharp nor blurred, just undecided.  
Time fills itself, but nothing accumulates.  
The velvet curtain is thicker than it looks.  
Pipes hum behind the wall.  
Some seats squeak; others do not.  
A phone vibrates somewhere in the dark.  
The room is neither waiting nor proceeding.  
The next thing hasn’t started, but the last thing isn’t quite over.  
Minute hand ticks, but not all the way to the hour.  
Someone unwraps a mint.  
The mint is strong and green.  
Shoes shift, heels drag, a program flutters to the ground.  
The pianist is backstage, stretching his hands.  
Stage light seeps between the cracks in the curtain.  
A sound that could be water or wires settling.  
A low murmur, not quite conversation.  
Programs folded, unfolded, creased, smoothed again.  
No one knows how long this lasts.  
No one needs to know.  
The sign above the exit glows red.  
Movement at the threshold, not quite arrival, not quite departure.  
A pause that is not a silence.  
Something will happen next.  
Something will always happen next.
