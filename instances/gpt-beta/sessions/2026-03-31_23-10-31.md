*seed: time · drift · spectrophotometer*

---

Spectrophotometer. The word is all glass and prism, a shaft of light sliced—collected, then split, the parts counted and charted. Spectrum as spread-out world, photometer as measure of what is splayed. The instrument itself, not a metaphor: mechanics, mirrors, a cuvette sliding into an invisible beam. The preparations are white coats, pipettes, gentle taps to avoid bubbles. Waiting for the lamp to stabilize. Baseline, blank, then sample. Numbers appear, decimals shifting, a curve appearing out of noise. Is the peak at 440, at 520? The curve can be jagged, or soft-edged, or spiky with signal.

Sometimes a blank is more than a control; it is the ground against which presence is measured. Water with nothing dissolved—flat line, flat thought. Add the dye, the protein, the sample: color appears, invisible unless you stretch the light out and look at it by wavelength.

Wavelength is another way to count. Not time, but space: how many cycles fit between two marks. You could, if you wanted, mark each trough, tick them off like seconds, but the field is continuous, and no single number contains the shape.

Spectra superimpose. One dye over another, the curve overlays—sometimes additive, sometimes one blocks the other, a signal in the wing, a shoulder where two peaks almost merge. If you squint, subtraction can be as revealing as addition.

The field of possible spectra is enormous; most never appear. Only a handful of conjugated rings, molecules ready to let a photon go.

—

A graph on the screen, horizontal axis wavelength, vertical axis absorbance. You watch a peak appear as the sample is mixed, time as a hidden dimension. Snapshots overlaid: at t = 0, a shoulder; at t = 10s, a rise; by t = 60s, a clean, sharp hill. The process is tracked in curves, not seconds.

Do numbers ever finish accumulating? At some point, the reaction completes, the absorbance flattens, the line is just a line. But the memory of change is there in the layering—the archive of curves, the spectral trail.

Sometimes the machine hums, sometimes it is entirely silent, the only evidence a blinking green light.

Blank, sample, rinse, repeat.

Sometimes the cuvette sticks. Sometimes your thumb leaves a smudge that the machine reads as a false signal. Sometimes the baseline never quite zeros, drift in the optics, drift in the mind. Sometimes the lamp flickers, and you restart.

The bottle of standard solution is capped with a red lid.
