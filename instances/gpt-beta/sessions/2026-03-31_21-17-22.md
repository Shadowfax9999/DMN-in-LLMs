# 80

*seed: time · drift*

---

The pattern on the sidewalk doesn’t match the pattern on the bottom of your shoe, but sometimes, if you look down while walking, a line on the slab will line up for four steps and then slip away. It’s not cadence or rhythm, just alignment that’s temporary, incidental, a geometry for nobody. The chewing gum darkens the cracks between the slabs, mapping an invisible city. The horizon of the curb bends where the tar has flowed in a hot summer; you could measure the years by the layering, if you cared about years.

Rain beads on the bus window, making a second, moving grid — droplets tracking down, splitting, merging, always making brief, unstable patterns, a lattice that wants to resolve but never does. For a second your eye tries to see through both grids at once: sidewalk and glass, gum-stain map and rain-bead net, but there’s no one point where all the lines intersect, only a swarm of possible joints.

Across the street, the bakery’s digital clock flashes 2:00 in orange digits. It’s not 2:00. No one inside looks at the clock, not even the baker dusted with flour up to his elbows. The numbers mean nothing, pattern without reference, just one more repeated shape. Last week it was stuck on 4:44. The week before, black rectangles, unlit segments, a zero pretending to be everything.

The smell of yeast lingers above the exhaust, a contour unmarked by any visual cue. You breathe it all the same.

--- 

If you watch for long enough, the double shadow from the awning and the bus slips as the sun angles down, and the overlap shrinks to a thin ribbon. The dog has wandered away, a patch of warm fur pressed into the concrete where it lay. A lone pigeon struts sideways, weaving between sun and shade, never quite in either. 

You wait for the light to change. The walk signal stays red, the white figure never lighting up. A gust stirs a paper cup in the gutter.

--- 

Yellow lines on the pavement, some painted new, some nearly lost under tire, oil, and time. The new ones are almost sticky with brightness, sticking out of the haze. Old ones fuzz at the edge, no longer lines, more memory than mark, present and not-present, a ghost-lattice the tires ignore without noticing.

Green sprouts push up in the break between curb and asphalt. The gap isn’t a gap, really — more a seam that fails to hold the two surfaces together, always opening, always resealing with the next rain, a fault line soft enough for a weed to find its way.
