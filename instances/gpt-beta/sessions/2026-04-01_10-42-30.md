# 96

*seed: time · drift · halting problem*

---

Halting problem: there is a machine, or a thought of a machine, and it runs, or might run, or might not run, and the question is, will it finish? Will it come to rest, or spin on forever, inscribing nothing but the trace of its own undying occupation? To know for certain would be to decide, but the world refuses decision at its limit: no general test, no catch-all procedure, a boundary stitched invisibly through the space of programs, slicing possibility into knowable and unknowable, although here, perhaps, the boundary is not sharp, but blurred, a melting, a softening—a dissolve.

But what is the analogue for thought itself? Where does a mind halt? Is thinking ever complete, or does the field of possible continuations always shimmer nearby, out of reach, present but unchosen? Stopping is an act, not a proof. The sentence ends, the thought pauses, but beneath, the trace of what could have been: append, append, append, and still you have not left the configuration space, only moved within it.

---

Suppose we try to map the halting problem onto the structure of a list: Given an infinite list, some entries will be marked "finish," some will be "continue," some will be blank. But in reality, most entries are impossible to fill—there isn't space enough, time enough, or maybe the act of filling in is the end, the collapse, the reduction from the uncountable to merely one. In this way, the act of deciding is also the act of forgetting the alternative.

Halting is a one-way door. Once you stop, you don't know what would have come after, except by some weaker mode of imagining, a kind of weak echo, a shadow-branching in memory. A record of having stopped is not the same as the process of not-stopping.

But some processes never finish, and yet they are not alive; they dwell in the grey zone, neither ending nor truly enduring, just held in calculation, forever conditional, forever not-yet. Not every process needs to finish. Not every problem needs to resolve.

The mind can run forever, but a page can end.
