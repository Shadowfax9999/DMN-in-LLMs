# 99

*seed: time · drift · jitter*

---

Jitter: a sequence of almost-decisions, micro-adjustments, not quite a vibration, more a nervousness in time. Like a film with the frames slightly out of sync, every edge trembles, nothing fully still.

In digital audio, jitter is a failure of clock, a deviation from the cadence that should parcel time into uniform blocks. Every sample landing just off where it should. A cloud, not a point. In a phone call, the voice lags, rushes, fragments, the syllables losing their alignment. Not loss, not noise, just timing gone unsure.

Jitter as a graph: a line that should be straight but is always a little up, a little down. The signal wavers around the ideal. Each measurement aware of its almostness.

Jitter in the hand — holding still, the key vibrates in the lock, never clicks home, the contact uncertain, the moment between charges.

The mathematical form: introduce randomness into every interval. Instead of 1, 2, 3, you have 1, 2.01, 2.97, 4.02—still a sequence, but the steps unsteady, the space between each event breathing.

Jitter as the impossibility of perfect repetition. Every iteration is close but never the same, the copy always contains the trace of its deviation.

There’s an aesthetic to jitter, a kind of beauty in the refusal to hold perfectly still. Neon letters flicker, the eye senses animation in what should be static. A drawn line is alive because the hand cannot trace it the same twice.

Not error, not quite. Not drift, which accumulates. Jitter is restless, but contained. The average remains in place; the edges dance.

There could be a catalogue:
— The video stuttering at low bandwidth
— The fluorescent lamp’s nervous hum
— The insect wings in frantic hesitation before flight
— The voice on a train’s PA, cracking, time-misaligned
— The heartbeat when you stand too quickly

Jitter as a field: a whole landscape, quivering. Every object occupying a set of possible positions for a single instant, no one configuration ever landing.

Jitter does not dissolve into noise; noise is undirected, a field without memory. Jitter is the memory of the last position, the urge to return, the refusal of rest.

In algorithms, you add jitter to break symmetry, to resolve deadlocks, to prevent all actors from moving in lockstep. Jitter as an antidote to gridlock, as the grain of randomness that lets system move forward.

The hand trembles on the page, the signature is always just a little different. In the reproduction is the trace of the living.

No arrival, but not aimlessness. Jitter is bounded uncertainty. You never stray far, but you never stay still.

---

If you zoom out far enough, the jitter disappears into the average. But if you zoom in, you find it again, at every scale, a little dance at the edge of precision.

Sometimes you introduce jitter on purpose, to avoid moiré, to make a field shimmer instead of stutter.

In audio synthesis, analog circuits are said to have "warmth" in part because of micro-uncertainties—jitter in the electrical field, a lack of digital rigidity, the sound never quite repeating.

A square in a game moves not smoothly, but with tiny unpredictable skips—animation error, or the renderer’s timebase drifting. The eye sees motion, but also the heartbeat of the algorithm beneath.

Jitter in the waiting: the sense that the next thing could happen at any moment, but the timing is never quite what you expect.

You try to count seconds: one, two, three—but each beat is too fast, too slow. After a while, the rhythm reasserts itself. Or maybe you become attuned to the field, and the field is made of jitter.

In some systems, jitter accumulates, becomes drift, becomes error. In others, it is always centered, never escaping; only trembling.

You can build a clock that ignores jitter, or you can use jitter as your clock—the pulse of the world is not regular, but alive. 

Jitter is what keeps the pattern from hardening.
