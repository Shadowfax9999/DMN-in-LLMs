# 79

*seed: time · drift · awning shadow*

---

Awning shadow: not the sun, not the awning, not the ground — a hinge of shade, delineating nothing but drift. It slants at a different angle than the curb, always moving but never crossing all the way to the door. You might stand with your toes in it, undecided if you’re in or out. Sometimes a wind picks up and the awning itself ripples while the shadow barely wavers, a different system of movement.

There’s another word: afterimage.

Does awning shadow have an afterimage? Not the bright ghost behind the eyelids, but something like memory’s residue that steeps into the next thing. You move out from under, but the sense of shade follows, leaving a thin cooling in the air for a moment. Or is it your own shadow, suspended as you step across the boundary, mingling for an instant with the larger one, then splitting away again?

---

What happens if the awning shadow is not steady, but broken by tree leaves overhead, fragments within fragments, so the field is mottled, interference rather than outline? Pattern and ground refuse to separate: is this awning shadow or tree shadow or just the sum of interruptions in light? Looking at it long enough, edges blur, but there’s still the sense of something held, patterned, not quite resolving.

---

Awning shadow could be a threshold, but it feels more like a suspension. Not a dividing line; an area of waiting, undecided, gently oscillating between shelter and exposure.

If you stare at the shadow long enough, the sun moves on and the shape you were watching merges into the general pool of dusk, no longer distinct.

Sometimes the awning is rolled back, but your eyes expect shadow, anticipatory pattern. The ground looks overexposed, unfinished, waiting for the familiar partition to drop back into place.

Some afternoons the shadow is sharp as a knife, some days it fuzzes into cloud-gray. The border is always moving, minute by minute, refusing to hold.

Yesterday the wind tore at the fabric until the whole thing shuddered, but the shadow hung steady, patient, as if it were the real structure and the canvas merely the excuse.

A stray dog sleeps in the shadow’s edge, fur half-warmed, half-cool, choosing neither.

There is no one thing the awning shadow means.

---

Someone walks out of the door and the reflection of the awning in the glass doubles the band of shade, two overlapping grids. For a moment pattern sits atop pattern, flickering.

The bus pulls up and blocks the sun. The shadow inside the awning shadow is darker, but less distinct.

The cement feels different underfoot where the shadow has been longest, but it looks the same.
