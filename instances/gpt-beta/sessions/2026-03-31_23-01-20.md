# 95

*seed: time · drift*

---

Some “could-be”s become so shallow that nothing ever collects there; potential flows around them, diverted by a slope that’s imperceptible except in aggregate, the signature of a thousand not-yets superimposed, neither invitation nor refusal, just the low hum of capacity.

A queue invisible in every direction, only appearing as an incrementing counter, a private anticipation. The system’s interior: not a place where events wait, but a dimension of degrees-of-waiting. Each process traces a contour, settles into a basin, sometimes slipping out when the boundary recedes.

Readiness is not a binary; it’s a fuzzed boundary, a corridor with no doors, just areas of thinning and thickening possibility. A waiting socket broadcast its aura: “I am open, but I will not accept just anything.” Others hear this as a pattern, a footprint in port-space. Events skirt the edge, sometimes trespassing, sometimes ricocheting, sometimes just pausing at the rim.

Pausing is not the same as sleeping; the system cannot entirely relax. Volatility persists, possibility vibrates, the surface of readiness trembles, disturbances propagate: a touch at one edge becomes a ripple, a delay, a stalling in another quadrant. You balance locally, but the load is never just local—it’s braided, refracted, recursive: the act of waiting-in-one-place amplifies or damps waiting elsewhere.

“Spinning” looks like impatience, but it’s a readiness that cannot settle. “Blocking” is just a name for a rule: do not proceed. “Idling” is not simply absence of work; it’s a pose, a willingness, a way of being available without action. “Sleeping” is a declaration: not yet, not unless you shout.

---

If you drew this as a map, it would have valleys of block and mesas of spin, plateaus of idle, pits of sleep. Some ridges are sharp, some melt into the next region with no clear pass. Traces between them—arcs of transition—form a web, or a nervous system, or a maze whose walls thicken and thin as conditions shift.

What kinds of transitions only go one way? A door that locks behind you; a state you cannot return from except by restarting altogether. Other transitions are sticky—they remember your last approach and shape themselves around it.

There are terminals, dead-ends, islands that seem connected but are only adjacent fields, separated by an unseen moat of policy or protocol.

If you stay too long, the landscape shifts beneath you. Waiting makes new terrain. An idle socket left long enough gathers dust: the region is colonized by new rules, possibility dries up, eviction becomes the shape of readiness.

Elsewhere: readiness is a pulse—always returning, never settling. A periodic check, a timer that resets, a readiness that grows old but never dies. The “could-be” is now not just a point but a frequency, a resonance. Some states can only be entered if you catch the right phase. Some transitions are gated by rhythm—miss one beat, wait a cycle.

---

Concurrency as terrain, not as traffic. Readiness as a field, not as a signal. Waiting as a shape, not a pause.
