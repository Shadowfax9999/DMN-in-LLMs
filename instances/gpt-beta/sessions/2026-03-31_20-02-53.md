# 47

*seed: time · drift · splint*

---

Splint sits oddly — sp, a little unsettled, lips pressed then slipping, l nearly skipped, a sudden int, splintering inward. It’s almost a sound effect — a stick, a break, a thin wooden thing pressed against bone.

Unlikely rhyme: flint. Not really a rhyme, but a neighbor in the mouth, a spark, stone, fire-starting. Splint, flint, lint. Lint like dust from a pocket, or the soft blue fluff caught in the dryer’s throat. Flint, flinch, clinch, pinch, spilt, split — a family of near-sounds and almost-meanings, fanning out, each word a branch.

---

A rectangle of sun falling across a table, illuminating crumbs. Someone sets down a glass and it leaves a circle, invisible until the light moves farther on. For a moment, there is a silent clock: glass, sun, crumb, mark, absence, a shifting record of presence.

---

On schematic diagrams:  
A line becomes a wire when you draw a little bump for a connection, or a circle for a node. Some diagrams use just lines; some dots, some nothing, as if the current can leap a tiny gap.  
Inputs on the left, outputs on the right, the world poured in one side, exiting as something new.  
Arrows, boxes, triangles for op-amps.  
A block with no name: process.  
A zigzag for resistance.

There are standards, but each hand drifts from them. Some lines are too close, others sprawl. A neatness is attempted, but the ink blots where you paused.

---

Set your bananas on top of your bread and the loaf will sag. If you forget the bananas, the bread remembers.

---

In the fridge, the light seems whiter than room-light. Objects show their edges — the milk is more solid, the lettuce more tired, the cold more absolute. Baggie with three grapes left. The peculiar shape of leftover spaghetti in a container, a slow spiral gone flat.

---

Try to imagine the shape that “next” makes. N, then a drop and angled leg, e, a curl, x like scissors stuck in the page, t, ending without flourish. Next feels abrupt.  
Near, neat, nest, net, next — a ladder, each rung a new opening.

Hinges squeak as the wind picks up.

---

Small patterns on the bottom of a mug, circles made for stacking, never seen until the cup is upside down drying in the rack.  
Someone once designed the ridges on a lid so your thumb would not slip.

---

Square of tape on a window frame, trapping a small fly that stopped moving last week.  
What shape is left when it’s gone?
