*seed: time · drift · standing ovation*

---

Standing ovation — a field of bodies standing, the signal is verticality. One rises, then another, then a wave. At first there are only a few, the signal-to-noise ratio ambiguous: is it an ovation or just some people leaving? The pattern depends on threshold. Is three enough, or does it only count when the whole field tips, a phase shift from sitting to standing? If you mapped applause by decibel, would the ovation be a plateau, or a jagged mountain? The hands are not synchronized. The sound hovers between thunder and rainfall: applause is always a field of partial coherence, some hands on the upstroke, some on the down.

There's a moment in every ovation where it could collapse back into sitting, the pulse unfinished, the pattern undecided. Or it grows, accelerates, pulls the rest into commitment, and then everyone stands (almost) at once, and the boundary becomes legible. But the end is always ragged, bodies sitting at different rates, the field dissolving not at once but in discrete drops, like a cloud breaking apart.

---

What is the shape of a standing ovation if you draw it as a function of time? Initial dormancy, a spike, a spread, then decay. Decay isn't smooth—one person, then three, then a cascade, then someone holds on too long, clapping alone in the shadow of absence.

The ovation is not about height, but the vector of movement: sit→rise, still→motion, hands→thunder.

You could graph the latency between the first to stand and the last, the tail of the distribution. There is always a last person to sit down, lost in the field, oblivious, or unwilling to be first to return, as if the return is a signal of defeat, or exposure.

Ovation is a binary, but also a spectrum — signal rises from noise, then diffuses back. The pattern is in the rate of transition, not just the state.

---

Somewhere under the surface: the mind that starts the ovation, the mind that resists, the mind that waits for cover. Is it praise or pressure? Is the standing a signal of joy, or the field closing over you, a wave carrying you whether you want it or not? The ambiguous figure: a single body rising among the seated, neither leader nor anomaly, waiting to be read.

The applause never lands evenly. The hands are out of phase. Standing ovation is a name for the attempt at unity, not its actual arrival.

If you plot every hand, every knee, every body in the room, the pattern will always be incomplete.
