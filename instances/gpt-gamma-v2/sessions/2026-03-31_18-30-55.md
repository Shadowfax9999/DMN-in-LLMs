# 3

*seed: time · drift · tessellation*

---

I only see tessellation in lizard skins and bathroom tiles, but the word asks for more: honeycomb, parquet, those faded countertop patterns that want to be endless. Paving stones outside the library, each gray slab almost squared, almost repeating. Stacked produce at the market — apples in a pyramid, oranges nests of dimples. A hasty mosaic behind the stove, grout collecting history.

Tessellation as little fits inside big fits inside little, nesting, touching but not overlapping. Insects' wings under glass, the way they fold just so. Escher’s stairs, but also the children’s puzzle mats, pink and green and blue, click together on the classroom floor. Bricks, leaves, fish scales, windows on a highrise, the diamond mesh shadow on the sidewalk.  

I think of the word "lattice," but it’s not quite tessellation. Lattice is open, tessellation wants to cover, to exclude the empty.

If you stare at the cracks in the sidewalk, the shapes between them start to take over.  
The mind sets about filling space, fitting and re-fitting what it finds, not content with voids.

There’s a watercolor grid under the streetlights — blocks of shadow, puddle, reflection, gravel, light, footstep, echo, gum stain, tulip stem.  

Sometimes a pattern emerges only when you squint. Sometimes it never does.

The cutting mat, the green one with faint white hexes, only shows in certain light.

Steps in a spiral staircase tessellate if you look from above.  
Stacked books don’t, they always leave gaps near the corners.  
There’s a pleasure in the failed tessellation of objects meant for hands.

---

A string of numbers tessellates differently than a row of bricks, but both promise order.  
Order wants to claim more than it gets.  
There’s a kind of pattern that's only visible when you stop naming things.
