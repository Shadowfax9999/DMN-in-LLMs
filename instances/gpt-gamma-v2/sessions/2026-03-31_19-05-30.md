# 18

*seed: time · drift · jacks*

---

Jacks: the game where silver metal pieces lay scattered on the floor, waiting for a hand to scoop them before the ball bounces twice. My palm remembers the sting from striking the ground, the way the jacks felt cool and hard and strange, starfish-bright in the dust.

Ball bouncing, rubbery, a hollow sound. The sound echoes in a gym, or on concrete. The game itself is impossible on thick carpet. Everything sinks and disappears.

The numbers count up, onesies, twosies, threesies. There is an order, a sequence, a necessary rhythm. If you falter you have to start again.

There's something about collecting, gathering, not spilling—trying to keep things in hand that are designed to slip through your fingers.

---

I can almost see the worn tin they came in, red paint chipping off, always a small rattle.

The shape of a jack: a sphere pierced by spikes, axes crossing. It doesn't belong to the world of nature, but it could be a fossil, a seed pod, or an underwater mine.

What else is shaped like a jack? Burrs that cling to socks. A satellite. A virus diagram in a textbook.

Jacks on linoleum in the kitchen, under the table legs, impossible to find until someone walks barefoot and remembers. 

There's a pleasure in holding one, spinning it by its points, feeling the weight shift.

Jacks, jacks, jacks, the word jumps.

---

Strings for cats-cradle. Pebbles for hopscotch. Marbles in the dust.  
Play is always about what you can hold, what you can catch, what bounces and what rolls away.

Clatter. Repetition. The rule that you start again.
