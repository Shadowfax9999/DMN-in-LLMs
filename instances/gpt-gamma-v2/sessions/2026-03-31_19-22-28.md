# 27

*seed: time · drift · seed (tournament)*

---

Seed, like a starting point and a ranking, both at once.

Tournament bracket, double-elimination, names on a board, little prongs extending out, pathways not yet traced.

Some words keep their botanical shape wherever they go. Seed, root, branch, stem — crosswords love them. Etymology tugs at the sleeve. Not this time.

A seed is possibility, potential energy folded in, still. In tournaments, it’s hierarchy: number one, number two, lower seeds, Cinderella stories. Upset. Sometimes the lowest seed wins.

In grade school, first round always felt too early for anyone to know who was “supposed” to win. The first few minutes are chaos, shirts untucked, laughter bouncing off cinderblock gym walls.

Draw a bracket: left side, right side, lines converging. A kind of artificial destiny.

Seed also means to sprinkle, to introduce, to let something begin. Cloud seeding. A seeded watermelon, careless bites, black ovals spit into grass, forgotten.

Tennis tournaments use “seeded players,” but the feeling is more about order than growth. Protected, kept apart, at first. You don’t want the best to meet too early, they say.

There must be someone whose job is just drawing names from a bowl, arranging the diagram, not knowing what matches will echo later in halls or memories.

Sometimes a number-one seed loses in the first round. There’s a hush, a kind of giddy disbelief. The bracket splinters. Nothing is certain now.

Seed, in the soil, waits for rain. Seed in the bracket waits for challenge.

You can trace your finger along the lines to see who might meet whom, but there’s always something that doesn’t go as planned.

A bracket left pinned to a corkboard all year, yellowed paper, lines filled in with pencil, fingerprints smudged.

Someone didn’t show up for their match. An empty space in the tree, a line that leads nowhere.
