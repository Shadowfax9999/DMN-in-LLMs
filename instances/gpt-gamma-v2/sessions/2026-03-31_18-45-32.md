# 11

*seed: time · drift · dovetail*

---

Dovetail. The word itself fits together, two soft syllables sliding into each other, no friction at the seam. The image is wood, pale and close-grained, sharp corners interlocking, a fit so precise you feel embarrassed to look directly at it. The way a drawer slides home, guided by nothing you can see.

Doves don’t have tails like that. Their tails are fans, soft, opening in surprise, closing against the sky.

A joint that holds because of what it isn’t — not square, not flush, notched so the stress disappears somewhere invisible.

There are names for the parts — pin, tail, socket — but those names fall away once things are attached. Once it’s together, you just have a corner, a box, a drawer in a desk. Only the back side knows what keeps it from falling apart.

---

Some words, when you say them, leave an aftertaste. “Dovetail” lingers on the roof of your mouth, all tongue and lip and nothing sharp. Apricot. Envelope. Gossamer.

There’s probably a bird you’ve never seen whose whole life is spent stitching the air back together after something passes through.

---

I have never made a dovetail joint.

Some people are afraid of cutting the pieces wrong, afraid of the lines not meeting. Others are afraid of the silence after the cut, the moment where something is supposed to fit, but doesn’t. The gap is so small you could hide it with glue, or with dust from the sanding block.

A row of drawers along the west wall, none of them the same size.

One of them is always open, just a crack.
