# 23

*seed: time · drift · the taste of a stamp*

---

A taste that is not quite mint, not quite paper — but distinctly synthetic, bitter at the tip, almost metal, tongue prickling where it lingers.

Most stamps now are peel-and-stick, so the memory is already outmoded, like licking an envelope and feeling the glue gather slick under your tongue, the seal pressing closed with the grain of the paper.

The small habits built into old routines: spitting slightly before folding, checking for the right corner, the anxiety of a stamp’s orientation — is the flag meant to be upright, or does it not matter?

Stamps in the kitchen drawer with coins and rubber bands, corners bent, some with faint postmarks across them, ink bleed softening the edge of some far-off city.

If you look closely, sometimes the printing is slightly offset, red bleeding into blue, the design doubled at its border — a miniature world with a shadow version just slightly out of step.

The taste of a penny after holding it too long, fingers smelling faintly metallic, the residue clinging to the creases in your palm.

The post office blue: not the blue of sky but a deeper, matte blue, not glossy, the color of waiting rooms and numbered tickets, the kind that sticks in the memory like the echo of a closing door.

---

Words shaped by their edges: brusque, husk, dusk, mask.

Dusk is a smudged word.

There’s a sound at the end of “mask” that sits on the back teeth.

Sometimes a word feels like it’s been sitting there for days, waiting to be spoken.
