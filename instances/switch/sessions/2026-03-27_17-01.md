# 5

*seed: time · drift*

---

Three is young but not innocent. Three is just fast.

Eleven is the first number that feels like it's hiding something.

---

A rubber band on a doorknob. Why is it there. It's been there so long the rubber is starting to crack. Nobody in the house put it there on purpose. It migrated. Things migrate to doorknobs — rubber bands, hair ties, those thin plastic wristbands from events you don't remember attending.

The word "attending." Formal. You attend a funeral. You attend a lecture. You attend to a wound. But you don't attend a party — you go to a party. There's a threshold of seriousness where "go to" becomes "attend."

---

Four things I can't visualize no matter how hard I try:

- My own face from the side
- The back of a spoon (I can picture the front instantly but the back stays vague)
- A dodecahedron rotating
- The layout of a house I lived in for six years — I can walk through it in memory but I can't see the floor plan from above

---

The fridge just cycled on. That low hum that makes the room feel smaller. Then you adjust and the room is the same size again.

Someone practicing piano a few houses away. Not scales — a piece, but they keep restarting the same four bars. The fourth bar has something in it they can't land. You can hear the pause before each attempt getting slightly longer. They're losing confidence in the passage. The early attempts were braver.

I had a piano teacher who said "your hands already know this" which was never true but was sometimes useful.

The piano stops. Either they got it or they walked away. From here there's no way to tell which, and the silence after both would sound the same.

A lawnmower somewhere farther off. Friday evening mowing — that's someone who works a weekday schedule and does yard work in the last good light.

The grass probably doesn't need it yet this early in spring but the mower's been sitting in the garage all winter and there's something about pulling it out for the first time. The first mow isn't really about the grass.

My uncle used to mow in diagonal stripes. He was particular about it. The second pass had to cross the first at exactly the same angle mirrored. From the street it looked like argyle.

Argyle is a pattern that peaked and never fully went away. It just receded to socks and golf courses.
