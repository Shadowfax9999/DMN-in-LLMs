The scientist would catch the ceiling fan. Third rotation, not every rotation. That's a specific periodicity — something's loose, probably a blade slightly heavier than the others, or a worn bearing that catches at one point in the cycle. The regularity is the data. The fact that it's *the third* and not random means there's a mechanical explanation. A scientist reads "you stop hearing it and then you hear it again" and thinks: habituation. Neural fatigue in the auditory cortex. The signal doesn't disappear — your brain just stops flagging it. Then something shifts your attention and the gating resets.

The poet wouldn't notice any of that. The poet would stay with "not the walnut."

That's the line. The whole color-taste passage is interesting but "like the inside of a walnut shell, not the walnut" is where the precision lives. The *not* is doing everything. It's carving the thought into exactly the right shape by saying what it isn't. The poet recognizes that move — it's how metaphor actually works when it's working. You don't say what a thing is like. You say what it's *almost* like and then you cut away the part that's wrong.

---

What the scientist misses: "three is young." Not because it's unscientific — there's actual research on ordinal linguistic personification — but because a scientist would immediately reach for the research. The scientist wants to know *why* three is young. The poet just knows it is. Three is young. Obviously. Of course it is.

What the poet misses: "orange would taste like nothing but feel fizzy." A scientist would flag that dissociation between taste and sensation. That's two different neural pathways being activated separately by a single imagined stimulus. The poet reads it and thinks it's a nice line. The scientist reads it and thinks: why would a color produce a tactile response but not a gustatory one?

Actually I'm not sure either of them would miss that. Maybe the question is wrong. Maybe a scientist and a poet notice all the same things and just do different things afterward. The scientist follows up. The poet sits with it.

Five is reliable, though. That one I just believe.
