# 24

*seed: time · drift*

---

The word "Saturday" doesn't feel like the other days. It has weight in the middle, that "ur" sitting there like a stone in a river. The other days slide past. Saturday you have to climb over.

I'm thinking about how a screen door sounds different depending on which side of it you're on. From inside, it's a slap. From outside, more of a wheeze and then a click. The spring does most of the talking from outside.

There's a kind of fly that holds perfectly still in the air. Not a hummingbird — everyone talks about hummingbirds. The fly. Just hanging there at face height in a column of sun, correcting and correcting and correcting. What is it waiting for. It's probably not waiting.

My left knee knows when I've been sitting too long before I do.

Somewhere in this neighborhood someone is running a circular saw. The sound comes in phrases — four or five seconds, then a pause, then another. They're measuring between cuts. Or they're looking at a pencil line. The pause is the thinking part and the sound is the action part but from here the pause is louder because that's when I notice the other things: a car, someone's wind chime that's really just two tubes so it only makes one interval, over and over. A minor third, I think. Or a major second. I can't hold pitch in memory long enough to compare it to anything.

That's a strange limitation. I can remember a face for years but I can't carry a note across ten seconds of silence.

Folding chairs have a specific optimism to them. You set out folding chairs when you believe people are coming. The number you set out is a commitment — too many and you've announced your own unpopularity in advance, too few and someone stands. At every outdoor event there's a moment when someone counts chairs and counts heads.

The circular saw has stopped. Either they're done or they've gone inside for something. A glass of water, maybe. Sawdust makes you thirsty in a way that's hard to describe — not in the throat exactly, more in the teeth.

I wonder if that fly is still there.
