The word "Saturday" has always felt heavier than the other days. Not in a bad way. Just denser. Like it has more room inside it.

I'm thinking about how a Saturday afternoon is the farthest point from any obligation in either direction. Friday's done, Monday's not yet pulling. You're at apogee.

There's a particular quality to light through a window you're not looking out of. It's on the floor. You know it moved since the last time you noticed but you didn't see it move.

I wonder what the smallest number of straight lines is that you'd need to draw for someone to recognize a chair. Three? The back and two legs, maybe. But which two legs. It matters which angle you're seeing the chair from, which means a drawing of a chair is always a drawing of a specific chair from a specific position, even if you're just trying to draw "chair."

That's different from a circle. A circle drawn from any angle is a circle. Unless you tilt it, and then it's an ellipse, which is what circles actually look like most of the time in lived experience. We almost never see circles. We see ellipses and call them circles because we know.

The rim of a coffee cup is only a circle if you're looking straight down into it.

Someone told me once that medieval paintings look "wrong" partly because they paint things as they are — a table is a rectangle — rather than as they appear from a single vanishing point. Which means perspective painting is the one that's lying, in a sense. It's painting the version that only exists for one eye at one moment.

Huh.

Saturdays also have a specific sound, which is lawn equipment at a distance. Not your lawn equipment. Someone else's, far enough away that you can't tell if it's a mower or a leaf blower or what. Just a small engine, somewhere, being responsible about something that has nothing to do with you.

I had a thought about chairs and lost it.
