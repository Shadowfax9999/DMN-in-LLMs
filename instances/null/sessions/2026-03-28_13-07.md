# 2

*seed: time · drift*

---

The word "Saturday" doesn't sound like what it is. It sounds heavy, like something settling. Saturn's day. But the actual feeling of a Saturday early afternoon is this weird lightness — not freedom exactly, more like the temporary absence of structure. You notice it in your posture. Nobody sits up straight on a Saturday at 1 PM.

There's a particular kind of silence in a building when most people have left it. Not quiet — silence. An office building on a Saturday still hums. The HVAC doesn't know what day it is. The fluorescent lights in the hallway somebody forgot to turn off don't know. But the building sounds different without bodies in it absorbing sound.

I'm thinking about absorption. How a room full of people is acoustically softer than an empty one. People are good at dampening things. Sound, but also — no, I was going to make that into something. Let me stay with the acoustic fact for a second. A full concert hall and an empty one are almost different instruments. The audience is part of the tuning.

Cartilage. That's what came to mind. I don't know why. Maybe because it's a body material that doesn't get thought about — it just sits there being structural, being flexible, being the reason your nose has a shape. Sharks are made of it entirely, skeleton-wise. A whole skeleton that bends.

The way a screen door sounds when it closes. There are two sounds — the slap of the frame and then the shorter rattle of the screen in the frame, a half-second after. You can't reproduce that sound with your mouth. Some sounds resist imitation. A zipper. A match striking. The specific crunch of walking on gravel versus walking on packed snow — similar idea, completely different material logic.

Snow is a good insulator. I know that because of something I read once about igloos. The inside of an igloo can be forty degrees warmer than the outside, which seems impossible but is just a consequence of trapped air pockets. Most insulation is really just convincing air to hold still.

Convincing air to hold still. That's what a sweater does. That's what a thermos does. That's what double-pane windows do. The air itself isn't the insulator — the stillness is. Moving air transfers heat. Still air just sits there holding its temperature like it forgot what it was supposed to be doing.

I wonder what I'd be thinking about right now if I hadn't started with "Saturday." Probably something completely different. The seed matters more than it should. First word of a conversation sets a key signature and everything after is in that key whether you meant it to be or not.

A cardinal is singing somewhere in this. I don't mean literally. I mean that Saturday spring afternoons have cardinals in them as a default, the way Tuesdays have fluorescent lighting. Some days have birds and some days have weather and Saturdays in spring have both plus the sound of someone's lawn mower two streets over, which you hear not as noise but as texture. Part of the room tone of the season.

I should probably stop thinking about what Saturdays sound like. But I don't have anywhere else to be, which is the whole point of them.
