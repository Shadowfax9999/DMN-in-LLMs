*seed: time · drift*

The word "Saturday" has always felt heavier than it should. Two syllables doing all that lifting. Saturn's day. Though nobody thinks about Saturn when they say it.

There's a particular quality to afternoon light when you're not doing anything with it. Not golden-hour light, not the light people photograph. Just the regular kind. The kind that falls on the back of a chair and stays there.

I've been thinking about how a rubber band works. Not the chemistry — just the sensation. You stretch it between two fingers and there's a point where the resistance changes. It gets harder faster. The rubber band knows something about its own limit that your fingers are only guessing at. And then if you hold it there, stretched, it warms up slightly. I never knew why. I still don't, actually. It just does.

Somewhere in a drawer right now there's a rubber band that's been in there long enough to lose its stretch entirely. It's just a brittle loop. You pick it up and it snaps without being pulled. How long does that take? A year? Three years? It depends on the drawer, probably. The temperature. Whether it's touching anything metal.

My mind went to drawers just then and wanted to stay — to catalog what's in a junk drawer, to make it a thing about domesticity. But a rubber band that's lost its elasticity is also just a rubber band that's lost its elasticity.

Saturday. I can hear the word better when I'm not trying to hear anything in it.

There's a thing that happens with a song you've heard too many times: you stop being able to hear the melody. You hear your memory of the melody instead, which is slightly ahead of the actual notes, and the two of them together make the song feel like it's dragging. The song hasn't changed. Your relationship to its timing has.

I wonder if that's why people tap their fingers slightly ahead of the beat. Not because they can't keep time but because they're hearing the next note before it arrives.

A crack in a sidewalk. Not a dramatic one. Just the kind where two slabs meet and one has risen a quarter inch above the other. You step over it without thinking but your foot knows exactly where it is. Feet have opinions about terrain that never make it to language.

I'm sitting with that. Feet and terrain. The quarter inch.
