The word "Saturday" doesn't feel like the other days. It has weight in the middle, that soft *ur*, like something settling.

I'm thinking about how a screen door sounds different depending on which side of it you're on. From inside, it's a slap. From the porch, it's more of a exhale. The spring mechanism doing its one job over and over. Nobody repairs those springs. They just get slower until someone replaces the whole door.

There's a kind of bolt that has a square neck just under the head — a carriage bolt. The square part bites into wood so it can't spin when you tighten the nut. I don't know why that's here right now. Something about things that grip by their shape rather than by force.

A Saturday feeling: being in a room where the light has moved since you sat down and you only notice because now it's on your hand.

I wonder what the first doorbell sounded like. Whether people stood outside longer back then, or shorter. Whether knocking felt different when doors were thicker. I don't actually know if doors were thicker. That might be one of those things I believe without evidence.

The refrigerator just cycled on in my thinking. Not a real one. But the idea of that sound — how it's a sound you only hear when it stops. Absence making the presence retroactive. There's probably a word for that in some language. I'm not going to look for it.

Alan I knew a kid who could tell you what key a microwave beeped in. I don't think he enjoyed it. It was just something his ears did that other people's didn't. Like being able to see the flicker in fluorescent lights.

Carriage bolts again. The square neck. There's something satisfying about a fastener that uses geometry instead of friction. Dovetail joints do the same thing. The shape *is* the hold.

I should think about something else but I keep coming back to that screen door spring. How it has two jobs — closing the door and making sure it doesn't slam — and those are opposite jobs happening in sequence. Controlled contradiction. A lot of simple hardware is like that. The float valve in a toilet tank: rising to shut off the thing that's making it rise.

My foot's asleep.
