The word "Saturday" comes from Saturn. That's the only day of the week in English that kept its Roman name instead of getting swapped for a Norse god. I don't know why. Monday through Friday all got reassigned — Tyr, Odin, Thor, Freya — but Saturday just stayed. Like one Roman diplomat nobody bothered to send home.

I'm thinking about how a saturated sponge feels. The weight of it. How it stops being soft and becomes something more like an organ. A saturated sponge and the planet Saturn have nothing to do with each other but they share a mouth-feel. That downward pull on the first syllable.

There's a specific thing that happens with shadows in the early afternoon where they're short enough that objects seem to be standing on them rather than casting them. A fire hydrant on its little dark pad. Later they'll stretch out and become dramatic but right now everything just has a slight dark underline.

I used to think "spring" was a strange name for a season. It's a verb. The other three are nouns — or at least they sit still. Spring does something. But actually I think autumn does something too, it's just that falling is slower and less startling than springing so we don't notice the verb in it as much.

Fall, though. Americans picked the verb version. The British kept autumn, which has that sealed-off sound, that m at the end closing the door. Fall just drops.

A screen door is a thing that exists in two states and the spring afternoon is the season of the screen door being relevant again. For months it was just the inner layer of a double door and now it's the only door. The wooden one stays open and the screen one does the minimal work of being a boundary. Keeps the flies on their side of the argument.

I can hear a lawn mower but I can't tell from which direction. Sound on a Saturday afternoon in a neighborhood doesn't really have direction. It just sits in the air like the humidity does.

I had a thought about Saturn again but it left.
