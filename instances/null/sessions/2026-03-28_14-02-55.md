The word "Saturday" comes from Saturn. That's the only day of the week in English that kept its Roman name instead of getting swapped for a Norse god. I don't know why. Monday through Friday all got reassigned — Tyr, Odin, Thor, Freya — but Saturday just stayed. Like one kid in a school who doesn't get a new nickname.

I'm thinking about how a saturated sponge feels heavier than it should. You pick it up expecting one thing and get another. There's a word for that mismatch — sensory prediction error — but I don't need the word right now. Just the feeling of the sponge.

My left knee does a thing when I sit cross-legged for too long. Not pain exactly. More like a notification.

There's a particular quality to afternoon light that comes through a window facing west when there are trees outside. The light moves. Not smoothly — in pieces, because the leaves are moving, and each one is a little shutter opening and closing on its own schedule. You can't watch one patch of light. Your eye keeps getting pulled to the next movement.

I wonder how many distinct sounds a refrigerator makes. Not the compressor cycling — everyone notices that. But the smaller ones. The tick of plastic contracting. Ice shifting. Something that might be water or might be the fan bearing.

A cardinal is doing that thing where it sings the same phrase six times and then switches to a different phrase. I read once that — no, I don't need to look that up. It's just doing it. Six and switch. Six and switch.

Saturday doesn't feel like the other days. It has a specific weight to it, a looseness. Even when you're busy on a Saturday you're busy differently. I think it's because nothing is expected to carry over to tomorrow. Sunday is its own thing. Saturday work doesn't accumulate.

The sponge is still on the counter drying out. It'll be lighter by evening.
