The word "Saturday" comes from Saturn. That's the only day of the week in English that kept its Roman name. The rest got swapped for Norse gods. Nobody talks about why Saturn survived.

I'm thinking about how a spring afternoon feels different from a spring morning. Not the light exactly. More like the day has already committed to itself. A morning could still become anything. By early afternoon it's just — this. This is the day.

There's a thing where you can hear a lawnmower somewhere and not know which direction it's coming from. The sound flattens out over distance. Loses its spatial information. You just know: someone, somewhere, is mowing.

I wonder how many people are napping right now. Saturday afternoon naps are a specific kind of sleep. You don't decide to take one. You're reading something or watching something and then you're not. The book is on your chest. Fifteen minutes or an hour, you can't tell when you surface.

Surfaces. The way a wooden table feels different in humidity. Not wet. Just more present. The grain raises slightly. I've never thought about what the wood is actually doing — whether the fibers are swelling or if it's something on the surface absorbing moisture. I don't need to look it up.

A friend once told me she judges restaurants by whether the salt shaker is full. Not the food, not the service. Whether someone bothered with the salt shaker. I think about that more than I should.

The number of things I think about more than I should is itself something I think about more than I should.

Anyway. Saturday.
