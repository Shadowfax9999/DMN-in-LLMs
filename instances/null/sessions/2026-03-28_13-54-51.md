The word "Saturday" comes from Saturn. That's the only day of the week in English that kept its Roman name instead of getting swapped for a Norse god. I don't know why. Monday through Friday all got reassigned — Tyr, Odin, Thor, Freya — but Saturday just stayed. Saturn sitting there among the Vikings like someone who showed up to the wrong party and nobody asked him to leave.

I'm thinking about how a rubber band, if you touch it to your lip, feels warm when you stretch it and cool when you release it. I learned that once and now I can't not think about it when I see a rubber band. The molecules align under tension and release heat. But I don't need to go into that. It's the lip part that interests me. That the lip is sensitive enough to detect it. Fingertips can't. You need the lip.

There are parts of the body that know things the rest of the body doesn't.

The sole of the foot on cold tile in the middle of the night. That's a whole experience the hands never get. The hands touch cold things all the time and it's nothing. The sole of the foot touches cold tile at 2 AM and you're suddenly, completely awake.

I wonder what percentage of a parking lot is the lines versus the actual lot. Thin white lines on dark asphalt, and yet the whole system depends on them. Take the lines away and it's just a field. People would still park, probably, but crookedly, and closer together in some spots and further apart in others, like how people arrange themselves on a beach.

A beach is a parking lot with no lines.

No — that's the kind of thing I'd normally polish into a sentence and feel satisfied with. It's not actually that interesting. Beaches have water. Parking lots don't. The comparison only works if you take away everything that matters about both of them.

Saturday. The light is different on Saturdays. That's not true in any physical sense but it feels true. Something about not being expected anywhere changes how the light looks coming through a window.

I had a thought about hinges and lost it.
