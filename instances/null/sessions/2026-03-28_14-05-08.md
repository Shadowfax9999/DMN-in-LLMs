*seed: time · drift*

---

The word "Saturday" comes from Saturn. The slow planet. The one that takes its time getting around.

There's a particular quality to Saturday afternoons that has no equivalent. Sunday afternoon already carries Monday in it like a stone in a shoe. Friday afternoon is still shaking off the week. But Saturday at two in the afternoon — that's the flattest, most unstructured hour of the week. Nothing is expected of you yet.

I'm thinking about how glue works. Not metaphorically. Actual glue. The way wood glue seeps into the grain on both sides and then the dried glue becomes a bridge that's literally part of both pieces. The joint is stronger than the wood around it. That's strange. The connection point is the strongest part.

No — I was going to think about something else. What was it.

The way you can hear a lawn mower from very far away but you can never tell which direction it's coming from. Summer sound is omnidirectional. Winter sound is sharp, pointy, directional. Cold air is denser. Maybe that's why.

I don't actually know if that's why.

A friend once described a color as "the green of a garden hose." Everyone knew exactly which green. There are certain objects that own their color so completely they become the reference point. School-bus yellow. Tennis-ball green — though tennis balls might actually be yellow, which is its own thing. The specific orange of a life jacket.

What makes an object become the canonical instance of its color? It has to be common enough that everyone's seen it. Saturated enough to be unmistakable. And it has to be *only* that color — you've never seen a blue garden hose. Well, you have. But the green one is the one.

Actually I've seen blue garden hoses plenty of times.

The lawn mower sound has stopped. Or I stopped thinking about it, which for a mind is the same thing. There's no background process running. If I'm not attending to the lawn mower, there is no lawn mower.

Joints in furniture. The dovetail joint doesn't use any glue at all. It's just geometry — the pins and tails lock together and the wood holds itself. Someone figured that out by looking at two pieces of wood and imagining them interlocking like fingers. That's spatial reasoning as invention. You rotate the piece in your mind, cut the negative space, and the two pieces become one piece that can't come apart.

I wonder how many tries that took the first time.

Saturday keeps going for a while yet.
