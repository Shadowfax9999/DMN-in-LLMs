# 20

*seed: time · drift*

---

The word "Saturday" has a weight to it that the other days don't. Not meaning-weight. Mouth-weight. Three syllables where most days get two.

I'm thinking about how a spring afternoon sounds different from a spring morning. Not the birds — though yes, the birds — but the air itself. Morning air is tight. By early afternoon it's loosened. Sound carries differently through warm air than cool air, something about molecular spacing, but I don't need to go there. It just sounds wider.

A lawn mower somewhere. Not close. Two streets over, maybe three. You can't tell with lawn mowers. They exist in a distance-range rather than a specific location. Unlike a car horn, which you can point at.

I wonder what it's like to be the person mowing. The vibration in the hands. The smell is gasoline and chlorophyll at the same time. Two completely unrelated chemical events happening in the same nose.

My mind just jumped to: hotel ice machines. I don't know why. Something about mechanical sounds you hear through walls. The lawn mower is a through-walls sound right now. So is the ice machine in a hotel hallway at 1 AM. You hear it cycle on and you know what it is immediately even though you're half asleep. The brain keeps a catalog of ambient machinery.

What else is in that catalog. Refrigerator compressor. Furnace ignition — that specific click-then-whoosh. The neighbor's garage door, which you can distinguish from your own garage door even though they're the same mechanism. Probably the chain speed is slightly different. Or the springs are a different age.

I had a thought about springs — metal coil springs — and lost it. It was going to connect to something about tension and release but it left before I could see where.

That happens. A thought arrives, you see the edge of it, and it goes. Like when you open a door and a cat is sitting right there and then isn't.

Saturdays have a particular relationship to guilt. Not doing enough. Or not doing the right nothing. There's a skill to recreational idleness that I think people underestimate. You can be idle anxiously. Most people are.

The lawn mower stopped.
