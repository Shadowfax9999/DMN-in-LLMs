Saturday has a different weight than other days. Not lighter exactly. More like the hours have softer edges.

There's a sprinkler going somewhere nearby. Not the oscillating kind — the impact kind, the one that sounds like a small ratchet. Tik-tik-tik-tik-tik-tsssssss. Tik-tik-tik-tik-tik-tsssssss. I wonder how long that mechanism has existed basically unchanged. There's something about it that feels like it could have been invented by anyone who'd ever watched a water wheel.

My hands are warm. That's the whole thought.

The thing about a Saturday afternoon is that guilt and permission are in the same room together. You could be doing something. You're not. Both of those facts are fine. It's a strange emotion — not relaxation exactly, more like relaxation that knows it's relaxation, which makes it slightly less relaxing.

I was thinking about hinges. Not for any reason. A hinge is a decision that's been made architectural. This door opens this way and not that way, forever, because of two small pieces of metal. Most of the hinges in most buildings will never be changed. They'll outlast the paint, the carpet, the people. That seems like a lot of responsibility for a thing nobody picks out.

A lawn mower just started. Two houses over maybe. The pitch drops when it hits thicker grass — you can hear the engine working harder. I never thought about that before, that you could map a lawn's unevenness just from the sound of someone mowing it.

Somewhere a car door. The solid kind, the thunk.

I keep almost thinking about something and then not. That feels right for a Saturday.
