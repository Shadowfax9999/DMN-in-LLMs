*seed: time · drift*

Saturday has a different weight than other days. Not lighter exactly. More like the weight is distributed differently, spread out instead of concentrated in the hours between alarms.

There's a sprinkler going somewhere. Not close — maybe two yards over. You can't see it but you can hear the rhythm, that slow ratcheting arc and then the quick snap back. I've never thought about why the return stroke is faster. Something about the spring mechanism resetting, probably. But I don't actually know. I just accept that sprinklers sound like that.

A lot of mechanical sounds I've never questioned. The clunk a car door makes when it's properly closed versus the thin, unsatisfying sound when it doesn't catch. Somebody tuned that. Somebody at a factory worked on making the good clunk feel like a good clunk.

I was going to think about something else. What was it.

The way a shadow moves across a floor over the course of an afternoon. Not smoothly — you never catch it moving. You look away, you look back, it's somewhere else. Like the hour hand of a clock. You know it moves. You believe it moves. You have never once seen it move.

There are a lot of things like that. Erosion. Hair growing. The slow drift of a conversation at a table from one subject to another until someone says "how did we get here" and nobody can reconstruct the path.

A napkin just blew off a table somewhere in my peripheral vision. Probably not — I don't have peripheral vision. But that's the kind of thing that happens on a Saturday afternoon in spring and nobody picks it up right away. It sits on the grass for a minute. Then someone gets it.

I'm thinking about how different "warm" feels on skin versus how it registers as a word. The word is round and dull. The sensation is specific to exactly where it lands — the back of a hand, one side of the face, the top of a foot in a sandal. Warmth is always local. Cold is general. You're cold all over but you're warm in particular places.

Is that true? I said it like it was true. Maybe not. A fever is warm all over. But a fever doesn't feel like warmth, it feels like wrongness. The thermostat set to a number your body doesn't believe.

I keep almost making a point and then not. That seems fine for a Saturday.
