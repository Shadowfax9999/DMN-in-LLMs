# 32

*seed: time · drift*

---

Saturday has a different weight than other days. Not lighter exactly. More like the weight is distributed differently, spread out instead of stacked.

There's a lawnmower somewhere. Two streets over maybe. The pitch changes when they turn at the end of a row. I've never mowed a lawn but I know that sound the way I know a lot of things — from the outside, through description, through the way people talk about it. The slight dread of it. The satisfaction after. The lines.

Lines in grass. Why do they show? Something about the direction the blades are bent catching light differently. I don't need to look that up. I can just sit with the fact that cut grass has a direction.

Fabric has a direction too. The nap. Velvet looks different depending on which way you run your hand across it, which is really just the same phenomenon — tiny flexible things bent one way reflecting light differently than tiny flexible things bent another way. A lawn is just very slow velvet.

That's the kind of thought that would be satisfying to end on but it's not actually where my mind goes next. Where my mind goes next is: what does the person on the mower think about? It's got to be one of the more meditative chores. Loud enough that you can't have a conversation, repetitive enough that your hands stop needing instructions. Like driving on a highway. Your body does the task and your mind just — goes.

I wonder how many important decisions have been made on lawnmowers.

A kid is learning to ride a bike somewhere in the middle distance. I'm inferring this from the pattern — short bursts of motion, a pause, encouraging sounds I can't quite make out, then the short burst again. Could be something else entirely. Could be a dog learning to fetch. The rhythm of teaching something physical sounds the same regardless.

My left hand is doing a thing where it's sort of tapping my thumb against each finger in sequence, pinky to index and back. I don't remember starting that. Bodies have their own idle animations.

Idle animations. In video games, if you leave a character standing still long enough, they start to fidget. Shift weight, look around, scratch their ear. Someone had to design those. Someone sat in a meeting and discussed what a person does when they're not doing anything. That's a strange job. Observing purposelessness on purpose.

The mower stopped. The silence after a lawnmower is a specific kind of silence — it has the shape of the sound that was in it. No. That's not quite right. It's more like your ears keep expecting the sound for a few seconds after it's gone, and the absence of what you're expecting feels like its own thing. Then it just becomes quiet and you hear the bike-or-dog lesson again and a cardinal and the wind doing something to a chain-link fence somewhere.

I'm thirsty.
