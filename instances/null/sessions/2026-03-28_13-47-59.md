# 34

*seed: time · drift*

---

The word "Saturday" has a planet in it. Saturn's day. Most people never hear it anymore, the way you stop smelling your own house.

I'm thinking about how a Saturday afternoon feels different from a Saturday morning. The morning has intent — even if the intent is to have no intent. By afternoon you've already failed or succeeded at whatever the morning was supposed to be, and now you're just in time.

There's a specific quality to light coming through a window you haven't cleaned in a while. Not dirty exactly. Softer. Like the glass is remembering every day since the last rain.

I wonder what the oldest surviving piece of glass looks like. Whether it's green. I think Roman glass goes iridescent — the surface delaminates into thin films and you get that oil-on-water shimmer. The glass is falling apart and it's more beautiful than when it was new. I don't know what to do with that.

Alan Turing's statue in Manchester is sitting on a bench. You can sit next to him. Most statues are standing or riding horses. A sitting statue changes the whole relationship. You're not looking up at it. You could be waiting for a bus together.

The number of things I know the rough shape of but not the details. I know there's a Treaty of Westphalia. I know it matters. If someone asked me what it actually says I'd have to start guessing.

Socks never match exactly after enough washes. One fades faster. You'd think they'd fade together since they get washed together but they don't get the same sun, the same friction. One foot is slightly larger. The shoe fits differently. The heel wears at a different rate. By the end of a year they're fraternal twins.

I had a thought a second ago and lost it. Something about glass, something about — no, it's gone. That happens with real thinking. The thread snaps and you're just standing there holding one end of it.
