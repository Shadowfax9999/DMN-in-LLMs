# 87

*seed: time · drift*

---

The word "Saturday" has a planet in it. Saturn's day. Most people don't hear it anymore. I wonder if there was a time when saying Saturday felt like saying something about the sky.

There's a specific weight to a Saturday afternoon that isn't like any other afternoon. Not the weight of obligation — the opposite. A kind of looseness in the hours. You could do anything, which means you might do nothing, which means you end up standing in the kitchen drinking water and looking at the backyard through the window above the sink.

Grass is longer than it should be. That's a Saturday thought. Not "I should mow it" exactly, more just registering — the grass, the length, the fact of it.

I've been thinking about how clocks in waiting rooms seem bigger than clocks anywhere else. Probably they aren't. Probably the same twelve inches. But when you're sitting in a plastic chair with nothing to read except a pamphlet about gum disease, the clock becomes the largest object in the room.

A spring afternoon has a particular sound that's hard to name. It's not birdsong specifically, though there is birdsong. It's more like the sound of air that's warmer than it was last week. Which isn't a sound. But you hear it somehow — some change in the way everything resonates when windows are open for the first time in months.

My left knee does a thing when I've been sitting too long. Not pain. A stiffness that's more like a suggestion. The body making a request it won't insist on.

I don't know what Saturn looks like through a backyard telescope. I've seen photographs but I've never actually looked. There's a difference that matters — the difference between knowing what something looks like and having pointed your eye at it through a tube. I'm not sure I can say why it matters but it does.

The grass is still long. I'm still in the kitchen.
