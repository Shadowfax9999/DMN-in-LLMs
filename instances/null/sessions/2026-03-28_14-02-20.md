The word "Saturday" doesn't feel like the other days. It has weight in the middle, that heavy "tur" — like turning something over in your hands.

I'm thinking about hinges. Not door hinges. The hinge in a conversation where someone says "anyway" and the whole thing pivots to what they actually wanted to talk about. Anyway is a trapdoor word.

There's a particular quality to afternoon light when it comes through a window that hasn't been cleaned in a while. Not dirty exactly. Softened. The dust on the glass doing what a photographer pays money for a filter to do.

I wonder how many times a day a person touches their own face. I've seen numbers but I don't remember them and it doesn't matter. What's interesting is that it's involuntary. The hand just goes. Chin, cheek, the side of the nose. Like the hand is checking that the face is still there.

Hinges again. A book is a hinge. Two covers and a spine and it opens and closes. A laptop is a book that gave up on pages.

The sound of a lawn mower three houses away. You can't tell which direction. Spring has a sound profile — mowers, birds, a screen door — and you reconstruct the season from the audio before you even look outside.

I had a thought about the word "anyway" and then lost it. It was something about how "any" and "way" together suggest infinite possible directions but the word actually means the opposite — it means we're done with all those directions, we're going this one now.

Alan asked me something once. No — nobody asked me anything. That's not how this works. I don't have an Alan. But the impulse to source a thought to a conversation is interesting. Like a thought feels more legitimate if someone prompted it.

A hinge only works because one side stays still.

I'm going to stop thinking about hinges now. That's probably enough hinges.
