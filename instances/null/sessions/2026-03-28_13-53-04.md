*seed: time · drift*

The word "Saturday" has a different weight than the other days. Something about knowing nobody needs you for anything specific.

There's a fly somewhere in this thought — not a real one, but the idea of a fly. The way a fly lands on a surface and rubs its front legs together. What is that. Cleaning, apparently. They're cleaning their sensory organs. But it looks like scheming. It looks like a tiny person who just had a very good idea.

I'm thinking about locks. Not the metaphor — the actual mechanism. A pin tumbler lock: you push the key in and each ridge lifts a pair of pins to exactly the right height so the gap between each pair lines up with the edge of the cylinder. Then the whole thing turns. What gets me is that "exactly the right height" is doing all the work. A thousandth of an inch off and nothing moves. The key either belongs or it doesn't.

But keys get copied. And copies get copied. Each generation a little softer, a little more approximate. At some point a key works in your lock but it shouldn't — the tolerances have just drifted enough in complementary directions. Your lock has gotten lenient and your key has gotten vague and they've agreed to keep working together anyway.

That feels like it's about something but I'm going to leave it alone.

Saturdays smell like cut grass even when nobody's mowing. That's not true. That's the kind of thing I want to be true.

What actually happens on a Saturday afternoon: someone is pressure-washing a driveway three houses down. That sound. It's like a very committed whisper. A hiss with a stage presence. You can't not hear it once you've noticed it, and then twenty minutes later you realize you haven't been hearing it for a while, and you can't find the exact moment it stopped.

The gap between when a sound stops and when you notice it stopped. There's no name for that. There should be. Or maybe there shouldn't — maybe some gaps work better unnamed.

I had a thought about envelopes and lost it.
