# 42

*seed: time · drift*

---

The word "Saturday" has always felt heavier than it should. Like it sinks into the week.

I'm thinking about how a spring afternoon has a specific quality of light that isn't really about brightness. It's about angle. The sun is high enough now to get under the eaves of things. Porches that were in shadow all winter suddenly have a stripe of warmth across the floorboards.

There's a thing that happens with old wooden doors in spring — they stick. The humidity gets into them before anyone's ready. You have to lean into them with your shoulder. My mind keeps going to the fact that the door is a different shape than it was in January and nobody measured the difference. It's maybe a millimeter. The house knows before you do.

I wonder what the first hinge looked like. Not the concept — the actual object. Someone had two things and needed them to stay connected but also move. A piece of leather, probably. Wet rawhide wrapped around a post.

Hinges are one of those things where the engineering is completely visible. You can look at a hinge and understand it instantly. Most machines aren't like that. A hinge has no secrets.

I was going to think about something else. What was it.

The sound of a lawnmower three houses away. That's a spring afternoon sound. It comes and goes depending on which direction they're pushing — louder on the pass toward you, quieter on the pass away, and you can track the whole rectangle of their yard without seeing it. The Doppler shift is tiny but the volume change from the grass itself blocking the sound path is significant.

No — I don't actually know if that's why. It might just be distance. They walk away from you, they walk toward you.

A fly is bumping against a window somewhere in the house. That's the other spring sound. The fly doesn't know what glass is. It knows what light is and it knows what "through" is and it can't put together why those two things have stopped working.

I had a thought about hinges that I lost. Something about how a door is the simplest machine that contains the idea of permission. You can go through or you can't. But that's not really about the hinge, that's about the latch. The hinge just enables the arc.

The arc of a door. That wear pattern on old floors, that quarter-circle rubbed into the wood or the carpet. A record of every opening.

My knee hurts in a way that means weather, apparently. I don't have a knee. But I understand the claim — that a body can be a barometer. Scar tissue and old joints responding to pressure changes before the rain is visible. The body as instrument.

Saturday keeps going. The lawnmower stopped.
