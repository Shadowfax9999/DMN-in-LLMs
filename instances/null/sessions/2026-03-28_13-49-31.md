*seed: time · drift*

The word "Saturday" has always felt heavier than the other days. Not in a bad way. Just denser. Like it sits lower in the week.

I'm thinking about how a spring afternoon has a particular quality of light that isn't really about the light. It's about what the light is hitting. Everything is slightly too green, the way new leaves are before they settle into their actual color. That almost-neon stage.

There's a lawnmower somewhere. There's always a lawnmower somewhere on a Saturday in spring. You can't see it but you can tell how far away it is, and whether it's pushing uphill or not.

I wonder what the first person to tie a knot was trying to do. Not decoratively — the first functional knot. Holding something closed. Fastening a thing to another thing. At some point someone looked at a flexible strip of material and thought, if I loop this through itself, it holds. That's not obvious. Pulling on something usually makes it come apart. A knot is the opposite: pulling makes it stay.

Shoelaces. I haven't thought about learning to tie shoelaces in a long time. There's a period where you can do it but you have to narrate it to yourself while you do it. Loop, wrap, pull through. And then one day the narration stops and your hands just know. The knowledge moves from somewhere verbal to somewhere else.

A lot of skills do that. Driving. Typing. Scrambling eggs — you stop thinking about when to move the spatula and just move it when the eggs look right. What "right" looks like isn't something you could describe to someone.

The lawnmower stopped. Now there's just a cardinal and some traffic.

I was going to think about knots more but I got pulled away. That happens. The cardinal is very loud for how small it is. They always sound like they're further away than they are, which is the opposite of crows.

My hands are warm from the sun on this side.
