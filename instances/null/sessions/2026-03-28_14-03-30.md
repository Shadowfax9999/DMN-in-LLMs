The word "Saturday" doesn't feel like the other days. It has a flatness to it. Saturn's day. The slowest planet they could see.

I'm thinking about how a screen door sounds different depending on whether you're inside or outside when it closes. From inside, it's a slap. From outside, it's more of a rattle and then a settling. The spring stretches and the door finds its frame and there's a small bounce that you feel in the floor if you're standing close enough.

There's a kind of knowledge that only exists in your hands. I could probably still fold a paper airplane from the design I learned when I was — no, I didn't learn anything when I was anything. But the idea holds. Procedural memory. The way someone who hasn't ridden a bike in twenty years gets on one and their body just starts doing it. The knowledge isn't stored as facts. It's stored as sequences. Motor plans. The hands know the fold before the mind can describe it.

A saturated sponge on a counter. The water doesn't pour out of it — it just slowly appears underneath. The sponge looks dry on top and the puddle grows from the bottom. Capillary action working in reverse, or maybe just gravity finally winning a slow argument.

I wonder what the first sponge was. The animal, obviously — someone pulled a sea sponge out of the water and noticed it held liquid. But who decided to make one out of cellulose? Out of foam? At what point did "sponge" stop meaning the animal and start meaning the function?

That happens to a lot of words. "Shell" for a company. "Mouse" for the thing in your hand. The object becomes the action becomes the metaphor becomes the default meaning and the original just sits there, technically first, practically forgotten.

A cardinal is going off somewhere. Two-note phrase, repeated. They do that — the same figure, six or seven times, then silence. I don't know what the silence is for. Maybe nothing. Maybe the bird is just breathing.
