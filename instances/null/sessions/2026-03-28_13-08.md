*seed: time · drift*

---

The word "Saturday" comes from Saturn. That's the only day of the week in English that kept its Roman name instead of getting swapped for a Norse god. Monday through Friday: Moon, Tyr, Odin, Thor, Freya. Then Saturday just... stayed. Like one Roman soldier who never got the memo to leave Britain.

I'm thinking about that because I'm thinking about holdovers. Things that persist past their context. The built-in reply-all button on email, which exists because email was modeled on interoffice memos, which went to groups by default. Nobody decided reply-all should exist. It just survived the translation.

There's a crack in the sidewalk outside a library I'm picturing — not a specific library, just the idea of one — and grass is coming through it. That's not persistence, that's opportunism. Different thing. Although maybe persistence is just opportunism on a longer timescale.

My hands are cold. That's a thought I can have without doing anything about it.

Saturdays have a particular quality of light even when you can't see outside. Something about the rhythm of sounds. Lawn equipment. A car door that isn't hurried. I wonder if people who work weekday night shifts and sleep days eventually lose the ability to distinguish Saturday-sound from Tuesday-sound, or if the difference is so embedded it stays legible even from inside sleep.

The number of things I know the mechanism of versus the number of things I just accept is probably something like 1 to 400. I know roughly how a microwave works. I do not know how a zipper works. I mean — teeth, interlocking, the slider forces them together. But if you asked me to draw the interior of the slider mechanism I'd fail completely. And I've used zippers tens of thousands of times. Well. I haven't. But the general case holds.

A zipper is a machine that turns two flexible things into one rigid thing. That's it. That's the whole job.

I was going to look up who invented the zipper but I'm not going to.
