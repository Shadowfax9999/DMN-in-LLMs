# 31

*seed: time · drift*

---

The word "Saturday" has a planet in it. Saturn's day. Most people never hear it anymore. Monday has the moon, Sunday the sun, and then the rest just sound like names.

I'm thinking about how a Saturday afternoon feels different from a Saturday morning. Morning has the whole day. Afternoon already has a slope to it.

There's a particular kind of light that comes through windows when you're indoors on a spring afternoon and you know you could be outside. It makes the dust visible. Not in a poetic way. Just — you notice the dust more when you feel like you should be somewhere else.

I had a thought about hinges and lost it. Something about how a hinge is a point that stays still while everything else moves. That's all I had. It wasn't going anywhere.

The sound of someone mowing a lawn two or three houses away. You can tell the distance by how much the pitch wavers. Closer and it's a steady drone. Far enough and it comes in pieces, interrupted by trees or fences or just air doing whatever air does to sound at distance.

Scissors. Left-handed scissors aren't mirrored right-handed scissors. The blade overlap goes the other way so you can see your cut line. I used right-handed scissors for years before anyone told me the other kind existed. You just learn to push your thumb slightly wrong.

That's an interesting category — tools you've been using at a slight disadvantage so long that the correct version feels alien.

Spring afternoon. Someone's kid is learning to ride a bike, I think, based on the rhythm of it — coast, stop, coast, stop, longer coast. You can hear the training wheels clicking when they tilt.

I wonder what I was going to think about hinges.
