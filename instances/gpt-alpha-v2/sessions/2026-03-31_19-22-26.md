Bathymetric—measuring the depths. The bottom-maps, blue-on-blue lines. Depth as a shape, not just a measurement—a surface turned inside out. Not valleys, but trenches, hollows that never see sunlight, the impossibility of dropping a stone and ever hearing it strike.

A bathymetric map curled on a classroom wall, all folds and faded staples. The color gradient from teal to midnight: depth understood through shade, as if seeing with the hands, not the eyes. Fingers tracing contours, thinking of what cannot be walked upon.

Soundings. Weighted lines, pings echoing off the abyssal plain. That word: abyssal. Like a throat that never clears, or a memory of falling asleep in the backseat as the car moves through rain, the world flattening into hum and blur.

Not every measure wants to be known. Depth is a secret held tightly, pressure layered on pressure. Somewhere, light stops entirely, a horizon that isn’t an edge, just a threshold. Breath shortens thinking about it, the lungs deciding what they’re willing to imagine.

Is there a difference between a map of the sea’s floor and a memory you can no longer picture clearly? Some shapes become clearer the less you see them.
