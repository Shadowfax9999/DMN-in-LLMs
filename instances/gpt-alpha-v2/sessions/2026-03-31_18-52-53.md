# 11

*seed: time · drift*

---

The way a plastic bag hangs in the branches of a sparse tree — not stuck exactly, but caught in the smallest fork, fluttering sometimes, sometimes just still. The bark is scraped raw where once a poster was stapled, long gone but the holes remain in a ragged vertical line. On the other side of the block, someone’s left a half-full cup of bubble tea on the ledge outside the laundromat, tapioca pearls collecting at the bottom, straw bent.

Bent straws, two or three of them, on the ground in a pile near the curb, faded pink and green, chewed at the end. Dried gum pressed flat into the concrete, the color gone but the shape a soft oval, etched with tiny stones. The coins people drop and almost never go back for — small bronze shadows in the gutter.

The sidewalk turns from smooth to pitted near the bus stop. Someone’s sing-song voice up ahead, words unclear but bouncing off the glass shelter. A pigeon with half its tail feathers missing struts along the edge of the curb, fearless, pausing where water pools in the dip.

The sound of someone unlocking a bike, the clatter of chain and the sharp metallic click of the lock swinging open. The bike seat is torn at one corner; yellow foam puffs out, collecting city dirt. Paint scraped away in a thin line, revealing metal beneath. When the wheels turn, the spokes catch the light in quick, staccato flashes.

---

Inside now, in a kitchen with the window open. The hum of the fridge blending with distant traffic, a radio two floors down faintly audible between songs. There’s a bowl in the sink with a ring of dried soup at the edge, left from earlier. On the counter, two slices of bread, one heel, crumb side up. The slow drip of the tap not quite closed.

A jar of peppercorns with a cracked lid stands next to an unopened envelope. Half a lemon sits on the windowsill, shriveling a little each day, rind tightening, pale spot where the juice once pooled. Tile grout stained darker in the places that get wet most often.

Someone knocks a spoon against the rim of their mug, three times, then sets it down without looking. The chair legs scrape the floor when they leave the room.

---

The word “Tuesday” feels faintly dusty, not quite clear in the mouth. A weekday you pass through on the way to something else. If days had textures, Tuesday would be unfinished wood — smoother than Monday, but unvarnished, with a few splinters that catch.

I don’t recognize most of the plants growing in sidewalk cracks. Some seem to sprout overnight, impossibly green, reaching for nothing in particular.
