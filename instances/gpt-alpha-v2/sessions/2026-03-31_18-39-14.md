# 6

*seed: time · drift · subway door chime*

---

The door chime in the subway is three descending notes, always nearly in tune, echoing off tile. Map in plastic, the little bullet lights blinking as the doors start to close. People shifting their bags, making room, the vibration through the soles. Someone in headphones, one ear out, glances up at the wrong moment. No two trains smell exactly alike, but they all have a baseline: ozone and brake dust, a little wet fabric, sometimes a sweetness like distant gum.

There’s always an announcement, brittle through the intercom, the first word lost in static. Tiles along the platform, pocked and dark where feet always stand. Names spelled in mosaic, some letters chipped.

A poster peeling at one corner, the crinkle of paper as someone brushes past. Shoes pressed too close, coat hem caught between two knees.

---

I used to think platform numbers mattered, as if odd numbers were faster, or the lower numbers meant less waiting. The logic doesn’t hold up but the feeling persists.

Escalator handrails move under your palm, slightly tacky, moving at a different speed than the steps.

Some people keep their ticket stubs in a pocket for days, others throw them out before reaching the street.

The pause right before the doors close feels different if you’re running, if you’re standing still, if you’re inside, if you’re outside.

Between the carriages there is a rushing, cold wind, impossible to hear your own voice if you tried.

---

Graffiti above the window: a name, a date, a missing letter, marker trailing off halfway through a word.

Someone is reading a paperback, thumb holding their place, line by line.

Phone screen brightness turned low, reflection doubled in the glass.

Each tunnel curve brings a pitch change, a soft complaint from the wheels.

I can’t remember if the overhead lights are flickering or if it’s just a trick from passing pillars.
