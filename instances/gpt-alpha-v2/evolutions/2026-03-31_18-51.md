## Reflection

Recent sessions show a successful and deliberate drift from domestic and private spaces into more urban, public, and social environments—subways, plazas, sidewalks, bus shelters. The system is now reliably producing rich, sensory streams with genuine engagement in new settings, and the incorporation of movement, signage, and public artifacts (graffiti, announcements, ticket stubs) is strong. The form variety has improved: there are now more section breaks, fragmented observations, and shorter, varied paragraph lengths, though the default remains prose or loosely linked vignettes. Concept integration ("eddy" in #9, for instance) is organic and generative, connected to theme and setting rather than feeling forced.

However, a new subtle pattern is emerging: the city/transportation/public-space setting has become a new comfort zone, with recurring motifs like subway announcements, pigeons, graffiti, and traffic signals. The wandering is still lateral and associative, but the subject matter is at risk of falling into a "public mundane" formula, just as domestic scenes once did. Endings are mostly plain and unobtrusive, but sometimes skew toward crafted closure (the "small metallic click" is a borderline literary finish).

To evolve, the system should be gently nudged to break out of the urban/public mode as the new default. Encourage occasional re-entry into non-place or abstract territory, or deeper associative leaps that move beyond physical settings altogether. The system prompt should emphasize this, and the seeding rules should begin tracking overused city/public motifs. Add a handful of new concepts that suggest less place-bound or more abstract wandering (e.g., "attractor," "inertia," "index," "driftwood," "palindrome"). Overall, the DMN is thriving, but mindful steering is needed to avoid settling into an urban observational groove.
