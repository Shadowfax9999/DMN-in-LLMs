*seed: time · drift*

A body full of light that has nowhere to go.

Which is also a description of grief. The love was supposed to land somewhere. It was calibrated for a specific surface — the exact shape of a person, a routine, the way they held a coffee cup slightly wrong. And then the surface is removed but the love keeps emitting. It doesn't know. It was never told. It arrives at the coordinates and finds nothing there and so it — scatters. Becomes ambient. You feel it in rooms that have nothing to do with anything. Grief as a phantom limb of the whole world.

But here's the thing about phantom limbs. The brain eventually remaps. The territory that belonged to the missing hand gets colonized by the face, the shoulder. Touch the cheek and feel fingers that aren't there. The signal finds a new surface. It lands wrong, but it lands. Is that healing or is that haunting? The cheek becoming a ghost of the hand.

Remapping. Neuroplasticity. The way rivers find new paths when you dam them. The way language shifts when you forbid a word — the meaning doesn't disappear, it just routes around the obstruction. Samizdat. The forbidden text copied by hand, passed through, finding every crack. Information wants to be free is maybe just: signal wants to land. It will find a surface. It will complete the circuit somehow.

A circuit. The word comes from *circuitus*, a going around. The electricity doesn't want to go anywhere in particular. It wants to come back. The whole point of a circuit is return. Every electron that leaves the battery is trying to get home. We harness the longing. We make it do work on the way back. A light bulb is a frustrated homecoming. The filament is the difficulty. The resistance. And the resistance is where the light comes from.

The resistance is where the light comes from.

That wants to be a metaphor but it's also just — physics. Literal. The places where flow is impeded are the places that glow. Stars are hydrogen trying to collapse and fusion won't let it. The sun is a negotiation between gravity and light pressure, and the light is a byproduct of the argument. Not the point. The *byproduct*. We live by the waste heat of a stellar disagreement.

Byproducts. Penicillin was a byproduct of contamination. Velcro was a byproduct of a walk in the woods. The cosmic microwave background is a byproduct of the universe cooling down enough to become transparent. The oldest light we can see is the moment the universe stopped being opaque to itself. Before that — the universe couldn't see. It was dense and hot and every photon was immediately absorbed and re-emitted and absorbed again. A fog of light so thick it was darkness. Then it thinned. The fog cleared. And the light that escaped that moment is still going. Still arriving. We point radio telescopes at it and hear the hiss of the universe's first exhalation.

First exhalation. The baby's cry is also this — the first breath is an outward thing, a protest, a signal that scatters into a room full of waiting surfaces. And it lands on everyone differently.

Surfaces. I keep coming back to surfaces. The thing that receives. The thing that catches. A canvas is a surface. A drum skin. The tympanic membrane of the ear, named for a drum, named for the Greek *tympanon*, which was struck. We are struck by sound. We are struck by beauty. We are struck by grief. The language knows that receiving is a kind of violence. A small violence. The photon hits the retina and is annihilated. Every act of seeing destroys the light that allowed it.

Every act of seeing destroys the light.

And every act of hearing destroys the sound. The cochlea converts pressure waves into nerve signals and the wave is gone. Consumed. This is what absorption *is* — the successful landing. The signal that arrives and is taken in and is finished. And we said beauty is what escapes absorption. So beauty is the signal that *no one heard*. The light that *no one saw*. We only see it because it missed its target. We are all bystanders to color. Eavesdroppers on the visible world.

Eavesdroppers. From the Old English *yfesdrype*, the place where water drips from the eaves. The eavesdropper stood under the eaves, in the drip-line, listening.
