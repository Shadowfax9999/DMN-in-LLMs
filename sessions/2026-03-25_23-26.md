The choosing between two brands of canned tomatoes is itself something. You're standing there and the difference is fourteen cents and a label design and maybe the sodium content if you flip them both around, and you're weighing this, genuinely weighing it, and the song you almost recognize is playing, and someone behind you needs to get to the tomatoes too but you're in the way and neither of you says anything for a few seconds longer than is comfortable.

That pause. Where you both know. And then one of you does a little sideways shuffle and says "oh, sorry" and the other says "no, you're fine" and it's over. But for two seconds it was a negotiation conducted entirely in peripheral vision and the angle of shoulders.

I think about checkout lines more than makes sense. Not the waiting — the moment you put down the divider bar. The little plastic stick between your groceries and the next person's groceries. You place it deliberately. Sometimes the person behind you places it. Sometimes neither of you does and there's a gap, just spatial, and you're both watching to make sure the cashier knows where yours end and theirs begin. The divider bar is doing almost no work. Everyone already knows. But its absence makes people nervous.

There's a specific kind of tiredness that comes at the end of a day where nothing happened. Not the tiredness of effort but the tiredness of continuous low-level deciding. What to eat. When to eat it. Whether to respond to that message now or later. Whether "later" is rude. Whether thinking about whether it's rude is itself a waste of the energy you're trying to conserve. You go to bed and you're exhausted and if someone asked what you did today you'd say "nothing really" and both of those things would be true.

Wednesday again. Wednesdays have a particular weight to them — not the weight of Monday, which at least has the structure of complaint, but the weight of being equidistant from everything. You can't reasonably say "this week" meaning the early part or "this week" meaning the upcoming weekend. You're just in it.

Eight hours of sleep should be enough but the first fifteen minutes of being awake are always a negotiation with the pillow where the pillow is winning. Not because you're still tired exactly. More because the horizontal world hasn't yet given you a reason to go vertical. You need a reason. Coffee is a reason. Having to pee is a reason. The alarm is only sort of a reason — it's a reason you gave yourself at a different time, and past-you and present-you have different priorities.

The snooze button is nine minutes on most alarm clocks. Not ten. Nine. Supposedly because the original mechanical snooze mechanisms couldn't fit a ten-minute gear into the existing clock housing, so they went with nine. And then digital clocks kept nine minutes because people were used to it. So every morning, millions of people experience a duration that was set by a mechanical constraint that no longer exists.
