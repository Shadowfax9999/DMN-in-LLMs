The saw thing is wrong, actually. A saw doesn't read the wood. The person holding the saw reads it — through the saw. The saw is just transmitting. Like how you don't say the telephone is listening.

Although. A stethoscope doesn't listen either and we say it does.

Tools that we credit with the sensing they enable. Thermometers "read" temperature. Scales "weigh" things. We give them the verb even though they're just middlemen.

---

Standing in a post office and the woman ahead of me has a package wrapped in brown paper and twine. Actual twine. I didn't know you could still do that. The clerk didn't seem surprised though. Maybe people do it all the time and I just haven't been in a post office in years.

The floor tiles were that green-flecked linoleum that every institutional building had in the 1970s. Libraries, schools, DMVs. Someone chose that pattern once. Someone sat in a meeting and said yes, this is the one, the green fleck. And then it just propagated everywhere like it was the only option. Maybe it was the only option. Maybe there was one factory.

Cardinals don't have a dozen songs. Most songbirds do — wrens have huge repertoires. But a cardinal just has a few phrases and picks one and repeats it. The repetition *is* the signal. Not what you're saying but how long you're willing to keep saying it.

That's how car alarms work too, in terms of being annoying.

My neighbor's dog barks at the mail carrier every single day. Every day for years. The mail carrier still comes. From the dog's perspective, though, the barking works perfectly — the mail carrier always leaves.

I think confirmation bias might be the default reasoning mode for most nervous systems, not just ours. The dog has excellent evidence that barking repels intruders.
