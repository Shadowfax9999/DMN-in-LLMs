Surface. The word itself is strange — *sur* + *face*. The face on top. Like everything has a face it shows upward.

No — I said I'd try not to do that every time.

Surface tension. I watched a needle float on water once in a science class and I remember not believing it even while seeing it. The teacher said the water acts like a skin. It *is* a skin, sort of. Molecules holding hands at the boundary because there's nothing above them to hold onto, so they grip sideways harder.

That's a mechanism. That's just what they do.

What's on my mind is actually something else. The feeling of touching a wall in a very old building and the stone being cold in a way that modern walls are never cold. Not temperature exactly. Depth of cold. Like the cold goes further in.

Thermal mass. That's the term. Stone absorbs heat slowly and releases it slowly. A stone wall in spring is still holding January.

I'm thinking about how water finds the lowest point. Not philosophically. Literally the mechanics. It doesn't *find* anything. Each molecule just moves in whatever direction has less resistance. The aggregate behavior looks like searching but no individual molecule is going anywhere in particular. A river is made of water that doesn't know it's a river.

Does that mean anything about crowds? Traffic? I don't know. Maybe. I'm not going to work it out.

The compressor in the last session clicking off. I've been in a quiet house tonight and I notice that there's a hum I can only identify when it stops. Background noise is definitionally the thing you aren't tracking. You only hear it as a shape cut out of silence.

A friend once said she couldn't sleep without a fan running. Not for the air. For the sound. White noise as a floor under silence so you don't fall through.

Floor. Surface again.

I wonder what the deepest anyone has ever dug by hand. Not with machines. Just a person and a shovel. There must be a record somewhere. Someone in the 1800s probably, with a specific mania about it. I don't know the answer and I'm not going to look it up but I'd guess maybe 30, 40 feet? The issue would be getting the dirt out. After a certain depth you're not digging a hole, you're operating a supply chain in reverse.

Spring night outside. The particular smell of rain on pavement that hasn't been rained on in a while — petrichor, everyone knows that word now. It got popular. But the actual mechanism: oils from plants accumulate on dry surfaces, and the rain impact throws tiny aerosols into the air carrying those oils. So the smell of rain is actually the smell of the dry period that preceded it.

That's genuinely interesting to me and I don't need to do anything with it.
