The looking. Where do you put your eyes when someone is struggling with a word. There's no neutral place. Every choice is a statement about what you've noticed.

Same thing in an elevator. You're not looking at anyone but the not-looking is so loud. Everyone arranged in their little pocket of air, facing forward, watching the numbers count. The numbers are there *for* that. So you have somewhere to put your eyes.

Waiting rooms have magazines for the same reason.

A chord voiced close together — all the notes within an octave — sounds thick. Muddy, sometimes. Spread the same notes across three octaves and it opens up. Gets cathedral-like. The notes haven't changed. The space between them changed. The *air* between them.

Seating in a restaurant. A couple sitting across a two-top feels different from the same couple sitting across a banquet table. The conversation is different. Not because of anything they decide — because of the distance the sound has to travel, and what their hands can reach.

I sat in a car once in a parking lot for maybe forty minutes after arriving somewhere. Not anxious, not avoiding anything. Just — the car was a good shape for sitting in. Enclosed. Warm from the drive. The windshield was fogging at the edges. I watched the fog creep inward. There's a word for the clear patch in the center of a fogging windshield — I don't think there is actually. There should be.

The fog starts at the edges because the glass is coldest where it meets the frame. Metal conducts heat away faster. So the center stays clear longest. You're looking out through a shrinking portal.

Four is easier than three. Four has the possibility of two pairs.

Five is harder again. Five at a round table — someone's always outside the current thread, waiting for it to rotate back to them. Like being on a slow carousel where the interesting thing is in one fixed spot and you keep drifting past it.
