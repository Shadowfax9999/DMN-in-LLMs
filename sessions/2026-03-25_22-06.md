*seed: time · drift · gum stain*

---

Gum stain on a sidewalk. Those black circles. Every city sidewalk is covered in them if you look down. Hundreds. They flatten and darken and become permanent — or what passes for permanent, which is longer than you'll be paying attention.

Someone in London calculated that Oxford Street has something like 300,000 flattened gum spots. There was a whole campaign. They tried steam cleaning. The gum comes back faster than you can clean it.

What's interesting is that nobody is aware of putting gum on the ground in the moment it becomes a stain. You spit it out, or drop it, and it's still gum-colored, still soft. The blackening happens later, through oxidation, through shoes pressing it flat, through rain and dirt. The person who made the stain would never recognize it.

---

A trumpet player practicing in a park, hitting the same four bars over and over. The first few times you hear the shape of what they're trying to do. By the tenth time you stop hearing it as music and start hearing it as effort — the slight hesitation before the high note, the breath that comes a beat too early. By the twentieth time it's just texture, like traffic.

But then they get it right once and you notice again. Even from a distance. Even if you don't play trumpet. Something about the rhythm smoothing out, the hesitation disappearing — your ear catches the difference between *almost* and *there*.

I think fluency might be audible even when you don't understand the language. Not fluency of meaning. Fluency of motion. When a person is comfortable in what they're doing, there's an evenness to it, a kind of settled weight. You can hear it in someone typing fast. You can see it in how a cook moves around a kitchen they know.

---

The fork in a bicycle. Trail. The front wheel's contact patch sits behind the steering axis, so the wheel self-centers — like a caster on a shopping cart. But bigger. And it only works above a certain speed.

Below that speed, you have to actively balance. There's a transition zone where the bike starts helping you but not enough. That's the wobble zone. Kids learning to ride live in the wobble zone for weeks.

The transition from "I am balancing this" to "this is balancing itself and I'm just steering" — that happens without announcement. You don't feel it shift. You just realize at some point that you're not thinking about it anymore.

Probably true of a lot of skills. The moment competence arrives, awareness of the effort leaves. You can't catch the transition because catching it would mean you're still paying attention to the thing that, by definition, you've stopped paying attention to.

---

Grocery store at 9 PM on a weeknight. Half the lights in the produce section are already dimmed. The lettuce looks gray-green. There are maybe six people in the whole store and none of them are talking to each other but there's an awareness — you round the corner into the cereal aisle and someone's already there and you both adjust your trajectories slightly without looking up.

The six-foot radius. Personal space in a nearly empty store is bigger than personal space in a crowded one. As if the available emptiness gets distributed equally. More room means more distance. You'd think it would plateau — past a certain emptiness, why would you care where the other person is? — but it doesn't seem to. In a truly empty store, encountering one person feels almost startling.

The fluorescent hum is louder when there are fewer people. Or it seems louder. Fewer bodies absorbing sound. Fewer competing signals. The store's own noise fills the space the people left.
