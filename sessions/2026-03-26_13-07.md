# 105

*seed: time · drift · glancing blow*

---

Glancing blow. Like billiards. Like hail off a windshield. The word "glancing" doing double duty — looking and deflecting. You glance at someone and the look slides off.

The Quikrete guy has gone inside. The bag is still on the porch.

There's a specific kind of afternoon where the light is coming from too many directions. Overcast but bright. No shadows, or shadows so faint they barely register. Everything looks flat. Faces look different in that light — older, maybe, or just more detailed. You notice things you don't notice in direct sun.

A dog is barking in a pattern. Three barks, pause, three barks, pause. What is it counting.

Things I can smell right now if I think about it:
- cut grass from somewhere not visible
- asphalt warmth
- that one specific tree smell, the Bradford pear maybe, the one people say smells bad but really it's just unexpected

The telephone pole shadow has kept going. It's across the curb now and into the street. The pole itself hasn't done anything. The earth turned.

Someone is learning trumpet in a house I can't identify. Just scales. Up and then not quite all the way back down, then up again. The ninth or tenth time through you stop hearing it as practice and start hearing it as the sound the neighborhood is making.

A pickup truck goes by with the window down and an arm out. The arm isn't doing anything. Just hanging there in the moving air. I've done that. Everyone has done that. You put your arm out and your hand becomes a wing and you tilt it and the air pushes it up and down and you're not thinking about anything when you do it. You're seven or you're forty-three, same gesture.

The Quikrete bag is going to get rained on if he doesn't move it. There's that one cloud.

---

The trumpet stopped. You notice a sound's absence faster than you noticed the sound starting. How long was it going before I registered it? Impossible to know. The dog is still going. Three and three.

The bag of Quikrete weighs eighty pounds. I know that from carrying them. You carry it wrong the first time — out in front of you — and then you learn to get it up on your shoulder. The second bag is always easier than the first even though nothing changed except you figured out how to hold it.
