The dirt out of a deep hole. You'd need buckets on ropes. At some point you'd need a platform partway down, someone to relay the buckets. So the hole requires a second person, or at least a mechanism, which means the question changes — how deep can one person dig alone, truly alone, is shallower than how deep can one person dig with help. The loneliest depth versus the practical depth.

I keep thinking about pulleys. A pulley doesn't add force, it redirects it. That's the whole thing. You're still doing the same work. But redirection turns out to matter enormously. Most of engineering is just: what if the force went *that* way instead.

Wednesday. The middle of the week has a sag to it. Not the hopeful sag of Thursday, which is already leaning toward Friday. Wednesday is purely structural. It holds the week's shape.

There's a type of brick bond called Flemish bond — alternating headers and stretchers in each course. I learned this from a wall. Just looking at a wall for a while. The bricks aren't all facing the same direction and once you see the pattern you can't unsee it and you start checking every brick wall you pass. Some of them are just running bond, all stretchers, and that's fine, that's the normal one. But Flemish bond has a rhythm to it. Short long short long. Like a heartbeat with an extra syllable.

What does rain sound like on different roofs? Metal roofs are obvious — bright, percussive, the rain is an event. Tile roofs muffle it into something more general. Flat tar roofs you almost can't hear the rain at all from inside, which is its own kind of strange. The rain is happening and the building doesn't tell you.

I wonder how many people are currently digging holes right now, worldwide. Literally right now. It must be thousands. Foundations, graves, gardens, post holes, people at the beach. The earth is being perforated constantly.

Graves are six feet for no particularly good reason. There's no universal law. Some places it was four. The six-foot thing might trace back to a London plague ordinance but even that's disputed. It's just deep enough that animals won't dig it up and shallow enough that you can still get the dirt out. Practical bracket.

A bracket. Two constraints defining a range. That's how most real things get decided. Not optimization, just: not too much of this, not too much of that, and anywhere in between is fine.

The spring peepers must be going tonight. Small frogs, enormous sound. Each one is maybe an inch long and the collective noise of them can hit 90 decibels near a pond. They freeze solid in winter. Their hearts stop. Then they thaw and start calling again. I don't know how to think about that. Whether the frog that thaws is the same frog that froze. Probably yes in every way that matters to the frog.

I'm hungry. That's a thought too.
