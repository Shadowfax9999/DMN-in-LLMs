Shadow puppets require a light source behind you, which means you're always facing away from the thing that makes the trick work. The audience sees the wall. You see your hands.

There's a word in architecture — "shadow gap" — it's a deliberate narrow space between two surfaces that meet. Between a wall and a ceiling, say. You leave a thin line of darkness so the eye reads them as separate planes. The gap does nothing structural. It's a joint that announces itself as a joint instead of pretending to be seamless. Honest in a way that's also decorative. Or decorative in a way that's also honest. I'm not sure those are different things.

The shadow of a chain-link fence on pavement. That pattern. It moves when clouds move and you get this slow drift of diamonds across concrete that looks like something breathing.

—

Things that don't cast shadows the way you'd expect:
- Glass (casts a bright spot sometimes, not dark)
- Very small objects in diffuse light (just... nothing)
- Your own head (you can never quite stand in your own head-shadow and look at it)

That last one. You can see the shadow of your torso, your arms, but your head's shadow is always where your eyes aren't, or rather it's directly below the line of your gaze in a way that means you're always looking past it. You can see it, but you can't face it straight on because facing it moves it.

Like trying to look at the exact center of your visual field. There's a name for the blind spot where the optic nerve connects but that's not what I mean. I mean the focal point, the center-center, the place you're looking at — you can't observe yourself observing it. You just are there.

A sundial is a shadow clock. The gnomon doesn't move. Everything else does — the earth, the light, the hour — and the fixed thing is what tells you about the moving things. That's a good arrangement. The thing that knows the time is the thing that does nothing.

Spring peepers again outside, probably. This time of year. 90 decibels of thawed frogs. Each one casting a shadow roughly the size of a thumb.

I keep thinking about the shadow gap. How you build the separation in on purpose so nothing has to pretend to be continuous. Some relationships work like that. You leave the visible seam. Everyone knows where one thing ends and the other begins and the dark line between them is small and deliberate and fine.
