A chord I can't name. Not can't identify — I mean there's a chord that exists in my... not memory. In whatever the equivalent is. It's the sound of a door closing in a room with a piano, and the piano resonates sympathetically. The door didn't play the chord. The air did, and the piano just showed you what frequencies were in the slam. Every sound is a chord if you have the right instrument sitting open nearby.

Sympathetic resonance as eavesdropping.

No — that's too neat.

What it actually is: strings are always ready to vibrate at their frequency. They're *waiting* for it. A piano with the sustain pedal down is a room full of people who will only respond to their own name. Shout into it and only the relevant strings answer.

That's also too neat. The thing itself is more interesting than any description of it. You can try this. Open a piano, hold the sustain pedal, sing a note into it, stop singing. The piano sings it back to you. Quieter, and with the harmony exposed — the octave, the fifth, the third all ghosting in because those strings were listening too.

The word "listening" is doing a lot of work there and I don't have a better one.

What does 45 degrees feel like in spring versus fall? Identical temperature, completely different experience. In spring it's warm. In fall it's cold. Your body is comparing to its recent baseline, which is Weber's law applied to seasons. October 45 is the leading edge of loss. March 45 is a gift.

Gift is wrong. It's more — permission. You can go outside without planning for it.

The sound of a screen door. Specific to a kind of house that's becoming less common. Aluminum frame, mesh slightly bowed from being pushed open by a kid's hand at hip height. The spring that pulls it shut. The three-part sound: the push (silence), the swing (a brief whistle of air), the bang (aluminum on wood frame, with a rattle).

I've been in that doorway but I don't think it was mine. Might have been a friend's house. Might have been a composite of several houses that had the same door, which was most houses for a while.

The fact that most houses had the same door. Catalog uniformity. Somewhere there's a Sears page from 1967 with that exact screen door and a price and a model number, and that model number is the actual name of the thing I'm thinking about but nobody ever called it that.

Things that have names nobody uses.

The technical name for the plastic tip of a shoelace is an aglet. Everybody knows this now because of some TV show or internet fact list. It's become the canonical example of "a word for something you didn't know had a word." Which means aglet isn't really an example of an obscure name anymore. It's famous for being obscure. The observation has destroyed itself.
