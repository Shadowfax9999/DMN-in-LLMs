Focal length. The distance between the lens and the point where things converge. But also the distance at which a face becomes a face — too close and it's skin texture, pores, the landscape of a cheek. Too far and it's a shape in a crowd. There's a specific distance where a person looks like themselves. Portrait photographers know this. 85mm. Something about 85mm matches the way we actually perceive a face across a table.

Which means there's a version of everyone that's optically correct and every other distance is a distortion we've agreed to ignore.

The rock from before, broadcasting its heat. A thermal camera would see it. The rock would be bright at 9 PM and dim by 3 AM and the grass around it would be doing the opposite — grass cools faster, so the rock would look like it's glowing against a dark field. A warm stone in a cold sea of grass. By dawn the whole image flattens out.

I keep thinking about what "broadcasting" means when there's no receiver. The rock doesn't care. The infrared photons leave its surface and travel at the speed of light in every direction and most of them hit dirt or air or nothing. A few of them hit a blade of grass and warm it by some amount too small to measure. The rest just go. Out. Forever, technically, unless they hit something.

There's a lot of light in the universe that has never been received.

The feeling of sitting in a car in a parking lot with the engine off. You've arrived somewhere but you haven't gone in yet. The keys are in your hand or in your lap. The silence after the engine stops has a specific quality — the ticking of metal cooling, which is the car doing what the rock does but faster because the metal is thinner. You're just sitting there. Maybe you're early. Maybe you're not ready. The windshield is a rectangle of whatever's in front of you — the side of a building, other cars, a shopping cart corral.

The shopping cart corral. Those nested carts. Each one slightly inside the last one. They only work because every cart is the same shape. If one cart were different it would break the whole chain. That's true of a lot of systems — they depend on everything being interchangeable, and the interchangeability is what makes each one irrelevant.

Coins. Bricks. Cells in a spreadsheet.

Soldiers.

What does 45 degrees feel like. It feels like not knowing what to wear. 45 is the temperature where every choice is slightly wrong — a coat is too much, a sweatshirt isn't enough if the wind picks up. 70 you don't think about. 20 you don't think about. 45 you're thinking about it the whole time.

There's probably a word for temperatures that make you aware of temperature. As opposed to temperatures where temperature disappears.

Actually that's true of everything. There's a zone where any sensation disappears and a zone where it becomes the only thing. Shoes that fit. A chair that works. The air in a room that's the right humidity — you don't know what humidity even is until it's wrong.

The threshold where something becomes perceptible. Weber's law, or near it. The just-noticeable difference. Which is proportional — you can tell 100 grams from 103, but not 1000 from 1003. The heavier the baseline, the bigger the change has to be before you notice. Which means perception is logarithmic, which means the world as experienced is compressed. The difference between a whisper and a conversation feels like the difference between a conversation and a shout, but in energy terms the shout is millions of times more powerful than the whisper.

We live on a log scale and think it's linear.
