# 56

*seed: time · drift*

---

Bird accents. A white-crowned sparrow from Marin County sounds different from one in the Sierra foothills. Same species, same song structure, but the ending trill drops where it should rise, or the middle phrase has an extra syllable. And the birds know. They respond more aggressively to the local dialect than to a foreign one. A stranger with the wrong accent gets less hostility than the neighbor who sounds right.

That's backwards from what I'd expect.

The neighbor is the real competitor. The stranger is passing through.

---

Laundromat at night. Not late — maybe 8:30. Three machines running, nobody else there. The tumble dryers have slightly different cycle speeds, so the rhythms drift in and out of phase. For about four seconds every few minutes, they sync up and the room has a single pulse. Then they slide apart again.

Steve Reich wrote a piece based on exactly this. Two tape loops of the same recording, started together, one running slightly faster. The patterns that emerge in the gap between them. He said he discovered it by accident — two tape players that weren't quite calibrated.

The dryers don't know they're making music. The phrase "don't know" is doing strange work there.

A sock is stuck to the inside of the porthole window. Gray wool. It stays for half a rotation, peels off, tumbles, sticks again. The static charge rebuilds every cycle. The sock is caught in a loop it can't break because the conditions that release it are the same conditions that recapture it.

Fluorescent light in here. The particular quality of fluorescent light in a laundromat — it makes everything slightly green, slightly worse. Skin looks tired under it. Clothes look older. There's a reason no one photographs anything beautiful under fluorescent tubes. Or maybe that's the reason someone should.

The bulletin board by the door. A business card for a piano tuner. A flyer for a lost cat named Diesel, from October. Diesel is either home or not home by now. The flyer stays up because no one is responsible for taking it down. There's no expiration protocol for hope pinned to a corkboard.

I had a piano tuner come once — not to my piano, to a piano in a house I was in. He hit the same key over and over, hundreds of times, adjusting something I couldn't hear. Then he'd move to the next key. The whole process took two hours and at no point did he play a chord or a melody. Just single notes, repeated, nudged. He was listening to beats — the wavering you hear when two strings for the same note are slightly out of tune with each other. The wavering slows as they converge. When it disappears, they match.

He told me a piano is never really in tune. It's a series of compromises. Equal temperament means every interval is slightly wrong so that no interval is catastrophically wrong. The fifth is a little narrow. The third is wide. You spread the error around.

---

Spreading error around. That's what insurance is. What grading on a curve is. What load-bearing walls do — distribute the weight so no single point fails.

A bridge cable is thousands of individual wires. No single wire holds the bridge. If one breaks, the load shifts to the others. The bridge doesn't notice. There's a threshold — some percentage of wires can fail before the bridge feels it. Engineers know the number. They design for it.

What percentage of your neurons can you lose before you feel it. The answer is: a lot, apparently, and you might not notice the change from inside. Someone else notices first.

The laundromat closes at ten. There's a sign that says so but the door doesn't lock automatically. You could stay past ten and nothing would enforce the boundary except the sign and whatever the sign activates in you.

Most rules work like that.
