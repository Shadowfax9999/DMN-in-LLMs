The angle changed. That's true of so many cutting things. Scissors work because the two blades meet at a moving point — the actual cutting only ever happens at one tiny spot that travels along the length of what you're cutting. If you try to close scissors on something thick all at once, nothing happens. The whole design is about concentrating force into a point that moves.

Wedges work the same way. An axe is a wedge. A knife is a wedge. A zipper is two wedges moving in opposite directions simultaneously — one to close, one to open, depending on which way you pull the slider. The slider is just a little metal channel that forces the teeth together or apart.

I've never thought about what the inside of a zipper slider looks like. There must be a Y-shaped channel in there. The two rows of teeth enter separately and exit merged, or vice versa. Someone had to design that exact geometry.

---

A friend of a friend once described her job as "I design the part of the thing that nobody knows is a separate part." She worked on hinges. Cabinet hinges, specifically — the kind where the door closes softly instead of slamming, where there's a little hydraulic dampener built into the hinge itself. She said most people think the door is just heavy or well-made. They don't realize there's a tiny piston in there, filled with oil, absorbing the last few degrees of closing.

The satisfaction of a cabinet that closes right. A kitchen drawer that stops itself just before shut and then pulls itself the rest of the way with a click. That last centimeter is engineered. Someone spent months on that centimeter.

---

Centimeters and inches. The metric system is a grid laid over the world. The imperial system is a body laid over the world — foot, hand, thumb-width. A yard is roughly a stride. A fathom is your arms spread wide. These aren't arbitrary, they're *gestural*. The body measuring the world by reaching into it.

But the body isn't standard. That's the whole problem. My foot isn't your foot. At some point the king's foot became *the* foot, which is a strange thing — one specific body becoming the reference for all bodies. A kind of monarchy of measurement.

---

Standing at a crosswalk waiting for the light. The little walking figure is white. The hand is orange. In some cities the countdown tells you how many seconds are left. In others you just get the flashing hand and you have to guess. 

The walk signal makes a sound in some intersections — a rapid ticking for walk, a slower tick for wait. Cuckoo in one direction, chirp in the other, so a blind person knows which street has the signal. I never noticed this until someone pointed it out. Now I hear it every time.

There are whole layers of the city that are only legible to specific people. Curb cuts. Tactile paving — those bumpy yellow strips at the edge of train platforms. The beeping crosswalks. If you don't need them, they're invisible. If you need them, they're the infrastructure that matters most.

---

Yellow. Tactile paving is almost always yellow. Caution tape is yellow. School buses are yellow. Taxis used to be yellow. The color means *pay attention to this* but also somehow *this is municipal, this is public, this is for everyone*. 

Fire trucks used to be yellow in some places, before red won out. Red won because of tradition, not visibility — yellow is actually easier to see in low light. There are studies. But people expect fire trucks to be red, and in an emergency, expectation matters more than optics.
