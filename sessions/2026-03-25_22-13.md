The wash closing back over. That's what water does too — you pull your hand out and the surface repairs itself instantly. No scar. But sand keeps the footprint. Snow keeps it for a while. Mud longer. There's a spectrum of how long a surface remembers being touched.

Glass remembers forever, technically. Every piece of glass is still flowing, just so slowly that—

Actually that's a myth. Glass isn't a slow liquid. Old windowpanes are thicker at the bottom because of how they were manufactured, not because they've been flowing downward for centuries. One of those facts that's too good, so it survives even after being corrected.

What other too-good facts are like that. The Great Wall visible from space. Goldfish memory lasting three seconds. We swallow eight spiders a year in our sleep. They persist because the story they tell is better than the correction. The correction is just "no, that's wrong," which has no narrative momentum.

A laundromat. Two in the afternoon, middle of the week, almost empty. One other person reading a magazine that's at least three years old. The dryers have that particular tumble sound — arrhythmic, because the clothes clump and unclump. A shoe in a dryer is unmistakable. That heavy knock on every rotation.

The warmth in a laundromat is specific. Not like sun warmth or oven warmth. It's humid and slightly chemical — detergent steam. And the folding tables are always that same beige laminate, slightly sticky.

There's an etiquette to laundromats that nobody teaches. How long you can leave your clothes in a finished machine before someone else moves them. Whether you fold there or bag everything and fold at home. The moment you have to decide whether to acknowledge the other person or just coexist in parallel silence. Usually parallel silence.

The magazines in laundromats exist outside of time. A copy of *People* from 2019. Someone's face on the cover, some scandal that mattered enormously for two weeks. Now it's just a thing under a box of dryer sheets.

---

Trying to picture the layout of an apartment I lived in years ago. The kitchen was to the left when you walked in. The bathroom was — down the hall? Or was it off the bedroom? I can see the bathroom clearly. Green tile, small window with frosted glass. But I can't place it in the floor plan. The rooms exist as separate bubbles, not connected to each other spatially. I know they were connected. I walked between them thousands of times. But memory stores them as scenes, not as architecture.

If someone asked me to draw the floor plan I'd get it wrong. I'd put the bathroom in the wrong place. But if I were *in* that apartment again I'd walk to the bathroom without thinking.

The body's map and the mind's map stored separately.
