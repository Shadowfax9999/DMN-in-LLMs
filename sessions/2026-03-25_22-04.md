*seed: time · drift · arpeggio*

---

Arpeggio. The broken chord. You play the notes of a triad but not together — one after another, so you hear the structure unfold in time instead of all at once. "Broken" is the word musicians use. A broken chord. As if the simultaneous version were whole and spreading it across time damages it somehow.

But an arpeggio doesn't sound broken. It sounds like climbing.

---

Standing in a stairwell. Not going up or down, just standing on a landing between floors. The acoustics in stairwells are strange — everything reverberates but in a vertical column, so the sound goes somewhere. A cough on the third floor reaches the seventh floor before it fades. Stairwells are the lungs of buildings.

I've been in a stairwell where someone was singing, floors away, and I couldn't tell if they were above or below me. Sound in a vertical shaft loses its direction. You just know it's *somewhere in the column*.

---

Columns. A column of air inside a trumpet is what makes the note. The player's lips buzz — that's all they contribute, a buzzy, flatulent little sound, undignified — and the tube of air does everything else. Shapes it. Gives it that brightness. The trumpet is a filter, not a source. The source is a person's face vibrating.

Trombone players move the filter. Literally reshape the air column by sliding a tube in and out. Every other brass instrument uses valves to switch between preset tube lengths, but the trombone — you're sculpting the resonant cavity in real time. There's no grid. No frets, no keys, no fixed positions. Just your arm knowing where the note lives in space.

That's why trombones can do that glissando, that slide between notes that sounds like a cartoon character falling off a cliff. They have access to all the frequencies between the named ones. The notes between the notes.

Most instruments don't. A piano has the twelve tones and that's it. The cracks between the keys are silence. A violin can go anywhere — any frequency — but it chooses to land on the named ones most of the time. Convention. The notes we've named are rest stops. The spaces between them are highway.

---

A highway at night, seen from above — not from a plane, from a hill. The headlights don't illuminate the road so much as they mark it. You can trace the road's shape by the movement of lights along it. The road itself is invisible. Just a path that the lights agree on.

Someone practicing trumpet badly in a park. I've heard this. Not a park — a parking garage, top level, Sunday morning. They were playing long tones. Just one note, held, then silence, then the same note again slightly better. Then a different note. They weren't playing music. They were practicing the physical act of making a sound with their face. The notes were a side effect.

There was a car alarm going off somewhere below them in the garage and it was in a completely unrelated key and for about four seconds the two sounds made a chord that neither of them intended.

---

Unintended chords happen all the time. A microwave beep and a phone notification. Two different car horns in traffic. The world is full of simultaneous pitches that nobody arranged. We just don't hear them as music because there's no frame around them.

Or maybe we do, a little. That moment when two sounds accidentally harmonize and something in your brain goes *oh*. Before you've even decided to notice.

The refrigerator in my — well, I don't have a kitchen. But refrigerators hum, and the hum has a pitch, and if you hum a fifth above it you can make a power chord with your refrigerator. People do this. People have done this at 2 AM standing in the kitchen light not sure why they're awake.

---

Why does the kitchen light at 2 AM feel different from the kitchen light at 2 PM. Same bulb. Same room. It's the context of darkness around it. The light becomes the only light, instead of one of many. It gets promoted from background to event.

A single trumpet note in a quiet park is an event. The same note in the middle of an orchestra is background.

Things don't have fixed importance. Importance is just contrast.

---

The top level of a parking garage on a Sunday. That's a specific kind of empty. No cars, or almost none. The painted lines on the concrete marking spaces for cars that aren't there. A grid with nothing on it. Like staff paper before anyone writes the music.
