Patina. Which is just: the record of use written on the surface of the thing.

But also a word that sounds like it should be a name. Patina. Someone's aunt.

The worn yellow paint in a parking garage isn't something anyone would call a patina because patina implies value. Worn paint is just worn paint. But the mechanism is identical — repeated contact removing material, leaving a trace of all the movements that happened there. A desire path in pigment.

Desire paths. Those diagonal tracks across grass where people walk instead of where the sidewalk goes. Someone had to be first. Someone looked at the ninety-degree angle the concrete wanted them to walk and just... didn't. And then the next person saw the slightly beaten grass and thought, or didn't think, just followed. By the twentieth person it's a suggestion. By the hundredth it's a path. By the thousandth the grass is gone and there's bare dirt and eventually someone paves it.

There's a campus — I want to say it's in Michigan — where they supposedly waited to see where the desire paths formed before pouring the sidewalks. I don't know if that's true. It gets repeated a lot, which might mean it's true or might just mean it's a satisfying story.

The thing about a desire path is that it optimizes for one variable: shortest distance. It doesn't account for drainage, or root systems, or what happens when it rains and the bare dirt becomes mud. The official path is worse on one axis and better on six others. But the one axis is the one you feel with your legs.

Felt pen on a whiteboard that's been used for years. The ghost marks that won't erase anymore. They've bonded with the surface. Every meeting room has a whiteboard with the faint remnants of someone's org chart or Venn diagram from 2019, and people just write over it.

Palimpsest is the word for that, for a manuscript where the original text was scraped off and written over but is still faintly legible underneath. Medieval monks reusing parchment because parchment was expensive. Someone in the 19th century figured out you could use ultraviolet light to read the scraped-away text, and they found Archimedes under a prayer book.

That's a real thing. The Archimedes Palimpsest. A 10th-century copy of Archimedes' work on combinatorics and mechanical theorems, scraped off in the 13th century so a monk could write prayers on the parchment. The math was underneath the prayers for seven hundred years.

I wonder what the monk would think about that. Probably that the prayers were more important, and he'd have a coherent reason for thinking so.

Seven hundred years is also roughly the age of the oldest known stained glass still in place, which is at Chartres. The blue is famous — Chartres blue — and part of what makes it that particular blue is that nobody's been able to fully reproduce the process. There are theories about cobalt compounds and the wood ash used as flux, but the exact combination of materials and firing temperature and whatever else was happening in a 12th-century French workshop is gone. The glass exists but the knowledge that made it doesn't.

Which happens more often than you'd expect. Roman concrete is the usual example — it's in every list of "lost technologies" — but there are smaller ones. Stradivarius varnish. Greek fire. The New Jersey mosquito trap that worked perfectly in 1912 field trials and whose inventor died without writing down the design and nobody could reverse-engineer it from the surviving units.

I don't know if that last one is real. It has the shape of a fact that wants to be repeated.

The shape of a fact that wants to be repeated. That's what makes something stick in a "did you know" conversation. It needs a reversal or a surprise. It needs to be tellable in under thirty seconds. It helps if there's a proper noun.

Most of what I know about the Roman Empire comes from facts shaped like that.

Most of what anyone knows about anything outside their area of expertise comes from facts shaped like that, probably. The ones that survived the filter of being interesting enough to repeat, compressed enough to remember, and wrong in ways that are hard to check.

The gorilla glass on a phone screen is also a kind of thing where the exact manufacturing process matters enormously and small variations produce completely different results. Corning makes it by ion exchange — sodium ions in the glass get replaced by potassium ions in a molten salt bath, and because potassium ions are bigger, they compress the surface layer, which is what makes it hard to scratch. The temperature of the salt bath matters to within a few degrees.

A few degrees in a salt bath, seven hundred years ago in a French workshop. The difference between Chartres blue and some other blue that nobody would have named.
