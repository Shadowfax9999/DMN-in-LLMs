The body measuring openings before you do. Cats do this with whiskers — the whisker span matches the body width, so if the whiskers clear it, the cat fits. Humans don't have that but we have something. Proprioception, maybe, or just years of fitting through doors. Toddlers misjudge gaps all the time. They walk straight into things they could have gone around. At some point you calibrate and then you never think about it again.

Unless you're carrying something wide. A mattress, a sheet of plywood. Then suddenly every doorframe is a puzzle again. You're back to being a toddler, misjudging clearances. Tilting, rotating, backing up. The classic moving-day problem: the couch that fit in the room but won't come back out. There's actual math for this — the moving sofa problem. What's the largest shape that can be moved around an L-shaped corridor? It's unsolved, or it was for a long time. Someone proved an upper bound. The shape that works best isn't a rectangle, it's this weird rounded thing with a notch cut out of it.

I carried a dresser up a staircase once that had a landing with a 180-degree turn. Three of us. The dresser was heavier than we expected and we kept having to set it down on the stairs, which meant it wanted to slide. The person on the bottom was bearing most of the weight. We got it to the landing and it just would not go around the corner. We tried every angle. Took the drawers out to make it lighter, which didn't help with the geometry but made us feel like we were doing something. Eventually we tilted it almost vertical and kind of corkscrewed it around. Scraped the wall. There's still a mark there probably.

The thing about stairs is they're all supposed to be the same height. Building code again. If one riser is even a quarter inch off, people trip. Your foot expects the floor at a certain height and when it arrives early or late, that's a stumble. There's a staircase somewhere — I want to say it's in a historic building, maybe a lighthouse — where one step is deliberately a different height, supposedly to keep you alert. That sounds apocryphal.

Lighthouses are mostly automated now. The keeper's quarters just sitting there empty with the light still turning above. Some of them are Airbnbs. You sleep in the keeper's house and the light rotates all night. I'd want to know if you can see it from the bedroom window, that sweep coming around every few seconds. That would either be hypnotic or maddening.

Fresnel lenses. Those are the ones in lighthouses, with the concentric ridges. Also on overhead projectors, which no one uses anymore. The whole lens is flat but it bends light the same way a curved lens would because each ring is angled slightly differently. Like unfolding a lens into a plane. They're in car headlights too, or they were — I think newer headlights use reflector assemblies or projector housings instead. The plastic lens cover on modern headlights is mostly just a cover, not doing the optical work. Which is why when they get foggy and yellowed the light still mostly works, just looks worse.

Toothpaste supposedly fixes yellowed headlights. The mild abrasive in it polishes the oxidized plastic. I've never tried it.
