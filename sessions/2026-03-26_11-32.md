# 90

*seed: time · drift · cork*

---

Cork is bark. That's the thing that's easy to forget — it's not manufactured, it's grown. The outer bark of one specific oak, *Quercus suber*, and they strip it off the living tree every nine years. The tree survives. Keeps growing new bark. There's something almost agricultural about it except the harvest cycle is longer than most people stay in one house.

The first harvest is no good for wine corks. Too rough, too irregular. They use it for flooring or insulation. It takes the second or third stripping before the bark is smooth enough to punch bottle stoppers out of. So a cork in a wine bottle might come from bark that started growing when the bottle's drinker was in middle school.

Portugal produces most of the world's cork. The forests are called *montados* and they're full of these strange pale-trunked trees where the bark's been recently taken — the exposed inner bark is this vivid reddish-orange that weathers to brown over the years before the next harvest. You can tell how recently a tree was stripped by its color.

The cellular structure is what makes cork work. Millions of tiny air-filled chambers per cubic centimeter, each one sealed. That's why it floats, why it compresses and springs back, why it insulates. Each cell is basically a tiny sealed room. When you push a cork into a bottle neck you're collapsing millions of rooms and they're all trying to re-expand, which is what makes the seal.

I had a bulletin board in college that was cork and I remember pushing thumbtacks into it and feeling that specific give — not soft exactly, more like dense foam. And when you pulled the tack out the hole would mostly close up. Those sealed cells again, re-expanding.

The popping sound when you open a bottle. That's the cork cells finally snapping back to their original shape as the cork clears the neck, plus the pressure differential if it's sparkling. Two events happening in the same instant that you hear as one sound.

Screw caps work better for preserving wine, apparently. Less variation, no risk of cork taint — that musty wet-cardboard taste from a compound called TCA. But there's the ritual of it. The foil, the corkscrew, the twist and pull. Screw caps don't have a ritual. You just open them like a bottle of water.

Nine years between harvests. Some of those trees in Portugal are over two hundred years old. Twenty-plus harvests from a single tree.
