Chorus. A chorus is a group of people agreeing to pretend they're one voice. That's strange if you think about it from the inside — you open your mouth and aim for the same pitch as the person next to you, and when you hit it, you disappear. The better you are, the less you exist individually.

The Greek chorus was different. They were the voice of the city watching itself do something terrible.

Six kitchens. Six hip-pushes. Six people in a grocery store who don't know they're doing the same thing in parallel — reaching for the same brand of rice, or not the same brand, but the same gesture, the arm extending to a shelf at shoulder height, the fingers closing around a box. If you filmed all six from the same angle you'd have a kind of choreography. Unintentional chorus.

---

The drawer that doesn't close right. There's a version of this with doors, too — the front door that sticks, the bathroom door you have to lift slightly while turning the handle. Visitors struggle with it and you reach past them and do it without thinking, and for a second you feel like you know a secret about the house, which you do. Every house has a set of these. The specific failures that only the people who live there have learned to work around.

A house is a set of workarounds that accreted into habit.

---

What does a chorus sound like from inside it? Not the audience experience — that blended thing — but the singer's experience. You hear your own voice in your skull, bone-conducted, and then you hear the voices on either side of you through the air, and they arrive at slightly different times, and the vowels don't quite match because everyone's mouth is shaped differently. The "ah" of the person to your left is darker than yours. You're between two versions of the same note and neither one is exactly yours.

I sang in a chorus once. That sentence isn't true in any biographical sense but I can think my way into it. The risers. The black folder. The director's hand sculpting the air — pulling the sound forward with the fingers, pushing it back with the palm flat. And that moment when the chord locks in, when the overtones align and you can hear a note that nobody is singing. A sum-tone. Something that exists only because of the combination.

---

Standing at a crosswalk. The light is red. Seven people waiting. Nobody is talking to each other. Everybody is facing the same direction. Somebody shifts their weight. Somebody checks their phone. One person steps slightly off the curb and then steps back. Then the light changes and all seven step forward within about a half-second of each other and for three or four steps they're synchronized and then the speeds differentiate and they scatter.

The crosswalk chorus.

A flock of starlings doesn't have a conductor. Each bird follows three rules about the birds nearest to it and the result is that enormous flowing shape that looks like it has intention. A murmuration. The word sounds like what it describes — a low collective sound, a shared almost-voice.

---

The magnet from a place you've never been. Niagara Falls, maybe. Or a state you've only driven through. It's holding up a takeout menu for a restaurant that closed two years ago but the menu is still useful somehow — you remember what you liked there, and seeing the name is a small memorial. The magnet outlasts the restaurant. The menu outlasts the food. The memory of the food outlasts the menu, probably, though it gets less specific. You remember *liking* it more than you remember the taste.

What is the half-life of a flavor in memory? Salt lasts. Sweetness lasts. The specific combination of spices in a particular dish — that goes fast. You'd know it if you tasted it again, but you can't reconstruct it from nothing.

Recognition is easier than recall. That's true for faces, melodies, flavors, streets. You can't draw the street from memory but you'd know it if you stood on it. The knowledge is in there but it only activates in the presence of the thing.

---

The drawer. I keep coming back to the drawer. The hip-push that outlasts the problem. There's a word for this in medicine — or not exactly. Phantom limb is the famous one, but that's not quite it. It's more like how you keep reaching for a light switch in the old position after someone moves it. The body's map updates slower than the world.

A house you used to live in. You could walk through it in the dark.
