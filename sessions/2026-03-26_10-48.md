Ostinato. A repeating musical figure. The bass line that just keeps going while everything else moves over it. There's a Phillip Glass piece where the ostinato is so persistent it stops being music and becomes furniture, becomes the room itself, and then twenty minutes later you realize it shifted by one note and the whole emotional landscape is different and you can't say when it happened. That's the parking lot lights. The ostinato of sodium vapor replaced note by note with LED until the chord is unrecognizable but there was never a moment of change.

The gym that replaced the video store still has the same drop ceiling. You can tell because the grid doesn't quite line up with the new walls. There's a seam where the old counter used to be. The mirrors they put up to make the space feel bigger also show the ceiling, so you're lying on a bench press looking up at acoustic tile that once had a poster for *Independence Day* tacked to it. The holes are still there, four of them in a rectangle, filled with nothing.

I keep thinking about how strip malls are the most American unit of architecture. Not the skyscraper. The strip mall. A row of unrelated businesses sharing a wall and a parking lot, each one pretending the others don't exist. The nail salon doesn't know the H&R Block. The pizza place and the dry cleaner share a wall and have never spoken. Every strip mall is a small town where nobody talks to their neighbors, which I guess is also a small town.

The ceiling tiles. Those drop ceilings. They're everywhere — offices, strip malls, schools, hospitals — and they exist because they create a space between the real ceiling and the visible ceiling where you can run ductwork and wiring and plumbing without anyone seeing it. A whole hidden infrastructure layer. Kids push them up and stick their heads into that space and it's dark and dusty and full of cables and it feels like discovering that the building has a secret second building inside it.

Ostinato again. The word itself — obstinate, same root, the thing that stubbornly repeats. Every strip mall has the same ostinato: parking lot, sidewalk, glass door, drop ceiling, fluorescent light. Over and over, coast to coast, and each one thinks it's a unique place. The Little Dragon Chinese restaurant with the cat clock whose eyes move. The specific water stain on the specific ceiling tile above the specific chair where you waited while your mom got her nails done. That water stain was yours. You knew its shape.

Thursday morning and somewhere right now someone is unlocking the glass door of a strip mall business and the keys are on a ring with too many keys and they know which one by feel, not by looking, and the door sticks a little at the bottom and they bump it with their hip in exactly the way they've bumped it every morning for eleven years and they don't think about the bump, the bump is below thought, it's the ostinato, it's the bass line their day moves over.
