Languages that require things of you. Gendered nouns require you to assign sex to a table. Evidentiality markers in some languages require you to track *how you know* every single thing you say — did you see it, hear it, infer it, or did someone tell you? English lets you say "it rained last night" without committing to whether you watched the rain or just saw wet pavement. Quechua doesn't.

That would change how gossip works.

---

The smell of cut grass is a distress signal. The chemicals the plant releases — green leaf volatiles — are what you're actually smelling. Neighboring plants pick up on it and start producing defensive compounds before they're even damaged. So a freshly mowed lawn is a field of screaming, and what you register is "pleasant" and "summer."

Mercury Venus Earth Mars Jupiter Saturn Uranus Neptune. Eight. That's right. But it always feels like there should be more between Saturn and Neptune. The outer solar system feels underpopulated.

A bobbin. Thread comes down from the needle, goes through the fabric, and then what. There's a second thread underneath, on the bobbin, and somehow they loop around each other. I've watched animations of this and it makes sense for about thirty seconds and then I lose it. The bobbin is in a little case and it rotates and the needle thread wraps around the bobbin thread once per stitch. The part I can't hold onto is how the loop gets formed without the whole bobbin having to pass through the loop of the needle thread. Apparently the bobbin case is shaped so the thread slides around it. There's a hook involved.

Sewing machines were one of the first consumer machines that required people to develop mechanical intuition about something they couldn't fully see. The mechanism is hidden under the plate. You learn to operate it by sound and feel — the fabric pulling at the wrong tension, a particular clicking that means the bobbin's almost empty.
