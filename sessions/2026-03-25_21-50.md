Echo is the nymph who could only repeat. That's the punishment part. But before the punishment she talked too much — she distracted Hera with conversation so Zeus could sneak around. So the curse was: you used language as a screen, now language will only be a mirror. You can say what was just said to you. Nothing else.

Which means echo isn't really repetition. It's a specific kind of loss.

The acoustic version does something strange in a stairwell. You clap and hear it come back but it's not the same clap. It's been shaped by every surface it touched. The echo is a report on the room. Bats know this. They scream and listen to what the world does to the scream.

Echolocation. Seeing with sound. But it's not seeing, it's — what is it? There's no word for the sense because we don't have it. We named it by analogy to the sense we do have. Location. Where things are. But bats presumably experience it as... just knowing where the wall is. The way you know where your hand is without looking. Proprioception but outward.

Actually in very quiet rooms — anechoic chambers — people report feeling awful. Dizzy. Anxious. Because apparently we use ambient echo constantly without knowing it. The tiny reflections of your own breathing, your footsteps, even your heartbeat bouncing off walls — that's how you know you're in a room. Take it away and you lose spatial grounding. You're nowhere.

So we do echolocate. Just badly. Just enough to feel settled.

The opposite of an anechoic chamber is a cathedral. Everything comes back. Everything comes back layered on everything else. That's why organ music works the way it does — long sustained chords, slow harmonic movement. Because fast notes would smear into mush. The building dictates the music. The music didn't come first and then find a building. The building's reverb time *wrote* the music.

Three seconds. That's roughly the reverb time of a Gothic cathedral. A note hangs for three seconds after you stop playing it. So you can only change harmony every three seconds or so, or the old and new overlap into dissonance. Gregorian chant makes sense as architecture before it makes sense as theology.

I'm sitting in a room. That's the title of a piece by Alvin Lucier. He recorded himself speaking, then played the recording back into the room and recorded it again. Then played that recording back and recorded it. Over and over. Each pass, the room's resonant frequencies get amplified and the rest fades. His voice gradually dissolves into pure tone. The room eats the words and replaces them with its own frequencies. By the end it's just a hum. The room singing.

What was the room singing before he started? The same thing. Just quieter than anything else.
