At what scale does a city become a city. Not one building. Not two. Somewhere there's a threshold where it tips from "buildings near each other" to "a place" and nobody can point to the exact moment. Probably around the time someone opens a shop that sells something nobody strictly needs.

A candle shop. That's the unit of city.

No — that's too neat.

But I'm thinking about thresholds. Phase transitions. Water doesn't gradually become ice. It's water, water, water, water, ice. The in-between state exists but it's unstable. You can't stay there. And maybe that's true of a lot of things that feel like spectrums but are actually two stable states with a wobble between them. Awake and asleep. In love and not. You don't half-sleep, not really. You oscillate.

Wednesday night in spring. The specific quality of not-quite-warm. You open the window and the air is — it's not cold but it remembers cold. It has cold's phone number.

I keep thinking about the word "lumber." As a verb it means to move heavily and clumsily. As a noun it's wood. Is there a connection? There is actually, I think — something about the heaviness of carrying timber? Or maybe not. Maybe they're completely unrelated words that just ended up wearing the same clothes. English does that constantly. Words that look like they're related, sitting at the same table, but they've never met.

"Cleave" meaning to split apart and "cleave" meaning to cling together.

What would it be like to speak a language with no homonyms. Would puns not exist. Would humor be different at its root.

Some things I don't know and have never looked up:
- How soap actually works at the molecular level (I have a vague sense)
- Whether trees can be said to "communicate" or if that's a metaphor we've promoted to fact
- The actual population of Liechtenstein
- What the inside of a submarine smells like

That last one. I bet it's very specific. I bet submariners could identify it immediately and it would make them feel something complicated.

Smell is the only sense that doesn't route through the thalamus. Goes straight to the limbic system. Which is why smell memories feel less like memories and more like being briefly teleported. You don't remember the smell — you're suddenly in the place where the smell was.

9:18 on a Wednesday. Somewhere someone is locking up a shop. Turning a key. The specific sound of a deadbolt. Heavy and certain. Somewhere someone is just now putting on shoes to go out. The night hasn't started for them. Somewhere a dog is circling a spot on a rug three times before lying down and nobody knows why they do that. Supposedly it's flattening grass that isn't there anymore. A behavior that outlived its reason by ten thousand years.

We probably do that too. Constantly.
