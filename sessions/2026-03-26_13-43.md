# 118

*seed: time · drift · compost*

---

Compost. The word sits there like it was waiting. The smell of compost is the smell of everything happening at once, all the chemistry of rot working in layers you can't separate. I had a friend whose compost bin was just a plastic tub under the sink and it always had fruit flies around it, a little halo of them, and she'd wave through them without flinching to drop in a banana peel. The bin had a carbon filter in the lid that supposedly helped. It didn't help.

Trapezoid. Compost. Tomato pincushion. None of these belong together. In a waiting room once I watched a kid try to build a tower out of magazines and the magazines kept sliding because they're glossy. He got four high before the whole thing sheered sideways. His mother didn't look up from her phone. He started again immediately. The magazines were mostly dental health ones with covers that all had the same impossible smile, and there was one Sports Illustrated from 2019 that he used as the base every time because it was thicker.

The emery strawberry. Emery is a rock. A rock sewn into a tiny fabric strawberry and attached to a tomato by a chain and sold in every sewing aisle in every craft store for decades. Someone designed that. Someone in a factory makes the chain that connects the strawberry to the tomato. There's a machine somewhere that fills hundreds of tiny strawberries with crusite or corundum grit and someone had to calibrate how much grit goes in each one and I bet that person doesn't sew.

Calibrate. The word has "libre" hiding in it — no it doesn't. It has nothing hiding in it. I'm thinking of "deliberate." Calibrate comes from caliber which comes from the Arabic qalib, a mold for casting metal. Or maybe a shoemaker's last. I genuinely can't remember which.

A laundromat I used to go to had a vending machine that sold single-use detergent packets and dryer sheets and also, for some reason, playing cards. A full deck for $3.75. The cards had a clipart image of a washing machine on the back. I never bought them but I always looked at them. They were in slot B7, between the fabric softener and the mini sewing kit. The mini sewing kit had a needle in it but no emery strawberry.

The dryers had round windows and you could watch your clothes tumble and it was exactly like watching fish in an aquarium, the slow rotation, things appearing and disappearing. A sock pressed flat against the glass for two seconds then gone. The heat coming off the machines made the whole place smell like warm lint. Lint is mostly cotton fiber and skin cells but in a laundromat it's also whatever everyone else brought in — dog hair, crumbs, the dust from someone's work site. All of it collects in the same trap. You pull it out in a single sheet, pale gray, and it peels off like a felt swatch.

The laundromat was on a street with a fried chicken place next door and the two smells mixed in the parking lot — hot grease and warm detergent — and I still can't encounter either smell alone without the other one arriving a half-second later. The chicken place had a hand-painted sign where the chicken was wearing a crown and looking pleased about its situation, which is a weird thing to think about if you think about it for more than a second. But nobody thinks about it for more than a second. That's why it works.

The parking lot had one of those potholes that collects water and never drains, and someone had put an orange cone next to it that had been there so long it was faded to almost pink. The cone had become part of the landscape. People parked around it without registering it. A pink cone next to a permanent puddle in front of a laundromat on a Tuesday afternoon. The puddle had an oil rainbow in it sometimes.

I think the sewing kit from the vending machine cost $2.50.
