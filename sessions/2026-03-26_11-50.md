My left knee clicks going down stairs but not going up. Which means it's the loading pattern, not the joint itself — eccentric versus concentric. Going down, you're braking. The quadricep is lengthening under load.

Braking is harder than accelerating in almost every context. Cars, running, skating. The thing that stops you takes more punishment than the thing that moves you.

Except swimming. In swimming there's no braking. You just stop pulling and the water stops you almost instantly. That frictionless-feeling thing where you glide off a flip turn and feel fast for about two seconds before the drag eats it all.

Chlorine smell isn't chlorine. It's chloramines — chlorine bonded to nitrogen from sweat and urine. The stronger the pool smells, the dirtier the water. Clean pools barely smell like anything.

I remember a hotel pool in maybe Tucson that was so heavily chlorinated your eyes burned through goggles. Small pool, too bright, the water almost green under fluorescent light. The vending machine in the pool area had off-brand sodas. There was a sign that said NO DIVING in four languages but the pool was only three feet deep everywhere so it wasn't really a temptation.

Three feet of water weighs — what. Per square foot of surface, three cubic feet, so about 187 pounds. That's a lot pressing on a pool floor. And then you add the people.

Hydraulic engineers have to think about that for dams. The pressure at the bottom of a reservoir is just depth times density times gravity. It doesn't matter how wide the lake is. A narrow column of water 300 feet tall exerts the same base pressure as a lake 300 feet deep. That's counterintuitive. It's called the hydrostatic paradox but it's not really a paradox, it's just surprising.

Lots of things called paradoxes aren't. The birthday paradox is just bad intuition about combinatorics. The Fermi paradox might not be a paradox either if the answer is just that space is big and life is rare and those two facts are sufficient.

I can never remember whether Fermi actually posed it as a paradox or just asked the question at lunch.

The sidewalk outside has that thing where tree roots have lifted one slab half an inch above the next. You catch your toe on it if you're not watching. Cities spend enormous amounts of money on that — either grinding the concrete down or replacing the slab or cutting the roots, which sometimes kills the tree. There's a version of the trolley problem in there somewhere but it's too boring to formulate.

Some tree roots go down and some go wide. Oaks go down. Maples go wide. That's why maples buckle sidewalks and oaks mostly don't.

I think the frets are made of nickel.
