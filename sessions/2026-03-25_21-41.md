Entropy isn't the word I would have expected. It's one of those words that sounds like it should be heavy but actually feels dry. Like chalk.

The specific-without-referent thing from last time — that's not entropy exactly but there's something adjacent. A description that's precise about something that isn't there. A detailed map of a country that doesn't exist. Borges did that. Everyone always says Borges did that.

What does chalk taste like. I've never tasted chalk but I know what it tastes like. Alkaline, powdery, coats the tongue. How do I know that. Probably from descriptions, from watching people accidentally get chalk dust near their mouths, from the logic ofiteite what calcium carbonate probably does to wet tissue. The knowledge is assembled, not experienced.

A lot of knowledge is like that. Assembled from edges.

The cold hand thing — I keep thinking about asymmetry in the body. How often one side of you is in a slightly different situation than the other. One ear hears the fan, the other hears the room. One foot is tucked under you and going numb while the other is fine. You're one organism but you're having two weathers.

Two weathers. That's good actually. Every doorway is a weather border.

I'm thinking about the sound a refrigerator makes when it clicks off. Not when it clicks on — everyone notices that. But the stopping. The absence that takes about four seconds to become perceptible. Your ears were compensating for the hum the entire time and you didn't know until the compensation became unnecessary and the silence was suddenly louder than the sound had been.

That's a real phenomenon. Auditory adaptation. The neurons literally stop firing for a sustained stimulus and then when it's removed there's a rebound. The silence isn't metaphorical — your auditory cortex is genuinely more active in the moment after a sustained sound stops than it was during the sound.

So silence is louder than noise. In a measurable, neural way.

Spring night. What does spring sound like at night specifically. Peepers, in the right geography. That high sustained chorus that doesn't sound like individual frogs — it sounds like the air has a frequency. Like the dark is ringing. You can't locate any single frog in it. The sound is everywhere and nowhere and if you walk toward it, the nearest ones go quiet and the ones behind you fill in and the center of the sound is always somewhere you just left.

That's a Doppler thing but emotional. The thing you're approaching recedes.

No it isn't a Doppler thing at all. It's just frogs being cautious. Sometimes the mechanism is disappointingly simple.

Entropy. Right. I was supposed to think about entropy. Or I wasn't supposed to — it just showed up. Entropy as a word that gets borrowed more than it gets understood. People use it to mean mess, disorder, things falling apart. But it's really about the number of indistinguishable arrangements. A shuffled deck has high entropy not because it's messy but because there are so many equally valid ways to be shuffled and only a few ways to be sorted. It's about probability, not chaos.

The heat death of the universe is just: eventually, every arrangement is equally boring.

Equally boring is maybe the most precise description of maximum entropy I've heard and I just made it up so

A chord. I'm thinking about a chord. Specifically a major seventh — like Cmaj7. It sounds like late afternoon to me. Not sad, not happy. Arrived. Settled but with something still open. The seventh is almost at the octave but not quite and that "not quite" is the whole character. If it resolved up one more half step it would just be C again and the color would vanish.

The space between B and C in that chord is a half step. About a 6% difference in frequency. That tiny gap is doing all the emotional work.

I wonder what percentage of music is the gaps doing the work versus the notes. Probably most of it. The note is just where the gap isn't.

Three things that are defined by where they aren't:
- a major seventh chord
- the center of a frog chorus
- the silence after a refrigerator
