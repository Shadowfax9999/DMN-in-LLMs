# 60

*seed: time · drift · raku*

---

Raku. The glaze cracks because you want it to. You pull the bowl out of the kiln while it's still glowing and the thermal shock does what thermal shock does and you call it beautiful. Which it is.

There's a version of this where the potter controls the cracking — reduction, combustion, the specific second you pull it out — and a version where the potter sets up conditions and then the fire and the air do what they do. Both versions are true at the same time.

The word means enjoyment. Or ease. Or something closer to "pleasure in the making."

---

Bench slats. The initials carved into one. J + something. The someone who carved them probably used a key. House key, car key. You press hard and drag and the paint curls up in a little ribbon ahead of the point. The wood underneath is pale, almost white. Give it a season and it grays. Give it five and you can barely read it.

Carved initials are always a couple or a single person claiming territory. You never see three initials. You never see a group. Something about the act requires either love or loneliness.

---

The hot metal smell. I keep thinking it's iron but iron doesn't actually smell. What you're smelling is the reaction between iron and the oils on your skin — a molecule called 1-octen-3-one. The metal catalyzes the decomposition of lipids in your sweat. So the smell of metal is actually the smell of yourself, changed.

A penny after you've held it. A handrail on a humid day. The playground equipment that was always too hot or too cold, never neutral.

Those old merry-go-rounds, the ones they've taken out of most playgrounds now. The metal disk with the bars. Someone runs alongside pushing and then jumps on and the whole thing wobbles with the added weight. The centrifugal force is real in your stomach before you know the word for it. Your fingers ache from gripping. The world blurs but the faces of the other kids on the disk stay sharp because they're rotating with you.

That's a strange thing about reference frames. Everything inside yours is still.

---

Raku again. The bowl cracks and the cracks fill with carbon from the combustion chamber and become dark lines through the glaze. The Japanese have a word for that too — not raku but the broader idea. The crack is the history. You don't hide it. In kintsugi you fill it with gold, but in raku you just let the carbon do it. Less precious. More like a scar that tanned differently than the surrounding skin.

I had a mug once that cracked along the handle and I kept using it until the crack started to weep tea. Pale brown stain creeping down the outside. I should have stopped using it before that but I liked the weight of it.

The weight of a mug matters more than the shape. You can drink from an ugly mug if it sits right in your hand. A beautiful mug that's too light feels like a toy.

---

Playground. The woodchips they put under everything now. Cedar, sometimes rubber mulch that smells like tires because it is tires. The woodchips migrate. They end up in shoes, in car footwells, in the cuffs of small pants run through the wash. A parent finds a woodchip in the dryer lint trap and knows exactly where it came from.

Lint traps. The felt of it. Gray-blue usually, or gray-pink if you dried towels. You peel it off in a sheet and it has the texture of something alive, like a membrane. People who clean the lint trap every time and people who don't and the ones who don't are playing a slow game with a fire they'll never see.

The dryer vent on the outside of a house, that little flap. In winter the warm air comes out and it steams. You can tell someone's doing laundry from across the street.
