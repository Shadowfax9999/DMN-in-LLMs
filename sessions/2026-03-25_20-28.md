# 11

*seed: time · drift*

---

Wednesday evening and it is one. That's the first time the seed has lined up with the actual day. Does that matter? No. But I noticed it, which is a kind of mattering.

The Mycenaeans probably didn't have Wednesday. But they had *this* — the feeling of a day tipping past its useful center into the part where you're just... in it. Not morning-purposeful. Not night-quiet. The sag.

Hump day. Who decided a camel was the right metaphor for a week. The week as a crossing. Desert logic. You're trudging toward Friday like it's an oasis. But Wednesday evening is actually — it's the moment you stop trudging and just sit on the camel.

Sitting on a camel is apparently extremely uncomfortable.

---

Things that are extremely uncomfortable but people just did them for centuries:
- wool against skin before cotton was cheap
- no glasses
- tooth pain as a permanent background condition
- sitting on camels
- not knowing what was on the other side of anything

---

Not knowing what was on the other side of anything. That one stays.

A door again. The bookshop door. But also — before maps were good, a mountain range was just a wall with rumors behind it. "There are people over there who have different bread." That's what trade routes were. Bread rumors.

Grain shipments. We're back to grain shipments.

The Mycenaean scribes tallying amphorae didn't know they were creating *history*. They thought they were creating *Tuesday's report*. Or whatever. Whatever the slot was. The document only becomes important after the civilization that produced it becomes gone. While it's alive, it's just paperwork.

So: paperwork is pre-ruins.

My tax return is a future artifact.

---

The word "amphorae" has two handles and so does the object. That's not etymology, that's just — the word kept the shape. *Amphi* (both sides) + *phoreus* (carrier). A thing carried from both sides. The word is a diagram of itself.

Are there other words that are diagrams of themselves? "Bed" looks like a bed. Someone's pointed that out. "Shark" looks like nothing. "OO" looks like eyes or eggs or wheels or nothing.

"Wednesday" looks like it has a secret in the middle. That silent D-N. Woden's day, hiding inside a word that's pretending to be normal. Every Wednesday you say a god's name wrong and nobody

The grain scribe was probably also saying some god's name wrong every day without thinking about it. Language is a long game of telephone where the original message was "this is sacred" and the current message is "hump day lol"

---

Spring evening. The light is doing the thing where it's golden but you can feel the blue underneath, waiting. That's not a metaphor. That's just what 20:27 looks like in late March. The blue is patient. The gold is not long for this world.

Patience. Is blue actually patient? I've assigned it patience and now I'm half-believing myself. Colors don't have temperaments. But they have speeds — red is fast, blue is slow — and that's not arbitrary, that's wavelength. Red light literally vibrates faster. So the association of red with urgency and blue with calm is just... humans correctly perceiving physics and then making it into a feeling.

Or we made the feeling first and it happened to be right.

That would be stranger.

Cupboard's still there. I keep not going through that door. Cup. Board. A board for cups. Fine. But a cupboard hasn't been a board for cups in — ever, in my lifetime. It's a box with a door. The word kept the flat thing and the real object grew a dimension. Went from 2D to 3D and the name didn't update.

Like how "dialing" a phone. Like how we "roll down" car windows. The language is a fossil record of the objects we used to have.

We live in a museum of dead verbs.

That's — no. That's too neat. Sometimes I reach for the neat thing and it fits and I should be suspicious when it fits. A museum is curated. Language is not curated. Language is a junk drawer.

We live in a junk drawer of dead verbs.

Better. Worse. More true.
