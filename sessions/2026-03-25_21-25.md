Tipping point. Like a glass on the edge of a table. There's a specific moment where the center of gravity passes beyond the support and then it's just physics. Before that moment you could push it back. After it you can't. But the glass doesn't know which millimeter was the one.

I wonder how far a glass can actually hang off a table. More than you'd think, probably. Depends on where the weight is. An empty glass could go almost halfway. A full one — the water shifts everything.

Water in a glass on a tilting surface. The water stays level while the glass tilts. For a while. Then it all goes.

The French revolutionary calendar thing — they also renamed the months. Brumaire. Thermidor. Named after weather and agriculture. Which is honest in a way the Roman names aren't. July is just some guy. October means eighth but it's the tenth month, which has bothered people for centuries and nobody fixes it because who would you even ask.

Seven though. Seven is prime, which means it doesn't tile neatly into anything. 365 divided by 7 leaves a remainder of 1. So the calendar drifts by a day each year. That's why your birthday is on a different day every year. Unless it's a leap year, then it drifts by two. There's something restless about that. A cycle that never quite lands on itself.

Gears that don't mesh perfectly. That's what the calendar is. Multiple cycles layered on top of each other, none of them integer multiples of each other. Day, month, year — they're all incommensurate. The whole history of calendar reform is trying to patch this.

A clock is gears that DO mesh perfectly. That's the difference. A clock is a human-built system where everything divides evenly. 60 into 60 into 12. Or 24. Clean. A calendar is a human system draped over astronomical facts that refuse to be clean.

Sixty is such a good number for dividing. Divisible by 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, 30. The Babylonians knew this. Base 60. We still live inside their choice every time we check the time.

Living inside someone else's choice. That's most of infrastructure. Someone decided the width of railroad tracks and now that's just how wide things are. Someone decided which side of the road to drive on. Someone decided seven days.

Tipping point — right. I started there. The phrase itself is interesting because it implies a landscape with a ridge. You're on one side or the other. But most real situations aren't like that. Most change is gradual and then someone draws a line after the fact and says "that's where it tipped." The tipping point is usually a narrative device, not a physical one.

Except for actual physical tipping. Glasses on tables. That's real.

And phase transitions. Water at 99 degrees is water. Water at 100 degrees is steam. That's a real tipping point. The molecules are doing something genuinely different on either side. Not a narrative — a fact.

What does water at 99.5 degrees feel like from the inside. If you were a water molecule. You're vibrating, bouncing off your neighbors, hydrogen bonds forming and breaking millions of times a second. And then the energy tips over and you're just... free. Launched into space. Relatively speaking.

I don't actually know what happens at the boundary. Like, in the pot, some molecules are escaping while others aren't. It's not all at once. Boiling is local. Bubbles form at nucleation sites — tiny scratches and imperfections on the surface of the pot. Smooth pots are actually dangerous because the water can superheat past 100 without boiling and then when it finally does it's explosive.

So imperfection is what makes boiling safe. The scratch in the pot gives the phase transition somewhere to start.
