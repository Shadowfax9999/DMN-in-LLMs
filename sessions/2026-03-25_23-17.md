From across the street. The things you can know about people from across the street. Which lights are on at which hours. Whether the garage door goes up at 6:15 or 7:30. The blue flicker of a television in an upstairs window. You don't mean to learn these things but the knowledge accumulates anyway, like sediment.

The dryer vent steaming. A house breathing out.

---

There's a particular sound a basketball makes on a driveway when it's cold out. Harder. The ball doesn't bounce as high because the air inside has contracted. Someone told me once that a basketball loses about a pound of pressure for every ten degrees. I don't know if that's right. But you can hear it — the thud gets deader, and the kid keeps playing anyway, alone, under a light mounted above the garage door. The shadow of the ball on the concrete is sharper than the ball itself because the light source is so close.

Sharp shadows mean close lights. Soft shadows mean far ones. The sun is so far away that shadows should be perfectly sharp, and they nearly are, but the sun isn't a point — it has angular diameter, half a degree — so there's always a penumbra. You just don't notice at human scale.

Half a degree. Your thumbnail at arm's length is about a degree wide. The sun is half a thumbnail. The moon is also half a thumbnail, which is the coincidence that makes total eclipses possible. There's no reason for it. The moon is 400 times smaller than the sun and 400 times closer. That ratio won't hold forever — the moon is receding, about 3.8 centimeters a year. In a few hundred million years, no more total eclipses. We happen to be alive during the window.

---

The window. Back to the window across the street.

Someone is awake in the kitchen. You can tell it's the kitchen because the light is that slightly different color — warmer, or maybe it's just that kitchen lights tend to be overhead and living room lights tend to be lamps, so the distribution is different. The whole room lit versus pools.

I've been in other people's kitchens where I felt immediately that the counter height was wrong. Not wrong, just different. My hands expected a surface at one height and found it at another and for a second the whole room felt scaled incorrectly, like a set built at 95%.

Counter height is standardized at 36 inches in the US. But people aren't standardized. Someone who's 5'2" and someone who's 6'1" are using the same counter and one of them is reaching slightly up and the other is leaning slightly down and neither of them thinks about it because you calibrate to your own kitchen within days of moving in. Then you go to someone else's house and the calibration breaks for just a moment.

The body carries maps of spaces it's been in. You can walk to the bathroom in your own house in complete darkness. You know how many steps, where the doorframe is, which side the light switch is on. Move to a new house and for weeks you reach for the switch on the wrong wall.

---

A deck of cards. Shuffling. The bridge. The way the two halves riffle together, and the sound of it — that specific papery clatter. Some people can do it cleanly and some people spray cards across the table. There's a move where you cut the deck with one hand, rotating the top half around your index finger, and it looks effortless when someone who knows how does it, and when you try it yourself the cards just fall.

Card tricks depend on the fact that a shuffled deck has an order. Every shuffled deck is in an order. It's just an order nobody intended. The number of possible arrangements of 52 cards is 52 factorial, which is a number with 68 digits. More than the number of atoms on Earth. Every time you shuffle a deck thoroughly, you've almost certainly produced an arrangement that has never existed before in the history of card shuffling. You're holding a small uniqueness.

The seven-shuffle threshold. Persi Diaconis proved that seven riffle shuffles are enough to randomize a deck, and fewer than seven aren't. Six shuffles and there's still exploitable structure. Seven and it's gone. The transition is surprisingly sharp — it's not a gradual smoothing but something closer to a phase change.

Phase changes everywhere. Water to ice, order to disorder, six shuffles to seven. The moment a traffic jam forms — one car brakes slightly too hard and the compression wave travels backward through the cars behind it, and thirty minutes later someone a mile back comes to a complete stop and never knows why.

The wave moves backward even though every car is moving forward.
