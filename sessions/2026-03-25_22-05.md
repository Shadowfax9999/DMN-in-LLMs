Petting a cat the wrong way — they really do mind. Not all of them, but most. There's a threshold somewhere along the back where the touch flips from welcome to irritating, and you can feel the muscle twitch under the skin before the cat even moves. Like a flinch that hasn't decided to become a flinch yet.

Threshold. The word for the bottom of a doorway. Supposedly from threshing — the bar that kept the grain on the floor from spilling out. You'd step over it. So every time you enter a room you're symbolically stepping over a barrier that kept wheat in.

That's probably folk etymology actually. I'm not sure.

Standing in line at a hardware store once, behind a man buying exactly one hinge. Just one hinge. A single brass hinge. And I thought about what situation leads to buying one hinge. A cabinet door that sags. A box someone's building. A repair so specific it means someone looked at a broken thing and figured out exactly what was wrong with it and came here for the one part. There's something satisfying about that. Knowing which piece failed.

Versus the people buying a cart full of lumber and looking slightly panicked.

The way a car sounds different when you're driving through a tunnel versus open road. The engine note rises — or it seems to. It's the reflection. The sound bounces back compressed. You hear yourself. The car hears itself? No, the car doesn't hear anything. But the acoustic space tightens and the pitch seems to shift and people rev their engines in tunnels on purpose because of this. Because it sounds good. Because the tunnel is a resonating chamber and your Honda Civic briefly sounds like something expensive.

There's a tunnel in Los Angeles — the 2nd Street tunnel maybe — where people honk just to hear it. A communal act. You enter the tunnel and you honk and the person ahead of you honks and it becomes a thing that the tunnel does to people. The architecture of the space changes the behavior.

---

A list of things that change your behavior without you deciding:

- a low ceiling makes you hunch
- a wide open field makes you walk slower or faster, depending on something I can't name
- a room with no windows makes you lose time
- a narrow hallway makes you speed up
- someone yawning

---

The yawn thing. It's not just mimicry. Dogs catch yawns from humans. Humans catch yawns from dogs. It crosses species. There's something about the yawn that's more fundamental than imitation — it's like a signal that passes through a channel older than language, older than facial expression reading, almost like a reflex that got networked.

Babies don't catch yawns until around age four. Which is roughly when theory of mind develops. So maybe it is tied to modeling other minds. You yawn because somewhere in you, you briefly became the other person, felt their tiredness, and your body responded to the feeling that isn't yours.

Or it's just a brainstem reflex and I'm making it more interesting than it is.

Both could be true at different levels.

Corduroy again. The word. *Corde du roi* — cord of the king. That's another folk etymology that might be wrong. Might just be English: cord + duroy, where duroy was a coarse woolen fabric. The royal origin story is better, which is probably why it persists. We keep the better story even when the boring one is more likely.

A fabric named after a king that was never named after a king.
