The guilt was immediate but the letting-go was slow. Unscrewing the lid, tilting the jar, waiting. Fireflies don't rush out. They walk to the rim and sit there like they're deciding something.

I've been thinking about jars. Mason jars specifically. The two-part lid — the flat disc and the threaded ring — is a design that separates the sealing function from the tightening function. You can reuse the ring but the disc is supposed to be single-use because the rubber compound on the underside deforms to match the jar's lip. People reuse them anyway. My grandmother reused them. She had a drawer of lids organized by nothing, just lids in a drawer, and she'd test each one by pressing the center to see if it still had pop. If it clicked, no good. If it was rigid, the seal was still viable. She could do this at speed. Thirty lids in a minute, sorted into two piles on the counter.

That gesture — pressing the center of a lid with your thumb — is so specific. There must be thousands of gestures like that, ones that belong to a particular skill and nowhere else. The way a baker pinches dough to test hydration. The way a mechanic runs a finger along a belt to check tension. You could identify someone's craft by their hands, not by calluses but by the movements their hands make when they're idle. A pianist's fingers micro-practicing on a tabletop. A surgeon's — actually I don't know what a surgeon's hands do at rest.

There's a vine growing up the chain-link fence behind the parking lot at the place where I get my oil changed. Every time I'm there — which is what, three times a year — the vine is in a different state. Brown and bare. Then suddenly dense and covered in white flowers. Then just green. I never see the transitions. It's like time-lapse photography where someone removed most of the frames.

That's how I experience most plants. In states, not in process. Grass is either long or just mown. The tree either has leaves or doesn't. Somewhere in there is the actual growing, but it happens at a pace that doesn't register against the rhythm of errands.

Except bamboo. Someone told me you can hear bamboo growing if it's quiet enough, that the shoots creak as they extend. I don't know if that's true. It sounds like the kind of thing that's repeated because it *should* be true. A lot of nature facts have that quality — they persist not because they're verified but because they satisfy something. The daddy longlegs venom thing. The Great Wall from space thing. They're folklore wearing a lab coat.

Oil change waiting rooms. The specific quality of sitting in a plastic chair watching a television you didn't choose, tuned to a channel you'd never select, at a volume that's either too low to follow or too loud to ignore. There are magazines from eight months ago. The coffee is from a machine that takes pods. Someone else is also waiting and you both perform the act of not-interacting with a precision that would be impressive if anyone were watching. Occasional glances calibrated to never overlap.

The mechanic comes out and says something about your air filter.
