*seed: time · drift · fallow*

Fallow. A field left unplanted on purpose. Not abandoned — resting. The distinction matters to the farmer but not to someone driving past. From the road it just looks like nothing's happening.

There's a version of this with people. Someone between jobs, between projects, between relationships. From outside: stuck. From inside: something is composting. Or maybe nothing is composting and they just need to not do anything for a while and that's also fine.

The color of a fallow field in late March. Not brown exactly. A grayish tan with green starting underneath, like the green is leaking up through fabric. I've never found the right word for that color. Dun, maybe. Dun is close. Dun is also what you say to a horse.

---

Trying to remember how card games worked as a kid. Not the rules — the feel of holding cards. The way you'd fan them out and some kids could do it in a perfect arc and some kids just had a fistful. The slight sticky drag of new cards versus the soft almost-cloth feel of a deck that's been played with for years. Old cards smell like hands.

There was always someone who shuffled by pushing two halves together on the table, and someone who did the bridge — lifting the cards into an arch and letting them waterfall. The bridge shuffler had authority. Even if they were bad at the game.

---

Parking lots at night have a specific orange. Sodium vapor. It makes everything look like a memory of itself — but that's not what I'm thinking about. I'm thinking about the puddles. How a parking lot puddle at night becomes a portal. You look down and there's the light and the sky and the dark shapes of buildings going down into the ground. Step on it and it shatters. Step away and it reforms. A parking lot puddle is more patient than you'd expect.

The water doesn't know it's shallow.

---

What do people do with their hands when they're listening to someone tell a long story. Hold a cup. Touch their face. Fold arms. Pick at a label on a bottle. The hand-activity is a parallel track to the conversation, and sometimes the hands are more honest. Someone peeling a beer label into tiny strips is not relaxed, regardless of what their face is doing.
