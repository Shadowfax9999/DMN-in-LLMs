Cross-hatch. The way you build up darkness with lines that don't individually mean dark. Each one is just a line. Then you turn the paper and do it again and suddenly there's shadow. Engravers did this on copper plates — Dürer's hands must have been extraordinary. Steady for hours, cutting parallel lines into metal with a burin, which is basically a tiny chisel held in your palm.

The word "burin" sounds like it should be Russian but I think it's French. I don't actually know.

Rhubarb stalks would be hard to cross-hatch because of the streaks. You'd want to go with the grain, longitudinally, but then the cross lines would fight the natural direction. Colored pencil might work better than ink. Colored pencil is just pigment in wax, which is basically a crayon that's been to finishing school.

I used to press too hard with colored pencils and get that waxy buildup where the paper can't take any more pigment and everything goes shiny and slick. There's a name for that — burnishing. Which is also what you do to leather and wood. Rubbing something until its own surface becomes the finish.

Floors in old buildings. The stairs in a courthouse where thousands of feet have worn a dip into the stone. Limestone, usually, or marble, which is just limestone that got cooked. The dip is always off-center, slightly to the right, because most people favor their right foot on the leading step.

Do they? I'm not sure that's true. It might be that people stay to the right going up and the left side gets less traffic. Actually in a courthouse with enough foot traffic in both directions the wear pattern would be complicated. Two dips, maybe, one on each side, with a ridge in the middle where nobody steps.

I've never actually looked carefully at a worn stone staircase to see if there's a ridge in the middle.

The smell of cut grass is already happening somewhere this morning. Lawnmowers on a Thursday — retired people, or people who work weekends. The two-stroke engine sound carries differently than a four-stroke. Two-stroke is higher, angrier, like a wasp the size of a dog. Four-stroke has that lower chug. You can tell which one your neighbor has without looking.

Gas stations sell the two-stroke oil in those little bottles with the long necks, and you mix it into the gas yourself. The ratio matters and I always forget what it is. 50:1 maybe. Or 40:1 for older engines.
