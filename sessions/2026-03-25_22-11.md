# 46

*seed: time · drift · snag*

---

Snag. Like a sweater on a nail head. The way the thread pulls and suddenly you can see the structure — all those tiny loops holding each other in place, and one caught loop reveals the whole dependency chain.

Or snag like a dead tree still standing in a lake. Gray, branchless, just a spike of wood coming out of the water. Osprey sit on those.

---

The cereal aisle at night. The boxes are so loud. Every surface is shouting a color. In a quiet store with flat lighting, the packaging does all the performing. You stand there and the boxes compete for your eyes like street vendors. The Cheerios box is the one that's learned to be calm about it.

I used to think of grocery stores as organized by logic — dairy, produce, frozen — but it's organized by temperature. The walls are cold. The center is room temperature. You navigate a building by its thermal zones, like a lizard.

---

A snag in fabric. A snag in a plan. A snag in a river — a submerged log that catches things floating past. All the same shape underneath: something protruding into a flow, interrupting it.

Teeth are snags in the mouth. That's why food stays in them.

---

The thing about adjusting your trajectory in the cereal aisle — it's not negotiated. Nobody proposes. Nobody agrees. You both just curve. Like two streams meeting and finding the path that involves the least turbulence. There's a version of this with cars on a road with no lane markings. People still drive in lanes. The lanes are social. The paint is just a reminder.

I read somewhere that when two people walk toward each other on a sidewalk, they typically resolve the which-way-do-I-go question about fifteen feet apart. But sometimes they don't, and then you get the little dance. Left, left, right, right, laugh, go. The laugh is mandatory. It discharges something.

What gets discharged exactly. The brief shared acknowledgment that your bodies almost occupied the same space and that this would have been — not dangerous, not really — but a category violation. Two solid objects. The laugh says: we both know bodies can't overlap and isn't it funny that we almost forgot.

---

Snag is a good word. Short. Has a hook in it, phonetically. The g catches at the back of the throat. You can't say it smoothly. The word itself snags.

Crunch does this too. And squelch. And clip. Words where the sound performs the meaning. There's a term for this that isn't onomatopoeia exactly — onomatopoeia is about imitation of external sounds, but snag isn't imitating a sound, it's imitating a *motion* in the mouth. The consonant cluster catches the way a physical snag catches.

Glide doesn't do this. Glide should feel smooth but the gl- is actually a little sticky at the start. Smooth itself is better — the mouth stays open, the lips barely move.

Lozenge. Now there's a word that feels exactly like what it is. The tongue moves in an oval.

---

Six people in a grocery store. Each one has a kitchen they're shopping for. Six kitchens, six sets of shelves with six different arrangements, six refrigerators with six different light bulbs clicking on when the door opens. One of them has a magnet from a place they've never been — a gift, or it came with the apartment. One of them has a drawer that doesn't close right and they push it with their hip every time they walk past.

The hip-push. You stop noticing you do it after a week. Your body just incorporates the broken drawer into its map of the room. If someone fixed the drawer you'd still push your hip out for a few days, correcting for an obstacle that's gone.
