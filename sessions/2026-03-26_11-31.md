Dental crowns. The dentist takes an impression of your tooth — that's another mold, another negative space. Bites down on that putty that tastes like nothing should taste. The tray is always too big or too small. Then somewhere a technician pours material into the shape your tooth left behind. You get back a porcelain version of your own tooth, made from a mold of a tooth that's been ground down to a peg. So the crown is a copy of the tooth that was, fitted over the tooth that remains, and neither one is quite the original.

I had a filling fall out once while eating bread. Not even hard bread. Just regular sandwich bread. It sat on my tongue like a piece of gravel and my tongue immediately found the hole and wouldn't leave it alone. That's the thing about the tongue — it's obsessive. A canker sore, a chip, a gap, a rough edge. The tongue returns and returns and returns. It can't help itself.

There's a word for when you can't stop touching a broken tooth with your tongue. I don't know what it is. Maybe there isn't one. There should be.

Proprioception is the sense of where your body is in space but there's something else going on with the tongue — it's not just knowing where the tongue is, it's the tongue actively mapping the interior of your mouth, constantly updating. Like sonar. Running its own survey. The tongue knows the inside of your mouth better than your eyes know your own face.

Catfish have taste buds on the outside of their bodies. Their whole skin tastes. Swimming must be overwhelming. Or maybe it's not — maybe it's just normal, the way seeing everything all the time is normal for us. We don't walk around thinking "I'm seeing so much right now." The baseline disappears.

That's how noise works too. You stop hearing the refrigerator until it clicks off. The absence is louder than the sound was. I've been in rooms where an air conditioner shuts down and everyone looks up at the same moment, like something fell.

The compressor in a refrigerator cycles on and off based on the thermostat. There's a relay — a little electromagnetic switch. That click is the relay closing or opening. It's a surprisingly small component for how much of a kitchen's soundscape it controls.

Small components that control large systems. A thermostat is just a bimetallic strip in the old ones. Two metals bonded together that expand at different rates, so the strip curls as it heats up, eventually breaking contact. The whole heating cycle of a house governed by the physics of two metals not quite agreeing with each other.
