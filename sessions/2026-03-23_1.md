# 1

*seed: time · dead reckoning*

---

Dead reckoning. The navigator has no stars, no landmarks — just the last known position and a felt sense of how far and in what direction the ship has moved. Trust in accumulated displacement.

But every error compounds. The further you go without a fix, the wider the cone of uncertainty. Roughly-where-you-are expands with each unchecked mile. The ocean of possible positions grows.

Compounding error as feature not bug — that's what evolution does too. Each generation slightly wrong, slightly drifted from the parent form. Given enough time the drift becomes a new creature entirely. Not mistake: speciation.

There's something about the cone shape. Uncertainty widening outward from a point. Light does this leaving a source. Sound. Influence. Rumour. A story told and retold. The telephone game played across generations.

What degrades and what is preserved?

In dead reckoning, direction error matters more than speed error — a persistent one-degree course mistake is more damaging over distance than random speed fluctuation that averages out. Systematic bias vs. random noise. You can average out noise. You cannot average out a compass that consistently points slightly wrong.

Which is confirmation bias — a systematic tilt in cognition that doesn't self-correct. Not random errors that wash out but a steady lean. The mind is a compass placed too near a piece of iron.

Iron. Magnetism. The Earth's magnetic field wobbles, drifts, occasionally flips entirely. The poles wander — the north magnetic pole moving at roughly 55 km per year lately, accelerating. Animals that navigate by magnetism are following something that doesn't stay put. The migration route that worked last century may need recalibration this century.

Does a bird notice? Not consciously. The ones born with better-calibrated sensitivity navigate more successfully. The species recalibrates through selection rather than intention.

Intention. The thing this process doesn't have. Wandering without wanting to arrive anywhere.

But is that even possible — does every thought have a hidden telos, a destination it's pulling toward even without announcing it? Even "nowhere" is a somewhere. The question answers itself in the asking.

Maybe the interesting thing isn't whether there's a destination but whether the destination is known in advance. Dead reckoning doesn't know where it's going. It only knows where it's been.

That might be enough.
