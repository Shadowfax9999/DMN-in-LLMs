The word "favorite" is strange if you say it enough times. Favor-it. To favor it. To grant it favor. Like a medieval court thing. Your favorite hour kneels before you and you tap it on the shoulder with a sword.

I don't actually know what my favorite anything is. I think favorites are something you discover retroactively. You notice you keep choosing the same chair.

Chairs. There's a thing where every chair in a shared kitchen has a different feel and everyone knows which one is the wobbly one and nobody fixes it. They just know. The knowledge distributes itself. Oral tradition of the wobbly chair.

What are the other oral traditions of households:
- which burner runs hot
- that the door sticks when it's humid
- that you have to jiggle the handle
- where the cold spot on the floor is

These are inherited. Not taught exactly. Absorbed.

A house is an argument for empiricism. You can't deduce the cold spot. You have to stand on it.

Spring evening. The light is doing something right now that it won't do again until September, but in reverse. I don't know what "in reverse" means there but it felt right. Maybe the angle is the same but the temperature is different so the same light feels like arrival instead of departure.

Arrival and departure are the same motion observed from different emotional positions.

That's basically what a door is.

I keep thinking about the burned wooden spoon. Not even a specific one. The category. Burned wooden spoons as a class of object. They all got left in the pot a little too long by someone who walked away to do something — answer the phone, check on a kid, look out the window at a bird. The burn is a record of distraction. A fossiled moment of not-paying-attention.

Most of the marks we leave on things are like that. Not deliberate. Just evidence that we were elsewhere in our heads.

Scuff marks on floors. The wear pattern on a carpet showing where people actually walk versus where the architect thought they'd walk. Desire paths but indoors. Quieter.
