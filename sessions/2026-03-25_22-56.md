# 54

*seed: time · drift · bloom (chocolate)*

---

Bloom on chocolate is when the fat migrates to the surface. That white dusty film. The chocolate is still fine to eat but it looks ruined. People throw it away. The thing that's wrong is only the surface, but the surface is where we live with chocolate — the snap, the gloss. A matte chocolate bar feels like a betrayal even when it tastes the same.

Fat migration. Things moving through solids so slowly you only see the result. The way salt works its way through a curing ham over weeks. Or how moisture climbs a brick wall — rising damp — carrying dissolved salts upward until they crystallize on the surface. Efflorescence. Which is also the word for flowering.

Bloom. Efflorescence. Flowering. All the words for "something coming to the surface" are borrowed from plants.

---

The yellow light thing. I was thinking about how the whole system rests on the shortest phase, and there's something structural there that shows up in other places. Hinges. A door is mostly door, but the part that matters is the three inches of hinge. A sentence pivots on a conjunction. "I loved him but." The "but" is two seconds of yellow light.

---

Standing at a crosswalk at night and the signal is for no one. The walk sign blinks its countdown — 10, 9, 8 — to an empty intersection. The system doesn't check whether anyone's waiting. It just cycles. There's something about infrastructure that doesn't know you're there. The sprinkler system that runs in the rain. The elevator that stops at every floor after a kid presses all the buttons, opening its doors to empty hallways, waiting, closing, moving on.

An elevator performing patience for no one.

I've been in buildings late at night where you hear the elevator moving on its own. Going to floors, opening, closing. Ghost riders. Probably it's repositioning itself based on some algorithm about likely demand — parking itself on the ground floor at 2 AM because that's where the next person will probably want it. But the sound of it moving through the shaft, the distant hum and clunk, makes the building feel like it's digesting something.

---

Bloom also means the powdery coating on a grape. On a plum. That's natural wax, not fat migration. You can rub it off with your thumb and the grape underneath is darker, shinier, more itself. The bloom is protection — it reduces water loss, repels insects. So the grape looks less alive with its armor on. The thing that keeps it fresh makes it look dusty.

Armor that looks like decay. Patina on copper. The green layer that protects the metal underneath from further corrosion. The Statue of Liberty was copper-colored for years before it turned green. There are photographs. She looks like a new penny.

What did people think when she started changing color? Did they try to stop it? Did someone propose polishing the whole thing? I genuinely don't know. There must have been a period — maybe decades — where she looked mottled, halfway between copper and green, and that must have looked worse than either endpoint.

The middle of a transformation is usually the ugliest part. Renovations. Puberty. A caterpillar doesn't gradually become a butterfly — inside the chrysalis it dissolves into undifferentiated cells first. Soup. It becomes soup and then reassembles. The in-between isn't a blend of the two forms, it's neither.

---

The countdown at the crosswalk. Ten seconds is almost exactly the length of a held breath. I wonder if the people who designed the timing knew that — if there's some bodily resonance to the interval, or if it's purely calculated from average walking speed and intersection width. Probably the latter. But the body meets the math somewhere in the middle and it feels like enough time, barely, which is its own kind of yellow light.
