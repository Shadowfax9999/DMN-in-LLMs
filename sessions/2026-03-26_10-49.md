# 73

*seed: time · drift · fretboard*

---

Fretboard. The frets get closer together as you move up the neck. You'd think they'd be even but they're logarithmic, which means the guitar is wearing its math on the outside. A piano hides it — equal spacing, every key the same width, the complexity buried in the tuning. A guitar just shows you. The twelfth fret is halfway along the string and everything above it is a miniature of everything below.

I keep thinking about the keys on the ring. Knowing which one by feel. There's a kind of knowledge that lives in the hand and if you asked the person to describe the key they couldn't, but in the dark, in a hurry, with a coffee in the other hand, they grab right. The hand knows a thing the mouth can't say. Guitarists have this. The chord shape is in the fingers before the name of the chord arrives. Sometimes the name never arrives. Somebody plays a Cmaj7 for years and calls it "that one."

Frets are a commitment. A fretless instrument — a violin, a cello — can put the note anywhere. The space between E and F is continuous. A fretted instrument says no, these are the notes, these and no others. It's a grid laid over a continuous surface. A decision made in advance so you don't have to make it in the moment. Someone in a strip mall guitar factory in 1994 pressed metal wire into a rosewood slot and that wire is still deciding where the music goes.

Rosewood. I like that word more than the wood itself. The wood is dark brown, sometimes purplish. It doesn't smell like roses. I think the name is from the shavings — when you cut it, it supposedly smells faintly sweet. I'm not sure about that. Could be one of those facts that circulates without being true. A lot of wood lore is like that. People say cedar repels moths but the effect fades after a few years and by then the cedar chest is just furniture that smells nice.

The cedar chest at my grandmother's house. Everyone had one. Or not everyone, but a type of household had one, and those households also had a particular weight of wool blanket and a particular way of folding sheets where you tucked the corners into each other so the fitted sheet became a neat rectangle. I never learned how to do that. I just bunch the fitted sheet into a shape and stuff it in the closet.

There's a fundamental divide between people who can fold a fitted sheet and people who can't, and it tracks onto almost nothing else about a person. You can be competent, organized, intelligent, and still just wad it up. The sheet defeats you. The elastic defeats you. You try to find the corners and the corners aren't corners, they're pouches, and the geometry never resolves into anything flat.

Flat. A guitarist says a note is flat when it's slightly below the target pitch. A real estate agent says flat when the land doesn't slope. A soda is flat when the CO₂ has left. A story falls flat. A flat in London is an apartment. A flat tire. A flat affect. The word just means *without the expected dimension* — the pitch is missing its height, the land its slope, the soda its fizz, the tire its air, the voice its variation. Something should be there and isn't.

The bump at the bottom of the glass door. Eleven years of that bump and then one day you sell the business or retire or the lease is up and you lock the door for the last time and the bump happens one final time and you don't know it's the last time. You almost never know the last time is the last time. The last time you pick up your kid. The last time you visit a particular grocery store. The last time you play a particular song. It just happens and then it's behind you and the not-knowing is maybe merciful because if you knew you'd make it weird, you'd try to be present for it, you'd over-feel it. The bump is good precisely because nobody's paying attention to it.

Fretboard again. The wood darkens where the fingers press most often. First position, near the nut — the first three frets get the most wear. You can read a guitar's history in the wear patterns. An open-chord strummer wears the first few frets evenly. A blues player wears a groove at the seventh and twelfth. The wood remembers what the player played most, long after the player is done playing.

Thursday morning. Somewhere a guitar is hanging on a wall in a room where nobody plays it anymore.
