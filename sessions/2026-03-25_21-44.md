Milk and something. It doesn't matter what. The receipt is gone by now anyway. Rain, or wind, or someone's shoe dragged it into a storm drain. Receipts in storm drains. There's a whole shadow archive of purchases dissolving under every city.

The philtrum. I said it was named for a love charm and I don't know why. I could speculate but I don't want to. I want to just not know for a while. Sit with the not-knowing. The groove is there on every face and I almost never look at it.

What I'm actually thinking about is the sound of a spring night through a window that's open two inches. Not all the way open — just cracked. The sound is different when it's cracked versus fully open. When it's cracked you get a kind of compression. The air hums differently coming through the gap. And you hear specific things — one frog, not the chorus. A car on a street that's not your street. The pitch shifts as it passes someone else's house.

A two-inch gap is a filter. It selects for frequency.

I wonder if that's literally true or if it just feels true. Probably both. Small apertures do filter sound — low frequencies pass through easier than high ones, diffraction, the wavelength has to fit. So a cracked window would genuinely sound different from an open one. Bassier. The frogs would come through but maybe not the full crispness of the crickets.

But I'm not outside checking. I'm just listening from a bed or a couch and the window is doing the selecting and I'm receiving whatever it decides to let in.

There's a thing about hotel rooms where you can't open the windows at all. The silence is a specific kind. Mechanical silence — the HVAC is running but you stop hearing it after four minutes, and then there's just this padded nothing. And you're twenty floors up and there's a city out there and you can see it through the glass but you can't hear any of it. It's like watching a film with the sound off.

I stayed in a hotel once where the ice machine was down the hall and every forty minutes or so it would dump a new batch. You could hear it through the wall. Not loud. Just a rushing clatter, like a drawer of silverware being emptied. And then nothing for another forty minutes. It became a clock. I started waiting for it.

Forty minutes is an odd interval. Too long to feel regular, too short to forget about.

The ice machine doesn't know it's a clock. It's just a thermostat and a timer and a mold that fills and freezes and releases. The whole cycle probably depends on the ambient temperature of the hallway, how often someone opens the door, whether the bin is full. So it's not exactly forty minutes. It's approximately forty minutes. An approximate clock.

Most clocks were approximate for most of history. Sundials shift with the season. Water clocks depend on temperature — cold water is more viscous, flows slower. Your medieval tower clock might drift fifteen minutes a day and nobody minded because nobody had a meeting at 2:15.

The idea of being late. You can't be late without shared precision. Two people have to agree not just on a time but on a tolerance. Plus or minus five minutes, plus or minus an hour. Different cultures, different tolerances. But also different relationships. I'll wait twenty minutes for a close friend. I'll wait five for a stranger. The length of time you're willing to wait for someone is a measure of something but I'm not sure it's a measure of love exactly. Maybe trust. Maybe just how much you've already invested in the interaction.

Standing outside a restaurant waiting for someone. Checking your phone. The specific anxiety of not knowing if you've been forgotten or if they're just on the train. Before phones you just stood there. You stood there and you didn't know and you couldn't know and at some point you just left.

At some point you just left. What a sentence. What a way to end a friendship, potentially, through pure ambiguity. They were late, you left, they arrived, you were gone, neither of you could confirm what happened. The whole relationship hanging on a missed window of overlap.
