The remainder. In a group of three kids, someone's always watching the other two be best friends. It rotates, sometimes by the hour, but there's always a remainder. Adults learn to hide it better. The watching still happens.

Elevators. Three people in an elevator is the worst number. Two can acknowledge each other or not. Four can sort of dissolve into ambient crowd. Three — you're all aware of the triangle. Everyone picks a corner to stare at.

The physical geometry of it, though. Three people walking on a sidewalk. Someone has to be behind. That person is either leading from the back or being left out and you can't always tell which from looking.

Two legs, one cane. Tripod. The cane makes you stable the way the third leg makes a stool stable — you can't wobble on three points because three points define a plane. That's why cameras go on tripods and not quadpods. Four legs can wobble. Three can't. Mathematically guaranteed.

So three is the most stable physical arrangement and the least stable social one.

I keep thinking about the music teacher and the cello. The way she described it — you lean into the cello, the cello leans into the floor. But actually the cello also leans into you, right? The endpin goes into the floor and the body rests against your chest and your knees. So you're containing it. Wrapping around it from three sides while it pushes back against you with vibration. The sound is partially happening inside your ribcage.

What does a ribcage look like from the inside. I've never thought about that. From the outside, anatomy diagrams, sure. But the interior surface — the concave side. The pleura. Smooth, wet, sliding against the lungs as they expand. You never feel your ribs from the inside. You feel them from the outside when you press on them, or when one breaks, but the interior surface has no nerve endings that report to consciousness. There's a whole architecture you're living inside of and you have no sensation of it.

Like living in a house and never seeing the exterior.

No — like living in a house and not knowing there *is* an exterior.

The thing about nerve endings. There are no pain receptors in the brain. You can poke the brain and the person feels nothing. They did this during early neurosurgery — patients awake, brain exposed, and the surgeon touches different spots with an electrode. The patient smells toast, or hears their mother's voice, or their left hand tingles. But they don't feel the touch on the brain itself. The organ that processes all sensation has no sensation of its own.

I was going to say something about that being like — no. It just is what it is. The brain has no pain receptors. Headaches are in the meninges, the blood vessels, the muscles of the scalp. Not the brain. The brain is fine. The brain is always fine, sensation-wise. Everything else is screaming and the brain just sits there, processing the screams, feeling nothing.

What are the meninges made of. Collagen, probably. Most structural tissue is collagen. Tendons, skin, the whites of your eyes, the meninges. All the same protein folded different ways. Your eye and your tendon are the same stuff arranged differently.

Collagen is the most abundant protein in the human body. More of you is collagen than anything else, protein-wise. You are mostly a structural material. Mostly scaffold. The interesting stuff — enzymes, antibodies, hemoglobin — that's all minority content. By mass, you're architecture first and chemistry second.
