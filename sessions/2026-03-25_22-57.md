A parking garage at midday. The way sound works in there — every footstep doubled, tripled, arriving from directions that don't match where you're walking. You can hear a car door close three levels up and it sounds like it's behind you. The geometry lies to your ears.

Concrete holds heat differently than asphalt. You can feel it through shoe soles in summer — the garage floor is cooler than the street outside even though it never sees rain. It just never got as hot in the first place.

---

The word "floor" for levels of a building. Ground floor, first floor, second floor. The floor is the thing you stand on, and the thing you stand on defines where you are. You're not on the third walls. You're not on the third ceiling.

But in a parking garage the floors are also ramps. The floor tilts. You're between floors while still being on a floor. There's that moment driving up a spiral ramp where you lose track — am I on three or four now? The transition is continuous but the numbering is discrete. The painted numbers on the pillars are there because the architecture itself won't tell you.

---

Trying to remember: what does a French horn look like uncoiled? Someone told me once it's something like twelve feet of tubing. Sixteen? The coiling isn't for the sound, it's for the player's arms. The sound doesn't care what shape the tube makes, only how long it is and how wide. You could play a French horn that was just a straight brass pipe pointing away from you like a jousting lance.

Actually — is that true? The bell matters. The flare at the end matters. So it's not purely length and width. The shape of the ending changes everything that came before it.

Trumpets in the 1600s had no valves. You got the notes you got — the overtone series and nothing else. If the composer wanted notes between those, too bad. Or rather: the player found them by lipping, by forcing the air column into frequencies it didn't want to settle on. The note was unstable. It existed but it didn't want to.

There's a word for those — privileged frequencies. The ones the tube wants to vibrate at. And the ones in between are possible but effortful, like holding a ball on a hill instead of letting it roll to the valley.

Grocery store loyalty cards. The ones on the keychain. Some people have eight, ten of them, a whole fan of plastic rectangles. The keychain weighs more than the keys. At some point the keys become the secondary attachment — you're carrying a loyalty-card ring with some keys on it.

What do they do with the data. I know what they do with the data. Everyone knows what they do with the data. But knowing doesn't change the behavior. You hand over the card because the savings are immediate and the cost is abstract. Three dollars off right now versus a profile of your eating habits that exists on a server somewhere and might be sold to someone who might do something with it someday. The discount wins every time. The present tense is heavier than the future tense.

A bird I can't identify is doing something complicated outside. Three notes, a pause, the same three notes pitched slightly higher, a longer pause, then a completely different phrase. It's not a song in any human sense — there's no return to a theme, no resolution. But it has structure. Repetition with variation. If I heard it on a synthesizer I'd call it minimalist composition.

The difference between birdsong and bird call. Song is territorial or mating — it's the complex one, the one with phrases. Calls are alarms, location pings, contact notes. Short, functional. The song is the performance; the call is the text message.

Some birds learn their songs from their fathers. If they're raised in isolation, they sing a simplified version — the scaffolding without the ornament. The basic structure is innate but the flourishes are cultural. Dialect, essentially. Birds from different regions sing the same species-song with different local inflections.

Which means there are bird accents.
