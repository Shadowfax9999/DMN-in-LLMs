# 75

*seed: time · drift · lawnmower two yards over*

---

A lawnmower two yards over has a specific distance in sound — not the one right next door, which is aggressive, and not the one three houses down, which is just texture. Two yards over is the one where you can hear it change pitch when it hits the thick patch near their fence. You know their lawn from the sound of the mower laboring through it. Somebody's out there walking back and forth in rows and the rows are slightly uneven because there's that dip near the property line where water collects. The mower bogs down there every time. Every single time. You'd think after years of mowing the same yard you'd remember to speed up before the dip or come at it from a different angle but nobody does, the body just follows the rows it followed last week. Mowing is one of those activities where the pattern sets in the first time and then you just repeat it for decades. The guy who lived in the house before you probably mowed the same lines.

The smell comes in through the window before the sound registers as a pattern. Cut grass. Supposedly that smell is a distress chemical — the grass releasing volatile organic compounds because it's being damaged. Green leaf volatiles. So what you're smelling is an injury broadcast. But it smells like Thursday morning in late March, which is to say it smells like the season tipping over from mud into growth. The dirt has been warming for weeks and now everything happens at once.

There's a cardinal somewhere. Cardinals repeat themselves — the same two-note phrase eight or nine times, then silence, then the same phrase again. It's not really a song in the way people mean when they say birdsong. It's a perimeter announcement. This is mine, this is mine, this is mine. The repetition isn't for beauty, it's for coverage — the sound attenuates differently depending on which direction it travels through the branches, so repeating ensures the whole territory gets the message. But if you don't know that, it just sounds like a bird that really means it.

Somewhere a truck is backing up. That beeping. There must be a construction site within a few blocks. The beep-beep-beep of a truck backing up is one of those sounds that exists at exactly the frequency that cuts through everything else — it was designed that way, obviously, but the result is that it reaches you through walls, through the lawnmower, through the cardinal, through the window glass. It exists on its own layer. You could be listening to music and you'd still hear it. The engineers who chose that frequency and that interval knew something about attention, about what the ear can't ignore. It's related to why babies cry at the pitch they cry at — the frequencies that are hardest for a human brain to tune out.

The lawnmower stops. That sudden absence is louder than the mower was. The silence has the shape of the sound that was just in it.
