The dripping is louder when you're trying to sleep than when you're standing right next to it.

Faucet washers come in a little plastic bag with like six different sizes and you hold each one up to the old one trying to figure out which matches. They all look the same diameter until you try to seat one and it's wrong.

There's a tool called a seat wrench. I think it's for smoothing the valve seat so the new washer seals properly. I've never used one.

Detour signs stopping — that happened to me on a drive through somewhere in Ohio, or maybe it was Indiana. Flat states where the roads are gridded so you think you can just navigate by direction, keep heading west, but then there's a creek or a rail line that bends the grid and suddenly west isn't available.

Grids that aren't actually grids. Manhattan does this too. Broadway cuts diagonally and creates those triangle buildings, the Flatiron. The acute angle at the front of that building is something like 25 degrees. Offices in the narrow end must be strange to sit in.

I wonder what the narrowest usable room is. Hotel rooms in Tokyo can be very narrow. Capsule hotels aren't really rooms at all, they're more like shelves. You climb in. The ceiling is close enough to touch without extending your arm fully.

A coffin is the narrowest room. That's morbid but it's where the thought went.

The width of a hallway in most building codes is 36 inches minimum. That's tight. Two people can't pass without turning sideways. Most hallways are 42 or 48. The 36-inch hallway is for closets and utility access, not for humans walking past each other.

Shoulders are wider than we think they are. Fitting through a gap you turn automatically — you don't decide to turn, the shoulders just rotate as you approach. Like the body has already measured the opening before you consciously registered it was narrow.
