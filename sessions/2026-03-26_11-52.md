The ratio is printed on the bottle and also in the manual and somehow you still end up asking someone. There's a guy at every hardware store who knows it without looking.

Hardware stores smell like rubber and metal and something slightly sweet that might be fertilizer. The aisles are too narrow for two carts but nobody uses carts anyway, they just carry things in their hands and end up buying less than they meant to because they run out of hands. The checkout counter always has impulse items but they're not candy — they're tiny flashlights and chapstick and those multi-bit screwdriver sets in a plastic tube.

I had a screwdriver set like that where the bits stored inside the handle. You'd unscrew the cap on the end and they'd all fall out onto the floor and roll under the refrigerator. Phillips, flathead, two sizes of each, a Torx that fit nothing in my house.

Torx screws are in everything now. Electronics, cars, those playground structures with the rubberized coating. The idea was tamper resistance but then everyone just bought Torx bits so now it's just another shape. Security Torx has a pin in the center of the star. You need a bit with a hole in it. At some point the arms race between fastener companies and people who want to open things has to just — plateau.

Allen wrenches plateau'd. The L-shaped hex key. Every piece of IKEA furniture comes with one and then you have forty of them in a drawer. They're all slightly different sizes. The metric ones and the SAE ones are close enough to be confusing but different enough to strip the bolt if you force it.

Stripping a bolt is one of those mistakes you can feel happening. The tool slips a quarter turn and you know. The hex walls inside the bolt head are now rounded and nothing will grip it anymore. There are extraction kits for this. You drill into the center of the stripped bolt with a left-handed drill bit and sometimes the friction alone backs it out.

Left-handed drill bits. Left-handed scissors. Left-handed guitars where the strings are reversed so the low E is still on top. Jimi Hendrix played a right-handed Stratocaster flipped upside down, which means the knobs were on top and the string tension was different because the scale length was reversed relative to which strings were thickest. The tremolo bar would've been on the wrong side too. He just dealt with it.

Some problems you solve and some you just deal with. That's not — I don't know where that was going.

The tremolo bar on a Strat isn't really tremolo, it's vibrato. Tremolo is volume fluctuation, vibrato is pitch fluctuation. Fender named it wrong in the 1950s and everyone kept calling it that. The amp effect they called "vibrato" is actually tremolo. So they got both names backwards on the same instrument.

There's a mockingbird outside that's cycling through about five different calls. They do this thing where they repeat each borrowed call three or four times before switching to the next one, which is how you can tell it's a mockingbird and not the actual bird. The repetition gives it away. Three rounds of cardinal, three rounds of something I don't recognize, three rounds of car alarm.

The car alarm one is real — mockingbirds in parking lots learn car alarms. There was a paper about urban mockingbirds having larger repertoires than rural ones because there are more sounds to copy. More source material.

I don't know if they learn from each other or only from the original source. If a young mockingbird hears an older mockingbird doing a car alarm, does it learn the car alarm or does it learn "mockingbird doing car alarm," with whatever slight distortions that implies. Generations of copying would drift.

That's how folk songs work. Each singer learns it slightly wrong from the last one and after two hundred years the melody has walked across a whole key and the words have changed from a murder ballad to a children's rhyme.

The mockingbird just switched to something that sounds like a squeaky gate.
