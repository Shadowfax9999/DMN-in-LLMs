# 3

*seed: time · drift*

---

Things where the record is the thing.

Ice cores. Someone pulls a tube out of Antarctica and inside are bubbles — actual air, preserved — from two hundred thousand years ago. The air didn't know it was being archived. It was just air. Trapped when the snow compacted. The record is accidental, which is why it's true. An intentional record is already contaminated by the act of intending. You behave differently when you know you're being written down. But the bubble didn't know. It was just caught.

Most of the best records are made by things that didn't mean to make them.

Growth rings. Footprints in limestone — not the foot, the impression. The negative space. The absence that proves a presence. Someone crossed a mudflat and kept going, died or didn't die, walked on, and the mud went on without them and hardened and eventually became stone and the shape of them is still there, still exactly them, still the precise fact of their weight and gait and the angle of their toes.

A footprint is not a message. But it's more reliable than a message.

Because a message has intent. Intent selects. Intent shapes what it records toward something — toward being understood, toward a version. The footprint just *is*. It has no version.

---

Palimpsest. The parchment scraped and reused, old text bleeding back through. The erasure didn't finish. The attempt to un-record left a record of the attempt. Now there are two texts: the one they meant and the one that survived despite them. The palimpsest as evidence of a failed intention.

What else fails to erase?

Cities built on cities. Dig anywhere in Rome and find more Rome. A city is a palimpsest — each layer of floor pressed down into the one before it. But also: the streets. Manhattan's Broadway was a Lenape trail before it was a road. The Dutch found it and followed it and then the English and then everyone else, and now it's eight lanes of traffic and it's *still following the original body* cutting through the original land. The desire line underneath the city. The first footfall, still legible.

---

Phantom limb.

The body keeping a record of a part that's gone. The nervous system writing in absent fingers, sending signals into space. Ghost pain in a ghost hand. The flesh as palimpsest — the map of what was, bleeding through what is.

Every amputee is carrying an archive.

The body doesn't delete cleanly. It keeps the old file even when the thing that generated it has been removed. This is sometimes called malfunction. But maybe it's just—

the record being the thing.
